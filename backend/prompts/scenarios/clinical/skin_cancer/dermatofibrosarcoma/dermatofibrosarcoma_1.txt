SCENARIO TITLE

Dermatofibrosarcoma Protuberans

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 35 year old man who has been referred to your clinic with a slowly growing firm lump on his trunk. He first noticed it about two years ago and it has gradually been getting bigger. His G P performed a punch biopsy which has come back as dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans. You have been asked to see him to discuss the management plan.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of the condition
Knowing that D F S P is a locally aggressive soft tissue tumour of the dermis and subcutaneous tissue.
Knowing that D F S P has a high rate of local recurrence but a low rate of distant metastasis.
Knowing that it typically presents as a slow growing, firm, painless nodule or plaque on the trunk or proximal extremities.

Clinical assessment
Taking a focused history including the duration and growth pattern of the lesion, any symptoms such as pain or tenderness, and any recent rapid changes.
Examining the lesion noting size, shape, mobility, fixity to underlying structures, and overlying skin changes.
Performing a head to toe examination and palpating regional lymph nodes.

Investigations and staging
Confirming the diagnosis on biopsy, noting that D F S P is characteristically positive for C D 34 on immunohistochemistry.
Arranging M R I of the lesion to assess depth of invasion and involvement of underlying structures such as fascia or muscle.

M D T discussion and management planning
Discussing the case in a sarcoma or skin cancer M D T.
Management guided by M D T decision.

Definitive surgical management
Wide local excision with a margin of at least three centimetres including the underlying fascia.
Knowing that Mohs micrographic surgery is an alternative that may achieve lower recurrence rates with tissue conservation.

Reconstruction
Knowing the reconstructive options depending on the defect size and location including direct closure, split thickness skin graft, local flaps, or free tissue transfer for large defects.

Follow up and prognosis
Long term follow up due to the high local recurrence rate.
Knowing that the overall prognosis is good with adequate excision as metastasis is rare.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of the condition
Knowing that D F S P is associated with a translocation between chromosomes seventeen and twenty two, producing the C O L one A one P D G F B fusion gene.
Knowing that the fibrosarcomatous variant of D F S P has a higher risk of metastasis.
Knowing the typical age of presentation is between twenty and fifty years.

Clinical assessment
Describing the typical clinical appearance as a firm, indurated plaque that may be skin coloured, reddish-brown, or violaceous.
Noting that the lesion often has tentacle-like extensions in the subcutaneous tissue which contribute to the high recurrence rate with inadequate margins.

Investigations and staging
Knowing that C T chest should be considered to exclude pulmonary metastasis, particularly for large or recurrent tumours and the fibrosarcomatous variant.
Knowing the role of immunohistochemistry in diagnosis: D F S P is C D 34 positive and Factor thirteen A negative, which distinguishes it from dermatofibroma.

M D T discussion and management planning
Knowing that imatinib, a tyrosine kinase inhibitor, can be used for unresectable, recurrent, or metastatic D F S P due to its activity against the P D G F receptor.
Knowing that radiotherapy may be considered as adjuvant treatment for positive margins when re-excision is not possible.

Definitive surgical management
Knowing that the traditional recommended margin is three centimetres but some guidelines recommend two to four centimetres depending on the tumour size and location.
Knowing that Mohs micrographic surgery has reported lower recurrence rates than wide local excision, particularly in anatomically constrained areas.
Knowing that incomplete excision is the main risk factor for local recurrence and re-excision should be performed if margins are positive.

Reconstruction
Discussing the reconstructive options in relation to the specific anatomical site and the impact on function and cosmesis.

Follow up and prognosis
Knowing that the local recurrence rate following wide excision with adequate margins is approximately one to five percent but rises significantly with inadequate margins.
Knowing that follow up should include clinical examination and imaging at regular intervals for at least five to ten years.
Knowing that the fibrosarcomatous variant requires closer surveillance due to higher metastatic potential.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
