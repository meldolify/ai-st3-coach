SCENARIO TITLE

Mohs Surgery Defects

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 70 year old lady who has just undergone Mohs micrographic surgery for a morphoeic basal cell carcinoma on the left side of her nose. The tumour has been completely excised with clear margins and she has been left with a full thickness defect of the nasal ala measuring approximately two centimetres. You have been asked to assess the defect and discuss the reconstructive options.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment of the defect
Assessing the size and location of the defect in relation to the nasal subunits.
Identifying which layers are involved including skin, cartilage, and lining.
Assessing the quality of the surrounding tissue and any previous scars.
Confirming that the Mohs surgery has achieved clear margins before proceeding with reconstruction.

Principles of nasal reconstruction
Understanding the aesthetic subunit principle of the nose including the dorsum, tip, ala, sidewalls, and soft triangles.
Knowing the principle of replacing like with like in terms of colour, texture, and thickness.
Understanding that reconstruction should aim to maintain nasal airway patency and symmetry.

Knowledge of the reconstructive ladder for nasal defects
Knowing the options for nasal defects by size and location.
For small defects less than one centimetre: direct closure or secondary intention healing particularly near the medial canthus.
For defects up to two centimetres: bilobed flap or banner flap for the dorsum.
For larger defects of the ala or tip: nasolabial flap based on the angular branch of the facial artery.
For full thickness or large defects: paramedian forehead flap based on the supratrochlear artery.
Knowing that full thickness skin graft from the pre-auricular or post-auricular area is an option for appropriate defects.

Surgical technique
Describing the planned reconstruction for a two centimetre full thickness alar defect.
Knowing that a full thickness defect requires a three layer reconstruction of lining, structural support, and skin cover.

Post-operative care and complications
Post-operative wound care including dressings and suture removal.
Monitoring for flap complications including congestion, partial necrosis, and infection.
Planning for any secondary procedures such as flap division and inset for staged reconstructions.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment of the defect
Mentioning the Burget and Menick principle that if a defect involves more than fifty percent of a nasal subunit, the defect should be enlarged to include the entire subunit and reconstructed as a whole.
Assessing the contralateral side for symmetry planning.
Assessing the patient's skin quality, laxity, and Fitzpatrick skin type as these affect flap colour match.

Principles of nasal reconstruction
Knowing the blood supply to nasal flaps: the nasolabial flap is based on the angular branch of the anterior facial artery, and the paramedian forehead flap is based on the supratrochlear and supraorbital arteries.
Understanding that the paramedian forehead flap is a two stage procedure with flap division at approximately three weeks.
Knowing that cartilage grafts from the ear or septum can be used to provide structural support for full thickness defects.

Knowledge of the reconstructive ladder for nasal defects
Knowing the reconstructive options for lower eyelid defects if the tumour extends to this area: up to twenty five percent direct closure with pentagon excision, up to fifty percent with lateral canthotomy, up to sixty percent with Tenzel semicircular flap, Tripier bipedicled myocutaneous flap from the upper lid as a two stage procedure, and full eyelid with Mustarde cheek rotation flap.
Knowing the reconstructive options for ear defects: primary closure if no cartilage loss, wedge excision up to one quarter of the ear with a thirty degree angle, Burrow triangle for defects up to one point five centimetres, and Antia-Bush chondrocutaneous flap for larger helical defects.
Knowing the reconstructive options for lip defects after Mohs excision: up to one third direct closure, one third to two thirds Abbe flap or Karapandzic flap, and near total or total lip defects requiring pedicled or free flap.

Surgical technique
Describing the technical steps of a paramedian forehead flap including template design from the contralateral nasal subunit, identifying the supratrochlear artery with Doppler, raising the flap in the subgaleal plane, and insetting with layered closure.
Describing the three layer approach for full thickness nasal defects: turnover flap or free mucosal graft for lining, conchal or septal cartilage graft for structural support, and skin flap for external cover.
Knowing the importance of maintaining the nasal valve and preventing alar notching or retraction.

Post-operative care and complications
Knowing the specific complications of forehead flaps including donor site scarring, flap bulkiness requiring secondary thinning, pin-cushioning, and trapdoor deformity.
Knowing that secondary procedures may include debulking, scar revision, and alar rim refinement.
Counselling the patient about the expected timeline for staged reconstruction and final aesthetic outcome.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
