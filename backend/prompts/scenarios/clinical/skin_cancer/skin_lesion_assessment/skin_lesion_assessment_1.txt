SCENARIO TITLE

Skin Lesion Assessment

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 58 year old lady who has been referred to your clinic by her G P with an ulcerated lesion on her left lower leg. It has been present for several months and appears to be getting bigger. She has a background of type two diabetes and varicose veins. You have been asked to assess her and formulate a management plan.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Differential diagnosis
Generating a structured differential diagnosis for a chronic ulcerated lesion of the lower limb including venous ulcer, Marjolin ulcer arising in a chronic wound, arterial ulcer, neuropathic ulcer, S C C, B C C, and pyoderma gangrenosum.
Recognising that a chronic non-healing ulcer that is getting bigger requires biopsy to exclude malignancy.

History and risk factors
Taking a focused history of the lesion including duration, evolution, and symptoms such as pain, bleeding, itching, or discharge.
Asking about risk factors and aetiology including previous ulcers in either leg, varicose veins, deep vein thrombosis, peripheral vascular disease, diabetes, previous surgery or trauma to the area, previous burns, and chronic inflammatory conditions such as Crohn disease.
General medical history including smoking, drug history, allergies, and social history including mobility and independence.

Systematic examination of the lesion
Examining the ulcer systematically including the base, edge, discharge, and any visible structures.
Knowing the significance of edge morphology: sloping edges suggest venous ulcer, punched out edges suggest arterial ulcer, rolled edges suggest B C C, and everted edges suggest S C C or Marjolin ulcer.
Palpating the regional lymph nodes for lymphadenopathy.
Performing a vascular examination including palpation of peripheral pulses and Doppler assessment if pulses are not palpable.

Investigations and biopsy
Performing a punch biopsy under local anaesthetic to obtain tissue diagnosis.
Sending microbiology swabs and tissue for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity.
Routine bloods including full blood count, urea and electrolytes, C R P, and nutritional markers such as albumin, magnesium, and phosphate.

M D T discussion and management planning
Discussing the case in a skin cancer M D T if malignancy is confirmed.
Understanding that Marjolin ulcer is an aggressive S C C arising in a chronic wound and requires urgent management.

Definitive surgical management and reconstruction
Wide local excision with appropriate margins. For Marjolin ulcer or high risk S C C, one centimetre margins down to fascia.
Reconstructive options based on the defect and patient comorbidities including negative pressure wound therapy, split thickness skin graft if no exposed structures, or local flap.

Follow up
Regular follow up to monitor for recurrence.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Differential diagnosis
Giving a comprehensive differential including vascular causes such as arterial and venous ulcers, neuropathic causes, traumatic causes such as pressure sores and burns, infective causes such as syphilitic ulcer, inflammatory causes such as pyoderma gangrenosum, and neoplastic causes including B C C, S C C, and Marjolin ulcer.

History and risk factors
Asking specifically about chronic venous insufficiency, previous D V T, and history of varicose vein surgery.
Inquiring about social history including mobility and level of independence.
Assessing the nutritional status of the patient.

Systematic examination of the lesion
Describing the ulcer in systematic detail including site, size, shape, base, edge, discharge, depth, and surrounding skin changes.
Examining for signs of chronic venous disease including varicose veins, haemosiderin staining, lipodermatosclerosis, and venous eczema.
Examining for signs of arterial disease including absent pulses, cold extremity, and hair loss.
Performing an ankle brachial pressure index measurement.

Investigations and biopsy
Considering imaging such as M R I of the lower limb to assess depth of invasion and exclude osteomyelitis.
Arranging imaging to assess the vascular status such as duplex ultrasound for venous disease.
Checking H B A one C in a diabetic patient.

M D T discussion and management planning
Knowing that Marjolin ulcer has a higher rate of metastasis than primary S C C and requires close follow up.
Considering sentinel lymph node biopsy for high risk lesions.
Involving the multidisciplinary team including vascular surgery, tissue viability, and dietetics.

Definitive surgical management and reconstruction
Explaining that the specimen should be sent to pathology with orientation to confirm complete excision with clear margins.
Knowing that reconstruction should be delayed until final histology confirms clear margins.
Knowing that if margins are positive, further excision or M D T discussion is required.

Follow up
Advising the patient on wound care and prevention of further ulceration.
Knowing the higher recurrence risk of Marjolin ulcer compared with primary S C C.
Arranging vascular follow up for the underlying venous disease.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
