SCENARIO TITLE

Basal Cell Carcinoma

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 68 year old gentleman who has been referred to your clinic by his G P with a slowly growing lesion on the tip of his nose. He first noticed it about a year ago and it has gradually been getting bigger. You have been asked to assess him and formulate a management plan.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis and clinical assessment
Recognising the clinical features of a B C C including pearly appearance, rolled edges, central ulceration, and telangiectasia.
Differential diagnosis including B C C, S C C, amelanotic melanoma, keratoacanthoma, and actinic keratosis.

History and risk factors
Taking a focused history of the lesion including duration, evolution, and symptoms such as pain, bleeding, or itching.
Asking about risk factors for B C C including exposure to U V radiation particularly at a young age, history of severe sunburns, Fitzpatrick skin type one or two, immunosuppression, and personal or family history of skin cancer.

Examination of the lesion
Close examination of the lesion noting size, colour, edges, ulceration, and surface features.
Performing a head to toe skin examination to identify any other suspicious lesions.
Palpating the relevant lymph node basins to exclude lymphadenopathy.

Investigations and biopsy
Performing a punch biopsy under local anaesthetic to confirm tissue diagnosis.
Sending to pathology for histological subtype, perineural infiltration, and depth of invasion.
Knowing the histological subtypes of B C C including nodular, superficial spreading, morphoeic, pigmented, infiltrative, and micronodular.

M D T discussion and management planning
Discussing the case in a skin cancer M D T meeting particularly for lesions in cosmetically sensitive areas.
Management guided by B A D guidelines for B C C.

Definitive surgical management and reconstruction
Excision with appropriate margins as per B A D guidelines. For low risk B C C a four millimetre peripheral margin is standard. For high risk B C C a five millimetre peripheral margin is required.
Excision should include a cuff of fat or down to the next clear anatomical plane.
Knowing the reconstructive options for nasal tip defects including direct closure, full thickness skin graft, and local flaps.

Follow up
Regular follow up given the significant risk of developing a second primary B C C.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis and clinical assessment
Mentioning dermatoscopy as an adjunct to clinical examination.
Recognising that morphoeic B C C has indistinct margins with a flat, yellowish, scar-like appearance which is more aggressive.
Knowing the difference between low risk and high risk B C C subtypes, with morphoeic, infiltrative, and micronodular being high risk.
Knowing the B A D risk stratification criteria based on location and size: Area L lesions up to twenty millimetres, Area M lesions up to ten millimetres are low risk. Area H lesions of any size are high risk. Recurrent B C C, immunosuppression, and a site of prior radiotherapy are also high risk factors.
Knowing that basosquamous differentiation with or without lymphovascular invasion, perineural invasion, and depth greater than six millimetres are pathological high risk features.

History and risk factors
Asking about syndromes associated with B C C including Gorlin syndrome, xeroderma pigmentosum, and albinism.
Asking about previous skin cancers and any prior treatment to the area.
General medical fitness including comorbidities and drug history that would affect surgical management.

Examination of the lesion
Considering the use of dermatoscopy to aid diagnosis.
Noting the specific nasal subunit affected to guide reconstructive planning.
Examining for visual disturbance if the lesion is near the eye.

Investigations and biopsy
Stating that multiple punch biopsies may be needed for larger lesions, particularly at the periphery and centre, to accurately characterise the subtype.
Knowing that incisional biopsy incorporating normal and abnormal skin is appropriate for cosmetically sensitive areas.

M D T discussion and management planning
Knowing that Mohs micrographic surgery should be considered for B C C in cosmetically sensitive areas and for high risk subtypes, particularly morphoeic B C C.
Explaining the principle of Mohs surgery as sequential horizontal excision using a topographic map with repeated excision until all margins are clear.

Definitive surgical management and reconstruction
Mentioning the Burget and Menick principle that if a defect occupies more than fifty percent of a nasal subunit, the defect should be enlarged to occupy the whole subunit and reconstructed as a whole.
Knowing the reconstructive ladder for nasal defects: direct closure for small defects, bilobed flap for defects up to two centimetres, banner flap for dorsal defects, nasolabial flap, and paramedian forehead flap for larger or full thickness defects.
Knowing the reconstructive ladder for lower eyelid defects: up to twenty five percent direct closure with pentagon excision, up to fifty percent with lateral canthotomy, up to sixty percent with Tenzel semicircular flap, and full eyelid with Mustarde cheek rotation flap.
Knowing what to do if the excision is incomplete: discuss in M D T with options including re-excision or Mohs, radiotherapy, watchful waiting with three monthly review, or topical agents such as Efudix for low risk superficial B C C only.
Explaining the surgical technique including marking margins, excision down to an appropriate depth, haemostasis, and layered closure with deep dermal and skin sutures.

Follow up
Stating that patients with B C C have up to a forty four percent three year risk of developing a second primary B C C.
Advising the patient on sun protection and self monitoring of skin.
Knowing that patients treated for recurrent disease or with a history of multiple B C C s require longer follow up.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
