SCENARIO TITLE

Merkel Cell Carcinoma

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 75 year old gentleman who has been referred to your clinic with a rapidly growing painless lump on his left cheek. It has appeared over the past three months and has been getting noticeably bigger. His G P performed a punch biopsy which has come back as Merkel cell carcinoma. You have been asked to see him to discuss the management plan.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of the condition
Knowing that Merkel cell carcinoma is a rare, aggressive neuroendocrine tumour of the skin.
Knowing that it has a high rate of local recurrence, regional lymph node metastasis, and distant metastasis.
Knowing the typical presentation as a rapidly growing, painless, firm, dome-shaped nodule that is often red or violaceous, typically on sun-exposed skin in elderly patients.

Clinical assessment
Taking a focused history including the duration, rate of growth, and any symptoms.
Asking about risk factors including advanced age, immunosuppression, excessive U V exposure, and Merkel cell polyomavirus infection.
Examining the lesion noting size, shape, colour, fixity, and surface features.
Palpating the regional lymph nodes for lymphadenopathy, in this case the cervical and pre-auricular nodes.
Performing a head to toe skin examination.

Investigations and staging
Confirming the diagnosis on biopsy with immunohistochemistry showing positivity for C K 20 in a perinuclear dot pattern and negativity for thyroid transcription factor one which distinguishes it from small cell lung cancer.
Arranging staging C T of the head, chest, abdomen, and pelvis to exclude distant metastasis.
Arranging ultrasound of the regional lymph node basin if nodes are not clinically palpable.

M D T discussion and management planning
Discussing the case in a skin cancer M D T or sarcoma M D T.
Management guided by M D T decision.

Definitive surgical management
Wide local excision with a margin of one to two centimetres where anatomically possible.
Knowing that margins may be modified in cosmetically sensitive areas such as the face.

Role of sentinel lymph node biopsy
Knowing that S L N B is recommended for clinically node negative Merkel cell carcinoma to stage the regional lymph nodes.
Knowing that a positive S L N B upstages the patient and may indicate the need for completion lymph node dissection and adjuvant therapy.

Adjuvant therapy and follow up
Knowing that adjuvant radiotherapy to the primary site and or the regional lymph node basin is often recommended.
Regular follow up with clinical examination given the high recurrence rate.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of the condition
Knowing the A E I O U mnemonic for clinical features: Asymptomatic, Expanding rapidly, Immunosuppression, Older than fifty, U V exposed site.
Knowing that Merkel cell polyomavirus is detected in approximately eighty percent of Merkel cell carcinomas.
Knowing that immunosuppressed patients including organ transplant recipients have a significantly increased risk.

Clinical assessment
Recognising that the clinical differential diagnosis includes B C C, S C C, amelanotic melanoma, cutaneous lymphoma, and skin metastasis from an internal malignancy.

Investigations and staging
Knowing the A J C C staging system for Merkel cell carcinoma.
Considering P E T C T for comprehensive staging particularly in locally advanced or recurrent disease.
Knowing that serum markers are not routinely available for Merkel cell carcinoma.

M D T discussion and management planning
Knowing the role of the clinical oncologist in planning adjuvant radiotherapy.
Understanding that immunotherapy with checkpoint inhibitors such as avelumab or pembrolizumab has shown significant activity in advanced Merkel cell carcinoma.

Definitive surgical management
Knowing that Mohs micrographic surgery may be considered in cosmetically sensitive areas to achieve margin control with tissue conservation.
Knowing that the primary tumour site should ideally receive adjuvant radiotherapy even after complete excision given the high local recurrence rate.

Role of sentinel lymph node biopsy
Knowing that approximately one third of clinically node negative patients will have a positive sentinel node.
Knowing that a positive S L N B significantly worsens the prognosis and guides adjuvant therapy decisions.
Understanding the role of completion lymph node dissection versus adjuvant radiotherapy to the nodal basin after a positive S L N B.

Adjuvant therapy and follow up
Knowing that adjuvant radiotherapy reduces local and regional recurrence rates.
Knowing that chemotherapy has limited long term efficacy in Merkel cell carcinoma and immunotherapy is now preferred for advanced disease.
Knowing that avelumab is approved as first line treatment for metastatic Merkel cell carcinoma.
Stating that follow up should be frequent, typically every three to six months for the first three years, given the high recurrence rate with the majority of recurrences occurring within two years.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
