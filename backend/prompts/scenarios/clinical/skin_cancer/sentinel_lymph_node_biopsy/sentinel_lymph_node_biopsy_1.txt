SCENARIO TITLE

Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 45 year old man who had an excision biopsy of a pigmented lesion on his right calf two weeks ago. The pathology has confirmed a malignant melanoma with a Breslow thickness of one point eight millimetres, no ulceration, and a mitotic rate of two per millimetre squared. He has been discussed in the skin cancer M D T and you have been asked to see him in clinic to discuss the next steps.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Staging and classification
Staging this melanoma as at least stage one B or stage two A using the A J C C classification based on the Breslow thickness of one point eight millimetres.
Knowing the key prognostic factors from the pathology report including Breslow thickness, ulceration, and mitotic rate.

Clinical assessment
Examining the biopsy scar and surrounding skin on the right calf.
Palpating the inguinal lymph nodes bilaterally for lymphadenopathy.
Performing a head to toe skin examination to identify any other suspicious lesions.

Indications for sentinel lymph node biopsy
Knowing that S L N B is indicated for melanomas staged p T one B and above, which includes Breslow thickness of nought point eight to one millimetre with ulceration, or any melanoma with Breslow thickness greater than one millimetre regardless of ulceration.
Stating that this patient meets the criteria for S L N B based on a Breslow thickness of one point eight millimetres.
Knowing that S L N B is a staging and prognostic procedure, not a therapeutic procedure.

Patient counselling and consent
Explaining to the patient that S L N B helps determine whether the melanoma has spread to the nearest lymph nodes.
Explaining that the result helps guide further treatment and provides important prognostic information.
Discussing the risks of the procedure including seroma, wound infection, lymphoedema, and nerve injury.

Surgical technique of sentinel lymph node biopsy
Stating that S L N B is performed at the same time as the wide local excision under general anaesthetic.
Knowing that preoperative lymphoscintigraphy is performed to identify the sentinel node using a radioactive tracer injected around the biopsy scar.
Using a combination of the radioactive tracer and blue dye injected at the time of surgery to identify the sentinel node intraoperatively.
Using a handheld gamma probe to locate the sentinel node in the relevant lymph node basin.

Management of a positive sentinel node
Knowing that a positive S L N B result means the patient is at least stage three.
Stating that the patient should undergo further staging with C T head, chest, abdomen, and pelvis before any further management is decided.
Knowing that the case must be re-discussed in the M D T to decide between completion lymph node dissection and surveillance with or without adjuvant therapy.

Follow up and surveillance
Regular clinical follow up with examination of the scar and regional lymph nodes.
Patient education on self examination of skin and lymph nodes.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Staging and classification
Knowing the full A J C C staging system for melanoma and being able to classify this patient's disease stage.
Stating that C T imaging is not recommended for stage one and two melanoma but is indicated for stage three B and above.
Knowing that serum lactate dehydrogenase should be measured in suspected stage four disease.

Clinical assessment
Checking the pathology report in detail and confirming the specimen was adequately excised with clear margins from the initial excision biopsy.
Knowing the wide local excision margins required: for this patient with Breslow thickness of one point eight millimetres, a one to two centimetre margin is required.

Indications for sentinel lymph node biopsy
Citing the Morton study demonstrating that S L N B is a safe and accurate staging procedure.
Knowing that S L N B provides important prognostic information with patients with a positive sentinel node having significantly worse five year survival than those with a negative node.
Understanding that there is ongoing debate about whether S L N B improves overall survival versus providing prognostic information alone.

Patient counselling and consent
Explaining that approximately one in five patients with melanoma in this Breslow thickness range will have a positive sentinel node.
Discussing that a negative S L N B does not guarantee the absence of micrometastatic disease.
Counselling that the procedure may cause temporary blue discolouration of the skin and urine from the patent blue dye.

Surgical technique of sentinel lymph node biopsy
Knowing that lymphoscintigraphy is typically performed the morning of surgery or the day before.
Understanding that the sentinel node is the first node in the lymphatic drainage basin and is the most likely site of early metastasis.
Knowing that all hot or blue nodes should be excised and sent separately for pathological analysis.
Stating that the specimen should undergo serial sectioning and immunohistochemistry for accurate assessment.

Management of a positive sentinel node
Knowing the options after a positive S L N B including completion lymph node dissection for the relevant basin.
For a positive inguinal sentinel node, knowing the difference between superficial inguinal lymph node dissection and pelvic clearance, and the indications for each.
Awareness of adjuvant therapy options including immunotherapy such as pembrolizumab or nivolumab, and targeted therapy such as dabrafenib and trametinib for B R A F positive tumours.
Knowing the M S L T two trial which showed no survival difference between immediate completion lymph node dissection and observation with ultrasonographic monitoring.

Follow up and surveillance
Stating the recommended follow up schedule based on stage, with more frequent reviews for higher stage disease.
Knowing that patients should be followed up for at least five years with clinical examination.
Advising on sun protection and skin cancer prevention.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
