SCENARIO TITLE

Lymph Node Dissection

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 55 year old man who had a melanoma excised from his right upper arm eight months ago. He has now presented with a new pigmented lesion near the previous scar and a palpable lump in his right axilla. He has been referred to you urgently. You have been asked to assess him and formulate a management plan.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis and assessment of recurrent or metastatic melanoma
Recognising that a new lesion near a previous melanoma scar with palpable lymphadenopathy is highly suspicious for melanoma recurrence with regional metastasis.
Treating this as an urgent cancer pathway referral.

History and examination
Taking a focused history including details of the original melanoma, its Breslow thickness, staging, and any previous treatment including sentinel lymph node biopsy.
Asking about the new lesion and the timing and progression of the axillary lump.
Examining the new pigmented lesion using the A B C D E approach.
Examining the axilla thoroughly for the size, number, and fixity of palpable lymph nodes.
Performing a head to toe skin examination and examining all lymph node basins.

Investigations and staging
Arranging ultrasound of the axilla with fine needle aspiration or core biopsy of the palpable node.
Performing an excision biopsy of the new pigmented lesion with a two millimetre margin.
Knowing that a patient with confirmed regional lymph node disease is at least stage three and requires staging C T of the head, chest, abdomen, and pelvis.
Checking serum lactate dehydrogenase.

M D T discussion and management planning
Discussing the case in the skin cancer M D T with pathology, radiology, oncology, and dermatology input.
Management guided by M D T decision and B A D guidelines.

Principles and types of lymph node dissection
Knowing the indication for lymph node dissection: confirmed regional lymph node metastasis on cytology, biopsy, or imaging.
Knowing the different types of axillary dissection: levels one to three with or without resection of pectoralis minor.

Surgical technique
Performing axillary lymph node dissection under general anaesthetic.
Identifying and preserving the long thoracic nerve and thoracodorsal nerve where possible.
Sending the specimen for histological analysis.

Post-operative care and adjuvant therapy
Post-operative wound care including drain management.
Physiotherapy for shoulder rehabilitation.
Discussing adjuvant therapy options in M D T.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis and assessment of recurrent or metastatic melanoma
Stating that melanoma recurrence is a clinical diagnosis and or a life threatening condition requiring urgent management.
Knowing the difference between local recurrence, in-transit metastasis, and regional lymph node metastasis.

History and examination
Asking about systemic symptoms such as weight loss, fatigue, or bone pain that might suggest distant metastasis.
Asking about the original treatment including whether a sentinel lymph node biopsy was performed and its result.

Investigations and staging
Knowing the A J C C staging system and being able to stage this patient based on the findings.
Knowing when P E T C T might be indicated in addition to or instead of conventional C T staging.
Arranging a biopsy of the new skin lesion and the lymph node simultaneously to confirm the diagnosis.

M D T discussion and management planning
Knowing the role of the oncologist in planning adjuvant or neoadjuvant therapy.
Understanding the role of neoadjuvant immunotherapy in advanced melanoma before surgery.

Principles and types of lymph node dissection
Knowing the types of neck dissection for head and neck melanoma: radical dissection of levels one to five, modified radical sparing the spinal accessory nerve, internal jugular vein and sternocleidomastoid, and extended radical including the parotid or posterior occipital chain.
Knowing the types of inguinal dissection: superficial inguinal lymph node dissection for a single clinical node or single positive sentinel node, and pelvic clearance for more than one palpable node, imaging evidence of multiple nodes, involvement of Cloquet node, or conglomerate lymph nodes.
Understanding the principle that surgery is the treatment of choice for locoregional disease.

Surgical technique
Knowing the anatomy of the axilla including the axillary vein, long thoracic nerve, thoracodorsal nerve and vessels, and the boundaries of the dissection.
Describing management of in-transit metastases including surgical excision, C O two laser ablation for multiple lesions, isolated limb infusion or perfusion, and electrochemotherapy.

Post-operative care and adjuvant therapy
Knowing the complications of lymph node dissection including seroma, lymphoedema, nerve injury causing winged scapula or shoulder weakness, wound infection, and skin flap necrosis.
Awareness of adjuvant immunotherapy options including checkpoint inhibitors such as pembrolizumab and nivolumab.
Awareness of targeted therapy with B R A F and M E K inhibitors for B R A F positive melanoma.
Knowing that palliative options for stage four disease include metastasectomy for isolated disease, systemic therapy, and radiotherapy.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
