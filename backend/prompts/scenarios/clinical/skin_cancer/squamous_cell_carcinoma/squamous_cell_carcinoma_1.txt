SCENARIO TITLE

Squamous Cell Carcinoma

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 72 year old gentleman who has been referred to your clinic with a lesion on his right temple that has been growing over the past few months. He is a retired builder who spent most of his career working outdoors. You have been asked to assess him and formulate a management plan.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis and clinical assessment
Recognising the clinical features of an S C C including a raised, erythematous, ulcerated lesion with a keratinised surface.
Differential diagnosis including S C C, B C C, amelanotic melanoma, keratoacanthoma, actinic keratosis, and Bowen disease.

History and risk factors
Taking a focused history of the lesion including duration, evolution, and symptoms such as pain, bleeding, or itching.
Asking about risk factors for S C C including exposure to U V radiation particularly occupational exposure, history of severe sunburns at a young age, Fitzpatrick skin type one or two, immunosuppression, and personal or family history of skin cancer.

Examination of the lesion
Close examination of the lesion noting size, colour, edges, ulceration, and surface features.
Performing a head to toe skin examination to identify any other suspicious lesions.
Palpating the relevant lymph node basins for lymphadenopathy, particularly the pre-auricular and post-auricular nodes and the cervical chain for a temple lesion.

Investigations and biopsy
Performing a punch biopsy under local anaesthetic to confirm tissue diagnosis.
Sending to pathology for tumour grade, degree of differentiation, depth, and perineural infiltration.

M D T discussion and management planning
Discussing the case in a skin cancer M D T meeting.
Management guided by B A D guidelines for S C C.

Definitive surgical management and reconstruction
Wide local excision with margins determined by risk stratification.
For low risk S C C less than two centimetres with no high risk features, a four millimetre peripheral margin is standard.
For high risk S C C greater than two centimetres, or with high tumour grade, perineural invasion, or poor differentiation, a six millimetre margin is required.
Excision down to an appropriate depth preserving important underlying structures.
Knowing the reconstructive options including direct closure, full thickness skin graft, and local flaps.

Follow up
Regular follow up to monitor for local recurrence and new primary lesions.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis and clinical assessment
Mentioning dermatoscopy as an adjunct to clinical examination.
Knowing the T N M 8 staging for cutaneous S C C: T one is less than or equal to two centimetres, T two is greater than two to four centimetres, T three is greater than four centimetres or with minor bone erosion or specified perineural invasion or depth greater than six millimetres, and T four involves gross bone or skull base invasion.
Knowing that high risk features include size greater than two centimetres, tumour grade two or above, perineural invasion of a nerve greater than nought point one millimetres diameter, depth greater than six millimetres or beyond subcutaneous fat, and location on the ear or lip.
Mentioning that S C C has an eighteen percent risk of recurrence.

History and risk factors
Asking about exposure to carcinogens such as arsenic.
Asking about viral infections including H P V and herpes.
Asking about prior radiation exposure to the area.
Knowing that immunosuppressed patients such as transplant recipients have a significantly higher risk of S C C.
Asking about the war compensation fund for ex-veterans with occupational sun exposure.

Examination of the lesion
Assessing the function of underlying structures particularly in the hand where extensor tendon function and activities of daily living should be assessed.
Considering the cosmetic and functional implications of the lesion location when planning excision and reconstruction.

Investigations and biopsy
Stating that a punch biopsy is preferable in cosmetically sensitive areas as a first step before definitive excision.
Knowing the features in the pathology report that determine risk stratification including tumour grade, differentiation, depth, and perineural infiltration.
Arranging ultrasound guided fine needle aspiration or biopsy if palpable lymphadenopathy is present.

M D T discussion and management planning
Knowing that high risk S C C should always be discussed in M D T.
Stating that patients with palpable lymph nodes should undergo ultrasound and fine needle aspiration or biopsy with lymphadenectomy for positive nodes.
Knowing that S L N B may be offered for high risk lesions without clinically palpable lymphadenopathy.

Definitive surgical management and reconstruction
Explaining the surgical technique including marking the margins, performing an elliptical excision along the lines of least tension, and sending the specimen with orientation for pathology.
Knowing the reconstructive options by site: direct closure along relaxed skin tension lines, single or double advancement flaps, transposition flaps, or full thickness skin graft.
Knowing what to do if the excision is incomplete: discuss in M D T with options including re-excision or Mohs as first line, or adjuvant radiotherapy if further surgery is not possible.
Mentioning that Mohs micrographic surgery may be considered for S C C in cosmetically sensitive areas.
Knowing the B A D guidelines for adjuvant radiotherapy: offer adjuvant radiotherapy for incompletely excised S C C where further surgery is not possible, for perineural invasion of a named nerve or nerve diameter greater than nought point one millimetres below the dermis, for recurrent disease, and for immunocompromised patients.
Awareness of immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy for locally advanced or metastatic S C C where curative surgery or radiotherapy is not feasible, except in organ transplant recipients or those with significant autoimmune conditions.

Lymph node management
Knowing that palpable nodes require radical neck dissection with or without contralateral node sampling.
Knowing that S L N B may be offered for clinically node negative high risk S C C.

Follow up
Advising the patient on sun protection and self monitoring.
Knowing the risk of developing further primary skin cancers.
Awareness that high risk S C C has a higher rate of local recurrence and metastatic potential compared to B C C.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
