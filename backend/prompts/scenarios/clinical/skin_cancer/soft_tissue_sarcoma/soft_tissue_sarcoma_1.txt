SCENARIO TITLE

Soft Tissue Sarcoma

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 50 year old man who has been referred to your clinic with a deep-seated lump in his right thigh that has been growing over the past four months. His G P arranged an ultrasound which showed a five centimetre solid mass deep to the fascia. A core biopsy has confirmed a high grade soft tissue sarcoma. You have been asked to see him to discuss the management plan.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of the condition
Knowing that soft tissue sarcomas are a heterogeneous group of malignant tumours arising from mesenchymal tissue.
Knowing that they are rare, representing approximately one percent of all adult cancers.
Knowing the common subtypes relevant to plastic surgery including undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma, liposarcoma, and fibrosarcoma.

Clinical assessment
Taking a focused history including the duration and rate of growth, any associated pain, and any neurological symptoms such as numbness or weakness.
Examining the lump noting size, consistency, mobility, fixity to underlying structures, and relationship to the deep fascia.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the limb distally.
Performing a general examination to exclude any signs of metastatic disease.

Investigations and staging
Knowing that M R I of the affected limb is the imaging of choice to assess the extent of the tumour, its relationship to surrounding structures, and to plan surgery.
Arranging C T of the chest to exclude pulmonary metastasis as the lungs are the most common site of distant spread.
Confirming the diagnosis and grade with a core biopsy or incisional biopsy performed along the line of the planned excision.
Knowing the staging system: T N M staging based on tumour size, depth, nodal status, and distant metastasis, along with the histological grade.

M D T discussion and management planning
Discussing the case in a specialist sarcoma M D T.
Management guided by M D T decision.
Knowing that sarcomas should be managed in specialist centres.

Definitive surgical management
Wide local excision with a margin of at least one centimetre of normal tissue or an intact fascial barrier in all directions.
Knowing that the aim is to achieve a wide margin without sacrificing a limb.
Knowing that compartmental excision may be required for tumours contained within a muscle compartment.

Adjuvant therapy
Knowing that adjuvant radiotherapy is often recommended for high grade or large tumours, positive or close margins, or deep tumours.
Knowing that neoadjuvant radiotherapy may be considered to downsize the tumour before surgery.

Follow up and prognosis
Regular follow up with clinical examination and imaging to monitor for local recurrence and pulmonary metastasis.
Knowing that the risk of recurrence is highest in the first two to three years.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of the condition
Knowing that deep-seated tumours greater than five centimetres are more likely to be malignant.
Knowing the common subtypes and their typical behaviour patterns.
Knowing the risk factors for soft tissue sarcoma including previous radiotherapy, lymphoedema, genetic conditions such as neurofibromatosis type one and Li-Fraumeni syndrome, and chemical exposure.

Clinical assessment
Assessing the functional impact of the tumour and the potential functional consequences of surgery.
Considering the patient's occupation and hand dominance when discussing treatment.

Investigations and staging
Knowing the role of P E T C T in staging and assessment of treatment response.
Knowing that the biopsy track must be planned along the line of the definitive excision to allow excision of the biopsy track with the specimen.
Knowing the Trojani grading system or the French Federation of Cancer Centres grading system for soft tissue sarcomas.

M D T discussion and management planning
Knowing that the sarcoma M D T typically includes an orthopaedic oncologist, plastic surgeon, clinical oncologist, radiologist, pathologist, and specialist nurse.
Understanding the role of neoadjuvant chemotherapy in selected high grade sarcomas, particularly in young patients with chemosensitive subtypes.

Definitive surgical management
Knowing that limb salvage surgery is possible in the majority of cases and amputation is rarely required.
Knowing that the planned excision should take the tumour along with a cuff of normal tissue, ideally within an intact fascial or muscular compartment.
Knowing the reconstructive options for large defects after sarcoma excision including local muscle flaps, pedicled flaps, and free tissue transfer.
Understanding the concept of planned positive margins in certain anatomical locations where sacrifice of a major nerve or vessel would cause unacceptable morbidity, with adjuvant radiotherapy to the margin.

Adjuvant therapy
Knowing the difference between neoadjuvant and adjuvant radiotherapy, including the advantages and disadvantages of each approach.
Neoadjuvant radiotherapy may reduce the dose required and treat a smaller field, but is associated with higher wound complication rates.
Adjuvant radiotherapy treats the surgical bed after healing, with lower wound complications but higher doses and larger fields.
Knowing that chemotherapy has a limited role in most soft tissue sarcomas but may be considered for specific subtypes.

Follow up and prognosis
Knowing that follow up typically includes clinical examination every three to four months for the first two to three years, then six monthly to five years, with chest imaging at each visit.
Knowing the prognostic factors including tumour size, grade, depth, and margin status.
Knowing that overall five year survival for high grade soft tissue sarcoma is approximately fifty to sixty percent.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
