SCENARIO TITLE

Melanoma

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 32 year old lady who has come to clinic because she has noticed a mole on her forearm that has been changing over the past few months. She is concerned because it seems to have grown slightly and the colour has changed. You have been asked to assess her and formulate a management plan.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis and clinical assessment
Recognising this as a potentially malignant pigmented lesion that requires urgent assessment.
Differential diagnosis including malignant melanoma, dysplastic naevus, compound or junctional naevus, and pigmented B C C.

History and risk factors
Taking a focused history of the lesion including duration, evolution in size, shape and colour, and any symptoms such as bleeding, itching or pain.
Asking about risk factors for melanoma including previous personal or family history of melanoma, sun exposure particularly severe sunburns at a young age, Fitzpatrick skin type one or two, immunosuppression, and occupational exposure.

Examination of pigmented lesion
Using the A B C D E approach to examine the lesion: Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Colour variation, Diameter, and Evolution.
Examining regional lymph node basins for lymphadenopathy.
Performing a head to toe skin examination to identify any other suspicious lesions.

Investigations and biopsy
Performing an excision biopsy with a two millimetre margin under local anaesthetic.
Sending to pathology for Breslow thickness, Clark level, mitotic rate, ulceration, and perineural infiltration.

Staging and M D T discussion
Discussing the case in a skin cancer M D T meeting.
Management guided by the B A D guidelines for melanoma and A J C C staging.

Definitive surgical management
Wide local excision with margins determined by Breslow thickness.
Knowing the excision margins: in situ melanoma requires five millimetre margins, less than one millimetre requires one centimetre margins, one to two millimetres requires one to two centimetre margins, two to four millimetres requires two to three centimetre margins, and greater than four millimetres requires three centimetre margins.

Follow up and surveillance
Regular follow up with skin surveillance.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis and clinical assessment
Mentioning dermatoscopy as an adjunct to clinical examination.
Knowing the types of melanoma including superficial spreading, nodular, lentigo maligna, acral lentiginous, desmoplastic, and amelanotic.
Giving a broader list of differential diagnoses including seborrhoeic keratosis, blue naevus, and pigmented B C C.

History and risk factors
Asking about familial atypical mole syndrome or more than one hundred compound naevi.
Asking about congenital naevi or xeroderma pigmentosum.
General medical history including comorbidities and drug history that would affect surgical management.

Examination of pigmented lesion
Commenting on lesion size, symmetry, and specific colour patterns.
Examining all lymph node basins including axillary, cervical, and inguinal.
Looking for evidence of distant metastatic disease.

Investigations and biopsy
Stating that excision biopsy is preferred over punch biopsy or shave biopsy for suspected melanoma.
Orientating the specimen with a marking stitch along the lines of least tension for potential future wide local excision.
Knowing that the excision should extend down to fat or fascia.

Staging and M D T discussion
Knowing the A J C C 8th edition T staging: T one A is less than nought point eight millimetres without ulceration, T one B is less than nought point eight millimetres with ulceration or nought point eight to one millimetre with or without ulceration, T two is greater than one to two millimetres, T three is greater than two to four millimetres, T four is greater than four millimetres, each subdivided into A without ulceration and B with ulceration.
Stating that C T imaging is not recommended for stage one and two disease but should be considered for stage three B and above.
Knowing when to check serum lactate dehydrogenase as a marker for stage four disease.
Knowing that B R A F mutation testing should be requested for p T two B and above as it guides targeted therapy options.
Knowing that vitamin D level should be measured in all patients diagnosed with melanoma.

Definitive surgical management
Knowing that S L N B is offered to patients with p T one B and above, which includes Breslow thickness nought point eight to one millimetre with ulceration, or greater than one millimetre regardless of ulceration status.
Explaining the W L E technique including elliptical excision along the lines of least tension, excision down to fascia, and direct closure or reconstruction if needed.
Knowing that S L N B is a diagnostic procedure and a positive result requires further M D T discussion and staging with C T head, chest, abdomen, and pelvis before deciding on completion lymph node dissection versus surveillance.
Citing relevant evidence such as the Veronesi study showing no difference between one and three centimetre margins for melanomas less than two millimetres deep.

Follow up and surveillance
Stating the schedule for clinical follow up based on stage.
Knowing that patients should be educated on sun protection and self examination of skin and lymph nodes.
Awareness of specific adjuvant therapy options: targeted therapy with trametinib for B R A F positive tumours, immunotherapy with nivolumab, pembrolizumab which may be offered from stage two B or two C, and ipilimumab. Knowing that a positive S L N B result leads to M D T discussion about completion lymphadenectomy versus adjuvant immunotherapy.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
