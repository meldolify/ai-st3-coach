ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification of congenital hand anomalies
Knowing the Swanson or O M T classification of congenital hand anomalies, now updated by the I F S S H classification: failure of formation, failure of differentiation, duplication, overgrowth, undergrowth, constriction band syndrome, and generalised skeletal abnormalities.
Knowing that thumb absence or hypoplasia falls under failure of formation.
Knowing the Blauth classification of thumb hypoplasia: type one is minor hypoplasia, type two has thenar hypoplasia and first web narrowing, type three A has thenar hypoplasia with a stable C M C joint, type three B has thenar hypoplasia with an unstable C M C joint, type four is a floating thumb or pouce flottant, and type five is complete absence.

Clinical assessment of thumb absence
Examining the hand to confirm the absence of the thumb and assess the remaining digits.
Assessing the function of the remaining fingers including grip, pinch, and grasp patterns.
Examining both upper limbs for any associated anomalies.
Examining for associated conditions: thumb hypoplasia can be associated with Holt-Oram syndrome involving cardiac anomalies, Fanconi anaemia, thrombocytopenia-absent radius syndrome, and V A C T E R L association.

Assessment of hand function
Knowing that the thumb is responsible for approximately fifty percent of hand function, and its absence significantly affects pinch grip, opposition, and fine motor tasks.
Assessing how the child is currently adapting: children often develop compensatory grip patterns using the index and middle fingers.

Surgical options for thumb reconstruction
Knowing the surgical options based on the degree of deficiency.
For Blauth type one and two: no surgery needed, or opponensplasty and first web deepening.
For Blauth type three A with a stable C M C joint: opponensplasty using the Huber transfer of abductor digiti minimi, first web deepening, and U C L reconstruction if needed.
For Blauth type three B and above with an unstable C M C joint, type four floating thumb, and type five absent thumb: pollicisation of the index finger is the treatment of choice.

Pollicisation technique
Knowing that pollicisation involves shortening and rotating the index finger on its neurovascular pedicle to function as a thumb.
Knowing the key steps: the index finger metacarpal is shortened, the finger is rotated into opposition, the intrinsic muscles are rebalanced to act as thenar muscles, and the skin is rearranged to create a first web space.
Knowing the timing: pollicisation is typically performed between twelve and eighteen months of age.

Post-operative rehabilitation
Splinting to protect the reconstruction.
Hand therapy for mobilisation and functional training.
Long-term follow up to monitor function and growth.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification of congenital hand anomalies
Knowing the updated I F S S H O M T classification system which includes malformation, deformation, dysplasia, and syndromes.
Knowing the distinction between radial longitudinal deficiency involving the thumb and radial side of the hand, and ulnar longitudinal deficiency involving the little finger side.

Clinical assessment of thumb absence
Knowing the screening investigations for associated conditions: echocardiogram for Holt-Oram syndrome, full blood count for Fanconi anaemia which presents with pancytopenia, and renal ultrasound for V A C T E R L association.
Knowing that chromosomal breakage studies may be needed to diagnose Fanconi anaemia which has implications for bone marrow failure and cancer risk.

Assessment of hand function
Knowing that children adapt remarkably well and many develop good compensatory function even without a thumb.
Knowing that the assessment should include observation of the child's spontaneous hand use in play.

Surgical options for thumb reconstruction
Knowing the Huber opponensplasty in detail: the abductor digiti minimi muscle is transferred on its neurovascular pedicle from the hypothenar eminence to the thumb metacarpal to provide opposition.
Knowing that free toe-to-hand transfer using the second toe or great toe wrap-around technique is an alternative to pollicisation for thumb reconstruction but is more complex and is typically reserved for post-traumatic cases or when pollicisation is not feasible.
Knowing the Buck-Gramcko technique of pollicisation which involves specific osteotomy and rotation manoeuvres of the index metacarpal.

Pollicisation technique
Knowing that the index finger extensor and flexor tendons are shortened and reattached to maintain appropriate tension.
Knowing that the first dorsal interosseous muscle is reattached to simulate the abductor pollicis brevis, and the first palmar interosseous simulates the adductor pollicis.
Knowing that the metacarpal head of the index finger becomes the new trapezium and should not be removed.
Knowing that the skin flaps must be carefully designed to create an adequate first web space.

Post-operative rehabilitation
Knowing that functional outcomes of pollicisation in children are generally excellent with good pinch and grip strength.
Knowing that the child may need further procedures as the hand grows, including web space deepening or tendon rebalancing.
Knowing the importance of hand therapy in teaching the child to use the new thumb in functional tasks.
