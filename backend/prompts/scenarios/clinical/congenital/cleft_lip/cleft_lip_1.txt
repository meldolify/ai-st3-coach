SCENARIO TITLE

Cleft Lip

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A baby has been born with a unilateral cleft lip on the left side. The parents are very anxious and have been told that a plastic surgeon will come to discuss the condition with them. The baby is otherwise well and is feeding with some difficulty. You have been asked to counsel the parents and discuss the management plan.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification of the cleft
Classifying the cleft: unilateral or bilateral, complete or incomplete, and whether it involves the lip only, the lip and alveolus, or the lip, alveolus, and palate.
Knowing the incidence: approximately one in seven hundred live births, with cleft lip with or without palate being more common in males.

Clinical assessment and exclusion of associated anomalies
Performing a thorough neonatal examination to exclude associated anomalies.
Knowing that cleft lip and palate can be isolated or part of a syndrome such as Pierre Robin sequence, Van der Woude syndrome, or Stickler syndrome.
Examining the palate carefully including the soft palate to determine whether the cleft extends to the palate.
Assessing feeding: the baby may have difficulty creating a seal for breastfeeding.

Feeding support and pre-surgical management
Involving the cleft specialist nurse for feeding support.
Options include specialist cleft feeding bottles such as the M A M or Haberman feeder, or breastfeeding with support.
Pre-surgical nasoalveolar moulding or taping may be used to narrow the cleft and improve the nasal shape before surgery.

Timing of surgical repair
Knowing the rule of tens: cleft lip repair is performed at approximately three months of age when the child weighs at least ten pounds, has a haemoglobin of at least ten grams per decilitre, and is at least ten weeks old.
Knowing that cleft palate repair is typically performed at approximately six to twelve months.

Surgical technique
Knowing the commonly used techniques for unilateral cleft lip repair: the Millard rotation-advancement technique and the Tennison-Randall triangular flap technique.
Knowing the key goals of cleft lip repair: accurate alignment of the vermillion border and white roll, reconstruction of the orbicularis oris muscle, correction of the nasal deformity, and achieving symmetry.

Multidisciplinary team involvement
Knowing that cleft care is delivered by a multidisciplinary team including cleft surgeon, orthodontist, speech and language therapist, cleft specialist nurse, audiologist, psychologist, and E N T surgeon.

Long-term follow up
Knowing that the child will require long-term follow up through the cleft team including dental and orthodontic care, speech assessment, hearing assessment, and potential secondary procedures.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification of the cleft
Knowing the embryology: cleft lip results from failure of fusion of the medial nasal process with the maxillary process during the fourth to seventh week of gestation.
Knowing the Kernahan stripy Y classification for documenting cleft type.
Knowing the aetiological factors: multifactorial inheritance with genetic and environmental factors including maternal smoking, alcohol, anticonvulsant medication, and folic acid deficiency.

Clinical assessment and exclusion of associated anomalies
Knowing that Pierre Robin sequence includes micrognathia, glossoptosis, and airway obstruction, and may be associated with cleft palate.
Knowing that approximately thirty percent of cleft lip and palate cases are syndromic.
Knowing that a submucous cleft palate may be occult and should be assessed by palpation of the posterior palate for a notch in the hard palate and a bifid uvula.

Feeding support and pre-surgical management
Knowing that pre-surgical orthopaedics such as nasoalveolar moulding using a Grayson type device can improve the alignment of the alveolar segments and the nasal cartilage shape before surgery, though its use is debated.
Knowing that lip taping can help approximate the cleft segments and stretch the tissues before repair.

Timing of surgical repair
Knowing the timeline of the full cleft care pathway: lip repair at three months, palate repair at six to twelve months, speech assessment at eighteen months to two years, alveolar bone graft at eight to ten years when the permanent canine is erupting, rhinoplasty and lip revision in adolescence, and orthognathic surgery if needed in late adolescence.
Knowing that early repair may improve parent-child bonding and feeding.

Surgical technique
Knowing the Millard rotation-advancement technique in more detail: a rotation incision on the medial lip element creates a downward rotation of the Cupid bow, and an advancement flap from the lateral lip element is advanced into the gap, reconstructing the philtral column.
Knowing the importance of primary rhinoplasty at the time of lip repair to correct the cleft nasal deformity including repositioning of the lower lateral cartilage.
Knowing the principles of bilateral cleft lip repair: the prolabium provides the central lip element but lacks muscle, and the lateral lip elements are advanced bilaterally. The Mulliken technique emphasises muscle reconstruction across the midline.

Multidisciplinary team involvement
Knowing that the cleft team meets regularly to review cases and coordinate care.
Knowing the role of the speech and language therapist in assessing for velopharyngeal insufficiency after palate repair.
Knowing the role of the orthodontist in managing dental alignment and preparing for alveolar bone grafting.

Long-term follow up
Knowing the secondary procedures that may be needed: lip revision, nose revision, pharyngoplasty for velopharyngeal insufficiency, alveolar bone graft, and orthognathic surgery for maxillary hypoplasia.
Knowing the psychological impact of cleft on the child and family and the importance of psychological support throughout childhood and adolescence.
Knowing that audit of outcomes is essential and the C L A P A standards in the United Kingdom guide cleft care delivery.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
