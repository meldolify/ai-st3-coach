SCENARIO TITLE

Cleft Palate

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 6 month old baby who was born with an isolated cleft of the soft palate. The baby has been managed by the cleft team and is now being brought to clinic to discuss the planned palate repair. The parents would like to understand the surgery, the risks, and the expected outcomes. You have been asked to counsel the parents.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification of cleft palate
Knowing that isolated cleft palate is different from cleft lip and palate in terms of embryology and genetics.
Classifying the cleft: soft palate only, hard and soft palate, or submucous cleft palate.
Knowing that isolated cleft palate is more common in females and has a higher association with syndromes.

Impact on feeding, speech, and hearing
Knowing that cleft palate causes feeding difficulty due to inability to create negative pressure for sucking.
Knowing that an unrepaired cleft palate causes velopharyngeal insufficiency leading to hypernasal speech and nasal air escape.
Knowing that children with cleft palate have a high incidence of otitis media with effusion due to eustachian tube dysfunction, and many will require grommets.

Pre-operative assessment
Ensuring the child has been seen by the multidisciplinary cleft team.
Audiological assessment and E N T review for consideration of grommets at the time of palate repair.
Speech and language baseline assessment.
Anaesthetic assessment.

Timing of palate repair
Knowing that palate repair is typically performed between six and twelve months of age.
Knowing the rationale: repairing before the onset of speech development to optimise speech outcomes, while allowing some growth of the palate.
Knowing the debate about early versus late repair: early repair benefits speech, but very early repair may restrict maxillary growth.

Surgical technique
Knowing the commonly used techniques: the Sommerlad technique using radical muscle dissection and intravelar veloplasty to reconstruct the levator veli palatini muscle sling, the von Langenbeck bipedicled flap technique, and the Veau-Wardill-Kilner V to Y pushback technique.
Knowing the key goal: reconstruction of the levator veli palatini muscle sling across the midline to create a functional velopharyngeal mechanism for speech.
Knowing that the mucoperiosteal flaps are raised and the nasal layer is closed separately from the oral layer to create a two-layer closure.

Complications
Bleeding which is the most common early complication.
Fistula formation: a breakdown of the repair creating a hole between the oral and nasal cavities.
Velopharyngeal insufficiency despite repair, which may require secondary surgery such as a pharyngoplasty.
Maxillary growth restriction.

Long-term management including speech and hearing
Knowing that speech and language assessment is performed at approximately eighteen months to two years of age.
Knowing that if velopharyngeal insufficiency persists, options include speech therapy, pharyngoplasty using a posterior pharyngeal flap or sphincter pharyngoplasty, or Furlow double-opposing Z-plasty.
Knowing that hearing should be monitored throughout childhood.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification of cleft palate
Knowing the embryology: the palate forms from the palatal shelves of the maxillary processes which fuse in the midline between the sixth and twelfth week of gestation. Failure of fusion results in cleft palate.
Knowing the Veau classification of cleft palate: class one is soft palate only, class two is hard and soft palate up to the incisive foramen, class three is complete unilateral cleft, and class four is complete bilateral cleft.
Knowing the features of a submucous cleft palate: bifid uvula, zona pellucida of the soft palate, and a palpable notch in the posterior hard palate. This may present late with speech problems.

Impact on feeding, speech, and hearing
Knowing that the eustachian tube dysfunction is caused by abnormal insertion of the tensor veli palatini muscle.
Knowing that recurrent otitis media can cause conductive hearing loss which affects speech and language development.

Pre-operative assessment
Knowing that Pierre Robin sequence with associated airway difficulties may require prone positioning, nasopharyngeal airway, or rarely a tracheostomy before palate repair can be undertaken.

Timing of palate repair
Knowing the Schweckendiek approach of early soft palate repair at six months and delayed hard palate repair at a later age, though this is not widely practiced in the U K where single-stage repair is preferred.
Knowing that in the United Kingdom, most cleft centres perform palate repair between six and twelve months.

Surgical technique
Knowing the Sommerlad technique in more detail: the abnormally inserted levator veli palatini muscles are detached from the posterior edge of the hard palate and the palatal aponeurosis, mobilised, and re-sutured across the midline to reconstruct the muscular sling. This is the key step for achieving velopharyngeal competence.
Knowing the importance of two-layer closure: the nasal mucosal layer and the oral mucosal layer should be closed separately with the muscle repair in between.
Knowing that the tensor veli palatini tendon may be divided or rerouted around the hamulus to facilitate closure.

Complications
Knowing that the fistula rate varies between two and fifteen percent depending on the technique and surgeon experience.
Knowing that small anterior fistulae may be asymptomatic and can be managed conservatively, while larger symptomatic fistulae require surgical closure.
Knowing that maxillary hypoplasia may result from palatal surgery and can require orthognathic surgery such as Le Fort one advancement in late adolescence.

Long-term management including speech and hearing
Knowing the assessment of velopharyngeal function using nasendoscopy and videofluoroscopy.
Knowing the different secondary speech surgeries: posterior pharyngeal flap raises a superiorly based flap from the posterior pharyngeal wall and inserts it into the soft palate, reducing the velopharyngeal gap. Sphincter pharyngoplasty uses myomucosal flaps from the posterior tonsillar pillars to narrow the velopharyngeal port.
Knowing the risk of obstructive sleep apnoea after pharyngoplasty.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
