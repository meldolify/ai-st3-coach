SCENARIO TITLE

Prominent Ears

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is an 8 year old boy who has been referred to your clinic by his G P because his parents are concerned about his prominent ears. He has been teased at school and it is affecting his confidence. The parents would like to discuss surgical correction. You have been asked to assess and counsel the family.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment and measurement
Measuring the conchoscaphal angle which is normally less than ninety degrees. An angle greater than ninety degrees indicates prominent ears.
Measuring the distance from the helical rim to the mastoid which is normally less than fifteen to twenty millimetres.
Assessing the ear from the front, side, and behind.
Assessing for symmetry between the two sides.

Anatomical causes of prominent ears
Knowing the two main anatomical causes: an underdeveloped antihelical fold and a deep or hypertrophic conchal bowl.
Knowing that the surgical technique should address the specific anatomical cause.
Knowing the pseudo-prominent ear causes: Stahl ear, Darwin tubercle, and mastoid prominence, which require different management.

Counselling the family
Discussing the impact on the child and ensuring the child is motivated for surgery, not just the parents.
Explaining the surgical procedure, expected outcome, and risks.
Discussing funding: N H S funding for otoplasty in children is generally available when there is demonstrated psychological impact.
Setting realistic expectations: the aim is improvement, not perfection, and some asymmetry is normal.

Timing of surgery
Knowing that the ear reaches approximately eighty five percent of adult size by age five to six years, so surgery can be performed from age five onwards.
Knowing that surgery is ideally performed before the child starts school or during primary school years to reduce the impact of teasing.
Knowing that in neonates, ear splinting or moulding within the first few weeks of life can correct prominent ears non-surgically due to the high circulating maternal oestrogen levels making the cartilage malleable.

Surgical technique
Knowing the two main techniques based on the anatomical cause.
For an underdeveloped antihelical fold: the Mustarde technique using horizontal mattress sutures to recreate the antihelical fold, placed through a posterior incision.
For a deep conchal bowl: conchal reduction by excising a strip of conchal cartilage and setting back the concha with concho-mastoid sutures.
Most cases require a combination of both techniques.
Performing the surgery under general anaesthetic in children or local anaesthetic in adults.
Making a posterior auricular incision to access the cartilage.

Post-operative care and complications
Applying a head bandage or ear compression band for one to two weeks post-operatively, then a compression headband at night for a further four to six weeks.
Advising avoidance of contact sports for six weeks.
Knowing the complications: haematoma, infection, suture extrusion, recurrence of prominence, overcorrection creating a telephone ear deformity, asymmetry, altered sensation, and keloid scarring particularly in darker-skinned patients.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment and measurement
Knowing that the incidence of prominent ears is approximately five percent in the white population.
Knowing the normal anatomy of the ear: the auricle has a helical rim, antihelix with superior and inferior crura, concha, tragus, antitragus, and lobule.
Knowing the normal conchoscaphal angle is approximately ninety degrees and the normal helical-mastoid distance is approximately fifteen to eighteen millimetres.

Anatomical causes of prominent ears
Knowing the cartilage anatomy in detail: the antihelix is a Y-shaped ridge formed by a fold in the cartilage. Failure of this fold to develop results in a flat, underdeveloped antihelix and a prominent ear.
Knowing that an excessively deep concha pushes the entire ear away from the head.

Counselling the family
Knowing the importance of assessing the child's own wishes rather than only the parents' desire for correction.
Knowing that some trusts require a psychological assessment or evidence of bullying before funding otoplasty.
Discussing the scar which is hidden behind the ear.

Timing of surgery
Knowing the neonatal splinting technique: ear moulds or splints such as the EarWell device are applied within the first one to two weeks of life and worn for six to eight weeks. Success rates are highest when started within the first week.
Knowing that after the neonatal period, the cartilage becomes rigid and splinting is no longer effective.

Surgical technique
Knowing the Mustarde technique in detail: horizontal mattress sutures of clear Prolene or Ethibond are placed through the cartilage via a posterior approach. The sutures are placed from the scapha to the antihelical fold position, creating a smooth antihelical fold when tied. Typically three to four sutures are needed.
Knowing the cartilage scoring technique as an alternative: anterior cartilage scoring through a posterior approach weakens the cartilage and allows it to fold, using the principle that scored cartilage bends away from the scored surface. This is known as the Chongchet technique.
Knowing that the Stenstrom technique uses anterior scoring through a percutaneous approach using a fine rasp.
Knowing the importance of checking the result from the front during surgery and aiming for slight overcorrection as there will be some loss of correction post-operatively.

Post-operative care and complications
Knowing that haematoma is the most serious early complication as it can cause cartilage necrosis and a cauliflower ear deformity. Suspected haematoma should be evacuated urgently.
Knowing that the telephone ear deformity results from overcorrection of the middle third of the ear without addressing the upper and lower thirds.
Knowing that suture extrusion may occur months or years later and requires removal of the offending suture.
Knowing that revision surgery for recurrence or asymmetry is needed in approximately five to ten percent of cases.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
