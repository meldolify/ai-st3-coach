SCENARIO TITLE

Polydactyly

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a newborn baby who has been born with an extra digit on the ulnar border of the right hand. The extra digit appears to be attached by a narrow skin pedicle and has no bony connection. The parents are anxious and would like the digit removed. You have been asked to assess and counsel the parents.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification of polydactyly
Knowing the classification by location: preaxial or radial polydactyly involving the thumb, central polydactyly involving the index, middle, or ring fingers, and postaxial or ulnar polydactyly involving the little finger.
Knowing the Stelling classification for postaxial polydactyly: type one is a soft tissue skin tag, type two is a partial duplication with some bony elements, and type three is a complete duplication with a fully formed extra digit.
This child has a type one postaxial polydactyly.

Clinical assessment and exclusion of associated conditions
Examining the extra digit: noting the attachment (pedunculated or broad-based), presence of a nail, mobility, and any bony elements within it.
Assessing the remaining hand for any other anomalies.
Examining both hands and feet.
Knowing that postaxial polydactyly is the most common form and is more common in patients of African descent where it may be familial and isolated.
Knowing that polydactyly may be associated with syndromes and should prompt assessment: postaxial polydactyly can be associated with Ellis-van Creveld syndrome, Bardet-Biedl syndrome, and trisomy thirteen.

Investigations
X-ray of the hand to determine whether the extra digit contains bony elements and to assess the skeletal anatomy.
If syndromic features are suspected, further investigations and genetics referral.

Timing and technique of excision
Knowing that a pedunculated type one postaxial polydactyly with no bony elements can be excised simply.
Knowing the debate about suture ligation in the neonatal period: tying a suture around the base of the pedicle to cause ischaemic necrosis and auto-amputation is a traditional method but carries risks of incomplete removal, painful neuroma formation, and an unsightly residual bump.
Knowing that formal surgical excision under general anaesthetic at three to six months is preferred by many surgeons as it allows proper identification and division of any neurovascular structures and a better cosmetic result.
For more complex duplications with bony elements, formal surgical excision is required with careful preservation of the collateral ligament and tendon insertions of the retained digit.

Post-operative care
Simple wound dressing.
Follow up to assess the wound and hand function.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification of polydactyly
Knowing the Wassel classification for preaxial or radial polydactyly which is based on the level of duplication from distal to proximal: type one is bifid distal phalanx, type two is duplicated distal phalanx, through to type seven which is a triphalangeal thumb.
Knowing that type four is the most common Wassel type which is duplicated proximal and distal phalanges with a shared metacarpal.
Knowing that central polydactyly is the rarest form and is often associated with syndactyly of the duplicated digit with its neighbour, known as polysyndactyly.

Clinical assessment and exclusion of associated conditions
Knowing the genetics: isolated postaxial polydactyly type A with a well-formed extra digit and type B which is a rudimentary digit. Type A is autosomal dominant, type B is also autosomal dominant with variable expression.
Knowing that preaxial polydactyly is more commonly associated with syndromes than postaxial.

Investigations
Knowing that M R I may be useful in complex duplications to assess tendon and joint anatomy before surgery.

Timing and technique of excision
Knowing that for preaxial or thumb polydactyly, the surgical approach is more complex: the less functional of the two thumbs is excised, but the collateral ligaments and tendons from the excised digit must be transferred to the retained digit to ensure stability and function.
Knowing that the Bilhaut-Cloquet procedure combines elements of both duplicated digits to create a single thumb of more normal width for Wassel type one and two duplications.
Knowing that Z-plasty of the first web space may be needed in thumb polydactyly to widen the web and improve function.
Knowing the risk of angular deformity of the retained digit if the surgery is not performed meticulously, which may require secondary osteotomy.

Post-operative care
Knowing the complications: angular deformity of the retained digit, joint instability, stiffness, and nail deformity.
Knowing that long-term follow up until skeletal maturity is needed as growth may unmask a residual angular deformity.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
