SCENARIO TITLE

Congenital Melanocytic Naevus

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 3 year old child who has been referred to your clinic with a large pigmented hairy lesion on the lateral aspect of her upper arm that has been present since birth. The parents are concerned about the appearance and have read about the risk of it becoming cancerous. The lesion measures approximately eight centimetres. You have been asked to assess and counsel the parents.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification and risk assessment
Knowing the classification by size: small is less than one point five centimetres, medium is one point five to twenty centimetres, large or giant is greater than twenty centimetres.
Knowing the risk of malignant transformation: approximately two to five percent for giant congenital melanocytic naevi, with lower risk for smaller lesions. The risk is highest in the first decade of life.
Knowing that giant congenital naevi carry the highest risk and require close surveillance.

Clinical examination
Examining the naevus noting size, shape, colour variation, surface characteristics, presence of hair, and any nodularity or ulceration which may suggest malignant change.
Assessing whether the lesion is adherent to underlying structures.
Palpating for any subcutaneous nodules.
Performing a head to toe skin examination for satellite naevi.
Examining the axillary lymph nodes.

Screening for associated conditions
Knowing that large congenital melanocytic naevi, particularly those in the posterior midline involving the head, neck, or paravertebral area, may be associated with neurocutaneous melanosis: melanocytic deposits in the leptomeninges which can cause seizures, hydrocephalus, and malignant transformation.
Arranging M R I of the brain and spine if the naevus is in a high-risk location, particularly cephalic, posterior cervical, or paravertebral.
Ophthalmology referral if the naevus involves the V one or V two dermatome distribution.
Neurosurgery involvement if neurocutaneous melanosis is identified.

Counselling and management options
Counselling the parents about the nature of the condition, the risk of malignant transformation, and the management options.
Knowing the management options: surveillance with regular dermatological review and photography, serial excision, excision and reconstruction, or non-surgical camouflage.
Explaining that surgery does not eliminate the risk of melanoma entirely as melanocytes may remain in deeper tissues.

Surgical planning and reconstruction
Knowing the surgical options depend on the size and location.
For an eight centimetre lesion on the upper arm: serial excision is likely feasible if the surrounding skin has sufficient laxity.
Other options include excision with full thickness or split thickness skin graft, tissue expansion of adjacent normal skin followed by excision and advancement, local flap, or free flap for very large defects.

Long-term follow up
Regular dermatological surveillance with clinical photography.
Educating the parents to monitor for changes in the naevus including rapid growth, colour change, nodularity, ulceration, or bleeding.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification and risk assessment
Knowing that the predicted adult size should be used for classification, calculated by multiplying the infant measurement by a growth factor based on the anatomical location.
Knowing that the risk of melanoma in giant congenital naevi is quoted variably in the literature but the commonly cited lifetime risk is approximately five to ten percent.
Knowing that melanoma arising in a congenital naevus may develop deep within the dermis or subcutis, making clinical detection difficult.

Clinical examination
Knowing that dermoscopy can be used to assess the naevus but has limited sensitivity for detecting early melanoma in congenital naevi due to the background pigmentation pattern.
Knowing that any new nodularity or rapid change warrants urgent biopsy.

Screening for associated conditions
Knowing that neurocutaneous melanosis occurs in approximately five to ten percent of patients with giant congenital naevi.
Knowing that symptomatic neurocutaneous melanosis carries a very poor prognosis.
Knowing that M R I of the brain and spine should be performed within the first six months of life as the myelination pattern makes imaging easier.

Counselling and management options
Knowing the psychological impact on the child and family and the importance of psychological support.
Knowing that the decision to operate should be balanced between the risk of malignancy, the cosmetic and psychological benefit, the surgical morbidity, and the family's wishes.
Knowing that prophylactic excision of giant congenital naevi is controversial as it does not eliminate the melanoma risk and involves significant surgical morbidity.

Surgical planning and reconstruction
Knowing the technique of tissue expansion: a tissue expander is inserted under the adjacent normal skin, gradually inflated over weeks to months to generate additional tissue, then the naevus is excised and the expanded skin is advanced to close the defect.
Knowing the advantages of tissue expansion: provides hair-bearing skin of matching colour and texture. Disadvantages include multiple operations, risk of expander infection or extrusion, and temporary disfigurement.
Knowing that for very large naevi, a combination of techniques may be needed including serial excision, tissue expansion, skin grafting, and flap reconstruction performed over multiple stages.
Knowing the use of cultured epithelial autograft or dermal substitutes for extensive naevi where conventional reconstruction is inadequate.

Long-term follow up
Knowing that follow up should continue into adulthood.
Knowing that any biopsy should be full thickness to assess for deep melanoma.
Knowing that patients and families should be counselled about sun protection.
