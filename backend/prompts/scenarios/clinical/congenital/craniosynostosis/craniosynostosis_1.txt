Craniosynostosis

The right side of the forehead appears flat and the right ear appears more forward than the left. The baby is otherwise well and developing normally. ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis and classification of craniosynostosis
Knowing that craniosynostosis is premature fusion of one or more cranial sutures.
Knowing the head shape associated with each suture fusion: sagittal synostosis causes scaphocephaly or dolichocephaly, coronal synostosis causes anterior plagiocephaly with ipsilateral forehead flattening, metopic synostosis causes trigonocephaly with a triangular forehead, and lambdoid synostosis causes posterior plagiocephaly.
This child has right unilateral coronal synostosis based on the right forehead flattening and right ear displacement.

Differentiation from positional plagiocephaly
Knowing the key differences: in positional plagiocephaly the ipsilateral ear is displaced posteriorly and the forehead is prominent on the same side, creating a parallelogram shape when viewed from above. In coronal synostosis the ipsilateral ear is displaced anteriorly and the forehead is flat on the same side.
Knowing that positional plagiocephaly is common and usually resolves with repositioning and physiotherapy, whereas craniosynostosis requires surgical correction.
Knowing that a palpable ridge along a fused suture confirms craniosynostosis.

Clinical assessment and exclusion of raised intracranial pressure
Measuring the head circumference and plotting it on a growth chart. A small or crossing centile head circumference suggests restricted cranial growth.
Palpating the fontanelle: a tense or bulging fontanelle may indicate raised intracranial pressure.
Examining the eyes for papilloedema using ophthalmoscopy, and for any restriction of eye movements.
Assessing developmental milestones.
Knowing that multi-suture craniosynostosis carries a higher risk of raised intracranial pressure than single suture.
Knowing that craniosynostosis can be isolated or syndromic, and syndromic cases should be identified: Crouzon syndrome involves craniosynostosis with mid-face hypoplasia, Apert syndrome involves craniosynostosis with syndactyly, and Pfeiffer syndrome involves craniosynostosis with broad thumbs and toes.

Investigations
C T scan of the skull with three-dimensional reconstruction to confirm the diagnosis and identify the fused suture.
Ophthalmology review for papilloedema and visual assessment.
Genetic testing if a syndrome is suspected.
M R I of the brain if there is concern about raised intracranial pressure or associated brain anomalies.

Timing and indications for surgery
Knowing that surgery is typically performed between six and twelve months of age for single suture craniosynostosis.
Knowing the indications: correction of the skull shape deformity, prevention or treatment of raised intracranial pressure, and in syndromic cases, protection of the developing brain and eyes.
Knowing that untreated craniosynostosis can lead to raised intracranial pressure, visual impairment, developmental delay, and psychosocial impact from the abnormal head shape.

Surgical techniques
Knowing the main surgical approaches: open cranial vault remodelling performed jointly by the craniofacial plastic surgeon and neurosurgeon, and the less invasive endoscope-assisted strip craniectomy with post-operative helmet therapy.
Knowing that for coronal synostosis, a fronto-orbital advancement is typically performed: the frontal bone and supraorbital bar are removed, reshaped, and repositioned to correct the forehead and orbital asymmetry.
Knowing that endoscopic strip craniectomy is an option for younger infants under approximately four months and requires post-operative helmet moulding therapy for six to twelve months.

Risks and complications
Knowing the risks of craniofacial surgery: blood loss which can be significant in infants, infection, dural tears with C S F leak, relapse of the deformity, and rarely brain injury.
Knowing the importance of adequate blood cross-matching and paediatric anaesthetic expertise.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis and classification of craniosynostosis
Knowing the incidence: approximately one in two thousand to two thousand five hundred live births. Sagittal synostosis is the most common.
Knowing the genetic basis of the common syndromes: Crouzon and Apert are caused by F G F R two mutations, and Muenke syndrome by F G F R three mutations.
Knowing that lambdoid synostosis is the rarest form and is most often confused with positional plagiocephaly.

Differentiation from positional plagiocephaly
Knowing the management of positional plagiocephaly: repositioning to avoid pressure on the flat area, tummy time, physiotherapy, and in persistent cases helmet moulding therapy.
Knowing that positional plagiocephaly does not require surgery.

Clinical assessment and exclusion of raised intracranial pressure
Knowing the features of copper beaten skull on X-ray or C T which indicates chronic raised intracranial pressure.
Knowing that intracranial pressure monitoring may be needed in multi-suture or syndromic craniosynostosis.

Investigations
Knowing the utility of three-dimensional C T in surgical planning including creation of cutting guides and planning the osteotomies.
Knowing that prenatal ultrasound may detect craniosynostosis but is not reliable for all suture types.

Timing and indications for surgery
Knowing that endoscopic strip craniectomy is ideally performed before three to four months of age when the bone is thin and the calvarial remodelling potential is greatest.
Knowing that the decision between open surgery and endoscopic technique depends on the age of the child, the suture involved, and surgeon expertise and preference.

Surgical techniques
Knowing the fronto-orbital advancement technique in detail: a bicoronal incision provides access. The frontal bone flap and supraorbital bar are removed as separate pieces. The bar is reshaped using barrel staving osteotomies, advanced, and secured with absorbable plates and screws. The frontal bone is reshaped and replaced. Bone gaps fill in with new bone formation.
Knowing the use of absorbable plates and screws rather than titanium in the growing skull to avoid migration and interference with growth.
Knowing the spring-assisted cranioplasty technique as an alternative: metal springs are inserted across the excised suture and gradually expand the skull over several months, then are removed in a second procedure.
Knowing the posterior vault expansion techniques for sagittal and lambdoid synostosis.
Knowing that syndromic craniosynostosis often requires multiple staged procedures including mid-face advancement such as a Le Fort three or monobloc osteotomy at a later age.

Risks and complications
Knowing that intraoperative blood loss can be up to one to two blood volumes in an infant, requiring transfusion.
Knowing the need for I T U or H D U post-operative care.
Knowing that re-synostosis can occur in approximately five to ten percent of cases.
Knowing the long-term follow up requirements including serial head circumference measurement, ophthalmology review, developmental assessment, and repeat imaging if there is concern about relapse or raised intracranial pressure.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
