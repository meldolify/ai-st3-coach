Hypospadias

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a baby boy who has been referred to your clinic at six months of age with a hypospadias. The urethral meatus is located on the ventral surface of the penile shaft at the mid-shaft level. There is a hooded foreskin and mild ventral chordee. You have been asked to assess and counsel the parents about the management.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification of hypospadias
Knowing the classification by meatal position: distal or anterior which includes glanular and subcoronal and accounts for approximately seventy percent, middle or mid-shaft which accounts for approximately fifteen percent, and proximal or posterior which includes penoscrotal, scrotal, and perineal and accounts for approximately fifteen percent.
This child has a mid-shaft hypospadias.

Clinical assessment
Confirming the position of the urethral meatus.
Assessing for chordee: ventral curvature of the penis which may be mild, moderate, or severe.
Noting the hooded prepuce which is dorsally hooded and deficient ventrally.
Assessing the size of the penis and the quality of the urethral plate.
Examining the testes to confirm they are both descended: proximal hypospadias with undescended testes requires investigation for a disorder of sex development.
Examining for any other genital anomalies.

Counselling and timing of repair
Counselling the parents that the child must not be circumcised as the foreskin tissue may be needed for the surgical repair.
Knowing that the recommended age for repair is between six and eighteen months.
Explaining the goals of surgery: to create a straight penis with the meatus at the tip, to correct any chordee, and to achieve a cosmetically normal appearance.
Discussing the success rates and potential complications.

Surgical principles and technique
Knowing that for a mid-shaft hypospadias with mild chordee, a single-stage repair is appropriate.
Knowing the commonly used techniques: the tubularised incised plate repair known as the T I P or Snodgrass repair is the most widely used technique for distal and mid-shaft hypospadias. It involves incising the urethral plate in the midline to widen it, then tubularising it over a catheter and covering with a vascularised dartos flap as a waterproofing layer.
Knowing the alternative techniques: the Mathieu meatal-based flap for distal hypospadias, and the two-stage Bracka repair for proximal hypospadias or where the urethral plate is of poor quality.
Knowing the importance of a dartos flap or tunica vaginalis flap as an interposition layer to reduce fistula formation.

Complications
Knowing the common complications: urethrocutaneous fistula which is the most common complication occurring in approximately five to ten percent, meatal stenosis, urethral stricture, wound dehiscence, and residual chordee.
Knowing that fistulae should not be repaired until at least six months after the primary surgery to allow the tissues to mature.

Long-term follow up
Follow up to assess the position of the meatus, the urinary stream, and the cosmetic result.
Knowing that the child should be followed until toilet trained to assess the functional result.
Knowing that further review may be needed at puberty to assess erections and residual chordee.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification of hypospadias
Knowing the incidence: approximately one in three hundred male births.
Knowing the aetiology: multifactorial with genetic and hormonal factors. The urethra develops from the urethral folds which fuse ventrally from proximal to distal between the eighth and fourteenth week of gestation. Failure of fusion results in hypospadias.
Knowing that familial recurrence is approximately seven percent if the father is affected.

Clinical assessment
Knowing that proximal hypospadias with bilateral undescended testes should prompt urgent investigation for a disorder of sex development including karyotype, hormonal assays, and pelvic ultrasound.
Knowing that the degree of chordee may not correlate with the position of the meatus and can only be fully assessed intraoperatively with an artificial erection test.

Counselling and timing of repair
Knowing the role of pre-operative testosterone cream or injections to increase penile size before surgery in cases with a small penis, particularly in proximal hypospadias.
Knowing that topical oestrogen cream may be used pre-operatively to improve tissue quality.

Surgical principles and technique
Knowing the Snodgrass T I P repair in detail: the urethral plate is preserved and incised in the midline from the meatus to the glans. The plate is then tubularised over a catheter using running subcuticular absorbable sutures. The neourethra is covered with a de-epithelialised dartos flap from the prepuce brought ventrally as a waterproofing layer. The glans wings are closed over the neourethra, and the skin is closed using the prepuce either as a dorsal Byars flap or a ventral preputioplasty.
Knowing the two-stage Bracka repair: in the first stage, a buccal mucosa graft from the inner cheek is laid onto the ventral surface of the penis as a free graft to create a new urethral plate. After six months, the second stage tubularises the grafted plate into a neourethra.
Knowing the role of the artificial erection test performed intraoperatively by injecting saline into the corpus cavernosum to assess for residual chordee after degloving.

Complications
Knowing that fistula repair involves excision of the fistula tract and multilayer closure with an interposition flap.
Knowing that meatal stenosis can often be managed with dilatation or meatoplasty.
Knowing that urethral diverticulum can occur if the neourethra is too wide.
Knowing the long-term psychological impact of hypospadias and the importance of age-appropriate counselling as the child grows.

Long-term follow up
Knowing that uroflowmetry can objectively assess the urinary stream.
Knowing that some patients will require revision surgery, with reported re-operation rates of approximately fifteen to twenty percent.
Knowing that long-term satisfaction with cosmetic and functional outcomes is generally good for distal and mid-shaft repairs but more variable for proximal hypospadias.
