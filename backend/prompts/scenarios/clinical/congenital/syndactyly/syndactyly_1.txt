ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification of syndactyly
Knowing the classification: simple syndactyly involves skin and soft tissue only, complex syndactyly involves bony fusion, and complicated syndactyly involves accessory or abnormal bones.
Knowing the classification by extent: complete syndactyly extends to the fingertips including the nails, and incomplete syndactyly does not extend to the fingertips.
This child has complete simple syndactyly of the ring and little fingers.

Clinical assessment and exclusion of associated conditions
Examining both hands and feet for any other congenital anomalies.
Assessing the function of the affected fingers including range of motion and grip.
Knowing that syndactyly may be isolated or part of a syndrome such as Apert syndrome, Poland syndrome, or constriction band syndrome.
Knowing that Apert syndrome is associated with bilateral complex syndactyly of the hands and feet with craniosynostosis.

Timing of surgery
Knowing that syndactyly release is typically performed between twelve and eighteen months of age.
Knowing that border digit syndactyly involving the thumb and index finger, or the ring and little finger where there is a significant length discrepancy, should be released earlier at six to twelve months to prevent the longer finger from being tethered and developing a flexion contracture.

Surgical technique
Designing interdigitating zigzag flaps along the sides of the fingers using a dorsal rectangular flap for the web space reconstruction.
Raising the flaps carefully preserving the neurovascular bundles which may be shared between the digits.
Creating the new web space using the dorsal rectangular advancement flap.
Closing the zigzag flaps on each side of the fingers.

Skin coverage and grafting
Knowing that there is always insufficient skin to close both fingers after separation, and full thickness skin grafts are needed to fill the remaining defects.
Knowing that full thickness skin grafts are taken from the groin crease, wrist crease, or hypothenar eminence for colour and texture match.
Knowing that split thickness skin grafts should be avoided in the hand as they contract significantly.

Post-operative care and complications
Applying a bulky dressing and a plaster of Paris backslab.
First dressing change at approximately seven to ten days.
Hand therapy for scar management and mobilisation.
Long-term follow up to monitor for web creep, scar contracture, and any functional limitations as the hand grows.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification of syndactyly
Knowing the incidence: approximately one in two thousand live births, more common in males.
Knowing the inheritance pattern: autosomal dominant with variable penetrance in familial cases.
Knowing the embryology: syndactyly results from failure of programmed cell death or apoptosis in the interdigital webs between the fifth and eighth week of gestation.

Clinical assessment and exclusion of associated conditions
Knowing that an X-ray of the hand should be performed to assess for bony fusion which would indicate complex syndactyly and may complicate the surgical approach.
Knowing Poland syndrome: unilateral syndactyly with absent pectoralis major sternal head and sometimes absent breast and nipple.
Knowing that bilateral syndactyly should prompt assessment for Apert syndrome including examination of the skull for craniosynostosis.

Timing of surgery
Knowing that only one side of a finger should be released at a time to avoid compromising the blood supply. If adjacent web spaces are involved, they should be released in staged procedures separated by at least three months.
Knowing that in Apert syndrome, a specific staged release protocol is followed with the border digits released first.

Surgical technique
Knowing the dorsal rectangular flap technique for web space reconstruction in detail: a rectangular flap is designed on the dorsum of the hand centred over the web space, raised and advanced distally to form the new web commissure. The flap should be positioned at the level of the normal web space which is at the level of the proximal interphalangeal joint.
Knowing that the neurovascular bundles should be carefully identified and preserved, and in complex syndactyly they may be shared or anomalous requiring meticulous dissection.
Knowing the use of the lateral perforator flap technique as an alternative to avoid the need for skin grafts.

Skin coverage and grafting
Knowing that graft contraction over joints can lead to flexion contractures and should be avoided where possible by using local flaps over the joints.
Knowing that grafts on the lateral surfaces of the fingers are more likely to contract.
Knowing the defatting technique for full thickness skin grafts to optimise take.

Post-operative care and complications
Knowing the complications: web creep which is the most common long-term complication where the web space gradually migrates distally, graft loss, infection, digital ischaemia from compromise of shared neurovascular bundles, scar contracture, and nail deformity.
Knowing that web creep may require secondary Z-plasty or further surgery.
Knowing that the functional outcome is generally very good for simple syndactyly but more variable for complex and complicated cases.
Knowing that long-term follow up should continue until skeletal maturity as the hand grows.
