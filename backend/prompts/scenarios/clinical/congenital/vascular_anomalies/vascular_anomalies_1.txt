Vascular Anomalies

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 4 month old baby who has been referred to your clinic with a rapidly growing red lesion on her right cheek. The parents report that it appeared as a small red mark shortly after birth and has been growing rapidly over the past few weeks. It now measures approximately three centimetres and is raised and firm. You have been asked to assess and formulate a management plan.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis and differentiation of vascular anomalies
Knowing the I S S V A classification which divides vascular anomalies into vascular tumours and vascular malformations.
Knowing the key difference: haemangiomas are benign vascular tumours characterised by endothelial cell proliferation, whereas vascular malformations are congenital structural anomalies of blood vessels that do not proliferate.
Infantile haemangioma typically appears as a herald patch at birth, begins rapid growth after the first month of life, reaches maximum size between six and twenty four months, and then gradually involutes. Fifty percent involute by five years, seventy percent by seven years, and ninety percent by nine years.
Vascular malformations are present from birth, grow in proportion with the child, and do not involute.

Natural history of infantile haemangioma
Knowing the proliferative phase from one to twelve months, the involuting phase from one to five years, and the involuted phase.
Knowing that fifty percent of patients will have some residuum after involution such as residual skin laxity, fibrofatty tissue, or telangiectasia.

Assessment for associated conditions
Knowing that large facial haemangiomas may be associated with P H A C E S syndrome: Posterior fossa anomalies such as Dandy-Walker malformation, Haemangioma, Arterial anomalies, Cardiac anomalies such as coarctation of the aorta, Eye anomalies, and Sternal or midline defects.
Asking about any coordination problems, diagnosed heart conditions, or visual problems.
Examining for any associated anomalies.
Assessing the impact on the child and family including whether the haemangioma affects vision, breathing, feeding, or causes ulceration or bleeding.

Indications for active treatment
Knowing that the majority of infantile haemangiomas can be observed as they will involute spontaneously.
Active treatment is indicated for: function-threatening haemangiomas affecting the eyes, nose, ears, airway, or genitalia; life-threatening haemangiomas such as airway haemangiomas; disfiguring haemangiomas such as large facial lesions; and ulcerated haemangiomas causing pain or bleeding.

Medical treatment with propranolol
Knowing that propranolol is the first-line medical treatment for problematic infantile haemangiomas.
Knowing the mechanism: propranolol causes vasoconstriction and decreases V E G F, essentially stimulating involution.
Knowing the dose: one to two milligrams per kilogram per day given orally.
Knowing that the child is typically admitted for monitoring when treatment is initiated, with monitoring of heart rate, blood pressure, and blood glucose.

Surgical and laser options
Knowing that surgical excision can be performed for residual deformity after involution or for haemangiomas that do not respond to medical treatment.
Knowing that pulsed dye laser can be used for superficial haemangiomas and residual telangiectasia.

Management of vascular malformations
Knowing the classification of vascular malformations by flow type: low-flow including capillary, venous, and lymphatic malformations, and high-flow which are arteriovenous malformations.
Knowing the treatment options by type.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis and differentiation of vascular anomalies
Knowing that congenital haemangiomas are a separate entity from infantile haemangiomas. They are fully formed at birth and may be rapidly involuting, non-involuting, or partially involuting.
Knowing that infantile haemangiomas are G L U T one positive on immunohistochemistry, which distinguishes them from other vascular tumours.

Natural history of infantile haemangioma
Knowing that ulceration is the most common complication of infantile haemangioma during the proliferative phase, causing pain and risk of infection and scarring.
Knowing that periorbital haemangiomas can cause amblyopia, astigmatism, and strabismus due to pressure on the developing visual axis.

Assessment for associated conditions
Knowing that multiple cutaneous haemangiomas may be associated with hepatic haemangiomas requiring abdominal ultrasound screening.
Knowing that segmental haemangiomas in the beard distribution may be associated with airway haemangiomas.
Knowing that lumbosacral haemangiomas may be associated with spinal dysraphism.

Indications for active treatment
Knowing the specific indications for urgent treatment: periorbital haemangiomas threatening vision, subglottic haemangiomas threatening the airway, and rapidly ulcerating haemangiomas.

Medical treatment with propranolol
Knowing the contraindications to propranolol: reactive airway disease, heart block, and phaeochromocytoma.
Knowing the side effects: bradycardia, hypotension, hypoglycaemia, bronchospasm, and sleep disturbance.
Knowing that treatment is typically continued for six to twelve months and then gradually weaned.
Knowing that rebound growth may occur if propranolol is stopped too early.
Knowing that before propranolol, systemic corticosteroids were the mainstay of treatment but are now second line due to side effects.

Surgical and laser options
Knowing the role of Y A G laser for deeper haemangiomas and residual fibrofatty tissue.
Knowing that surgical excision of involuted haemangioma residuum can be performed using a lenticular excision technique.

Management of vascular malformations
Knowing the specific malformation types in more detail.
Port-wine stain or naevus flammeus is a capillary or venous malformation most commonly on the face along the trigeminal nerve distribution. If in the V one distribution, screening for Sturge-Weber syndrome is needed including M R I head for leptomeningeal malformations and ophthalmology review for glaucoma. Treatment is with pulsed dye laser with or without Y A G laser.
Knowing that if port-wine stain is left untreated, seventy percent develop into cobblestone ectasia.
Knowing Klippel-Trenaunay syndrome: extremity port-wine stains with deeper underlying venous or lymphatic malformations and skeletal hypertrophy.
Venous malformations deflate when elevated and fill when dependent. Colour Doppler ultrasound distinguishes low-flow from high-flow lesions. Treatment options include sclerotherapy, surgical excision, or laser.
Arteriovenous malformations are high-flow lesions that may require embolisation before surgical excision.
Knowing that M R I is the imaging of choice for characterising vascular malformations.
