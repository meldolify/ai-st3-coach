SCENARIO TITLE

Mallet Finger

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 30 year old man who has jammed his right little finger while catching a cricket ball earlier today. He has noticed that the tip of his finger is drooping and he cannot straighten it. He has presented to the emergency department. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism: forced flexion injury to the extended D I P joint.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, and sporting activities.
Noting the time of injury.

Clinical diagnosis and examination
Recognising the clinical presentation of mallet finger: inability to actively extend the D I P joint with a flexion deformity.
Assessing the degree of extensor lag at the D I P joint.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the finger.
Examining for any wound over the dorsum of the D I P joint which would indicate an open mallet injury.

Investigations
X-ray of the finger including A P and lateral views.
Looking for an avulsion fracture of the dorsal base of the distal phalanx.
Assessing whether the D I P joint is subluxed volarly.

Classification
Knowing the classification: tendinous mallet (closed soft tissue injury to the terminal extensor tendon) versus bony mallet (avulsion fracture of the dorsal base of the distal phalanx).
Knowing that the presence of a large bony fragment involving more than one third of the articular surface with volar subluxation of the distal phalanx may require surgical intervention.

Conservative management with splinting
Knowing that the majority of mallet injuries are managed conservatively with continuous splinting of the D I P joint in full extension or slight hyperextension.
Using a mallet splint such as a Stack splint or a custom-made thermoplastic splint.
Knowing that splinting duration is six to eight weeks of continuous splinting followed by a further six weeks of night splinting.
Emphasising that the D I P joint must not be allowed to flex at any point during the splinting period as this resets the clock on healing.

Indications for surgical management
Knowing the indications for surgery: open mallet injury requiring washout and repair with or without bone anchor, bony mallet with fracture involving more than fifty percent of the articular surface, bony mallet with greater than two millimetres of gap displacement, volar subluxation of the distal phalanx, failed conservative treatment, or a chronic mallet deformity.

Complications
Knowing the risk of swan neck deformity if mallet finger is left untreated, caused by proximal migration of the extensor mechanism leading to P I P joint hyperextension.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Asking about any delay in presentation as late presentation is common and does not preclude conservative management.
Asking about previous injuries to the same finger.

Clinical diagnosis and examination
Differentiating between an acute mallet finger and a chronic established deformity.
Assessing the P I P joint for early swan neck deformity.

Investigations
Knowing that a true lateral X-ray is essential to assess for volar subluxation of the D I P joint.
Assessing the size of the bony fragment as a percentage of the articular surface.

Classification
Knowing the Doyle classification of mallet injuries: type one is closed tendinous, type two is open tendinous, type three is deep soft tissue loss with tendon injury, and type four is bony mallet subdivided into four A (paediatric physeal fracture), four B (fracture involving less than fifty percent of the joint surface), and four C (fracture involving more than fifty percent with volar subluxation).

Conservative management with splinting
Knowing that even delayed mallet injuries up to three months can respond to splinting.
Counselling the patient on skin care under the splint and the importance of strict compliance.
Knowing that a residual extensor lag of five to ten degrees is common and acceptable after treatment.

Indications for surgical management
Knowing the surgical techniques for bony mallet: extension block pinning using a K-wire to maintain reduction, or open reduction and internal fixation for large displaced fragments.
Knowing the surgical technique for chronic mallet deformity: central slip tenotomy or spiral oblique retinacular ligament reconstruction.

Complications
Explaining the mechanism of swan neck deformity: loss of terminal extensor function leads to relative overaction of the central slip at the P I P joint, causing P I P hyperextension and D I P flexion.
Knowing that skin complications from splinting include maceration and pressure sores.
Knowing the potential for D I P joint stiffness after prolonged splinting.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
