SCENARIO TITLE

Fingertip Amputation

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 45 year old right hand dominant carpenter who has amputated the tip of his left index finger on a table saw approximately two hours ago. He has brought the amputated part wrapped in a damp cloth. He has presented to the emergency department. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism: saw injury to the fingertip.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, and the functional importance of this finger.
Noting the time of injury and any first aid given.
Asking about tetanus status, last meal, smoking history, and drug history.

Assessment of the stump and amputated part
Examining the stump: level of amputation, bone exposure, nail bed involvement, and soft tissue condition.
Examining the amputated part: viability, level of amputation, and whether it is suitable for replantation or composite grafting.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the remaining finger.

Classification of the injury
Knowing the Allen classification of fingertip injuries based on the level of amputation: type one is pulp only, type two is pulp and nail bed, type three is pulp, nail bed, and partial distal phalanx, and type four is proximal to the lunula.
Knowing that the level and angle of injury determine the reconstructive approach.

Investigations
X-ray of the finger and the amputated part to assess the bony level of amputation and exclude any associated fractures.

Preservation of the amputated part
Knowing the correct preservation technique: wrap the part in saline-soaked gauze, place in a sealed plastic bag, and place the bag in a container with ice. The part should not be placed directly on ice or submerged in saline.

Reconstructive options
Knowing the options based on the level and type of injury.
For small defects with no bone exposure: healing by secondary intention which heals remarkably well and often restores sensation, with or without nail ablation to prevent hook nail deformity.
For defects with exposed bone: shortening and primary closure which allows faster return to work and less donor site morbidity, or reconstruction with local flaps to preserve length.
Local flap options including V-Y advancement flap for transverse or dorsal oblique amputations, and cross finger flap or thenar flap for volar oblique amputations.
Composite graft: replacing the amputated tip as a composite graft which works best in children and for small parts.
Split thickness or full thickness skin graft for defects with a good vascularised bed.

Post-operative care
Appropriate dressings and splinting.
Analgesia and wound care advice.
Hand therapy referral.
Follow up to monitor healing.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Asking about the angle of the amputation: transverse, volar oblique, or dorsal oblique, as this determines the reconstructive approach.
Asking about contamination of the wound and the amputated part.
Considering the patient's occupation and expectations for return to work.

Assessment of the stump and amputated part
Distinguishing between a guillotine-type clean amputation and a crush or avulsion amputation which affects the suitability for replantation.
Knowing that replantation of a single fingertip distal to the F D P insertion is rarely indicated in adults due to poor functional outcomes, with the exception of the thumb.

Classification of the injury
Knowing the Ishikawa classification for subzonal fingertip replantation.
Knowing that the angle of amputation is as important as the level in determining the reconstructive approach.

Investigations
Knowing that the X-ray of the amputated part helps determine the suitability for composite grafting or replantation.

Preservation of the amputated part
Knowing the warm ischaemia time for digital tissue is approximately twelve hours and cold ischaemia time is up to twenty four hours, but for fingertip amputations without significant muscle content, tolerance is longer.

Reconstructive options
Knowing the technique of a V-Y Atasoy advancement flap for transverse amputations: a triangular flap based on the volar pulp with advancement over the stump.
Knowing the technique of a Kutler lateral V-Y advancement flap using bilateral flaps.
Knowing the Moberg advancement flap for thumb tip amputations: a volar advancement flap based on both neurovascular bundles.
Knowing that the cross finger flap is a two stage procedure with flap division at approximately two to three weeks.
Knowing the indications for replantation of amputated fingertips: thumb amputations, multiple digit amputations, paediatric patients, and amputations in zone one or two with a sharp mechanism.
Knowing the concept of cap replantation for fingertip amputations using microsurgical technique.

Post-operative care
Advising the patient about expected healing times and sensory recovery.
Knowing the potential complications including cold intolerance, hypersensitivity, hook nail deformity, and neuroma formation.
Knowing that sensory recovery following flap procedures depends on the type of flap: innervated flaps such as the Moberg provide better sensation than non-innervated flaps.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
