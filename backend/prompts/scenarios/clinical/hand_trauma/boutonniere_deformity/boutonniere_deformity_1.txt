SCENARIO TITLE

Boutonniere Deformity

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 42 year old man who jammed his right middle finger in a car door three weeks ago. He did not seek medical attention at the time. He has now presented to the emergency department because his finger has developed a deformity with the middle joint bent down and the tip joint hyperextended. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Clinical diagnosis and mechanism of deformity
Recognising the boutonniere deformity: flexion at the P I P joint and hyperextension at the D I P joint.
Knowing the mechanism: disruption of the central slip of the extensor tendon at its insertion on the base of the middle phalanx, causing the lateral bands to migrate volarly below the axis of rotation of the P I P joint, resulting in P I P flexion and D I P hyperextension.

History and examination
Asking about the mechanism and timing of injury.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, and functional impact.
Assessing the degree of flexion deformity at the P I P joint and hyperextension at the D I P joint.
Assessing whether the P I P joint deformity is passively correctable, which distinguishes a supple from a fixed deformity.
Performing the Elson test: flexing the P I P joint over the edge of a table at ninety degrees and asking the patient to extend against resistance. In a central slip injury, the P I P joint cannot extend but the D I P joint becomes rigid in hyperextension.

Investigations
X-ray of the finger to assess for an avulsion fracture at the base of the middle phalanx and to exclude any joint subluxation.

Classification as acute or chronic
Knowing that this is a subacute injury at three weeks.
Knowing that acute injuries are within the first three weeks, subacute between three and six weeks, and chronic beyond six weeks.
Knowing that a flexible deformity has a better prognosis than a fixed deformity.

Conservative management with splinting
Knowing that acute and subacute boutonniere deformities with a passively correctable P I P joint should be managed with splinting.
Applying a P I P extension splint holding the P I P joint in full extension while leaving the D I P joint free to flex.
Active D I P flexion exercises are important as they help draw the lateral bands dorsally back to their anatomical position.
Splinting duration is six weeks of continuous P I P extension splinting.

Indications for surgical management
Knowing the indications for surgery: failed conservative treatment, fixed P I P joint contracture, open injury with central slip laceration, or displaced avulsion fracture from the base of the middle phalanx.

Complications
Knowing the risk of P I P joint stiffness and residual flexion contracture.
Knowing that chronic boutonniere deformity is more difficult to treat and outcomes are less predictable.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Clinical diagnosis and mechanism of deformity
Being able to draw or describe the anatomy of the extensor mechanism at the P I P joint: central slip, lateral bands, triangular ligament, transverse retinacular ligament, and oblique retinacular ligament.
Knowing that boutonniere means buttonhole in French, referring to the P I P joint herniating through the torn central slip like a button through a buttonhole.

History and examination
Differentiating between traumatic boutonniere and rheumatoid boutonniere which is caused by synovitis weakening the central slip.
Knowing the Burton and Melchior classification of acute boutonniere injuries.

Investigations
Knowing that ultrasound can be useful to assess the integrity of the central slip if the diagnosis is uncertain.

Classification as acute or chronic
Knowing that chronic fixed boutonniere deformity may require serial casting or a dynamic extension splint before surgery to achieve passive P I P extension.

Conservative management with splinting
Knowing that compliance with splinting is critical and the patient must understand that allowing the P I P to flex during the splinting period compromises the outcome.
Knowing the options for splinting: relative motion extension splint, capener splint for dynamic extension, or custom thermoplastic splint.

Indications for surgical management
Knowing the surgical options: direct central slip repair for acute open injuries, central slip reconstruction using a lateral band transfer for chronic injuries, Fowler central slip tenotomy for fixed deformities, and extensor tenolysis for stiffness.
Knowing that joint release surgery such as a checkrein ligament release may be needed for fixed P I P contractures.
Knowing that in severe chronic deformity, P I P joint fusion in a functional position may be the best option.

Complications
Knowing the difference between a true boutonniere deformity and pseudo-boutonniere caused by P I P volar plate scarring without central slip injury.
Knowing that the results of surgical reconstruction for chronic boutonniere deformity are often disappointing and patients should be counselled about this.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
