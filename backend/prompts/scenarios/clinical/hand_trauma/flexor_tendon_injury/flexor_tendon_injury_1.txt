SCENARIO TITLE

Flexor Tendon Injury

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 28 year old right hand dominant chef who has cut himself on a kitchen knife across the volar aspect of his left ring finger at the level of the proximal phalanx. He is unable to bend the tip of his finger. He has presented to the emergency department about an hour after the injury. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism: sharp laceration from a knife.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, hobbies, tetanus status, last meal, and drug history.
Noting the time of injury.

Examination and tendon testing
Testing F D P by holding the proximal interphalangeal joint in extension and asking the patient to flex the distal interphalangeal joint.
Testing F D S by holding the other fingers in extension and asking the patient to flex the affected finger at the proximal interphalangeal joint.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the finger including digital nerve sensation and capillary refill.
Examining the wound noting its location and any visible structures.

Flexor tendon zones
Identifying the zone of injury. A laceration at the level of the proximal phalanx is zone two.
Knowing the five zones of flexor tendon injury: zone one is distal to F D S insertion, zone two is the fibro-osseous tunnel from A one pulley to F D S insertion, zone three is the palm, zone four is the carpal tunnel, and zone five is the forearm.
Knowing that zone two is historically known as no man's land and has the most challenging outcomes due to the confined space of the fibro-osseous tunnel.

Investigations
X-ray of the finger to exclude an associated fracture or foreign body.

Surgical management and repair technique
Knowing that flexor tendon repair should be performed within two weeks as per B S S H guidelines, ideally within four days.
Performing the repair under regional or general anaesthesia with an arm tourniquet.
Using a Brunner zigzag incision to extend the wound for exposure.
Identifying the severed tendon ends, retrieving the proximal end if retracted.
Performing a core suture repair using a four strand technique with a non-absorbable braided suture such as three-oh or four-oh Prolene.
Adding an epitendinous suture with a continuous running suture such as six-oh Prolene to smooth the repair surface.
Checking that the tendon glides smoothly through the pulleys after repair.

Post-operative rehabilitation
Placing the hand in a dorsal blocking splint with the wrist in slight flexion and the metacarpophalangeal joints in flexion.
Starting early active mobilisation within the first few days under hand therapy supervision.
Knowing the importance of a structured rehabilitation protocol to prevent adhesion formation and re-rupture.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Asking about contamination of the wound.
Assessing the patient's understanding of the injury and compliance for rehabilitation which is critical for outcome.

Examination and tendon testing
Knowing that a partial tendon laceration may still allow some movement but with weakness and pain.
Testing each tendon individually and documenting the findings.
Assessing all digital nerves with two point discrimination if the patient is cooperative.

Flexor tendon zones
Knowing the significance of the pulley system: A two and A four pulleys are critical for preventing bowstringing and must be preserved or reconstructed.
Knowing that zone two injuries involve both F D P and F D S within the tight fibro-osseous tunnel and that both should be repaired where possible, although some surgeons excise one slip of F D S to reduce bulk.

Investigations
Knowing that ultrasound may be useful for assessing partial tendon injuries if there is diagnostic uncertainty.

Surgical management and repair technique
Knowing specific repair techniques such as the pulley-sparing four-core Adelaide repair using three-oh Prolene with a Silfverskiold epitendinous repair using five-oh Prolene.
Knowing that for partial lacerations of less than sixty percent, only an epitendinous repair is needed with no benefit from adding a core suture. For lacerations greater than sixty percent, a core suture plus epitendinous repair is required.
Knowing the principles of atraumatic tendon handling to avoid damaging the tendon surface.
Knowing that the A two and A four pulleys must be preserved. A one, A three, and A five can be vented to allow gliding if necessary.
Knowing that if both F D P and F D S are injured, repairing F D P alone is acceptable as repairing both may increase bulk and impede gliding.
Knowing that concurrent digital nerve repair should be performed at the same sitting if the nerve is also lacerated.
Knowing the management of delayed presentation beyond two weeks: the tendon retracts, muscle contracts, and the sheath scars. A tight repair risks the Quadriga effect. Options include single-stage tendon graft using palmaris longus, plantaris, extensor indicis, or F D S of the same finger if the flexor sheath is intact. For later presentation, a two-stage repair may be needed: first inserting a silicone rod to form a pseudosheath with passive range of motion exercises, then three months later removing the rod and performing a tendon graft using a Pulvertaft weave proximally and direct insertion or bone anchor distally.

Post-operative rehabilitation
Knowing the different rehabilitation protocols: early active motion, controlled active motion, and passive motion protocols.
Knowing that early active mobilisation has been shown to produce better outcomes than passive protocols in terms of range of motion and grip strength.
Knowing the complications of flexor tendon repair including adhesion formation, re-rupture, stiffness, and swan neck deformity.
Knowing that re-rupture occurs in approximately five to ten percent of cases and is most common in the first six weeks.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
