SCENARIO TITLE

Digital Nerve Injury

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 35 year old right hand dominant woman who has cut herself on a broken glass, sustaining a laceration to the ulnar border of her right index finger at the level of the proximal phalanx. She has noticed numbness on the ulnar side of the finger tip. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism: sharp laceration from broken glass.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, and specific requirements for fine sensation in this finger.
Asking about tetanus status, last meal, and allergies.

Examination and sensory testing
Testing light touch and two point discrimination on both sides of the finger. Normal static two point discrimination is less than six millimetres.
Comparing with the contralateral finger and the unaffected radial digital nerve of the same finger.
Documenting the area of sensory loss.

Assessment for associated injuries
Testing flexor and extensor tendon function as the digital nerve runs alongside the digital artery and flexor tendons.
Assessing capillary refill and digital perfusion to check for concurrent digital artery injury.
Examining the wound for any visible structures.

Investigations
X-ray of the finger to exclude fracture or foreign body, particularly glass which is radio-opaque.

Surgical management and nerve repair technique
Knowing that a digital nerve laceration with sensory loss requires primary repair.
Performing the repair under regional or general anaesthesia with an arm tourniquet and loupe magnification or an operating microscope.
Extending the wound using a Brunner zigzag incision for adequate exposure.
Identifying the proximal and distal nerve ends and freshening the edges.
Performing a direct epineural repair without tension using fine non-absorbable sutures such as nine-oh or ten-oh Nylon.
Ensuring the repair is performed without tension. If there is a gap, a nerve conduit or nerve graft may be required.

Post-operative care and expected outcomes
Splinting the finger in a protected position to avoid tension on the repair for approximately three weeks.
Knowing that sensory recovery begins distally and progresses at a rate of approximately one millimetre per day.
Advising the patient that recovery may take several months and may be incomplete.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Asking about the exact position of the hand at the time of injury as the level of tendon and nerve injury may differ from the skin wound.
Assessing the impact of sensory loss on the patient's occupation and daily activities.

Examination and sensory testing
Knowing the difference between static and moving two point discrimination.
Performing the Semmes-Weinstein monofilament test for objective sensory assessment.
Knowing that the digital nerves provide protective sensation and loss increases the risk of injury to the fingertip.

Assessment for associated injuries
Knowing that a combined digital nerve and artery injury may result in a cold, pale, or blue finger requiring concurrent vascular repair.
Knowing the anatomy of the digital neurovascular bundle and its relationship to the flexor tendon sheath.

Investigations
Knowing that a Doppler assessment may be useful if there is any concern about digital perfusion.

Surgical management and nerve repair technique
Knowing the principles of nerve repair: tension-free repair, correct fascicular alignment, and meticulous technique.
Knowing the options for bridging a nerve gap: nerve conduit for gaps less than three centimetres, or autologous nerve graft using a donor nerve such as the posterior interosseous nerve or lateral cutaneous nerve of the forearm.
Knowing that synthetic nerve conduits are available and are suitable for short gaps in sensory nerves.
Knowing the importance of repairing both digital nerves if both are injured as the radial digital nerve of the index finger is critical for pinch grip sensation.

Post-operative care and expected outcomes
Knowing the Highet classification of nerve recovery: S zero is no recovery, S one is deep cutaneous pain, S two is some superficial sensation, S three is recovery of two point discrimination greater than fifteen millimetres, S three plus is good recovery with two point discrimination between seven and fifteen millimetres, and S four is complete recovery.
Knowing that age is the most important predictor of nerve recovery with children achieving better outcomes.
Knowing the role of sensory re-education in the rehabilitation process.
Knowing the complications of nerve repair including neuroma formation, cold intolerance, and hypersensitivity.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
