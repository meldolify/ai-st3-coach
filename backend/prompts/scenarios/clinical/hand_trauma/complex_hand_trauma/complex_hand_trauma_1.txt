SCENARIO TITLE

Complex Hand Trauma

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 38 year old right hand dominant factory worker who has sustained a severe crush injury to his dominant hand in a roller press machine. He was brought in by ambulance with a heavily bandaged hand. The emergency department team has stabilised him and he is haemodynamically stable. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Primary survey and stabilisation
Performing an A T L S primary survey to ensure the patient is haemodynamically stable and there are no other associated injuries.
Controlling any active haemorrhage with direct pressure and elevation rather than blind clamping.

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism: crush injury in a roller press.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, hobbies, and the functional demands of his hand.
Asking about the timing of injury and any treatment given in the field.
Taking a medical history including tetanus status, drug history, allergies, and last meal.

Systematic hand examination
Performing a systematic examination once the dressings are removed.
Examining the skin and soft tissue for the extent of injury, viability, and contamination.
Assessing the skeleton by palpating each bone and testing for instability and deformity.
Testing each flexor tendon individually: F D P by holding the P I P in extension and asking the patient to flex the D I P, and F D S by holding the other fingers extended and asking the patient to flex at the P I P.
Testing each extensor tendon by asking the patient to extend against resistance.
Testing the neurovascular status of each finger: capillary refill, digital Allen test, and two point discrimination on both sides of each digit.

Investigations
X-ray of the hand including A P, lateral, and oblique views to assess fractures, dislocations, and any foreign bodies.

Initial wound management
Wound irrigation and debridement of any grossly contaminated or devitalised tissue.
Intravenous antibiotics.
Updating tetanus prophylaxis.
Analgesia.
Temporary wound dressing and splinting in the position of safe immobilisation.

Surgical planning and approach
Performing surgical exploration under general or regional anaesthesia with an arm tourniquet.
Taking a systematic approach to repair: skeletal stabilisation first, then tendon repair, then vascular repair, and finally nerve repair.
Documenting all injuries and the repair performed.

Post-operative care and rehabilitation
Hand elevation.
Appropriate splinting in the position of safe immobilisation.
Early hand therapy involvement for rehabilitation planning.
Follow up in the hand clinic.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Primary survey and stabilisation
Knowing that mangled extremity cases can be life threatening due to blood loss and associated injuries.
Knowing that life takes priority over limb.

History and mechanism of injury
Asking about the contamination level of the injury site.
Asking about any ischaemia time for any devascularised digits.
Considering the occupational implications and early involvement of the occupational therapy team.

Systematic hand examination
Breaking the examination down by anatomical location for each injured area.
Assessing the viability of each digit and making early decisions about salvageability.
Identifying which structures can be repaired primarily and which may need staged reconstruction.

Investigations
Knowing that C T may be useful for complex fracture patterns.
Knowing that angiography may be needed if there is concern about major vessel injury in the hand or wrist.

Initial wound management
Knowing the position of safe immobilisation: wrist in twenty to thirty degrees extension, M C P joints in seventy to ninety degrees flexion, interphalangeal joints in full extension, and thumb in abduction.
Knowing that pulsatile lavage is not recommended for hand wounds as it can drive contamination deeper.

Surgical planning and approach
Knowing the instruments and equipment needed: hand set, K-wire set, micro set, microscope, and C-arm.
Knowing the order of repair: bone fixation for skeletal stability, tendon repair for motor function, vascular repair for perfusion, and nerve repair for sensation.
Knowing that a skin and soft tissue plan must be made to ensure coverage of all repaired structures, using local flaps or skin grafts as needed.
Making early decisions about amputation versus reconstruction based on the M E S S score or clinical judgement when digits are not salvageable.

Post-operative care and rehabilitation
Knowing the importance of early active mobilisation protocols when skeletal fixation is stable.
Planning for staged secondary procedures such as tendon grafting, nerve grafting, or flap coverage if needed.
Considering the psychological impact of hand trauma and offering appropriate support.
Knowing that the functional outcome depends on the severity of injury, quality of repair, and patient compliance with rehabilitation.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
