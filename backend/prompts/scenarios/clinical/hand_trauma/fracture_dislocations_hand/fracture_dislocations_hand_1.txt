SCENARIO TITLE

Fracture Dislocations of the Hand

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 28 year old man who has dislocated his right ring finger P I P joint during a rugby match. The finger was pulled back forcefully by another player. It was reduced on the pitch by the team physio but the finger remains swollen and painful. He has presented to the emergency department. The X-ray shows a small volar lip fracture of the base of the middle phalanx with a congruent joint. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism: hyperextension injury causing dorsal dislocation.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, and sporting involvement.
Asking whether the joint was reduced and by whom.

Examination and stability testing
Examining the finger for swelling, deformity, and skin integrity.
Assessing active and passive range of motion at the P I P joint.
Testing the collateral ligaments for stability with the P I P joint in slight flexion.
Assessing the stability of the joint through a gentle arc of motion to determine the angle at which the joint redislocates.
Testing the central slip by performing the Elson test to exclude a boutonniere injury.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the finger.

Radiological assessment
Interpreting the X-ray: identifying the volar lip fracture fragment, assessing its size as a percentage of the articular surface, and confirming joint congruency.
Knowing that a true lateral X-ray is essential to assess joint congruence and fracture fragment size.

Classification of P I P joint injuries
Knowing the classification based on fracture fragment size: stable if the fracture involves less than thirty percent of the articular surface with a congruent joint, unstable if greater than thirty percent, and fracture-dislocation if the joint is incongruent.
Knowing the difference between dorsal dislocations (most common, hyperextension mechanism, volar plate injury) and volar dislocations (rare, central slip disruption).

Conservative management
Knowing that stable P I P joint injuries with a congruent joint and less than thirty percent articular involvement can be managed conservatively.
Neighbour strapping to the adjacent finger and early active mobilisation.
Extension block splinting if the joint is stable within a limited arc, blocking the last degrees of extension where the joint subluxes.
Knowing that the most important principle is early mobilisation to prevent stiffness.

Indications for surgical management
Fracture involving more than thirty to forty percent of the articular surface.
Joint incongruence or subluxation on the lateral X-ray.
Unstable joint that redislocates within a functional range of motion.
Failed conservative management with persistent subluxation.

Rehabilitation and complications
Early active mobilisation is critical for outcome.
Knowing the risk of P I P joint stiffness which is the most common complication.
Knowing the risk of post-traumatic arthritis.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Asking whether there was any rotational component to the injury.
Asking about any previous injuries to the same finger.

Examination and stability testing
Knowing that the collateral ligaments are assessed with the joint in thirty degrees of flexion as they are relaxed in extension.
Performing a dynamic fluoroscopic assessment under anaesthesia if there is uncertainty about stability.

Radiological assessment
Knowing that C T may be useful for complex fracture patterns to assess articular congruity and fragment size.
Identifying the V sign on lateral X-ray which indicates joint subluxation.

Classification of P I P joint injuries
Knowing the Hastings and Carroll classification of P I P fracture dislocations.
Knowing that pilon fractures of the P I P joint from axial loading carry a worse prognosis.

Conservative management
Knowing the technique of extension block pinning as a minimally invasive option for unstable dorsal fracture dislocations: a K-wire is placed dorsally to act as a block preventing the last degrees of extension while allowing flexion.
Knowing the Suzuki traction technique using a dynamic external fixator for comminuted or pilon-type fractures.

Indications for surgical management
Knowing the surgical options: extension block pinning, open reduction and internal fixation with mini screws or a plate, volar plate arthroplasty for chronic injuries, and hemi-hamate autograft for large volar lip defects.
Knowing the principles of volar plate arthroplasty: the volar plate is advanced into the defect and secured with a pull-out suture.
Knowing that hemi-hamate arthroplasty uses an osteochondral graft from the hamate to reconstruct the volar base of the middle phalanx.

Rehabilitation and complications
Knowing that swan neck deformity can result from chronic volar plate laxity at the P I P joint.
Knowing the long term risk of post-traumatic arthritis particularly with articular incongruity.
Knowing that P I P joint stiffness is extremely common and difficult to treat once established, which reinforces the importance of early mobilisation.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
