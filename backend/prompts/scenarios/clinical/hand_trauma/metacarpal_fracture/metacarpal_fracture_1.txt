SCENARIO TITLE

Metacarpal Fracture

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 22 year old right hand dominant man who has punched a wall in anger and has a swollen and painful right hand. He has presented to the emergency department. The X-ray shows a displaced fracture of the neck of the fifth metacarpal with approximately forty degrees of angulation. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism: punch injury.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, and functional demands.
Considering the possibility of a fight bite injury and examining specifically for wounds over the M C P joint.
Asking about tetanus status and allergies.

Examination
Examining for swelling, deformity, and rotational malalignment.
Assessing rotational alignment by asking the patient to make a fist and checking that all fingers point towards the scaphoid without scissoring or overlap.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the hand.
Specifically examining for any wound over the M C P joint that could indicate a fight bite.

Radiological assessment
Interpreting the X-ray showing a fracture of the neck of the fifth metacarpal, known as a boxer fracture.
Assessing the degree of angulation, displacement, and any shortening.

Classification and acceptable angulation
Knowing the acceptable angulation for metacarpal neck fractures varies by digit: up to fifteen degrees for the index and middle metacarpals, up to thirty degrees for the ring metacarpal, and up to forty degrees for the little finger metacarpal due to the greater compensatory motion at the C M C joint.
Knowing that rotational malalignment is never acceptable regardless of angulation.

Conservative versus operative management
Knowing that most boxer fractures can be managed conservatively if the angulation is within acceptable limits and there is no rotational deformity.
Conservative management with neighbour strapping or ulnar gutter splint with the M C P joint in flexion.
Knowing the indications for surgical fixation: unacceptable angulation, rotational deformity, open fracture, multiple metacarpal fractures, or intra-articular fractures.

Surgical technique
Knowing the options for fixation including K-wire fixation, plate and screw fixation, and intramedullary techniques.
For fifth metacarpal neck fractures, closed reduction and percutaneous K-wire fixation is the most common technique.

Post-operative care and rehabilitation
Post-operative splinting and dressing.
K-wire removal at approximately four weeks.
Hand therapy referral for mobilisation.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Considering the social context of the injury and any safeguarding concerns.
Asking about alcohol or substance use at the time of injury.

Examination
Checking for loss of the normal knuckle contour which indicates metacarpal neck fracture.
Examining the cascade of the fingers in extension as well as flexion for rotational deformity.

Radiological assessment
Requesting three views: A P, lateral, and oblique.
Knowing how to measure the angulation on the lateral view.

Classification and acceptable angulation
Knowing the differences between metacarpal head, neck, shaft, and base fractures and their management principles.
Knowing that metacarpal shaft fractures have less tolerance for angulation than neck fractures.
Knowing that thumb metacarpal base fractures include Bennett fracture (intra-articular fracture-dislocation) and Rolando fracture (comminuted intra-articular), both of which usually require surgical fixation.

Conservative versus operative management
Knowing that early mobilisation produces better functional outcomes than prolonged immobilisation.
Knowing that neighbour strapping alone may be sufficient for minimally angulated fifth metacarpal neck fractures.

Surgical technique
Describing the technique of closed reduction and K-wire fixation: reduction under image intensifier, percutaneous insertion of one or two K-wires from the metacarpal head across the fracture site.
Knowing the alternatives: antegrade intramedullary K-wire from the base, retrograde K-wire from the head, or cross K-wire to the adjacent metacarpal.
Knowing the indications for plate and screw fixation: shaft fractures, multiple fractures, or fractures requiring anatomical reduction.

Post-operative care and rehabilitation
Knowing that K-wires should be left protruding through the skin for easy removal in clinic.
Knowing the complications: pin site infection, K-wire migration, malunion, stiffness, extensor lag, and refracture.
Knowing that most patients return to normal function by three months.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
