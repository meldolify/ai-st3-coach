SCENARIO TITLE

Extensor Tendon Injury

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 40 year old man who has sustained a laceration over the dorsum of his right hand at the level of the metacarpophalangeal joint of his middle finger after a glass injury at a pub. He is unable to fully extend the finger. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism: glass laceration.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, tetanus status, last meal, and allergies.
Considering the possibility of a human bite injury particularly over the M C P joint and asking specifically about this, as a fight bite has a different management pathway.

Examination and tendon testing
Testing extension of the affected finger at the M C P joint, P I P joint, and D I P joint.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the finger.
Examining the wound noting its location and any visible structures.
Examining the wound with the M C P joint flexed to see if the tendon is lacerated at a different level to the skin wound.

Extensor tendon zones
Identifying the zone of injury. A laceration at the M C P joint level is zone five.
Knowing the extensor tendon zones: zone one is over the D I P joint, zone two is the middle phalanx, zone three is over the P I P joint, zone four is the proximal phalanx, zone five is over the M C P joint, zone six is the dorsal hand, zone seven is under the extensor retinaculum, and zone eight is the forearm.

Investigations
X-ray of the hand to exclude fracture, foreign body, or joint involvement.

Surgical management and repair technique
Performing the repair under regional or general anaesthesia with an arm tourniquet.
Thorough wound washout and exploration, particularly if there is any possibility of a bite injury.
Repairing the tendon using a core suture technique appropriate for the zone and tendon thickness.
For zone five injuries, a figure of eight or modified Kessler suture with a non-absorbable suture such as four-oh Prolene.
Wound closure in layers.

Post-operative splinting and rehabilitation
Splinting the wrist in thirty degrees extension and the M C P joints in neutral with the interphalangeal joints free to move.
Knowing the duration of splinting is approximately six weeks for zone five injuries.
Hand therapy referral for supervised rehabilitation.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Recognising a fight bite or human bite injury which requires washout, antibiotics, and should not be closed primarily due to the risk of joint sepsis.
Asking about alcohol intake as this may affect consent and compliance.

Examination and tendon testing
Knowing that E D C may have juncturae tendinae which allow partial extension even with a complete laceration through the contribution of adjacent tendons.
Testing the extensor indicis proprius and extensor digiti minimi separately as these have independent function.

Extensor tendon zones
Knowing the significance of odd numbered zones which overlie joints and are more complex injuries.
Knowing that zone five injuries may involve the sagittal band which can lead to extensor tendon subluxation if not repaired.

Investigations
Knowing that if a fight bite is suspected, the wound should be explored in theatre for joint washout even if the X-ray is normal.

Surgical management and repair technique
Knowing that extensor tendon repairs are generally easier than flexor repairs as the tendons are more accessible and have fewer pulleys.
Knowing that fight bite injuries over the M C P joint require formal joint washout, intravenous antibiotics covering oral flora including Eikenella corrodens as the typical culprit organism, such as co-amoxiclav, and should be left open or loosely closed.
Knowing that the sagittal band should be repaired if disrupted to prevent tendon subluxation.

Post-operative splinting and rehabilitation
Knowing the different splinting protocols for different zones.
Knowing that zones one and two injuries, mallet injuries, are treated with splinting alone in most cases.
Knowing the relative motion splinting protocol as an alternative for dynamic rehabilitation.
Knowing the complications of extensor tendon repair including adhesion formation, loss of flexion, extensor lag, and tendon rupture.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
