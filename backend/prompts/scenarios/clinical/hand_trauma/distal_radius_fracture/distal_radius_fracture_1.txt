SCENARIO TITLE

Distal Radius Fracture

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 62 year old right hand dominant lady who has fallen onto her outstretched left hand while walking her dog. She has a painful, swollen, and deformed left wrist. She has presented to the emergency department. The X-ray shows a displaced and comminuted distal radius fracture with dorsal angulation and intra-articular extension. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism: fall onto outstretched hand.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, functional demands, and pre-injury level of independence.
Considering osteoporosis as an underlying cause in a post-menopausal woman.
Asking about drug history, allergies, and medical comorbidities.

Examination
Assessing for the classic dinner fork deformity of a dorsally displaced distal radius fracture.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the hand, specifically testing median nerve function as acute carpal tunnel syndrome can complicate distal radius fractures.
Assessing the skin for any open wounds.
Examining the ipsilateral elbow, forearm, and hand for associated injuries.

Radiological assessment and classification
Interpreting the A P and lateral X-rays: dorsal angulation, radial shortening, loss of radial inclination, and intra-articular involvement.
Knowing the normal radiological parameters: radial inclination twenty two degrees, radial height eleven millimetres, volar tilt eleven degrees.
Knowing the common eponymous fracture patterns: Colles fracture is dorsally displaced and angulated, Smith fracture is volarly displaced, and Barton fracture is an intra-articular fracture-dislocation.

Initial management and reduction
Providing analgesia including a haematoma block with local anaesthetic injected into the fracture site.
Attempting closed reduction under appropriate anaesthesia if the fracture is displaced.
Applying a below-elbow backslab in a position of slight flexion and ulnar deviation after reduction.
Obtaining post-reduction X-rays to assess the quality of the reduction.

Indications for operative versus conservative management
Knowing the indications for operative fixation: intra-articular fracture with articular step greater than two millimetres, dorsal angulation greater than ten degrees after reduction, radial shortening greater than three millimetres, open fracture, associated carpal injury, and fractures that redisplace in plaster.
Knowing that stable, minimally displaced extra-articular fractures can be managed conservatively in a cast for six weeks.

Surgical options
Knowing the surgical options including volar locking plate fixation, external fixation, and percutaneous K-wire fixation.
Knowing that volar locking plate is the most commonly used technique for unstable distal radius fractures.

Post-operative care and rehabilitation
Post-operative splinting or casting as appropriate.
Elevation and finger mobilisation from day one.
Hand therapy referral for wrist mobilisation once fixation allows.
Follow up with serial X-rays to monitor healing.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Considering the need for a bone density assessment in a post-menopausal woman with a fragility fracture.
Asking about a history of previous fractures which may suggest osteoporosis.
Referring for a D E X A scan and considering bone protection therapy.

Examination
Performing a thorough median nerve assessment: sensation in the thumb, index, and middle fingers, and opposition strength.
Knowing the signs of acute carpal tunnel syndrome: progressive numbness, severe pain, and weakness of thumb opposition, which may require urgent carpal tunnel release.
Assessing the distal radio-ulnar joint for instability.

Radiological assessment and classification
Knowing the Frykman, Melone, or A O classification systems for distal radius fractures.
Knowing that C T may be useful for complex intra-articular fractures to assess articular congruity and plan operative fixation.

Initial management and reduction
Describing the technique of haematoma block: inject local anaesthetic into the fracture haematoma using aseptic technique.
Describing the reduction manoeuvre: traction, disimpaction, and correction of dorsal angulation and radial deviation.
Knowing the acceptable reduction parameters: less than ten degrees dorsal angulation, less than two millimetres articular step, less than three millimetres radial shortening.

Indications for operative versus conservative management
Knowing that age and functional demands influence the decision: younger active patients generally benefit from anatomical reduction and fixation, while elderly low-demand patients may accept some malunion.
Knowing that fractures that redisplace in plaster within the first two weeks should be considered for operative fixation.

Surgical options
Describing the technique of volar locking plate fixation: volar approach through the flexor carpi radialis sheath, reduction of the fracture, application of an anatomically contoured locking plate, and fluoroscopic confirmation of reduction and screw placement.
Knowing the advantages of volar locking plate: stable fixation allowing early mobilisation, low profile, and reduced risk of extensor tendon irritation compared with dorsal plating.
Knowing the role of external fixation as bridging or non-bridging for highly comminuted fractures.
Knowing the role of fragment-specific fixation for complex articular patterns.
Knowing the role of bone grafting or bone substitutes for metaphyseal defects.

Post-operative care and rehabilitation
Knowing the complications: malunion, stiffness, complex regional pain syndrome, tendon rupture particularly extensor pollicis longus, carpal tunnel syndrome, distal radio-ulnar joint instability, and post-traumatic arthritis.
Knowing that complex regional pain syndrome should be recognised early with features of disproportionate pain, swelling, colour changes, and stiffness, and managed with hand therapy, analgesia, and escalation to a pain team if needed.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
