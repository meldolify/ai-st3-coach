SCENARIO TITLE

Scaphoid Fracture

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 25 year old right hand dominant man who has fallen onto his outstretched left hand while playing football. He has pain and swelling over the radial side of his wrist. He presented to the emergency department and the initial X-ray has been reported as normal. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism: fall onto outstretched hand.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, sporting activities, and any previous wrist injuries.
Noting the time of injury and any treatment given.

Examination of the wrist
Tenderness in the anatomical snuffbox which is the hallmark clinical sign of scaphoid fracture.
Tenderness over the scaphoid tubercle on the volar aspect of the wrist.
Pain on axial compression of the thumb, the telescoping test.
Assessing the range of movement of the wrist.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the hand.

Investigations and imaging
Knowing that initial plain X-rays may not show a scaphoid fracture in up to twenty percent of cases.
Requesting scaphoid series X-rays including posteroanterior, lateral, and oblique views with the wrist in ulnar deviation.
If the X-ray is normal but clinical suspicion is high, arranging further imaging. M R I is the gold standard for occult scaphoid fracture. C T scan can also be used to assess fracture configuration.
Arranging repeat X-rays at ten to fourteen days if M R I is not available, as a fracture line may become visible with resorption at the fracture site.

Initial management
Immobilising the wrist in a scaphoid cast or splint below the elbow including the thumb to the interphalangeal joint.
Analgesia.
Arranging follow up for further imaging or clinical review.

Classification and definitive management
Knowing the classification of scaphoid fractures by location: waist, proximal pole, distal pole, and tubercle.
Knowing that non-displaced waist fractures can be managed conservatively in a scaphoid cast for eight to twelve weeks.
Knowing the indications for surgical fixation including displaced fractures, proximal pole fractures, and fractures with associated ligament injury.
Knowing that surgical fixation is typically with a headless compression screw such as a Herbert screw.

Complications
Knowing the risk of avascular necrosis particularly of the proximal pole due to the retrograde blood supply of the scaphoid.
Knowing the risk of non-union which is more common in proximal pole fractures, displaced fractures, and delayed treatment.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Asking about smoking which affects fracture healing.
Asking about contact sport involvement and return to sport expectations.

Examination of the wrist
Assessing for associated injuries including distal radius fracture and ligamentous injuries of the wrist.
Knowing that scaphoid fracture can be associated with perilunate dislocation or scapholunate ligament injury.

Investigations and imaging
Knowing that M R I has the highest sensitivity and specificity for occult scaphoid fracture.
Knowing the role of C T in assessing fracture displacement, comminution, and humpback deformity to guide operative planning.
Knowing that bone scan is sensitive but less specific than M R I.

Initial management
Knowing the debate between below elbow and above elbow casting and that current evidence supports below elbow scaphoid casts.
Knowing that early surgical fixation may be offered to patients who cannot tolerate prolonged casting such as athletes or manual workers.

Classification and definitive management
Knowing the blood supply of the scaphoid: the dorsal branch of the radial artery supplies approximately eighty percent of the scaphoid in a retrograde fashion, making the proximal pole vulnerable to avascular necrosis.
Knowing the surgical technique for scaphoid fixation: either dorsal or volar approach depending on the fracture location, reduction, guidewire placement under fluoroscopy, and headless compression screw insertion.
Knowing that proximal pole fractures have a higher rate of non-union and avascular necrosis and may require bone grafting.
Knowing the management of established scaphoid non-union including vascularised bone graft from the distal radius, non-vascularised bone graft such as Matti-Russe procedure, and salvage procedures such as proximal row carpectomy or four corner fusion for advanced arthritis.

Complications
Knowing the long term consequence of scaphoid non-union advanced collapse, known as S N A C wrist, and its stages.
Knowing that avascular necrosis is identified on M R I and can be assessed intraoperatively by the presence or absence of punctate bleeding.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
