SCENARIO TITLE

Phalangeal Fracture

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 32 year old right hand dominant man who has caught his left index finger in a rugby tackle. His finger is swollen, bruised, and angulated. He has presented to the emergency department. The X-ray shows an oblique fracture of the proximal phalanx with rotational deformity. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism of injury.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, sporting activities, and functional demands.
Asking about tetanus status and allergies.

Examination with emphasis on rotational alignment
Assessing for swelling, deformity, angulation, and shortening.
Critically assessing rotational alignment: asking the patient to flex the fingers and checking that all fingertips point towards the scaphoid tubercle without scissoring.
Comparing the nail plate alignment of the injured finger with the adjacent fingers when viewed end-on.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the finger.
Assessing tendon function: flexor and extensor tendons.

Radiological assessment
Interpreting the A P and lateral X-rays showing an oblique proximal phalanx fracture.
Assessing for angulation, shortening, and intra-articular extension.

Classification and fracture pattern
Knowing the classification by location: base, shaft, neck, and condylar fractures of the proximal phalanx.
Knowing that oblique and spiral fractures tend to shorten and rotate, making them unstable.
Knowing that transverse fractures tend to angulate with dorsal apex angulation due to the interosseous muscles flexing the proximal fragment and the central slip extending the distal fragment.

Conservative versus operative management
Knowing that stable, non-displaced fractures without rotational deformity can be managed conservatively with neighbour strapping and early mobilisation.
Knowing that this fracture has rotational deformity and therefore requires operative fixation.
Knowing the indications for surgery: rotational malalignment, significant angulation, shortening, intra-articular fractures with a step, open fractures, and multiple fractures.

Surgical technique
Knowing the fixation options: K-wire fixation, lag screw fixation for oblique and spiral fractures, plate and screw fixation, and interosseous wiring.
For oblique fractures, lag screw fixation provides interfragmentary compression and allows early mobilisation.

Post-operative rehabilitation
Early active mobilisation under hand therapy supervision to prevent stiffness.
Knowing that phalangeal fractures are prone to adhesion of the extensor mechanism leading to loss of flexion.
Follow up with serial X-rays to monitor healing.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Asking about previous injuries to the same finger.
Assessing the patient's ability to comply with the rehabilitation programme.

Examination with emphasis on rotational alignment
Knowing that even five degrees of rotational deformity at the proximal phalanx results in one point five centimetres of finger overlap at the fingertip, making it functionally unacceptable.
Examining the tenodesis effect: passively extending the wrist should cause the fingers to flex in a cascade, which can reveal subtle rotational deformity.

Radiological assessment
Knowing that true lateral X-rays can be difficult to obtain for individual fingers and a Brewerton view may be useful for condylar fractures.

Classification and fracture pattern
Knowing that condylar fractures are intra-articular and are inherently unstable, almost always requiring operative fixation.
Knowing that base fractures may be associated with collateral ligament avulsion.

Conservative versus operative management
Knowing that early mobilisation produces better functional outcomes than prolonged immobilisation for stable fractures.
Knowing that neighbour strapping allows guided motion while protecting the fracture.

Surgical technique
Describing the technique of lag screw fixation for oblique fractures: the screw is inserted perpendicular to the fracture line through a mid-lateral approach, achieving interfragmentary compression.
Knowing the advantages and disadvantages of plate fixation: provides stable fixation but requires more soft tissue dissection and can cause extensor tendon adhesion.
Knowing that K-wires are simple and minimally invasive but do not provide compression and require supplementary splinting.
Knowing the importance of a mid-lateral approach to avoid disrupting the extensor mechanism.

Post-operative rehabilitation
Knowing that the most common complication of phalangeal fractures is stiffness due to extensor tendon adhesion.
Knowing that early active mobilisation within the first few days reduces the risk of adhesion.
Knowing the role of tenolysis as a secondary procedure for established stiffness that does not respond to therapy.
Knowing that malunion with rotational deformity may require corrective osteotomy.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
