SCENARIO TITLE

Thumb Ulnar Collateral Ligament Injury

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 35 year old right hand dominant man who has injured his left thumb while skiing. His thumb was forced into radial deviation when he fell with his hand caught in the ski pole strap. He has a painful and swollen thumb base. He has presented to the emergency department. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism: forced radial deviation or abduction of the thumb, which is the classic skier's thumb or gamekeeper's thumb mechanism.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, and functional demands, particularly pinch grip.
Noting the time of injury.

Examination and stress testing
Examining the thumb M C P joint for swelling, bruising, and tenderness over the ulnar aspect.
Performing a valgus stress test of the thumb M C P joint in extension and in thirty degrees of flexion, comparing with the contralateral thumb.
Knowing that more than thirty degrees of laxity or more than fifteen degrees compared with the contralateral side indicates a complete tear.
Knowing that stress testing in flexion isolates the proper collateral ligament, while testing in extension assesses the accessory collateral ligament.
Assessing for a palpable lump on the ulnar side of the M C P joint which may represent a displaced ligament end, known as a Stener lesion.

Investigations
X-ray of the thumb including A P, lateral, and oblique views.
Looking for an avulsion fracture from the base of the proximal phalanx on the ulnar side.
Assessing for any associated fracture or subluxation of the M C P joint.

Classification of the injury
Differentiating between a partial tear, where there is pain and some laxity but a firm endpoint on stress testing, and a complete tear, where there is significant laxity and no firm endpoint.
Knowing the concept of a Stener lesion: the torn ulnar collateral ligament displaces superficial to the adductor aponeurosis, which prevents healing and is an absolute indication for surgery.

Conservative versus operative management
Knowing the clinical decision pathway: if an avulsion fracture is present and displaced less than two millimetres, do not stress test further and immobilise in a thumb spica for four weeks. If there is no fracture, perform a stress test with or without local anaesthetic. If there is a firm endpoint, this is a sprain or partial tear and should be immobilised in a thumb spica for four weeks. If there is no firm endpoint, arrange ultrasound to check for a Stener lesion. If a Stener lesion is confirmed, operative repair is required.
Knowing that a Stener lesion cannot heal without surgery as the adductor aponeurosis prevents the ligament from returning to its anatomical position.

Surgical technique
Performing the repair through an incision over the ulnar aspect of the thumb M C P joint.
Identifying and protecting the dorsal sensory branch of the radial nerve.
Identifying the Stener lesion with the ligament displaced superficial to the adductor aponeurosis.
Repairing the ligament back to the bone using a suture anchor or transosseous sutures.
If there is an avulsion fracture, fixing the bony fragment with a K-wire or small screw.

Post-operative rehabilitation
Thumb spica splint or cast for six weeks post-operatively.
Gradual mobilisation after splint removal.
Hand therapy for strengthening and return to function.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Knowing the difference between skier's thumb (acute injury) and gamekeeper's thumb (chronic attritional injury from repetitive stress).
Asking about previous thumb injuries.

Examination and stress testing
Knowing that stress testing should be performed gently initially and with local anaesthetic or a digital nerve block if the patient is too painful.
Knowing that stress X-rays under anaesthesia can confirm the degree of laxity.
Knowing that ultrasound or M R I can identify a Stener lesion preoperatively if the clinical diagnosis is uncertain.

Investigations
Knowing that M R I has high sensitivity and specificity for diagnosing both the degree of ligament tear and the presence of a Stener lesion.
Knowing that stress radiographs can be performed under local anaesthetic to objectively quantify the degree of laxity.

Classification of the injury
Knowing the grading system: grade one is a sprain with no laxity, grade two is a partial tear with increased laxity but a firm endpoint, and grade three is a complete tear with no endpoint.

Conservative versus operative management
Knowing that delayed presentation beyond three weeks may require ligament reconstruction rather than primary repair.
Knowing that chronic U C L insufficiency causes weakness of pinch grip which significantly affects hand function.

Surgical technique
Knowing the anatomy: the U C L is a fan-shaped ligament running from the metacarpal head to the base of the proximal phalanx, reinforced by the adductor aponeurosis and the volar plate.
Knowing the surgical options for chronic U C L insufficiency when primary repair is not possible: ligament reconstruction using a palmaris longus tendon graft or adductor advancement.
Knowing that M C P joint fusion in a functional position of approximately twenty degrees flexion may be considered as a salvage procedure for chronic instability with arthritis.

Post-operative rehabilitation
Knowing that return to sport typically takes three to four months after surgical repair.
Knowing the complications of surgery: stiffness, persistent weakness, nerve injury to the dorsal sensory branch of the radial nerve, and re-rupture.
Knowing that long term outcomes of primary repair are generally good with over ninety percent of patients achieving a stable, painless thumb.
