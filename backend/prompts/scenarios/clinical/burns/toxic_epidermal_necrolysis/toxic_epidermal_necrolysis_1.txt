SCENARIO TITLE

Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 50 year old woman who has been admitted to the medical ward with a rapidly progressing blistering skin eruption affecting approximately thirty five percent of her body surface area. She was started on allopurinol for gout three weeks ago. She has widespread erythema with confluent blistering and areas of skin sloughing. The Nikolsky sign is positive. The dermatology team have diagnosed toxic epidermal necrolysis and have asked for a plastic surgery opinion. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of the condition and its causes
Knowing that T E N is a severe, life-threatening mucocutaneous reaction most commonly caused by medications.
Knowing the common causative drugs: allopurinol, anticonvulsants such as carbamazepine and phenytoin, antibiotics such as sulphonamides, and N S A I D s.
Knowing the spectrum: Stevens-Johnson syndrome involves less than ten percent T B S A, S J S and T E N overlap involves ten to thirty percent, and T E N involves greater than thirty percent T B S A.
Knowing that the hallmark is widespread epidermal necrosis with skin detachment and a positive Nikolsky sign.

Clinical assessment and severity scoring
Performing a rapid A to E assessment as these patients can be critically unwell with fluid losses and sepsis.
Assessing the extent of skin involvement as a percentage of T B S A, including detached and detachable skin.
Examining the mucous membranes including oral, ocular, and genital mucosa which are frequently involved.
Knowing the S C O R T E N severity scoring system which predicts mortality based on seven parameters: age greater than forty, malignancy, T B S A greater than ten percent, heart rate greater than one hundred and twenty, serum bicarbonate less than twenty, blood glucose greater than fourteen, and blood urea greater than ten.

Identification and withdrawal of the causative drug
Knowing that the single most important step is immediate withdrawal of the causative medication.
Reviewing the patient's drug chart and stopping allopurinol immediately.
Knowing that the causative drug is typically started within the preceding one to three weeks.

Supportive management in a burns or I T U setting
Knowing that T E N should be managed in a burns unit or I T U as the management principles are similar to a major burn.
Fluid resuscitation: these patients have significant fluid losses through denuded skin, similar to burn patients, and require intravenous fluid replacement guided by urine output and haemodynamic status.
Temperature management: keeping the patient warm as thermoregulation is impaired.
Analgesia: T E N is extremely painful and adequate pain management is essential.
Nutritional support: early enteral nutrition via a nasogastric tube.
Infection prevention: strict barrier nursing. Prophylactic systemic antibiotics are not routinely recommended but should be started if there is evidence of sepsis.
Regular monitoring of fluid balance, electrolytes, and renal function.

Wound management
Knowing the options for managing the denuded skin: leaving detached epidermis in situ as a biological dressing if it is not infected, or gently debriding sloughing skin.
Applying non-adherent dressings to open areas.
Knowing that Biobrane or allograft can be used as a temporary wound cover for large areas of denuded skin.
Regular ophthalmology review for ocular involvement, which may include lubricating eye drops, removal of pseudomembranes, and amniotic membrane transplantation if needed.

Specific therapies
Knowing that the mainstay of treatment is supportive care and drug withdrawal.
Knowing that the role of immunomodulatory therapy is controversial but options include intravenous immunoglobulin, cyclosporine, and systemic corticosteroids. This would be a consultant-level decision discussed with dermatology.

Complications and prognosis
Knowing that the mortality rate for T E N is approximately thirty percent.
Knowing the acute complications: sepsis, fluid and electrolyte imbalance, acute kidney injury, and multi-organ failure.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of the condition and its causes
Knowing the pathophysiology: T E N involves widespread keratinocyte apoptosis mediated by drug-specific cytotoxic T lymphocytes and Fas ligand and granzyme B pathways.
Knowing the H L A associations: H L A B 5801 is associated with allopurinol-induced T E N, and H L A B 1502 is associated with carbamazepine-induced T E N, particularly in patients of South-East Asian descent.
Knowing the differential diagnosis including staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome which occurs in children and is caused by staphylococcal exfoliative toxin. In S S S S the split is superficial within the epidermis at the granular layer, whereas in T E N the split is at the dermo-epidermal junction, which can be distinguished on a skin biopsy.

Clinical assessment and severity scoring
Knowing the S C O R T E N score in detail and its mortality predictions: a score of zero to one predicts three percent mortality, two predicts twelve percent, three predicts thirty five percent, four predicts fifty eight percent, and five or more predicts greater than ninety percent mortality.
Knowing that the S C O R T E N should be calculated within the first twenty four hours and repeated at day three.
Knowing the Cole and Shakespeare criteria as an alternative severity assessment.

Identification and withdrawal of the causative drug
Knowing that a drug causality assessment should be performed using the A L D E N algorithm or similar tool.
Knowing that multiple drugs may be responsible and all non-essential medications started in the preceding weeks should be stopped if possible.

Supportive management in a burns or I T U setting
Knowing the specific nursing requirements: warmed room, low-adherence bedding, careful handling to minimise further skin loss, and regular turning to prevent pressure sores.
Knowing that urinary catheter care is important as urethral and genital mucosal involvement is common.
Knowing that bronchial involvement can occur causing respiratory failure and may require intubation and ventilation.

Wound management
Knowing that surgical debridement of necrotic skin is generally not recommended as the remaining epidermis provides some biological protection.
Knowing that allograft application can reduce pain and fluid loss.
Knowing that wound swabs should be taken regularly and infection managed promptly.
Knowing the importance of oral care: regular mouth rinses, topical anaesthetic gel, and soft diet.
Knowing the importance of genital mucosal care to prevent adhesions and strictures.

Specific therapies
Knowing the evidence for and against I V I G: the theoretical rationale is that pooled immunoglobulins contain anti-Fas antibodies that may block keratinocyte apoptosis. Evidence is mixed with some retrospective studies showing benefit at high doses of two to three grams per kilogram but randomised controlled trials are lacking.
Knowing that cyclosporine has emerging evidence suggesting it may reduce mortality by inhibiting T cell mediated keratinocyte apoptosis.
Knowing that systemic corticosteroids are controversial: they may reduce inflammation but could also impair wound healing and increase infection risk. If used, they should be given early and for a short course.
Knowing that plasmapheresis has been described but evidence is limited.
Knowing the antibiotics for suspected secondary infection: clindamycin and vancomycin combination.

Complications and prognosis
Knowing the long-term complications: chronic ocular surface disease including dry eyes, corneal scarring, and visual impairment; skin pigmentary changes; nail dystrophy; genital mucosal adhesions and strictures; and psychological morbidity.
Knowing that the patient and their family should be counselled about the specific drug allergy and that it should be clearly documented in their medical records and allergy alert systems.
Knowing that the patient should carry an allergy alert card and that cross-reactivity with related drugs should be considered.
Knowing that follow up should include ophthalmology, dermatology, and psychology.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
