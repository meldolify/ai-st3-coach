SCENARIO TITLE

Burn Wound Management

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 28 year old woman who sustained a scald burn to her right forearm three days ago when a pot of boiling water spilled on her at home. She was initially treated in the emergency department with dressings. She has been referred to the burns clinic for review. The burn covers approximately five percent of her total body surface area and appears to be a mixture of superficial and deep partial thickness. You have been asked to assess and formulate a wound management plan.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Burn depth reassessment at three days
Knowing that burn depth assessment at three days is more reliable than on the day of injury as the burn has had time to declare.
Reassessing depth by examining colour, capillary refill, sensation, and the condition of the wound bed.
Superficial partial thickness burns should be pink, painful, have blisters, and brisk capillary refill, and are expected to heal within fourteen days with dressings alone.
Deep partial thickness burns are mottled pink and white, have reduced sensation, sluggish capillary refill, and are unlikely to heal within three weeks without surgery.

Dressing options for partial thickness burns
Knowing the principles of burn wound dressings: maintain a moist wound environment, prevent infection, reduce pain, and allow assessment.
For superficial partial thickness burns, appropriate dressings include Biobrane, silver-based dressings such as Acticoat, alginates, foam dressings, or paraffin-based dressings such as Jelonet.
Knowing that Biobrane is a biological dressing consisting of a nylon mesh with porcine collagen and a silicone outer layer. It is applied in theatre after tangential debridement and adheres to the wound until healing occurs beneath. There is Grade A evidence supporting its use in superficial partial thickness burns in children.
Knowing that burns should be debrided by washing with saline and removing any loose skin or blisters before dressing application.

Indications for surgical management
Knowing that deep partial thickness and full thickness burns that will not heal within three weeks should be managed surgically to reduce scarring.
Knowing that burns greater than five percent T B S A should be debrided in theatre.
Knowing that hand and face burns should be addressed within fourteen days.
Knowing that early excision and grafting reduces hospital stay, infection risk, and scarring.

Surgical technique of tangential excision
Performing the procedure under general anaesthetic in a warm operating theatre.
Pre-operative preparation including ensuring blood products are available, dermatome and mesh equipment, sterile preparation, and infiltration with tumescent adrenaline solution at one in one million concentration.
Tangential excision using a Watson knife or Humby knife, serially shaving layers of the burn until a healthy bleeding wound bed is obtained.
Meticulous haemostasis using adrenaline-soaked swabs, diathermy, and topical thrombin if needed.
Sending wound bed samples for microbiology if infection is suspected.

Skin cover options including autograft and skin substitutes
Split thickness skin graft is the gold standard for definitive wound cover.
Knowing that sheet grafts should be used for cosmetically important areas such as the face and hands, and over joints.
Knowing that meshed grafts allow coverage of larger areas and can be expanded up to four to one ratio for very large burns.
Knowing the donor site options and that the thigh is the most common donor site.
Knowing that silver-based dressings such as Acticoat should be applied over meshed grafts.

Post-operative wound care and rehabilitation
Appropriate dressing of the graft site and donor site.
Immobilisation and elevation of the grafted area for five days to allow graft take.
First graft check at five days.
Early hand therapy and physiotherapy involvement, particularly for burns over joints.
Compression garments once healing is established to reduce hypertrophic scarring.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Burn depth reassessment at three days
Knowing that laser Doppler imaging can objectively assess burn depth and predict healing potential. It is most accurate between two and five days post-burn and is recommended by N I C E for indeterminate depth burns.
Knowing that burns that are predicted not to heal within twenty one days are likely to develop hypertrophic scarring and should be offered early surgery.

Dressing options for partial thickness burns
Knowing the advantages and disadvantages of different dressings: Biobrane reduces pain and healing time but must be applied to a clean wound and removed if infection develops. Silver dressings provide antimicrobial activity but require regular changes. Negative pressure wound therapy may be used for burns with exposed structures.
Knowing that glycerol-preserved allograft is an alternative biological dressing that can be used for larger burns when Biobrane is not suitable.
Knowing that Integra is a dermal regeneration template consisting of bovine collagen and a silicone outer layer, used for full thickness defects. The silicone layer is removed once the neodermis has formed and a thin autograft is applied on top.

Indications for surgical management
Knowing the concept of burn wound conversion: an initially superficial burn can progress to a deeper burn due to infection, poor perfusion, or inadequate first aid, and regular reassessment is essential.
Knowing that infected burn wounds may require more urgent surgical debridement.

Surgical technique of tangential excision
Knowing the surgical endpoints for massive burns: two to three hours operating, up to ten units transfused, temperature below thirty six degrees, or the lethal triad. Up to thirty percent T B S A per sitting.
Knowing the difference between tangential excision which shaves layers preserving viable dermis, and fascial excision which removes all tissue down to fascia and is used for very deep burns. Fascial excision heals with a poorer cosmetic outcome.
Knowing that the largest burn areas should be addressed first, prioritising the trunk to reduce the total open wound area.
Giving prophylactic intravenous antibiotics at induction.

Skin cover options including autograft and skin substitutes
Knowing the role of allograft as a temporary biological dressing for large burns when there is insufficient autograft donor skin available.
Knowing that cultured epithelial autograft can be used for very large burns where donor sites are limited, though it is fragile and expensive.
Knowing the role of dermal substitutes such as Integra and Matriderm in providing a scaffold for dermal regeneration before thin autograft application, which may improve the quality of the healed skin.
Knowing that negative pressure wound therapy can be used to improve graft take in difficult areas.

Post-operative wound care and rehabilitation
Knowing the risk of hypertrophic scarring and the role of pressure garments, silicone sheeting, and steroid injections in scar management.
Knowing that burn scar contractures over joints may require secondary procedures including Z-plasties, skin grafting, or flap reconstruction.
Knowing the importance of long-term follow up including scar management, psychological support, and functional rehabilitation.
Knowing the role of the multidisciplinary burns team including surgeons, nurses, physiotherapists, occupational therapists, dietitians, and psychologists.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
