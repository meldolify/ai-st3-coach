SCENARIO TITLE

Fluid Resuscitation in Burns

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 45 year old man weighing eighty kilograms who has sustained a thirty five percent total body surface area flame burn to his torso and both lower limbs in an industrial accident. He arrived at your burns unit two hours after the injury. He has been intubated for airway protection and has two large bore cannulae in situ. His initial fluid boluses have been given by the paramedics but he remains tachycardic with a heart rate of one hundred and twenty and his urine output over the last hour was fifteen millilitres. You have been asked to take over his fluid management.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment of fluid status
Assessing the patient's haemodynamic status including heart rate, blood pressure, capillary refill, and peripheral perfusion.
Confirming the T B S A and the patient's weight as these are the two variables in the resuscitation formula.
Confirming the time of the burn injury, not the time of arrival, as the formula calculates from the time of injury.
Checking the urine output which is the most reliable clinical indicator of adequate resuscitation.
Reviewing the fluids already given by the paramedics.

Fluid resuscitation formula and calculation
Knowing the modified Parkland formula: three to four millilitres multiplied by body weight in kilograms multiplied by percentage T B S A.
For this patient: three to four millilitres times eighty kilograms times thirty five equals eight thousand four hundred to eleven thousand two hundred millilitres in the first twenty four hours.
The first half is given in the first eight hours from the time of injury and the second half over the following sixteen hours.
Knowing that the fluid used is Hartmann solution, which is a crystalloid.
Knowing that the formula is a guide and must be titrated to clinical response.

Monitoring and titration of fluids
The primary target is urine output of greater than nought point five millilitres per kilogram per hour in adults, which for this patient is at least forty millilitres per hour.
Knowing that the current urine output of fifteen millilitres per hour is inadequate and the fluid rate should be increased.
Monitoring heart rate, blood pressure, lactate, and base excess as additional markers of perfusion.
Regular blood gas analysis to monitor for metabolic acidosis.

Urine output targets
Knowing the target urine output: greater than nought point five millilitres per kilogram per hour in adults and greater than one millilitre per kilogram per hour in children.
Knowing that in electrical burns the target is higher at one to one point five millilitres per kilogram per hour due to the risk of myoglobinuria.
Knowing that dark or cola-coloured urine suggests myoglobinuria and requires more aggressive fluid resuscitation.

Nutritional support
Knowing that early enteral nutrition is important in major burns.
Inserting a nasogastric tube for enteral feeding and gastric decompression in burns greater than fifteen percent T B S A in adults or ten percent in children.
Knowing the Curreri formula for calorie requirements: twenty five times body weight in kilograms plus forty times percentage T B S A.

Management of inadequate response to fluids
Knowing that if the urine output remains low despite calculated fluid volumes, the fluid rate should be increased in incremental boluses.
Knowing that persistent under-resuscitation leads to organ failure, while over-resuscitation leads to oedema, abdominal compartment syndrome, and pulmonary oedema.
Knowing that an arterial line and central venous pressure monitoring may be needed for closer haemodynamic assessment.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment of fluid status
Knowing that burns resuscitation is required for burns greater than fifteen percent T B S A in adults and greater than ten percent T B S A in children.
Knowing that the patient's pre-burn weight should be used, not the weight after fluid resuscitation which will be artificially elevated.
Knowing that children require maintenance fluids in addition to the resuscitation formula, using five percent dextrose in nought point four five percent saline with potassium supplementation.

Fluid resuscitation formula and calculation
Knowing the original Parkland formula used four millilitres per kilogram per T B S A but the modified formula uses three millilitres per kilogram per T B S A, and E M S B guidance now recommends starting at three millilitres.
Knowing that the formula gives the total volume for the first twenty four hours from the time of injury: if the patient arrives two hours after the injury, the first half must be given in the remaining six hours, not eight.
Knowing that colloid such as albumin may be considered after the first twenty four hours to help maintain intravascular volume and reduce total fluid requirements.

Monitoring and titration of fluids
Knowing the complications of under-resuscitation: hypovolaemic shock, acute kidney injury, multi-organ failure, and burn wound conversion from partial to full thickness due to reduced perfusion.
Knowing the complications of over-resuscitation, sometimes called fluid creep: pulmonary oedema, abdominal compartment syndrome, limb compartment syndrome, and cerebral oedema.
Knowing that abdominal compartment syndrome from over-resuscitation can be measured by intravesical pressure monitoring via the urinary catheter.

Urine output targets
Knowing that polyuria from mannitol or glycosuria in diabetic patients can give a falsely reassuring urine output.
Knowing that in myoglobinuria, the urine should be alkalinised with sodium bicarbonate to prevent myoglobin precipitation in the renal tubules.

Nutritional support
Knowing that the hypermetabolic response to burns increases calorie requirements by up to twice the normal rate.
Knowing that albumin levels should be monitored and supplemented if low.
Knowing that micronutrient supplementation including vitamin C, vitamin A, zinc, and selenium is important for wound healing.
Knowing that proton pump inhibitors or H two blockers should be given for stress ulcer prophylaxis, known as Curling ulcers in burns patients.

Management of inadequate response to fluids
Knowing that if urine output remains poor despite adequate calculated volumes, other causes should be considered including myocardial dysfunction, abdominal compartment syndrome, and unrecognised bleeding.
Knowing that vasopressors should be avoided in burns resuscitation if possible as they further compromise burn wound perfusion.
Knowing the role of transoesophageal echocardiography or other advanced haemodynamic monitoring in refractory shock.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
