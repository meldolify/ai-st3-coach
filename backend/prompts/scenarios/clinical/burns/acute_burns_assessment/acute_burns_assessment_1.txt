SCENARIO TITLE

Acute Burns Assessment

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 35 year old man who has been brought to the emergency department by ambulance following a house fire. He was trapped in a room for several minutes before being rescued. He has extensive burns to his torso, both upper limbs, and his face. He is conscious but in significant pain. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S and E M S B primary survey
Following A T L S and E M S B principles to assess and resuscitate the patient.
Airway assessment with cervical spine control, looking specifically for signs of inhalation injury including singed nasal hair, soot around the mouth, hoarse voice, oral cavity burns, and facial swelling. Early anaesthetic involvement for intubation if there is any concern about airway compromise, as swelling may make delayed intubation impossible.
Breathing assessment including respiratory rate, oxygen saturations, chest expansion, and auscultation. Looking for circumferential chest burns that may require escharotomy. Giving high flow oxygen.
Circulation assessment including heart rate, blood pressure, and capillary refill. Gaining intravenous access through unburnt skin or intraosseous access if necessary. Inserting a urinary catheter to monitor urine output. Sending bloods including full blood count, urea and electrolytes, liver function tests, clotting, cross-match, carboxyhaemoglobin, and an A B G.
Disability assessment including G C S, pupils, and blood glucose. A low G C S may indicate carbon monoxide or cyanide poisoning.
Exposure: undressing the patient and preventing hypothermia using a bear hugger. Estimating the T B S A.

Burn size estimation using Lund and Browder
Using the Lund and Browder chart to estimate the total body surface area burned, which is more accurate than the rule of nines.
Knowing the rule of nines as a rapid initial estimate: head nine percent, each upper limb nine percent, anterior trunk eighteen percent, posterior trunk eighteen percent, each lower limb eighteen percent, and perineum one percent.
Knowing the patient's palm including fingers represents approximately one percent T B S A for smaller or scattered burns.

Burn depth assessment
Assessing depth by examining colour, capillary refill, sensation, and the presence of blisters.
Superficial burns are red, painful, and blanch. Superficial partial thickness burns have blisters and are painful with brisk capillary refill. Deep partial thickness burns are mottled, have reduced sensation, and sluggish capillary refill. Full thickness burns are white or charred, insensate, and non-blanching.

Fluid resuscitation
Calculating fluid requirements using the modified Parkland formula: three to four millilitres per kilogram per percentage T B S A. The first half is given in the first eight hours from the time of injury and the second half over the following sixteen hours, using Hartmann solution.
Titrating fluids to achieve a urine output of greater than nought point five millilitres per kilogram per hour in adults.
Knowing that resuscitation is required for burns greater than fifteen percent T B S A in adults and greater than ten percent in children.

Referral criteria for burns centre transfer
Knowing the criteria for referral to a burns centre including burns greater than ten percent T B S A in adults, any burn in a child, burns to face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, or major joints, circumferential burns, full thickness burns, inhalation injury, electrical or chemical burns, and burns in patients with significant comorbidities.
Knowing that life-saving interventions such as escharotomy and airway management should be performed before transfer.

Surgical strategy
Knowing the principles of surgical management: early excision and skin cover reduces morbidity and mortality.
Burns greater than five percent T B S A should be debrided in theatre.

Post-operative and multidisciplinary care
Recognising the need for a multidisciplinary approach including I T U, nursing, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, dietetics, and psychology.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S and E M S B primary survey
Knowing the A M P L E history: allergies, medications, past medical history, last meal, and events surrounding the injury.
Knowing the specific questions for flame burns: was there an explosion, was the patient trapped, any loss of consciousness, and what first aid was given.
Knowing that carbon monoxide poisoning is treated with one hundred percent oxygen. Carboxyhaemoglobin levels should be measured on A B G.
Knowing that cyanide poisoning from burning synthetic materials may present with a low G C S and metabolic acidosis, and is treated with Cyanokit which is five grams of hydroxocobalamin given intravenously over fifteen minutes.
Arranging clinical photography of the burns.
Performing initial bedside debridement by washing with saline and applying clingfilm or Jelonet dressings to decrease fluid loss and pain.
Inserting a nasogastric tube for burns greater than fifteen percent T B S A in adults or ten percent in children to decompress the stomach and provide early enteral nutrition.
Knowing that analgesia should be intravenous morphine titrated in two to ten milligram doses.
Tetanus prophylaxis.

Burn size estimation using Lund and Browder
Knowing that laser Doppler imaging can be used to objectively assess burn depth, particularly for indeterminate depth burns, and is most accurate when performed between two and five days post-burn.
Knowing that thermography and vital dyes are alternative methods of assessing burn depth.

Burn depth assessment
Knowing the difference between superficial, superficial partial thickness, deep partial thickness, and full thickness burns and the expected healing times: superficial heals within seven days, superficial partial thickness within fourteen days, deep partial thickness may take three or more weeks and is likely to scar, and full thickness will not heal without surgery.

Fluid resuscitation
Knowing that the Parkland formula is a guide and the fluid rate should be titrated to clinical response, particularly urine output.
Knowing the nutritional requirements calculated by the Curreri formula: twenty five times body weight in kilograms plus forty times percentage T B S A burned.
Knowing that enteral feeding should be started early and that albumin levels and nutritional markers should be monitored.

Referral criteria for burns centre transfer
Knowing that the secondary survey should be completed before transfer.
Knowing that adequate documentation and clinical photography should accompany the patient.

Surgical strategy
Knowing the surgical approach: tangential excision in a warm operating theatre, using adrenaline-infiltrated tumescent solution to reduce bleeding.
Knowing the surgical endpoints: two to three hours operating time, up to ten units of blood transfused, temperature dropping below thirty six degrees, or the lethal triad of hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy. Up to thirty percent T B S A can be excised per sitting.
Knowing that the largest area of burns should be addressed first, and hand and face burns should be addressed last but by fourteen days.
Knowing that sheet grafts should be used for the face, hands, and over joints, while meshed grafts up to four to one ratio may be used for larger areas.
Knowing the skin cover options: autograft using split thickness skin graft, allograft for temporary cover if insufficient donor sites, and skin substitutes such as Biobrane for superficial partial thickness burns and Integra for full thickness defects.
Knowing that prophylactic intravenous antibiotics should be given at induction but routine prophylactic antibiotics are not recommended for non-surgical management.

Post-operative and multidisciplinary care
Knowing the importance of splinting burns over joints in the position of function to prevent contracture.
Knowing the role of early range of motion exercises and compression garments.
Knowing the psychological impact of major burns and the importance of early psychological support.
Knowing that I T U care includes continued fluid management, ventilatory support, nutritional optimisation, and regular surgical planning for further debridement and skin cover.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
