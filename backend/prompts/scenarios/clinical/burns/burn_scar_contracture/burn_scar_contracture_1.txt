SCENARIO TITLE

Burn Scar Contracture

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 25 year old woman who sustained a deep partial thickness flame burn to the anterior aspect of her neck eighteen months ago. She was treated with split thickness skin grafting at the time. She has now developed a tight band of scar tissue across the front of her neck which is limiting her ability to extend her neck fully and is pulling her chin down towards her chest. She has been referred to your clinic for assessment. You have been asked to assess and formulate a management plan.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and impact assessment
Taking a focused history including the original burn injury, treatment received, and healing time.
Assessing the functional impact of the contracture on neck extension, daily activities, driving, and occupation.
Assessing the cosmetic impact and the psychological effect on the patient.
Asking about any previous scar management treatments received.
Asking about the patient's ideas, concerns, and expectations.

Examination and classification of the scar
Examining the scar noting its location, size, colour, thickness, pliability, and the degree of contracture.
Measuring the range of motion of the affected joint, in this case neck extension.
Differentiating between hypertrophic scarring and contracture. Hypertrophic scars are raised, red, and remain within the boundaries of the original wound. Contracture is the functional tightening that limits movement.
Assessing the quality of the surrounding skin for potential donor tissue.
Considering the use of the Vancouver Scar Scale to objectively document the scar characteristics: vascularity, pigmentation, pliability, and height.

Conservative scar management
Knowing the conservative options for burn scar management: pressure garments worn at twenty five millimetres of mercury for up to two years, silicone gel or silicone sheeting applied for at least three months, massage, and moisturising.
Knowing the role of steroid injections for hypertrophic scars, which can be repeated every six to eight weeks with risks including dermal thinning, atrophy, telangiectasia, and pigmentary change.
Knowing that conservative measures should be tried first and that scars continue to mature for up to two years.

Indications for surgical release
Knowing that surgery is indicated when there is significant functional limitation that has not responded to conservative measures and the scar has matured.
Knowing that surgery should ideally be delayed until scar maturation at twelve to eighteen months unless there is severe functional impairment.

Surgical techniques for contracture release
Knowing the options for releasing a linear or band contracture: Z-plasty, multiple Z-plasties, or five-flap plasty which are local tissue rearrangement techniques that lengthen the scar and break up the linear contracture.
Knowing that for broader sheet contractures where there is insufficient surrounding tissue for local flaps, excision of the scar and resurfacing with a graft or flap is required.

Skin cover and reconstruction
Knowing the skin cover options after contracture release: full thickness skin graft provides better quality skin with less secondary contracture than split thickness graft and is preferred for neck contractures. Split thickness skin graft for larger defects. Local flaps or tissue expansion for optimal cosmetic results.
Knowing that tissue expansion of adjacent unburned skin can provide like-for-like tissue of matching colour and texture for reconstruction.

Post-operative rehabilitation and scar management
Splinting the released area in the extended position post-operatively.
Early hand therapy and physiotherapy.
Pressure garments and silicone therapy on the new scar.
Long-term follow up for scar management and potential revision procedures.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and impact assessment
Knowing the difference between keloid and hypertrophic scars: hypertrophic scars remain within the boundaries of the wound and regress with time, whereas keloids extend beyond the wound margins, progress with time, have disorganised collagen, and are more common in patients of African or Asian descent.
Knowing that keloids have twenty times higher collagen synthesis and fourteen times higher collagenase synthesis compared to normal skin.

Examination and classification of the scar
Knowing the modified Vancouver Scar Scale and its components for objective scar assessment.
Knowing the Patient and Observer Scar Assessment Scale as an alternative validated scar assessment tool.
Assessing whether any of the contracture is capsular versus scar related by passively testing the range under relaxation.

Conservative scar management
Knowing the role of laser therapy in scar management: pulsed dye laser for vascular scars, fractional C O two laser for scar resurfacing, and Y A G laser for residuum.
Knowing that radiotherapy can be used as an adjuvant for keloid scars to prevent recurrence after excision by inhibiting fibroblast proliferation.
Knowing that excision alone for keloid scars has a high recurrence rate and should be combined with adjuvant therapy such as steroid injection, radiotherapy, or pressure therapy.

Indications for surgical release
Knowing that in children, contractures across joints should be released earlier to prevent skeletal growth deformity.
Knowing that web space contractures, axillary contractures, and contractures across the elbow and knee are also common sites requiring surgical release.

Surgical techniques for contracture release
Knowing the specific techniques: a single Z-plasty with sixty degree angles provides a seventy five percent increase in length along the line of the original scar. Multiple Z-plasties distribute the lengthening over a longer scar. A five-flap plasty or jumping man plasty is useful for web space contractures.
Knowing the Y to V advancement and V to Y advancement techniques for small contractures.
Knowing that for severe neck contractures, a supraclavicular artery island flap or a free flap may be needed.

Skin cover and reconstruction
Knowing the advantages of tissue expansion: provides tissue of matching colour, texture, and thickness, maintains sensation, and has a low donor site morbidity. Disadvantages include the need for multiple operations, risk of expander infection or extrusion, and the period of disfigurement during expansion.
Knowing that Integra dermal regeneration template followed by thin split thickness skin graft may be used for large contracture releases where local tissue is insufficient and provides a better cosmetic result than split thickness skin graft alone.
Knowing the concept of serial excision for smaller scars where the surrounding skin has sufficient laxity.

Post-operative rehabilitation and scar management
Knowing that prolonged splinting in the position of maximum extension may be needed for six or more months to prevent recurrence of the contracture.
Knowing that occupational therapy assessment for adaptive aids and return to work planning is important.
Knowing that psychological support is an important component of burn scar rehabilitation.
Knowing that patients should be counselled that scar revision surgery improves but does not eliminate the scar.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
