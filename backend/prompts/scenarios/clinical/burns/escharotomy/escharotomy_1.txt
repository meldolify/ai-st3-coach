SCENARIO TITLE

Escharotomy

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 30 year old man who has been admitted to your burns unit following a flame burn affecting approximately thirty percent total body surface area including circumferential full thickness burns to both upper limbs and the anterior chest. He is intubated and ventilated. The nurses have contacted you because his oxygen saturations are dropping and his hands are becoming mottled and cool. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of the need for escharotomy
Recognising that circumferential full thickness burns cause a rigid eschar that cannot expand, leading to a tourniquet-like effect.
In the chest, circumferential eschar restricts chest wall expansion causing reduced ventilation, rising airway pressures, and falling oxygen saturations.
In the limbs, circumferential eschar causes vascular compromise with loss of distal perfusion, cool extremities, absent pulses, and ultimately compartment syndrome.
Knowing that escharotomy is an emergency procedure that should be performed immediately when indicated.

Indications for escharotomy
Chest: circumferential or near-circumferential full thickness chest burns with deteriorating respiratory function, increasing ventilatory pressures, or decreasing chest wall compliance.
Limb: circumferential or near-circumferential full thickness limb burns with signs of vascular compromise including absent or diminishing pulses, progressive pain, paraesthesia, mottled or cool extremities, and decreased capillary refill.
Knowing that escharotomy can be performed at the bedside and does not require a trip to theatre if the clinical situation is urgent.

Chest escharotomy technique
Incisions are made along the bilateral anterior axillary lines from the clavicle to the costal margin.
A transverse incision may be added across the upper abdomen below the costal margin connecting the two vertical incisions if the burn extends to the abdominal wall.
Incisions are made using monopolar diathermy or a scalpel.
The incision should extend from unburnt skin to unburnt skin.
The depth of the incision is through the full thickness eschar down to the subcutaneous fat. The incision should release the constricting band but not extend deeper than the subcutaneous tissue.
Full thickness eschar is insensate so the procedure can be performed without general anaesthetic if necessary, though sedation and analgesia should be given.

Limb escharotomy technique
Incisions are made along the mid-axial lines of the affected limb, on the medial and lateral aspects.
The incision extends from unburnt skin to unburnt skin and across joints.
At the elbow, the incision should avoid the ulnar nerve at the medial epicondyle.
At the knee, the incision should avoid the common peroneal nerve posterior to the fibular head.
The incision depth is through the eschar into the subcutaneous fat.

Digital escharotomy
Performed along the mid-axial lines of the digits, on the ulnar side of the index, middle, and ring fingers and the radial side of the thumb and little finger to avoid damage to the pinch surfaces.
Knowing that digital escharotomies are rarely needed and should be performed with caution.

Post-procedure monitoring
Confirming improved chest wall expansion and oxygen saturations after chest escharotomy.
Confirming return of distal pulses, warmth, and capillary refill after limb escharotomy.
Applying haemostasis with diathermy and dressing the escharotomy wounds.
Continuing to monitor for recurrence as ongoing swelling may necessitate re-assessment.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of the need for escharotomy
Knowing the difference between escharotomy and fasciotomy: escharotomy releases the constricting burnt skin and is performed through the eschar into the subcutaneous fat, whereas fasciotomy releases the fascial compartments and is a deeper procedure performed for compartment syndrome, particularly in electrical burns.
Knowing that escharotomy should be performed before transfer to a burns centre if transfer will be delayed and the patient is compromised.

Indications for escharotomy
Knowing that in ventilated patients, increasing peak inspiratory pressures and decreasing tidal volumes are objective signs of chest wall constriction.
Knowing that pulse oximetry and Doppler assessment of distal pulses can be used to monitor limb perfusion.
Knowing that if circumferential limb burns are present but perfusion is intact, the limb should be elevated in a Bradford sling with regular monitoring rather than immediate escharotomy.

Chest escharotomy technique
Knowing that the incisions should not cross the clavicle or extend into the inguinal area.
Knowing that bilateral incisions are needed for circumferential chest burns.
Knowing that the procedure provides immediate improvement in ventilation which can be confirmed by the anaesthetist noting reduced airway pressures and improved tidal volumes.

Limb escharotomy technique
Knowing that if a single mid-axial incision on each side is insufficient, additional incisions along the dorsal mid-line of the forearm may be needed.
Knowing that for forearm escharotomies, the incision should extend across the wrist to decompress the carpal tunnel if necessary.
Knowing that haemostasis from escharotomy wounds can be significant and should be controlled with diathermy.

Digital escharotomy
Knowing that the incisions should be on the non-pinch surfaces to preserve hand function.
Knowing that in small children, needle escharotomy using a large-bore needle may be an alternative to incisional escharotomy.

Post-procedure monitoring
Knowing that escharotomy wounds are left open and are managed as part of the overall burns wound management plan.
Knowing that the escharotomy sites will eventually require excision and skin grafting as part of the definitive burn surgery.
Knowing that failure to improve after escharotomy may indicate the need for a deeper fasciotomy, particularly in electrical burns or if there has been prolonged ischaemia.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
