SCENARIO TITLE

Chemical Burns

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 40 year old man who works as a glass etcher and has presented to the emergency department with a burn to his right hand. He was using hydrofluoric acid at work and the substance splashed onto his hand approximately one hour ago. His hand is very painful and swollen. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of the type of chemical burn
Knowing that hydrofluoric acid is one of the most dangerous chemical burn agents due to its systemic toxicity.
Knowing the difference between acid and alkali burns: acids cause coagulation necrosis with eschar formation which limits the depth of injury, whereas alkalis cause saponification of fat and penetrate deeper, leading to progressive tissue destruction. Alkali burns are generally worse than acid burns.

A T L S and E M S B assessment
Following A T L S and E M S B principles.
Ensuring the patient has adequate analgesia.
Airway assessment and ensuring the patient is on high flow oxygen.
Ensuring the patient is cardiovascularly stable. As hydrofluoric acid can cause cardiac arrhythmias and bradycardia, the patient must be placed on continuous E C G monitoring.
Gaining intravenous access and sending bloods including full blood count, urea and electrolytes with specific attention to calcium, magnesium, and potassium, renal function, and cross-match.
Examining the eyes to rule out any chemical splash injury.

Systemic complications of chemical burns
Knowing that hydrofluoric acid causes systemic hypocalcaemia by the fluoride ion binding to calcium. This can lead to life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias including bradycardia and ventricular fibrillation.
Knowing that hyperkalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, and acute kidney injury with bony decalcification can also occur.
Knowing that neurological complications include tetany and seizures from hypocalcaemia.

First aid and decontamination
Removing all contaminated clothing.
Continuous irrigation of the burn with water. For acid burns, irrigation should continue for at least two hours or until the skin p H normalises. For alkali burns, irrigation should continue for longer as alkalis penetrate deeper.
Performing the litmus paper p H test to assess the skin p H. Normal skin p H is four point five to five point five. Irrigation should continue until the p H normalises.
Clipping the patient's nails to remove any trapped chemical.
Knowing that diphoterine can be used as a universal decontamination agent for chemical burns.

Specific management of hydrofluoric acid burns
Applying topical calcium gluconate gel to the affected area to neutralise the fluoride ions locally.
If there is evidence of systemic hypocalcaemia, starting intravenous calcium gluconate and monitoring serum calcium levels closely with repeated blood tests.
Continuous cardiac monitoring with E C G.
Cardiology involvement if any arrhythmias are detected.
Electrolyte correction.

Cardiac monitoring and electrolyte management
Regular monitoring of serum calcium, potassium, and magnesium.
Knowing the E C G changes associated with hypocalcaemia: prolonged Q T interval.
Correcting hypocalcaemia urgently with intravenous calcium gluconate.

Surgical management
Knowing that the patient should be referred to a burns unit.
If the burn is full thickness, the patient will require surgical debridement in theatre using tangential excision down to healthy tissue.
Reconstruction with split thickness skin graft if there are no exposed structures.
Clinical photography.
Tetanus prophylaxis.
Keeping the patient warm and keeping them nil by mouth for theatre.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of the type of chemical burn
Knowing the specific agents associated with different occupations: hydrofluoric acid in glass etching, semiconductor manufacturing, and pottery glazing. Cement is an alkali burn. Phenols cause C N S and cardiopulmonary collapse.
Knowing that certain chemicals should never be irrigated with water as they ignite: sodium phosphate and lithium.
Knowing that cement burns generate an exothermic reaction when mixed with water, and sweat alone is sufficient to initiate the reaction.

A T L S and E M S B assessment
Knowing the specific systemic complications by chemical agent: phenols cause C N S and cardiopulmonary collapse, dichromate causes renal failure, and hydrofluoric acid causes hypocalcaemia and cardiac arrhythmias.
Asking about the exact chemical agent, duration of exposure, protective equipment worn, and any first aid measures taken.
Contacting the national toxicology line if the agent is unknown, and obtaining information from the employer who should have a C O S H H data sheet.

Systemic complications of chemical burns
Knowing that hydrofluoric acid effects can continue for days after the initial exposure.
Knowing that myoglobinuria from rhabdomyolysis can occur in severe cases and urine output should be monitored.

First aid and decontamination
Knowing that for cement burns, the p H should be tested with litmus paper and irrigation continued until the p H normalises to four point five to five point five. A p H of seven point two indicates the burn has not yet neutralised and further irrigation is needed.
Knowing that eye involvement from alkali chemical burns can cause irreversible corneal damage within three minutes and requires immediate and copious irrigation with saline and urgent ophthalmology review.

Specific management of hydrofluoric acid burns
Knowing that topical calcium gluconate gel should be reapplied every thirty minutes until pain subsides.
Knowing that if topical treatment is insufficient, subcutaneous injection of ten percent calcium gluconate around the burn site may be considered.
Knowing that intra-arterial calcium gluconate infusion has been described for severe cases affecting the digits.
Knowing the indications for urgent surgical debridement: full thickness burns, extensive tissue necrosis, or failure to respond to topical and systemic calcium treatment.

Cardiac monitoring and electrolyte management
Knowing that E C G monitoring should continue for at least twenty four hours after the last calcium replacement.
Knowing that hyperkalaemia from tissue destruction may also contribute to cardiac arrhythmias and requires separate management.

Surgical management
Knowing that circumferential chemical burns to the limbs carry a risk of compartment syndrome and may require fasciotomy.
Knowing that the depth of chemical burns may be difficult to assess initially and repeated assessment is needed as the injury may progress.
Knowing that alkali burns in particular may appear initially benign but can progress to full thickness injury over subsequent days.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
