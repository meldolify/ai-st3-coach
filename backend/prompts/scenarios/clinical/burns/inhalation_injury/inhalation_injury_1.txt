SCENARIO TITLE

Inhalation Injury

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 40 year old man who has been rescued from a house fire where he was trapped in a smoke-filled room for approximately ten minutes. He has facial burns with singed eyebrows and nasal hair, and soot deposits around his mouth and nostrils. He is conscious but his voice is becoming hoarse. He has burns to approximately twenty percent of his total body surface area. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of inhalation injury
Recognising the clinical signs that suggest inhalation injury: singed nasal hair, singed eyebrows or eyelashes, soot around the mouth and nostrils, carbonaceous sputum, hoarse voice, facial burns, and history of entrapment in a confined space with smoke.
Knowing that inhalation injury significantly increases the mortality of burns.
Knowing that the hoarse voice indicates laryngeal oedema which will progress and may lead to complete airway obstruction.

Clinical signs and types of inhalation injury
Knowing the three types of inhalation injury: upper airway thermal injury causing oedema and obstruction, lower airway chemical injury from inhaled smoke particles causing bronchospasm and mucosal damage, and systemic toxicity from carbon monoxide and cyanide.
Knowing that heat is rapidly dissipated in the upper airway so thermal injury is predominantly supraglottic. The exception is steam inhalation which can cause lower airway thermal injury.

Airway management and early intubation
Knowing that early intubation is essential when inhalation injury is suspected because progressive oedema may make delayed intubation extremely difficult or impossible.
Requesting urgent anaesthetic involvement for intubation.
Giving high flow one hundred percent oxygen via a non-rebreather mask while awaiting intubation.
Knowing that the decision to intubate should be made early and is based on clinical assessment rather than waiting for deterioration.

Carbon monoxide poisoning
Knowing that carbon monoxide binds to haemoglobin with two hundred and forty times greater affinity than oxygen, forming carboxyhaemoglobin which reduces oxygen delivery to tissues.
Knowing the clinical features: headache, confusion, reduced G C S, cherry-red skin colouration which is a late sign, and cardiac arrhythmias.
Knowing that treatment is high flow one hundred percent oxygen which reduces the half-life of carboxyhaemoglobin from four hours on room air to approximately sixty minutes.
Measuring carboxyhaemoglobin levels on A B G. Knowing that levels may have already fallen by the time of hospital arrival due to oxygen therapy en route.

Cyanide poisoning
Knowing that cyanide is produced by combustion of synthetic materials such as plastics, foam, and nylon.
Knowing that cyanide inhibits cytochrome oxidase causing cellular hypoxia and lactic acidosis despite adequate oxygen saturations.
Suspecting cyanide poisoning in a patient with unexplained metabolic acidosis, reduced G C S, or cardiovascular collapse following smoke inhalation.
Knowing the treatment: Cyanokit which is hydroxocobalamin five grams given intravenously over fifteen minutes.

Ongoing respiratory management
Knowing that ongoing management of inhalation injury includes ventilatory support, humidified oxygen, regular bronchial suctioning, and bronchoscopy if needed to assess lower airway injury.
Knowing that I T U care is required for ventilated patients with inhalation injury.

Burns management alongside inhalation injury
Knowing that the burns should be managed concurrently following E M S B principles including fluid resuscitation and wound management.
Knowing that inhalation injury increases fluid requirements beyond the calculated Parkland formula.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of inhalation injury
Knowing that inhalation injury is an independent criterion for referral to a burns centre regardless of the T B S A of the cutaneous burn.
Knowing that up to thirty percent of patients with major burns have an associated inhalation injury.

Clinical signs and types of inhalation injury
Knowing that fibreoptic bronchoscopy can be used to directly visualise the lower airway and assess the severity of inhalation injury, looking for carbonaceous deposits, mucosal oedema, erythema, and ulceration.
Knowing that xenon ventilation-perfusion scanning is an alternative method of assessing inhalation injury.
Knowing that the A B S I score, abbreviated burn severity index, incorporates inhalation injury as a prognostic factor.

Airway management and early intubation
Knowing the specific indications for immediate intubation: stridor, respiratory distress, inability to swallow secretions, circumferential neck burns with rapidly developing oedema, and deep facial burns.
Knowing that an experienced anaesthetist should perform the intubation as it may be technically difficult due to oedema, and a surgical airway may be needed as a backup.
Knowing that once intubated, the endotracheal tube should be secured with ties rather than tape as tape will not adhere to burnt or oedematous skin.

Carbon monoxide poisoning
Knowing the carboxyhaemoglobin levels and their significance: levels above ten percent are significant, above twenty five percent cause confusion and cardiac symptoms, and above fifty percent are often fatal.
Knowing that pulse oximetry is unreliable in carbon monoxide poisoning as the pulse oximeter cannot distinguish between oxyhaemoglobin and carboxyhaemoglobin and may give falsely normal readings.
Knowing that hyperbaric oxygen therapy has been described for severe carbon monoxide poisoning but is not widely available and its role remains controversial.

Cyanide poisoning
Knowing that blood cyanide levels can be measured but results are not available rapidly enough to guide acute management, so treatment is empirical based on clinical suspicion.
Knowing the alternative antidote: sodium thiosulphate which converts cyanide to thiocyanate for renal excretion, though it is slower acting than hydroxocobalamin.
Knowing that the combination of high lactate, metabolic acidosis, and reduced G C S in a fire victim should prompt empirical treatment for cyanide poisoning.

Ongoing respiratory management
Knowing that regular bronchial lavage with heparinised saline and N-acetylcysteine may be beneficial in managing lower airway inhalation injury.
Knowing the risk of A R D S following inhalation injury and the importance of lung-protective ventilation strategies.
Knowing that tracheostomy may be needed for prolonged ventilatory support.
Knowing that pneumonia is a common complication of inhalation injury and burns.

Burns management alongside inhalation injury
Knowing that patients with inhalation injury have increased metabolic demands and require early and aggressive nutritional support.
Knowing that the airway must be secured before transfer to a burns centre.
Knowing that escharotomy of circumferential chest burns should be performed before transfer if the patient has respiratory compromise.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
