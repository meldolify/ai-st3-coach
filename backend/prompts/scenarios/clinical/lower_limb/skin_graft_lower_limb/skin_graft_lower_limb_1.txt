SCENARIO TITLE

Pretibial Laceration and Skin Grafting

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

You are called to the emergency department to see a fifty-five year old lady who has sustained a pretibial laceration after falling against a low table. There is a large flap of skin over the anterior shin measuring approximately six by four centimetres. The flap is dusky and thin, and there is exposed subcutaneous tissue at the base. She takes warfarin for atrial fibrillation and has a history of peripheral oedema.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification (Lo modification of Duncan):
Type one: laceration or skin flap that can be approximated
Type two: skin flap with limited closure, cannot fully approximate without tension
Type three: skin flap with full thickness skin loss, cannot be closed
Type four: full thickness skin loss exposing deep structures
Type five: degloving injury with skin loss and damage to deep tissues

Why pretibial lacerations are challenging:
Thin skin over the tibia with poor blood supply
Elderly patients with atrophic skin, often on anticoagulants or steroids
Limited subcutaneous tissue — poor wound healing environment
Dependent position of the leg promotes oedema
High complication rate with suturing due to tissue tension

Initial management:
Irrigate wound thoroughly
Assess flap viability — colour, bleeding, thickness
Trim any clearly non-viable tissue but be conservative
Steri-strips are preferred over suturing for pretibial lacerations — suturing creates tension and can further compromise the fragile skin flap
Adhesive strips allow gentle approximation without additional tissue damage
Non-adherent dressing, light compression bandage
Elevate the leg

Anticoagulation management:
Check I N R — reverse if actively bleeding and I N R supratherapeutic
Liaise with cardiology or anticoagulation clinic regarding warfarin management
Haemostasis with pressure and elevation first

Skin grafting:
Indicated for type three to five injuries where primary closure not possible
S S G (split-thickness skin graft) is the standard — can use the non-viable flap as a full-thickness graft (Wolfe graft) if tissue quality allows
Wound bed must be clean with granulation tissue — may need V A C or dressings first
Meshed S S G improves take on irregular surfaces and allows fluid drainage
Graft fixation with staples, glue, or tie-over dressing
Five days of immobilisation then first graft check

Postoperative care:
Immediate mobilisation is important in the elderly — do not keep patients on bed rest
Elevation when seated
Compression hosiery once graft healed to manage oedema
Comprehensive geriatric assessment — falls risk, bone health, medication review
Physiotherapy for mobility and falls prevention

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Discussion of using the non-viable skin flap defatted as a full-thickness graft (biological dressing)
Negative pressure wound therapy to improve graft take in the pretibial region
Role of dermal substitutes (Integra) for deep defects before grafting
Consideration of V T E prophylaxis in immobilised elderly patients
Discussion of the economic burden and prolonged healing times in pretibial lacerations
Fish skin grafts (Kerecis) as biological wound dressings for pretibial defects
Topical negative pressure over grafts to improve take rates in high-risk areas
