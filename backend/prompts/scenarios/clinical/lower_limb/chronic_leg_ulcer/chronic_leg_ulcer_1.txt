SCENARIO TITLE

Chronic Leg Ulcer

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

You are asked to see a seventy-two year old lady in clinic who has been referred by her G P with a non-healing ulcer on her left lower leg. The ulcer has been present for approximately eight months and has failed to respond to dressings in the community. She has a background of varicose veins and previous D V T in the same leg.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Differential diagnosis:
Venous ulcer is the commonest cause, typically in the gaiter area with irregular edges and lipodermatosclerosis
Arterial ulcers are painful, punched out, and found on pressure points distally
Neuropathic ulcers occur over pressure areas and are painless
Mixed aetiology is common and must be considered
Malignant transformation (Marjolin ulcer) should be suspected in any chronic non-healing ulcer

Ulcer assessment:
Site, size, depth, edge morphology, base, surrounding skin changes
Rolled edges suggest B C C, everted edges suggest S C C
Undermined edges suggest infection or vasculitis
Sloping edges suggest healing

Investigations:
A B P I is essential before compression — must be above 0.8 for full compression
Duplex ultrasound to assess venous incompetence
Bloods including albumin, H b A 1 c, inflammatory markers
Biopsy if ulcer present longer than six weeks without healing, or suspicious features
Swab only if clinical signs of infection, not routinely

Conservative management:
Four-layer compression bandaging for venous ulcers with A B P I above 0.8
Leg elevation and mobilisation
Wound bed preparation and appropriate dressings
Nutrition optimisation
T V N nurse involvement

Marjolin ulcer:
S C C arising in chronic wound or scar, typically after many years
More aggressive than de novo S C C with higher metastatic rate
Management requires biopsy confirmation, wide excision with at least one centimetre margin, and sentinel lymph node biopsy consideration
Amputation may be required for advanced disease

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Reduced A B P I of 0.5 to 0.8 allows modified compression with reduced pressure
Venous ulcer recurrence rates are high without addressing underlying venous insufficiency
Skin grafting can be used for large clean venous ulcers — S S G or pinch grafts
Pentoxifylline has some evidence for improving venous ulcer healing
Classification of ulcer edge morphology as a diagnostic tool
Discussion of venous surgery (ablation or stripping) to reduce recurrence

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
