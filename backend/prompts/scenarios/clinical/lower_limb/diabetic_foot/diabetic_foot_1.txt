SCENARIO TITLE

Diabetic Foot

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

You are asked to see a sixty-two year old gentleman in the diabetic foot clinic. He has a long history of type two diabetes with poor glycaemic control. He presents with a plantar ulcer under his right first metatarsal head that has been present for three months and is now worsening. On examination, you can probe to bone at the base of the ulcer.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment:
Full diabetic history including duration, control (H b A 1 c), complications (neuropathy, nephropathy, retinopathy)
Ulcer assessment: site, size, depth, probe to bone test
Probe to bone has a high positive predictive value for osteomyelitis
Neurological assessment: monofilament testing, vibration sense, reflexes
Vascular assessment: foot pulses, capillary refill, skin changes, hair loss

Vascular assessment:
A B P I may be falsely elevated in diabetics due to medial arterial calcification
Toe pressures or T B I (toe-brachial index) are more reliable in diabetics
A T B I below 0.7 suggests significant peripheral vascular disease
Duplex ultrasound or C T angiography if intervention likely needed

Investigations:
Bloods: H b A 1 c, white cell count, C R P, E S R, renal function, albumin
Plain X-ray of the foot for bony changes
M R I is the gold standard for diagnosing osteomyelitis
Bone biopsy for culture and sensitivity if osteomyelitis suspected
Vascular assessment as above

M D T approach:
Diabetologist for glycaemic optimisation
Vascular surgeon for assessment and possible revascularisation
Orthotist for offloading and therapeutic footwear
Podiatrist for nail and callus care
Tissue viability nurse for wound management
Microbiologist for antibiotic guidance

Initial management:
Offloading is critical — total contact cast or removable cast walker
Antibiotic therapy guided by culture results, prolonged course for osteomyelitis (six to twelve weeks)
Glycaemic control optimisation
Wound debridement of necrotic tissue
Vascular optimisation — revascularisation if critically ischaemic

Surgical management:
Debridement and drainage of any abscess
Ray amputation for localised toe or metatarsal osteomyelitis
Transmetatarsal amputation if more extensive forefoot involvement
Below-knee amputation as last resort
Revascularisation (angioplasty or bypass) should precede or accompany any amputation where possible

Charcot foot:
Neuroarthropathy caused by uncontrolled inflammation in a neuropathic foot
Acute phase: hot, swollen, red foot — can be confused with infection
Diagnosed clinically and with M R I
Treatment: total contact cast immobilisation until inflammation settles (may take months)
Chronic phase may result in rocker-bottom deformity requiring custom orthoses
Surgical reconstruction considered for unstable deformity at risk of ulceration

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Wagner classification of diabetic foot ulcers (grades zero to five)
University of Texas classification as an alternative staging system
Five-year mortality after major lower limb amputation in diabetics approaches fifty percent
Negative pressure wound therapy for large defects post-debridement
Free flap reconstruction is possible in selected patients with adequate vascular inflow
Hyperbaric oxygen therapy has limited evidence but is used in some centres
Discussion of Ipswich Touch Test as a bedside screening tool for neuropathy

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
