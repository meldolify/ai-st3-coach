SCENARIO TITLE

Pressure Sores

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

You are asked to review a seventy-eight year old gentleman on the spinal injuries ward. He is wheelchair-bound following a spinal cord injury two years ago and has developed a large sacral pressure ulcer. The nursing staff are concerned because they can see exposed bone at the base of the wound. His albumin is fourteen and he has been losing weight.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Classification:
N P U A P classification from Grade one to Grade four
Grade one is non-blanchable erythema of intact skin
Grade two involves partial thickness skin loss
Grade three is full thickness skin loss with visible subcutaneous fat
Grade four involves exposed bone, tendon, or muscle
This patient has a Grade four ulcer with exposed bone

Risk assessment:
Waterlow score assesses pressure ulcer risk — includes weight, continence, skin type, mobility, neurological deficit, nutrition, medication
Spinal cord injury patients are very high risk due to immobility, loss of sensation, and neurogenic bladder

Nutritional optimisation:
Albumin must be above twenty before considering surgical reconstruction
Dietician involvement is essential
Nutritional supplementation including protein, vitamin C, and zinc
May require nasogastric or parenteral feeding if oral intake inadequate

M D T approach:
Tissue viability nurse for wound assessment and dressings
Dietician for nutritional optimisation
Occupational therapist for seating assessment and pressure-relieving cushions
Physiotherapist for positioning
Microbiologist if infection suspected
Spinal injuries team for overall rehabilitation

Conservative management:
Pressure relief is fundamental — Kosiak principle: five minutes of pressure relief every two hours prevents ulceration
Appropriate pressure-relieving mattress and cushion
Regular repositioning schedule
Wound bed preparation with debridement of necrotic tissue
V A C therapy can be useful as a bridge to surgery
Treat infection if present — tissue biopsy more reliable than swabs

Surgical options:
Sacral ulcers: gluteal rotation flap, gluteal V-Y advancement flap
Ischial ulcers: hamstring V-Y advancement flap, gracilis flap, posterior thigh flap
Trochanteric ulcers: T F L flap (V-Y advancement or rotation)
Principles: use muscle or musculocutaneous flaps to fill dead space, preserve adjacent flap options for future recurrence, reusable flaps are preferred

Postoperative care:
Strict pressure relief for six weeks on appropriate bed
Gradual reintroduction of sitting, increasing by thirty minutes per day
Suction drains to prevent seroma
Careful wound monitoring
Long-term pressure relief education

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recurrence rates for pressure sore reconstruction are as high as thirty to sixty percent
Reusable flaps allow re-advancement if ulcer recurs at the same site
Negative pressure wound therapy as bridge to surgery reduces bacterial load and promotes granulation
Flap sensibility (sensate flaps) may reduce recurrence in patients with some preserved sensation
Ostectomy of bony prominence may be performed at time of flap coverage to reduce future pressure
Discussion of the cost burden of pressure ulcers to the health service

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
