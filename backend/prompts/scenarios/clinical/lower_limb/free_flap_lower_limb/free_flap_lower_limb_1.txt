SCENARIO TITLE

Free Flap for Lower Limb Reconstruction

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

You are reviewing a forty year old man in the orthoplastic clinic. He sustained an open tibial fracture six weeks ago in a road traffic accident. Despite debridement and external fixation, he has a persistent soft tissue defect measuring eight by twelve centimetres over the distal third of his right tibia with exposed metalwork and bone. The wound has been managed with V A C dressings. He now requires definitive soft tissue coverage.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment and planning:
Defect in distal third means no reliable local muscle flaps — free flap required
Patient fitness for prolonged surgery (typically four to eight hours)
Smoking status, diabetes, peripheral vascular disease all affect outcomes
Previous injuries or surgery may have damaged potential recipient vessels
Assessment of donor site morbidity and patient preference

Vascular assessment:
C T angiography is the standard for planning recipient vessels
Must assess patency of anterior tibial, posterior tibial, and peroneal arteries
End-to-side anastomosis preferred to preserve distal limb perfusion
Vein grafts may be needed if recipient vessels damaged in zone of injury
Venae comitantes or long saphenous vein for venous drainage

Flap selection:
A L T flap (anterolateral thigh): versatile, can be thinned, long pedicle, fasciocutaneous
Gracilis muscle flap with S S G: good for moderate defects, minimal donor morbidity
Latissimus dorsi: large muscle flap for extensive defects, skin grafted
Rectus abdominis: reliable but higher donor morbidity
Muscle flaps are preferred when there is dead space, exposed bone, or chronic infection
Fasciocutaneous flaps preferred for superficial defects requiring contour

Operative principles:
Thorough debridement before flap inset — excise all non-viable tissue
Two-team approach speeds surgery (one team prepares recipient, one harvests flap)
Recipient vessels should be outside the zone of injury where possible
Microvascular anastomosis using operating microscope
At least one artery and one to two veins for adequate drainage
Flap inset without tension, drain deep space

Postoperative monitoring:
Clinical observation: colour, temperature, capillary refill, turgor
Hourly checks for first forty-eight to seventy-two hours
Implantable Doppler monitoring of the pedicle
Patient to be kept warm, well hydrated, haemoglobin adequate
Avoid vasoconstrictors, caffeine, and nicotine

Complications:
Venous congestion (most common vascular complication): dusky, congested, rapid capillary refill
Arterial insufficiency: pale, cool, no capillary refill
If vascular compromise suspected, return to theatre within one hour for re-exploration
Take-back rate approximately five to ten percent, salvage rate approximately fifty percent
Partial flap loss, infection, haematoma, seroma as other complications

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Perforator-based flaps (A L T, deep inferior epigastric perforator) allow tailored tissue harvest
Flow-through flaps can reconstruct a vessel gap and provide soft tissue cover simultaneously
Supermicrosurgery techniques for distal vessel anastomosis
Discussion of negative pressure wound therapy over the flap to reduce seroma and improve inset
Bone reconstruction options: vascularised fibula flap for segmental bone loss
Functional muscle transfer (gracilis) for associated compartment loss
Indocyanine green angiography for intra-operative perfusion assessment

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
