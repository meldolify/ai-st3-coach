SCENARIO TITLE

Peripheral Vascular Disease and Critical Limb Ischaemia

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

You are asked to review a sixty-eight year old gentleman who has been referred to your clinic by the vascular surgeons. He has critical limb ischaemia of his left leg with rest pain and a small area of tissue loss on his fourth toe. He has had a femoral-popliteal bypass six months ago which has now occluded. The vascular team feel there are no further revascularisation options and have asked for your input regarding wound management and potential amputation level.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Critical limb ischaemia:
Defined by rest pain for more than two weeks, ulceration, or gangrene attributable to arterial disease
A B P I less than 0.4 or ankle pressure less than fifty millimetres of mercury
Toe pressure less than thirty millimetres of mercury
Fontaine classification: stage one asymptomatic, stage two intermittent claudication, stage three rest pain, stage four tissue loss
This patient is Fontaine stage four

Assessment:
Vascular examination: pulses, capillary refill, skin temperature, colour changes
Buerger test: elevation pallor and dependent rubor
Extent of tissue loss and viability of remaining foot
Assessment of contralateral limb
Comorbidities: ischaemic heart disease, cerebrovascular disease, diabetes, renal function
Functional status and mobility pre-morbidly

Investigations:
A B P I and toe pressures (may be falsely elevated in diabetics)
Transcutaneous oxygen pressure (T c P O 2) — values above forty predict healing, below twenty predict failure
Duplex ultrasound or C T angiography to confirm no further revascularisation options
Bloods: renal function, H b A 1 c, albumin, clotting
Echocardiography and cardiology review if major surgery planned

Amputation levels:
Toe amputation: for isolated digital gangrene with adequate forefoot perfusion
Ray amputation: includes metatarsal, preserves more functional foot
Transmetatarsal: forefoot gangrene with viable hindfoot
Below-knee amputation (B K A): most common major level, preserves knee for prosthetic use
Through-knee: easier wound healing than B K A but bulky prosthesis
Above-knee amputation (A K A): when B K A not viable, higher energy cost for prosthetic walking
Aim to preserve the most distal functional level that will heal

Principles of amputation:
Adequate blood supply at amputation level is essential for healing
Long posterior flap technique for B K A (Burgess technique)
Myodesis or myoplasty to stabilise muscle over bone end
Nerve should be transected under gentle traction to allow retraction
Bone ends should be smooth to prevent pressure on soft tissues

Rehabilitation:
Early physiotherapy and rehabilitation planning
Phantom limb pain management: gabapentin, mirror therapy, T E N S
Prosthetic fitting typically six to eight weeks post-amputation
Psychological support for adjustment to limb loss
Occupational therapy for activities of daily living
Wheelchair and home adaptation assessment

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Discussion of Rutherford classification as alternative to Fontaine
S P A R C (Standardised Protocol for the Assessment and Rehabilitation of Cohort) for amputee rehabilitation
Energy expenditure: B K A increases walking energy cost by forty to sixty percent, A K A by ninety to one hundred percent
Gritti-Stokes amputation (supracondylar) as alternative to through-knee
Discussion of pre-amputation multidisciplinary assessment including pain team
Contralateral limb surveillance — high risk of bilateral amputation in P V D patients
Role of spinal or epidural anaesthesia for amputation surgery
Five-year mortality after major amputation approaches fifty percent

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
