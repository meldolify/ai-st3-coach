SCENARIO TITLE

Chronic Osteomyelitis of the Lower Limb

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

You are asked to see a forty-five year old man in clinic who has a chronic wound over the anterior aspect of his right tibia with a discharging sinus. He had an open tibial fracture treated with intramedullary nailing three years ago. The wound has intermittently discharged since surgery, and he has had multiple courses of antibiotics from his G P. He is otherwise fit and well, and a non-smoker.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Diagnosis:
Chronic discharging sinus following open fracture fixation is osteomyelitis until proven otherwise
History of open fracture with metalwork provides the mechanism (haematogenous seeding or direct inoculation)
Systemic inflammatory markers may be normal in chronic osteomyelitis
Sinus tract culture does not reliably reflect the causative organism — bone biopsy is essential

Classification:
Cierny-Mader classification:
Anatomical type: type one medullary, type two superficial, type three localised, type four diffuse
Physiological host: A host (normal), B host (compromised locally or systemically), C host (treatment worse than disease)
This patient is likely type three (localised) in an A host

Investigations:
Bloods: C R P, E S R, white cell count, albumin
Plain X-rays: may show sequestrum, involucrum, periosteal reaction
M R I is the imaging modality of choice — high sensitivity for marrow oedema and sequestra
White cell labelled scan if M R I contraindicated
Bone biopsy at time of surgery for culture and histology — stop antibiotics two weeks before biopsy if possible
Multiple deep tissue samples (at least five) sent for culture

Metalwork considerations:
If the fracture has united, metalwork should be removed — biofilm on metalwork perpetuates infection
If the fracture has not united, staged approach may be needed
Biofilm on implants makes eradication with antibiotics alone virtually impossible

Surgical debridement:
Radical debridement of all necrotic and infected bone (the paprika sign — bleeding bone indicates viable tissue)
Excise sinus tracts and scarred soft tissue
Debridement until bleeding healthy bone margins (positive paprika sign)
Thorough irrigation

Dead space management:
Local antibiotic delivery: antibiotic-loaded cement beads or spacer (P M M A with gentamicin or vancomycin)
Bioabsorbable antibiotic carriers as alternative to cement
Masquelet technique (induced membrane): cement spacer for six to eight weeks, then remove spacer and fill with bone graft within the induced membrane
Bone graft options: autologous cancellous (iliac crest), R I A (reamer-irrigator-aspirator) graft
Bone transport (Ilizarov) for segmental defects

Soft tissue coverage:
Local muscle flaps improve vascularity and antibiotic delivery to the area
Gastrocnemius flap for proximal third defects
Soleus flap for middle third
Free flap (A L T, gracilis, latissimus dorsi) for distal third or large defects
Well-vascularised soft tissue coverage is essential for infection eradication

Antibiotic therapy:
Prolonged systemic antibiotics guided by bone culture (typically six to twelve weeks)
Initial intravenous therapy then step down to oral if suitable agent available
Microbiologist involvement essential for antibiotic selection
Staphylococcus aureus is the most common organism
Rifampicin-based combinations have good biofilm penetration but must not be used as monotherapy

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Discussion of the concept of biofilm and its role in chronic implant-associated infection
Single-stage versus two-stage approach — single stage possible in selected cases with good host status
Antibiotic-loaded hydrogels and bioabsorbable carriers as alternatives to P M M A
Phage therapy as an emerging treatment for resistant organisms
Role of P E T-C T for assessing disease activity and treatment response
Vascularised bone transfer (free fibula) for large segmental defects with osteomyelitis
Discussion of suppressive antibiotic therapy when definitive surgery is not possible
Hyperbaric oxygen therapy as adjunct — limited evidence

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
