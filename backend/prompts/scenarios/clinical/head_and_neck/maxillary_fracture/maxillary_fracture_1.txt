SCENARIO TITLE

Maxillary Fracture

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 35 year old man who has been involved in a road traffic collision as an unrestrained passenger. He has significant mid-face swelling, bilateral periorbital bruising, and his face appears elongated. He has malocclusion and C S F rhinorrhoea has been noted. He is currently in the emergency department resuscitation bay. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S primary survey with emphasis on airway
Following A T L S principles. Airway management is the priority as mid-face fractures can cause airway obstruction from haemorrhage, oedema, and posterior displacement of the maxilla.
Controlling haemorrhage: anterior and posterior nasal packing, head elevation, and in severe cases Foley catheter placement in the nasopharynx.
Knowing that nasopharyngeal airways are contraindicated in suspected base of skull fractures due to the risk of intracranial placement.
Cervical spine immobilisation and assessment for associated injuries.

Le Fort classification of mid-face fractures
Knowing the Le Fort classification.
Le Fort one: horizontal fracture through the maxilla above the teeth, separating the alveolus and hard palate from the rest of the maxilla. The palate is mobile.
Le Fort two: pyramidal fracture through the nasion, medial orbital walls, orbital floor, and lateral maxillary walls. The nose and maxilla move as a unit.
Le Fort three: complete craniofacial disjunction. The fracture passes through the nasofrontal suture, medial and lateral orbital walls, and zygomatic arch, separating the entire face from the cranium. The whole mid-face is mobile.
Knowing that Le Fort fractures are often asymmetric and may be different on each side.

Clinical examination
Inspecting for mid-face elongation or dish-face deformity, bilateral periorbital bruising, and subconjunctival haemorrhage.
Assessing for mid-face mobility by grasping the anterior maxilla and attempting to move it while stabilising the forehead.
Checking the occlusion which will be deranged.
Assessing for C S F rhinorrhoea by looking for clear fluid from the nose and testing for the halo sign on a gauze or testing for beta two transferrin.
Testing infraorbital nerve sensation bilaterally.
Examining the eyes for visual acuity, pupillary reflexes, and eye movements.

Management of C S F rhinorrhoea
Knowing that C S F rhinorrhoea indicates a dural tear with a base of skull fracture.
Initial management is conservative: head elevation, bed rest, and avoidance of nose blowing, straining, and sneezing.
The majority of C S F leaks will resolve spontaneously within seven to ten days.
If the leak persists, neurosurgical involvement for possible dural repair.
Prophylactic antibiotics for C S F leak are controversial but many units prescribe them.

Investigations and imaging
C T scan of the face with coronal and axial views and three-dimensional reconstruction is the investigation of choice.
C T head to exclude intracranial injury.
O P G for dental and mandibular assessment.

Timing and principles of surgical repair
Knowing that definitive repair should be performed once the patient is medically stable and the swelling has begun to settle, typically at five to ten days.
Knowing that restoration of the pre-injury occlusion, facial height, and facial projection are the goals.
Knowing that intermaxillary fixation is used to establish the occlusion before plating.

Surgical technique
Open reduction and internal fixation using miniplates at the buttresses of the mid-face: nasomaxillary, zygomaticomaxillary, and pterygomaxillary buttresses.
Surgical approaches include upper buccal sulcus incision for the maxilla, subciliary or transconjunctival approach for the orbital floor, and a coronal approach for the frontozygomatic region in Le Fort three fractures.
Restoring the occlusion with intermaxillary fixation first, then plating the buttresses from inferior to superior and from medial to lateral.

Post-operative care
Soft diet for six weeks.
Oral hygiene and chlorhexidine mouthwash.
Monitoring for C S F leak resolution.
Follow up to assess occlusion, facial contour, and infraorbital nerve recovery.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S primary survey with emphasis on airway
Knowing that massive mid-face haemorrhage may require posterior nasal packing, interventional radiology for embolisation of the maxillary artery, or rarely external carotid artery ligation.
Knowing that a surgical airway may be needed if oral or nasal intubation is not possible due to the fracture pattern.

Le Fort classification of mid-face fractures
Knowing that in practice, Le Fort fractures are rarely symmetrical and often involve a combination of patterns.
Knowing that the pterygoid plates are fractured in all Le Fort fractures and their involvement can be confirmed on C T.

Clinical examination
Knowing the guerin sign: ecchymosis of the palate indicating a Le Fort one fracture.
Knowing that raccoon eyes and a battle sign indicate a base of skull fracture associated with Le Fort two and three.
Knowing that traumatic telecanthus may occur in Le Fort two and three fractures due to disruption of the medial canthal ligament and naso-orbito-ethmoidal complex.

Management of C S F rhinorrhoea
Knowing the test for C S F: the halo sign where clear fluid spreads on a gauze around a central blood spot, and laboratory testing for beta two transferrin which is specific to C S F.
Knowing that a lumbar drain may be considered by neurosurgery for persistent C S F leaks.

Investigations and imaging
Knowing the role of C T angiography if there is concern about vascular injury, particularly internal carotid artery injury in Le Fort three fractures.
Knowing that three-dimensional C T reconstruction is invaluable for operative planning.

Timing and principles of surgical repair
Knowing the concept of the buttress system of the mid-face: the vertical buttresses resist masticatory forces and the horizontal buttresses resist posterior displacement. Reconstruction of the buttresses restores facial height and projection.
Knowing that delayed repair beyond three weeks makes reduction significantly more difficult due to early bony union.

Surgical technique
Knowing the specific buttresses to plate: nasomaxillary buttress medially, zygomaticomaxillary buttress laterally, and the frontozygomatic suture for Le Fort three.
Knowing the role of the coronal approach for wide exposure in Le Fort three fractures, allowing access to the frontozygomatic suture, zygomatic arch, and naso-orbito-ethmoidal complex.
Knowing the medial canthal ligament should be reattached with a transnasal wire if disrupted in naso-orbito-ethmoidal fractures to prevent telecanthus.
Knowing that bone grafting may be needed for comminuted areas or to restore orbital volume.

Post-operative care
Knowing the complications: malocclusion, facial asymmetry, enophthalmos from inadequate orbital floor reconstruction, persistent numbness, telecanthus, epiphora from lacrimal damage, and chronic sinusitis.
Knowing that persistent malocclusion may require revision fixation or orthodontic treatment.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
