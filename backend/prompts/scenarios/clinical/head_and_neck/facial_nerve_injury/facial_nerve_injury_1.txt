SCENARIO TITLE

Facial Nerve Injury

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 30 year old woman who has been referred to your clinic after sustaining a deep laceration to the right side of her face two days ago which was sutured in the emergency department. She has noticed that she cannot raise her right eyebrow and cannot close her right eye fully. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Facial nerve anatomy and branch identification
Knowing that the facial nerve exits the stylomastoid foramen and enters the parotid gland where it divides into its five terminal branches: temporal, zygomatic, buccal, marginal mandibular, and cervical.
Knowing the functions of each branch: temporal innervates the frontalis, zygomatic innervates the orbicularis oculi, buccal innervates the buccal muscles and upper lip elevators, marginal mandibular innervates the lower lip depressors, and cervical innervates the platysma.

Clinical assessment and branch testing
Testing all five branches systematically and documenting the findings.
Identifying that this patient has a temporal branch injury causing inability to raise the eyebrow and a zygomatic branch injury causing inability to close the eye.
Assessing the location of the laceration in relation to the surface anatomy landmarks.
Knowing the danger zones: the temporal branch is vulnerable where it crosses the zygomatic arch within the superficial temporal fascia, and the marginal mandibular branch is vulnerable where it crosses the mandible.

Determining the need for exploration and repair
Knowing the key decision point: the vertical line drawn from the lateral canthus of the eye divides the face. Injuries posterior to this line are likely to involve the main trunk or major branches and require surgical exploration and repair. Injuries anterior to this line may recover spontaneously due to the extensive anastomotic network between the terminal branches.
Knowing that this patient's laceration and the pattern of nerve injury should be assessed against this landmark to determine whether exploration is needed.
Knowing that exploration should be performed as soon as possible, ideally within seventy two hours before the distal nerve ends degenerate and can no longer be stimulated to aid identification.

Surgical technique for nerve repair
If exploration is indicated, performing the repair under general anaesthetic with loupe magnification or an operating microscope.
Extending the wound for adequate exposure along the course of the nerve.
Identifying the proximal and distal nerve ends using a nerve stimulator for the distal end if within seventy two hours of injury.
Performing a direct epineural repair without tension using fine non-absorbable sutures such as nine-oh or ten-oh Nylon.
If a gap exists, interposition nerve grafting using a donor nerve such as the great auricular nerve or sural nerve.

Eye protection in facial nerve palsy
Knowing that inability to close the eye risks exposure keratopathy and corneal ulceration.
Immediate measures: lubricating eye drops during the day and lubricating ointment at night, taping the eye closed at night, and a moisture chamber.
Ophthalmology referral for assessment and monitoring.

Long-term management and reconstruction
If the nerve does not recover, knowing the reconstructive options for eye closure: gold weight insertion into the upper eyelid, temporalis muscle transfer, or free gracilis muscle transfer with cross-face nerve graft for dynamic reanimation.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Facial nerve anatomy and branch identification
Knowing the course of the temporal branch: it crosses the zygomatic arch within the superficial layer of the deep temporal fascia, approximately one point five centimetres anterior to the tragus. Pitanguy's line from the tragus to a point one point five centimetres above the lateral eyebrow approximates its course.
Knowing the course of the marginal mandibular branch: it runs superficial to the facial artery and vein and crosses the mandible up to one centimetre below its lower border.
Knowing that the buccal branch has the most extensive anastomotic connections and isolated buccal branch injuries often recover spontaneously.

Clinical assessment and branch testing
Knowing the difference between an upper motor neuron and lower motor neuron facial nerve palsy: a lower motor neuron palsy affects the entire ipsilateral face including the forehead, whereas an upper motor neuron palsy spares the forehead due to bilateral cortical innervation.
Knowing that bilateral frontalis innervation means that a temporal branch injury can sometimes be partially compensated.
Using the House-Brackmann grading system to document the degree of facial nerve dysfunction.

Determining the need for exploration and repair
Knowing that electrophysiological studies including electroneurography and electromyography can be useful in assessing the degree of nerve injury after two to three weeks when Wallerian degeneration has occurred.
Knowing the Sunderland classification of nerve injuries: neurapraxia, axonotmesis, and neurotmesis, and how each relates to recovery potential.
Knowing that sharp lacerations are more likely to recover after repair than avulsion or crush injuries.

Surgical technique for nerve repair
Knowing that the great auricular nerve, which is found at Erb's point where it wraps around the posterior border of the sternocleidomastoid at the junction of its upper and middle thirds, is a convenient donor nerve for grafting in the same operative field.
Knowing that cross-face nerve grafting using the sural nerve can be used to provide innervation from the contralateral facial nerve for delayed reconstruction.
Knowing the concept of the baby-sit procedure: a hypoglossal to facial nerve transfer to provide tone while awaiting cross-face nerve graft reinnervation.

Eye protection in facial nerve palsy
Knowing that a tarsorrhaphy can be performed as a temporary measure to protect the cornea.
Knowing that upper eyelid gold or platinum weight insertion is a relatively simple procedure that uses gravity to assist eye closure and is reversible.

Long-term management and reconstruction
Knowing the options for facial reanimation: static procedures such as fascial slings for facial symmetry at rest, and dynamic procedures such as free gracilis muscle transfer innervated by a cross-face nerve graft or the masseteric nerve for smile restoration.
Knowing that the masseteric nerve is increasingly used as the motor nerve for free muscle transfer due to its proximity and reliable anatomy, though the smile is initiated by biting rather than being spontaneous.
Knowing that botulinum toxin can be used to treat synkinesis or to weaken the contralateral side for improved symmetry.
Knowing the time-sensitive nature of facial nerve reconstruction: muscle transfer should ideally be performed within eighteen to twenty four months of denervation before irreversible motor endplate degeneration.
