SCENARIO TITLE

Facial Laceration

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 22 year old man who has been brought to the emergency department after being assaulted with a glass bottle outside a pub. He has a deep laceration running from just in front of his left ear, across the cheek, to the corner of his mouth. There is significant bleeding. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S primary survey
Following A T L S principles to ensure the patient is haemodynamically stable and there are no other injuries.
Controlling active haemorrhage with direct pressure.
Asking about the mechanism, timing, tetanus status, last meal, allergies, and alcohol intake.

Assessment of the laceration and underlying structures
Examining the laceration noting its length, depth, and which anatomical zones of the face it crosses.
Knowing that a laceration in the pre-auricular region running across the cheek to the mouth crosses the territory of the parotid gland, parotid duct, facial nerve branches, and the buccal fat pad.
Assessing for any visible structures in the wound including fat, muscle, or glandular tissue.

Facial nerve examination
Testing all five branches of the facial nerve: temporal branch by asking the patient to raise the eyebrows, zygomatic branch by asking the patient to close the eyes tightly, buccal branch by asking the patient to smile and show the teeth, marginal mandibular branch by asking the patient to purse the lips, and cervical branch by asking the patient to platysma.
Documenting the findings before any local anaesthetic is given.

Parotid duct and gland assessment
Knowing that the parotid duct runs along a line from the tragus to the mid-upper lip, roughly along the middle third of the masseter.
Assessing for parotid duct injury by milking the parotid gland and looking for saliva emerging from the wound rather than from Stensen duct intraorally.
Examining the intraoral opening of the parotid duct opposite the upper second molar.

Investigations
X-ray or C T of the face if there is concern about an underlying fracture or foreign body such as glass.
Routine bloods if the patient requires a general anaesthetic.

Surgical repair
Knowing that deep facial lacerations with potential nerve or duct injury should be explored and repaired in theatre under general anaesthetic with good lighting and magnification.
Thorough wound washout and debridement of any devitalised tissue. Minimal tissue debridement on the face due to the excellent blood supply.
Repairing the parotid duct over a stent if it is injured.
If a facial nerve branch is lacerated, performing microsurgical epineural repair if the laceration is posterior to a vertical line dropped from the lateral canthus. Injuries anterior to this line may recover spontaneously due to the extensive anastomoses between terminal branches.
Layered closure: repairing the muscle layer with absorbable sutures, then the skin with fine non-absorbable sutures such as five-oh or six-oh Nylon or Prolene.
Meticulous alignment of anatomical landmarks including the vermillion border if the lip is involved.

Post-operative care
Wound care and suture removal at five to seven days on the face.
Tetanus prophylaxis.
Antibiotics if the wound is contaminated, particularly bite injuries.
Follow up to assess wound healing and facial nerve function.
Scar management advice.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S primary survey
Considering the possibility of associated injuries including cervical spine injury, head injury, and dental injury.
Asking about alcohol and drug intake which may affect the assessment and consent.
Considering the medico-legal implications and documenting the injury carefully for potential legal proceedings.

Assessment of the laceration and underlying structures
Knowing the surface anatomy landmarks: the parotid duct lies along a line from the tragus to the mid-upper lip. The marginal mandibular nerve runs approximately one centimetre below the mandible.
Knowing that the buccal fat pad may herniate through a deep cheek laceration and should be replaced and the wound closed in layers.

Facial nerve examination
Knowing that the facial nerve exits the stylomastoid foramen and enters the parotid gland where it divides into its five terminal branches.
Knowing the danger zones of the face where nerve injury is most likely: the temporal branch is vulnerable as it crosses the zygomatic arch in the superficial temporal fascia, and the marginal mandibular branch is vulnerable as it crosses the mandible one centimetre below its lower border.
Knowing that injuries to facial nerve branches anterior to the lateral canthus generally do not require repair due to the rich anastomotic network.

Parotid duct and gland assessment
Knowing that an unrepaired parotid duct injury may result in a salivary fistula or sialocele.
Knowing that the parotid duct can be repaired over a fine catheter or stent which is left in situ for two weeks.
If the duct cannot be repaired, knowing that the proximal end can be ligated or re-routed intraorally, or the gland can be allowed to atrophy.

Investigations
Knowing that C T with contrast may be useful if there is concern about vascular injury.

Surgical repair
Knowing the principles of facial wound repair: minimal debridement, meticulous alignment of anatomical landmarks, layered closure, and fine sutures with early removal.
Knowing that the vermillion border of the lip must be aligned precisely as even one millimetre of misalignment is noticeable.
Knowing that any associated dental injuries should be assessed and managed in conjunction with maxillofacial surgery.
Knowing the importance of repairing the orbicularis oris muscle in lip lacerations to restore oral competence.

Post-operative care
Knowing the complications of facial lacerations including infection, hypertrophic scarring, facial nerve palsy, parotid fistula, and poor cosmetic outcome.
Knowing that secondary scar revision may be considered after twelve to eighteen months if the scar is cosmetically unacceptable.
Advising sun protection to the scar for at least twelve months.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
