SCENARIO TITLE

Parotid Injury

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 35 year old man who has sustained a knife wound to his left cheek during an assault. The wound is deep and is located over the masseter muscle. There is clear fluid leaking from the wound which is not blood. He has presented to the emergency department. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment and identification of parotid injury
Knowing that a wound over the cheek in the region of the masseter with clear fluid leaking from the wound is suspicious for parotid gland or duct injury.
Following A T L S principles and ensuring haemodynamic stability.
Asking about the mechanism, timing, tetanus status, and allergies.

Surface anatomy of the parotid gland and duct
Knowing that the parotid gland lies over the masseter and the ramus of the mandible.
Knowing that the parotid duct, Stensen duct, runs along a line from the tragus to the mid-upper lip, approximately along the middle third of a line from the tragus to the midpoint of the upper lip.
Knowing that the duct crosses the masseter, pierces the buccinator muscle, and opens intraorally opposite the upper second molar tooth.
Knowing that the facial nerve passes through the parotid gland and injuries in this region carry a high risk of associated facial nerve injury.

Assessment for associated facial nerve injury
Testing all five branches of the facial nerve and documenting the findings before any local anaesthetic is given.
Knowing that the buccal branch of the facial nerve runs close to the parotid duct and may be injured concurrently.

Investigations
X-ray or C T to exclude underlying fracture or foreign body.
Confirming the parotid duct injury: milking the parotid gland and looking for saliva emerging from the wound rather than from the intraoral papilla opposite the upper second molar.
Alternatively, cannulating the duct intraorally and injecting methylene blue or saline, which will emerge from the wound if the duct is transected.

Surgical exploration and repair
Knowing that a parotid duct injury requires formal surgical exploration and repair under general anaesthetic.
Exploring the wound with adequate exposure, identifying both ends of the transected duct.
Repairing the duct over a fine stent such as a silicone catheter or cannula, using fine absorbable sutures.
The stent is left in situ for approximately two weeks to maintain duct patency while healing occurs.
Checking for and repairing any concurrent facial nerve branch injury.
Layered wound closure.

Management if the duct cannot be repaired
Knowing the options if primary repair is not possible: rerouting the proximal duct end intraorally to create a new opening, ligating the proximal duct which will cause the gland to atrophy over time, or performing a superficial parotidectomy.

Post-operative care
Antibiotics to cover oral flora.
Tetanus prophylaxis.
Soft diet to reduce salivary flow initially.
Follow up to assess wound healing, duct patency, facial nerve function, and to remove the duct stent.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment and identification of parotid injury
Knowing the difference between a parotid gland parenchymal injury and a parotid duct injury. Parenchymal injuries may be managed conservatively with pressure dressings and aspiration of any sialocele that develops. Duct injuries require repair.
Knowing that a sialocele is a collection of saliva in the tissues and can develop after untreated parotid injury.

Surface anatomy of the parotid gland and duct
Knowing that the parotid duct is approximately five centimetres long and three to four millimetres in diameter.
Knowing the relationship of the facial nerve to the parotid gland: the nerve enters the gland at its posteromedial surface and divides within the gland into its five terminal branches. The nerve lies superficial to the retromandibular vein which acts as a landmark during parotid surgery.

Assessment for associated facial nerve injury
Knowing that the temporal and marginal mandibular branches are the most vulnerable as they have the fewest anastomotic connections.
Knowing that injuries to the buccal branch often recover spontaneously due to cross-innervation from the zygomatic and marginal mandibular branches.

Investigations
Knowing that sialography can be used to assess duct integrity by injecting contrast into the duct intraorally, though this is rarely needed acutely.
Knowing that ultrasound may be useful to identify a sialocele or parotid gland swelling in the follow-up period.

Surgical exploration and repair
Knowing that identification of the cut duct ends can be aided by injecting methylene blue into the distal duct intraorally, which will highlight the distal end in the wound.
Knowing that microsurgical technique may be needed for duct repair, particularly if the duct is small.
Knowing that concurrent facial nerve injuries should be repaired at the same sitting if identified.

Management if the duct cannot be repaired
Knowing that ligation of the duct is the simplest option and results in painless atrophy of the gland over several weeks.
Knowing that anticholinergic medication such as hyoscine or glycopyrrolate can be used temporarily to reduce salivary flow.
Knowing that botulinum toxin injection into the parotid gland can reduce secretion if a salivary fistula develops.

Post-operative care
Knowing the complications: salivary fistula, sialocele, parotid gland infection, facial nerve palsy, and Frey syndrome which is gustatory sweating caused by aberrant reinnervation of the sweat glands by parasympathetic secretomotor fibres.
Knowing that a salivary fistula may develop if the duct repair fails and can be managed with pressure dressings, aspiration, anticholinergic medication, or botulinum toxin injection.
Knowing that Frey syndrome can be managed with topical antiperspirants or botulinum toxin injection.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
