SCENARIO TITLE

Scalp Reconstruction

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 60 year old gentleman who has undergone wide local excision of a squamous cell carcinoma from his vertex scalp. The excision has resulted in a defect measuring eight by six centimetres with exposed calvarium. The periosteum has been excised with the specimen. The margins are confirmed as clear. You have been asked to discuss the reconstructive options.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment of the defect
Describing the defect: size, location, depth, and what structures are exposed.
Knowing that exposed calvarium without periosteum will not support a skin graft and requires a flap for coverage.
Assessing the surrounding scalp tissue for laxity and any previous scars.
Checking for any tethering to underlying structures or infiltration into the calvarium.

Layers of the scalp
Knowing the five layers of the scalp using the mnemonic S C A L P: Skin, Connective tissue or subcutaneous fat, Aponeurosis or galea aponeurotica, Loose areolar tissue, and Periosteum or pericranium.
Knowing that the galea is the key structural layer and is continuous with the frontalis anteriorly, the occipitalis posteriorly, and the temporoparietal fascia laterally.
Knowing that the pericranium is the periosteum of the calvarium and is a vascularised layer that will support a skin graft if intact.

Reconstructive options based on defect characteristics
For small defects with intact pericranium: split thickness or full thickness skin graft.
For small to moderate defects: local rotation or advancement flaps. Galeal scoring can increase flap mobility by making parallel incisions through the galea on the undersurface of the flap.
For larger defects: large rotation flaps such as the pinwheel flap using multiple rotation flaps to close the defect.
For defects with exposed calvarium without periosteum: a flap is required as a graft will not take. Options include local flaps with galeal scoring, tissue expansion of the adjacent scalp, or free tissue transfer.
For defects with full thickness calvarial loss: free flap with bone reconstruction.

Surgical technique
Describing the chosen technique for this defect based on its size and the absence of periosteum.
If a local rotation flap is possible: designing a large rotation flap with a back-cut or Burrow triangle, undermining in the subgaleal plane, and galeal scoring if needed.
If tissue expansion: inserting a tissue expander under the adjacent scalp to generate additional tissue over several weeks before definitive reconstruction.

Post-operative care
Drain placement under the flap.
Head elevation to reduce swelling.
Wound care and monitoring of flap viability.
Follow up for oncological surveillance and scar management.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment of the defect
Arranging a C T head to check for calvarial infiltration by the tumour if there is any suspicion on clinical examination.
Knowing that if the outer table of the calvarium is involved, burring of the outer table may be required before reconstruction.
Assessing the need for pre-operative imaging to plan the blood supply of proposed flaps.

Layers of the scalp
Knowing the blood supply of the scalp: five paired arteries including the supratrochlear, supraorbital, superficial temporal, posterior auricular, and occipital arteries. The rich anastomotic network means scalp flaps have a reliable blood supply.
Knowing that the scalp has limited laxity and primary closure is often not possible for defects greater than three centimetres.

Reconstructive options based on defect characteristics
Knowing that tissue expansion is the ideal technique for scalp reconstruction as it provides hair-bearing tissue that matches the surrounding scalp. The disadvantage is the need for multiple operations and the period of disfigurement during expansion.
Knowing that for full thickness calvarial defects, the options include burring of the outer table and skin grafting onto the inner table, titanium mesh cranioplasty with flap coverage, or free tissue transfer with a bone flap such as fibula or iliac crest.
Knowing that free flaps for scalp reconstruction include the latissimus dorsi, anterolateral thigh, and radial forearm flaps, depending on the size of the defect.
Knowing that the Orticochea three-flap technique uses three large rotation-advancement flaps to close very large scalp defects.

Surgical technique
Knowing that galeal scoring involves making parallel incisions through the galea perpendicular to the direction of advancement, spaced approximately one centimetre apart, to increase flap extensibility by up to two to three centimetres.
Knowing that undermining in the subgaleal loose areolar tissue plane is the correct plane for scalp flap elevation as it is relatively avascular and preserves the pericranium.
Knowing that if the pericranium is intact, a split thickness skin graft can be applied directly as a simpler option.
Knowing the concept of the crane principle: a flap is raised to cover an avascular bed, and once granulation tissue has formed under the flap after several weeks, the flap is returned to its donor site and the granulating bed is skin grafted.

Post-operative care
Knowing the complications of scalp reconstruction: flap necrosis, haematoma, infection, alopecia at the flap edges, contour irregularity, and donor site morbidity for free flaps.
Knowing that wound healing on the scalp is generally excellent due to the rich blood supply.
Knowing the importance of oncological follow up and surveillance for local recurrence.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
