SCENARIO TITLE

Ear Reconstruction

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 70 year old gentleman who has undergone Mohs micrographic surgery for a squamous cell carcinoma of the right helix. He has been left with a full thickness defect of the upper helix measuring approximately two centimetres. You have been asked to discuss the reconstructive options.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment of the defect
Describing the defect: full thickness loss of approximately two centimetres of the upper helix involving skin, cartilage, and posterior skin.
Confirming that margins are clear from the Mohs surgery before proceeding with reconstruction.
Assessing the surrounding skin quality and any previous scars.

Principles of ear reconstruction
Knowing the goal is to maintain the ear contour, symmetry with the contralateral ear, and structural support.
Knowing that the ear is composed of a skin-cartilage sandwich and full thickness defects require reconstruction of all three layers: anterior skin, cartilage framework, and posterior skin.

Reconstructive options based on defect size and location
Knowing the reconstructive ladder for ear defects.
For defects with no cartilage loss: primary closure.
For defects up to one quarter of the ear: wedge excision and direct closure. The wedge should be designed at thirty degrees to minimise dog-ear formation. Full thickness excision through anterior skin, cartilage, and posterior skin, closed in layers.
For defects up to one point five centimetres: Burrow triangle technique converting the area into a star shape with three Burrow triangles and approximating each layer.
For larger helical defects: Antia-Buch chondrocutaneous advancement flap which mobilises the helical rim on both sides of the defect to close it.
For conchal defects: flip-flop flap from the posterior auricular surface.

Surgical technique
Describing the chosen technique for a two centimetre helical defect: an Antia-Buch flap or wedge excision depending on the laxity of the remaining ear.
Closing in layers: cartilage with P D S sutures, posterior skin, and anterior skin with Nylon.

Post-operative care
Appropriate dressings protecting the ear.
Suture removal at seven to ten days.
Follow up for wound healing and oncological surveillance.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment of the defect
Assessing the contralateral ear for comparison and symmetry planning.
Noting the specific subunit of the ear affected: helix, antihelix, concha, lobule, or tragus as each has different reconstructive considerations.

Principles of ear reconstruction
Knowing the blood supply to the ear: the superficial temporal artery and posterior auricular artery.
Knowing that the ear has minimal subcutaneous tissue making it susceptible to ischaemia with tight closures.

Reconstructive options based on defect size and location
Knowing the Antia-Buch technique in detail: a V-Y advancement of the helical rim based on the chondrocutaneous flap. The helical rim is mobilised superiorly and inferiorly by making a full thickness incision through the scaphal cartilage, allowing the helical segments to advance and close the defect.
Knowing that for larger defects not amenable to local flaps, options include a post-auricular skin flap with costal cartilage graft for framework reconstruction, or a temporoparietal fascia flap with skin graft.
Knowing the Nagata technique for total ear reconstruction using costal cartilage framework in congenital microtia, though this is less relevant for post-oncological defects.
Knowing that prosthetic ear reconstruction using osseointegrated implants is an option for large defects or when multiple previous surgeries have failed.

Surgical technique
Knowing that cartilage sutures should approximate the cartilage edges to maintain contour without excessive tension.
Knowing that meticulous haemostasis is important as haematoma can compromise the thin skin flaps.
Knowing the importance of avoiding pressure on the reconstructed ear post-operatively by using a bulky protective dressing.

Post-operative care
Knowing the complications: haematoma, infection, cartilage necrosis, notching of the helical rim, and ear asymmetry.
Knowing that secondary revision procedures may be needed to refine the contour.
Knowing the importance of oncological follow up with regular skin surveillance.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
