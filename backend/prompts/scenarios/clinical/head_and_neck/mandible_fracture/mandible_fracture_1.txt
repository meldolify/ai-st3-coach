SCENARIO TITLE

Mandible Fracture

CASE INTRODUCTION

This is a 25 year old man who has been punched in the jaw during an assault. He has pain, swelling over the left side of his jaw, difficulty opening his mouth, and his bite feels abnormal. He has presented to the emergency department. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S assessment
Following A T L S principles, particularly assessing the airway as mandible fractures can compromise the airway due to loss of anterior tongue support, haemorrhage, and swelling.
Ruling out associated injuries including other facial fractures, head injury, and cervical spine injury.
Asking about the mechanism, timing, dental symptoms, malocclusion, numbness of the lower lip, and any difficulty swallowing or breathing.

Clinical examination of the mandible
Inspecting for swelling, bruising, deformity, and asymmetry.
Palpating along the entire mandible from the condyles to the symphysis for tenderness, step deformity, and crepitus.
Examining intraorally for mucosal lacerations, sublingual haematoma which suggests a fracture of the anterior mandible, and loose or damaged teeth.
Assessing the occlusion by asking the patient if the bite feels normal.
Testing the inferior alveolar nerve by checking sensation of the lower lip and chin.

Dental and occlusal assessment
Assessing the dentition for any missing, loose, or damaged teeth.
Noting the pre-injury occlusal relationship.
Knowing that restoration of the pre-injury occlusion is the primary goal of mandible fracture management.

Investigations and imaging
O P G which is the most useful initial investigation for mandible fractures, showing the entire mandible on a single image.
C T face with three-dimensional reconstruction for complex fractures or condylar fractures.
P A mandible view as an adjunct.

Classification and common fracture patterns
Knowing the common sites of mandible fracture: condyle (the most common), angle, body, symphysis and parasymphysis, ramus, and coronoid.
Knowing that mandible fractures often occur in pairs because the mandible is a ring structure, so always look for a second fracture.
Classifying the fracture as favourable or unfavourable based on the direction of the fracture line and the pull of the muscles: an unfavourable fracture is one where the muscle forces tend to displace the fragments.

Operative versus conservative management
Knowing that undisplaced or minimally displaced condylar fractures in adults and most paediatric mandible fractures can be managed conservatively with a soft diet, analgesia, and maxillomandibular fixation or training elastics.
Knowing the indications for open reduction and internal fixation: displaced fractures, fractures with malocclusion, open fractures, and fractures that cannot be adequately reduced by closed methods.

Surgical technique
Performing the procedure under general anaesthetic, typically with a nasal intubation to allow intraoperative assessment of the occlusion.
Restoring the occlusion first using intermaxillary fixation with arch bars or I M F screws.
Performing open reduction and internal fixation through an intraoral or transcutaneous approach depending on the fracture site.
Plating the fracture using miniplates and monocortical screws following the Champy ideal line of osteosynthesis.

Post-operative care
Soft diet for six weeks.
Oral hygiene including chlorhexidine mouthwash.
Antibiotics, typically co-amoxiclav, for open fractures.
Follow up to assess the occlusion, wound healing, and inferior alveolar nerve function.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S assessment
Knowing that bilateral parasymphyseal or body fractures can cause posterior displacement of the anterior mandible and tongue with life-threatening airway obstruction.
Knowing that temporary airway management in mandible fractures includes pulling the tongue forward, suturing the tongue to the lip, or performing a chin lift.

Clinical examination of the mandible
Knowing the tongue blade test: the patient bites on a wooden tongue depressor and the examiner twists it. If the patient can hold the blade without pain, a fracture is unlikely. If the blade breaks easily or causes pain, a fracture is probable.

Dental and occlusal assessment
Knowing the Angle classification of dental occlusion for describing the pre-injury and post-injury bite.
Knowing how to identify a tooth in the fracture line and whether it should be removed: a tooth in the fracture line should be removed if it is loose, fractured, or prevents adequate reduction, but retained if it helps with fracture alignment and is not infected.

Investigations and imaging
Knowing that O P G may be unreliable for condylar fractures and a C T scan is often needed.
Knowing the role of three-dimensional C T reconstruction in complex fractures for operative planning.

Classification and common fracture patterns
Knowing the Champy ideal lines of osteosynthesis: for the symphysis and parasymphysis two plates are used, one at the lower border and one along the subapical region. For the body and angle a single plate along the external oblique ridge is often sufficient.
Knowing the classification of condylar fractures: intracapsular, neck of condyle, and subcondylar, and the management differences.

Operative versus conservative management
Knowing the debate around open versus closed management of condylar fractures: closed management with maxillomandibular fixation and early mobilisation is traditional, but open reduction and internal fixation with endoscopic or pre-auricular approaches is increasingly used for displaced condylar fractures in adults.
Knowing that in children, condylar fractures are managed conservatively due to the remodelling potential, and that aggressive open surgery risks damaging the growth centre of the condyle.

Surgical technique
Knowing the advantages of the intraoral approach: no external scar, direct visualisation of the fracture in the body and angle region. Disadvantages: limited access for posterior fractures.
Knowing the indications for an external transcutaneous approach: fractures of the condyle, ramus, or posterior body where intraoral access is inadequate.
Knowing the risk of damage to the marginal mandibular nerve with a transcutaneous submandibular approach and the steps to avoid it: incision two finger breadths below the mandible, identification and preservation of the nerve.
Knowing the Risdon approach for submandibular access and the retromandibular approach for condylar fractures.

Post-operative care
Knowing the complications: malocclusion, malunion, non-union, infection, plate exposure, inferior alveolar nerve injury, and temporomandibular joint dysfunction.
Knowing that persistent malocclusion after fixation requires early re-assessment and possible revision.
Knowing the role of maxillomandibular fixation using training elastics post-operatively to guide the occlusion during healing.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
