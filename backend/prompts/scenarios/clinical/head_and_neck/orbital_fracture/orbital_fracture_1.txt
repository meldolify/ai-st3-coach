SCENARIO TITLE

Orbital Fracture

CASE INTRODUCTION

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S assessment
Following A T L S principles and ruling out associated injuries including other facial fractures, head injury, and cervical spine injury.
Asking about the mechanism, timing, visual symptoms including diplopia and visual loss, and any associated injuries.

Clinical examination including eye and nerve assessment
Examining the orbit for periorbital swelling, bruising, ecchymosis, and subcutaneous emphysema.
Assessing for enophthalmos which may be masked initially by swelling.
Testing eye movements in all directions and documenting any restriction, particularly on upgaze, which suggests entrapment of the inferior rectus or orbital contents in the fracture.
Performing the forced duction test if entrapment is suspected.
Testing visual acuity.
Testing pupillary reflexes looking for a relative afferent pupillary defect which may indicate optic nerve injury.
Testing infraorbital nerve sensation which supplies the cheek, upper lip, and upper teeth. Numbness indicates involvement of the infraorbital nerve in the fracture.
Examining for any associated zygomatic fracture by palpating the orbital rim, zygoma, and zygomatic arch.

Investigations and imaging
C T scan of the orbits with coronal and sagittal reconstructions to assess the fracture pattern, size of the defect, position of the orbital contents, and any herniation of fat or muscle into the maxillary sinus.
Looking for the teardrop sign on coronal C T which represents herniated orbital contents in the maxillary sinus.
Assessing whether the fracture is a blow-out fracture of the orbital floor, a trapdoor fracture, or part of a more complex fracture pattern.

Indications for conservative versus operative management
Knowing that many orbital floor fractures can be managed conservatively with observation, ice, nose-blowing precautions, and oral antibiotics.
Knowing the indications for surgical repair: significant enophthalmos of greater than two millimetres, persistent diplopia with evidence of entrapment on C T, a large orbital floor defect involving greater than fifty percent of the floor, and a trapdoor fracture with acute entrapment, particularly in children where urgent surgery is needed to prevent ischaemic necrosis of the entrapped muscle.
Knowing that surgery is typically performed at seven to fourteen days once the swelling has settled, unless there is an acute trapdoor entrapment which requires urgent intervention.

Surgical technique
Describing the surgical approach: subciliary, transconjunctival, or subtarsal incision to access the orbital floor.
Identifying and releasing any entrapped tissue.
Reconstructing the orbital floor defect with an implant such as a titanium mesh, porous polyethylene, or autologous bone graft from the outer table of the calvarium or the iliac crest.
Performing a forced duction test at the end of the procedure to confirm no residual entrapment.

Post-operative care
Ice application and head elevation.
Antibiotics and nose-blowing precautions.
Follow up with ophthalmology for assessment of diplopia resolution and eye movements.
Serial assessment of enophthalmos once the swelling has settled.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S assessment
Knowing that a retrobulbar haematoma causing raised intraocular pressure is an ophthalmic emergency requiring immediate lateral canthotomy and cantholysis to decompress the orbit and prevent optic nerve ischaemia.
Knowing the signs of retrobulbar haematoma: proptosis, tense globe, reduced visual acuity, relative afferent pupillary defect, and a rock-hard eye on palpation.

Clinical examination including eye and nerve assessment
Knowing the Hertel exophthalmometer for measuring enophthalmos or proptosis.
Knowing that in a paediatric trapdoor fracture, the child may have an oculocardiac reflex with nausea, vomiting, and bradycardia caused by vagal stimulation from the entrapped muscle.
Knowing that a white-eyed blow-out fracture in a child with minimal external signs but significant restriction of eye movement is an emergency requiring urgent surgery.

Investigations and imaging
Knowing the difference between a blow-out fracture where the orbital rim is intact and the floor has buckled, and a blow-in fracture where fragments are displaced into the orbit.
Knowing that three-dimensional C T reconstruction can help with operative planning for complex fractures.

Indications for conservative versus operative management
Knowing that isolated infraorbital nerve numbness alone is not an indication for surgery as it usually recovers over weeks to months.
Knowing that diplopia immediately after injury often improves as swelling resolves and should be reassessed at two weeks before deciding on surgery.
Knowing the difference between mechanical entrapment which requires surgery and diplopia from oedema or haematoma which resolves spontaneously.

Surgical technique
Knowing the advantages and disadvantages of different surgical approaches: subciliary gives good access but has a risk of lower lid malposition, transconjunctival avoids an external scar and has a lower risk of ectropion, and subtarsal is a compromise between the two.
Knowing the advantages of titanium mesh: anatomically pre-formed, rigid support, low infection rate, and can be contoured and trimmed intraoperatively.
Knowing the role of intraoperative navigation or the use of a mirror image of the contralateral orbit on C T for accurate reconstruction of orbital volume.

Post-operative care
Knowing that persistent diplopia after surgery may require prism correction or strabismus surgery.
Knowing the complications of orbital floor repair: persistent diplopia, enophthalmos, ectropion from the surgical approach, infraorbital nerve damage, implant infection or migration, and visual loss.
Knowing that the infraorbital nerve numbness typically improves over three to six months but may be permanent.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
