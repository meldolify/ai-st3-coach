SCENARIO TITLE

Lip Reconstruction

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 65 year old gentleman who has undergone excision of a squamous cell carcinoma of the lower lip. The excision has resulted in a full thickness defect involving approximately forty percent of the lower lip. The margins are confirmed as clear. You have been asked to discuss the reconstructive options for this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment of the defect
Describing the defect: full thickness loss of forty percent of the lower lip.
Noting whether the commissure is involved.
Assessing whether the defect is partial thickness involving skin and muscle only, or full thickness involving skin, orbicularis oris, and mucosa.
Noting the subunit involvement and any distortion of the cupid bow or white roll.

Goals and principles of lip reconstruction
Knowing the goals of lip reconstruction in order of priority: oral competence by restoring the orbicularis oris sphincter, preservation of oral aperture, speech, cosmesis including preserving the vermillion border and white roll alignment, and sensation.
Knowing the principle of replacing like with like: lip tissue is best reconstructed with lip tissue from the opposite lip or adjacent tissue.

Reconstructive ladder for lower lip defects
Knowing the reconstructive options based on the size of the defect.
Up to one third: direct closure using a V-shaped, shield-shaped, or W-plasty excision, not crossing the labiomental fold.
One third to two thirds: Abbe flap from the upper lip for central defects, Estlander flap for defects involving the commissure, or Karapandzic flap which is a neurovascular advancement rotation flap that preserves the orbicularis oris and sensation.
Two thirds to total: Gillies fan flap, McGregor flap, or bilateral Karapandzic flaps.
Total or near-total: pedicled or free flap reconstruction such as radial forearm free flap, gracilis, or anterolateral thigh flap.

Reconstructive ladder for upper lip defects
Knowing the options: up to one third direct closure, greater than one third unilateral or bilateral crescenteric advancement flap, Abbe flap from the lower lip for central defects, or perialar advancement flaps.

Surgical technique
For this forty percent defect, describing the chosen reconstruction such as a Karapandzic or Abbe flap.
Knowing the anaesthesia for lower lip procedures: bilateral mental nerve blocks by infiltrating local anaesthetic into the buccal fold in front of the mandibular premolars.

Post-operative care
Soft diet post-operatively.
Wound care and suture removal.
If an Abbe flap is used, knowing it is a two-stage procedure with pedicle division at two to three weeks.
Follow up for scar management and functional assessment.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment of the defect
Confirming the histology has clear margins before embarking on definitive reconstruction.
Assessing the patient's dentition and oral hygiene.
Assessing for any associated nerve involvement, particularly the mental nerve.

Goals and principles of lip reconstruction
Knowing the Estlander flap is used specifically for commissure defects and involves cross-lip transfer from the opposite lip including the commissure.
Knowing the difference between the Abbe flap which is a cross-lip flap based on the labial artery used for central defects, and the Estlander which is used for lateral or commissure defects.

Reconstructive ladder for lower lip defects
Knowing the Karapandzic flap technique: circumoral incisions are made through skin and muscle with preservation of the neurovascular bundles which run within the orbicularis oris. The flap is advanced as a neurovascular island maintaining both motor function and sensation. The disadvantage is microstomia for large defects.
Knowing the Gillies fan flap: a nasolabial-based rotation flap that reconstructs the lip but does not contain muscle and therefore does not restore dynamic function.
Knowing that free flap reconstruction of the lip provides bulk and tissue coverage but does not restore sphincteric function or sensation and results in a poorer functional outcome.

Surgical technique
Knowing the blood supply to the lips: the superior and inferior labial arteries which are branches of the facial artery and form an arterial ring around the mouth.
Knowing the importance of layered closure: mucosa, orbicularis oris muscle, and skin must be closed separately.
Knowing that precise alignment of the vermillion border is critical as even one millimetre of mismatch is cosmetically noticeable.
Knowing the anaesthetic landmarks for the infraorbital nerve block for upper lip procedures: one centimetre below the infraorbital rim at the mid-pupillary line.

Post-operative care
Knowing the complications: oral incompetence, microstomia, asymmetry, scarring, and reduced sensation.
Knowing that commissure reconstruction after Estlander flap may require secondary commissuroplasty to recreate the angle of the mouth.
Knowing the importance of oncological follow up given the underlying malignancy.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
