SCENARIO TITLE

Frontal Sinus Fracture

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 28 year old man who was struck in the forehead with a cricket bat. He has a depressed area over his forehead with overlying laceration, periorbital bruising, and clear fluid draining from his nose. He is conscious and orientated. He has presented to the emergency department. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S assessment
Following A T L S principles with particular attention to head injury assessment.
Assessing for associated injuries including intracranial injury, orbital fractures, and naso-orbito-ethmoidal fractures.
G C S assessment and neurological examination.

Clinical examination
Inspecting the forehead for depression, laceration, and any visible bone fragments.
Palpating for a step deformity of the anterior table.
Assessing for C S F rhinorrhoea indicating a posterior table fracture with dural tear.
Examining the eyes for visual acuity, pupillary reflexes, eye movements, and periorbital swelling.
Testing supraorbital and supratrochlear nerve sensation.

Investigation and C T interpretation
C T scan of the head and face with axial and coronal views is essential.
Assessing for anterior table fracture and the degree of depression or displacement.
Assessing for posterior table fracture and any displacement.
Assessing for involvement of the nasofrontal duct which drains the frontal sinus.
Looking for intracranial air, intracranial haematoma, and dural tears.
Neurosurgical involvement if there is intracranial pathology.

Management of C S F leak
Knowing that C S F rhinorrhoea indicates a posterior table fracture with dural tear.
Conservative management: head elevation, bed rest, avoidance of straining and nose blowing.
Most C S F leaks resolve within seven to ten days.
Neurosurgical involvement for persistent leaks.

Classification and surgical decision making
Knowing the management algorithm based on which tables are fractured and whether the nasofrontal duct is involved.
Isolated anterior table fracture without displacement: observation.
Displaced anterior table fracture: open reduction and fixation to restore forehead contour.
Posterior table fracture with C S F leak: neurosurgical involvement, possible cranialization of the sinus with obliteration.
Nasofrontal duct involvement: the duct must be assessed and if damaged, the sinus should be obliterated to prevent mucocele formation.

Surgical technique
For a displaced anterior table fracture: coronal approach or through the existing laceration if adequate, reduction and fixation of the fragments with miniplates.
For posterior table involvement: cranialization involves removal of the posterior table, repair of the dura, removal of the sinus mucosa, and obliteration of the nasofrontal duct with bone or fat graft, allowing the brain to expand into the former sinus space.

Post-operative care
Neurosurgical monitoring if there is intracranial involvement.
Antibiotics if there is a C S F leak.
Follow up to monitor for mucocele formation which can present months to years later.
Scar management for the coronal or forehead incision.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S assessment
Knowing that frontal sinus fractures require significant force and there is a high incidence of associated injuries including intracranial injury in up to forty percent of cases.

Clinical examination
Knowing the anatomy of the frontal sinus: the anterior table forms the forehead contour, the posterior table forms the anterior cranial fossa floor, and the nasofrontal duct drains into the middle meatus of the nose.
Knowing that the frontal sinus is typically not pneumatised until the age of twelve so these fractures are rare in young children.

Investigation and C T interpretation
Knowing that the degree of posterior table displacement determines the risk of dural tear: displacement greater than one table width is associated with a high risk of dural tear.
Knowing that intracranial air on C T scan confirms a dural tear.

Classification and surgical decision making
Knowing the concept of sinus obliteration: removing all sinus mucosa and filling the sinus with fat, bone, or hydroxyapatite to prevent mucocele formation. The nasofrontal duct is plugged to prevent communication with the nose.
Knowing the concept of sinus cranialization: removing the entire posterior table and allowing the frontal lobe dura to expand forward into the sinus space after obliterating the nasofrontal duct and removing all mucosa.
Knowing the concept of the Riedel procedure: complete removal of the anterior table, posterior table, and sinus floor with forehead collapse, used as a last resort.
Knowing the long-term risk of mucocele formation: a mucocele is a mucus-filled cyst that forms when sinus mucosa is trapped, which can present years later with a forehead swelling, proptosis, or intracranial complications.

Surgical technique
Knowing the coronal approach for frontal sinus access: a bicoronal incision behind the hairline, elevation of the scalp flap in the subgaleal plane anteriorly, transitioning to the subperiosteal plane over the supraorbital ridge.
Knowing the importance of identifying and preserving the supraorbital and supratrochlear neurovascular bundles.

Post-operative care
Knowing the complications: mucocele formation which is the most feared late complication, forehead contour irregularity, frontal bone osteomyelitis, meningitis, brain abscess, and supraorbital nerve injury.
Knowing that long-term follow up with imaging may be needed to monitor for mucocele, particularly if the sinus was obliterated.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
