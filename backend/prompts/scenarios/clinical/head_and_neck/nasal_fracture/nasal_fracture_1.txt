SCENARIO TITLE

Nasal Fracture

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 20 year old man who has been punched in the face during an altercation. He has a visibly deviated nose with swelling and bruising around both eyes. There is no active epistaxis. He has presented to the emergency department. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S assessment and exclusion of associated injuries
Following A T L S principles if the mechanism suggests significant force.
Ruling out associated facial fractures including orbital, zygomatic, and maxillary fractures.
Assessing for any signs of base of skull fracture including C S F rhinorrhoea, periorbital bruising, and Battle sign.
Controlling any active epistaxis with anterior nasal packing if present.

Clinical examination of the nose
Inspecting the nose from the front, lateral, and basal views for deviation, deformity, swelling, and asymmetry.
Palpating the nasal bones for tenderness, crepitus, and step deformity.
Assessing the nasal airway for obstruction by asking the patient to breathe through each nostril.
Performing anterior rhinoscopy to examine the septum and exclude a septal haematoma.

Exclusion of septal haematoma
Knowing that a septal haematoma is a collection of blood between the perichondrium and the septal cartilage.
Knowing that it appears as a boggy, cherry-red swelling of the septum on anterior rhinoscopy.
Knowing that an untreated septal haematoma will lead to cartilage necrosis and septal perforation or saddle nose deformity, and can become infected forming a septal abscess.
Knowing that a septal haematoma requires urgent incision and drainage under local anaesthetic followed by anterior nasal packing or a quilting suture and antibiotics.

Investigations
X-rays of the nasal bones are of limited value and are not routinely required. The diagnosis is clinical.
If there is concern about more extensive facial fractures, a C T scan of the face should be arranged.

Timing of manipulation
Knowing that the nose should be reassessed once the swelling has subsided, usually at five to seven days after the injury.
Knowing that manipulation under anaesthetic should be performed within two weeks of the injury, ideally between seven and fourteen days, before the bones begin to set in the displaced position.
If the patient presents after two weeks, a formal septorhinoplasty may be required at a later date.

Technique of manipulation under anaesthetic
Performing the procedure under general anaesthetic or local anaesthetic with sedation.
Using Walsham forceps for the nasal bones and Ash forceps for the septum to reduce the fracture.
Applying a nasal splint or plaster of Paris to maintain the reduction.
Anterior nasal packing may be used to support the reduction.

Post-procedure care and follow up
Nasal splint for one to two weeks.
Anterior nasal packing removed at twenty four to forty eight hours.
Advising the patient to avoid contact sports for six weeks.
Follow up to assess the result and nasal airway function.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

A T L S assessment and exclusion of associated injuries
Asking about alcohol or drug intake and any loss of consciousness which may indicate a head injury.
Examining the teeth for dental trauma.
Considering the medico-legal implications and documenting the injuries.

Clinical examination of the nose
Comparing with any pre-injury photographs if available to assess the baseline nasal appearance.
Noting any pre-existing deviation which the patient may attribute to the new injury.

Exclusion of septal haematoma
Knowing the technique of draining a septal haematoma: incision along the anterior margin of the cartilaginous septum, aspiration or drainage of the haematoma, and either anterior nasal packing or a quilting suture through the septum to prevent re-accumulation.
Knowing that bilateral septal haematomas can cause airway compromise in addition to cartilage necrosis.

Investigations
Knowing that nasal bone X-rays have poor sensitivity and specificity and do not change the management which is based on clinical assessment.

Timing of manipulation
Knowing that manipulation after three weeks is unlikely to be successful and the patient should be referred for formal septorhinoplasty at three to six months.
Knowing that in children the nasal bones begin to set more quickly and manipulation should ideally be performed within seven to ten days.

Technique of manipulation under anaesthetic
Knowing that the septum must be reduced as well as the nasal bones to restore the nasal airway and prevent persistent deviation.
Knowing that topical cocaine or co-phenylcaine spray provides both anaesthesia and vasoconstriction for nasal procedures.
Knowing the Cottle test: pulling on the cheek to open the internal nasal valve, which helps assess whether nasal obstruction is due to bony deviation or internal valve collapse.

Post-procedure care and follow up
Knowing the complications of nasal fracture: persistent deviation, nasal obstruction, saddle nose deformity from septal haematoma, and cosmetic dissatisfaction.
Knowing that approximately fifteen to twenty percent of patients will require a formal septorhinoplasty for a persistent deformity after manipulation.
Knowing the role of septoplasty for isolated septal deviation causing nasal obstruction.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
