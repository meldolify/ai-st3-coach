SCENARIO TITLE

Eyelid Reconstruction

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 72 year old lady who has undergone excision of a basal cell carcinoma from the middle third of her right lower eyelid. The excision has resulted in a full thickness defect involving approximately thirty percent of the lower eyelid. The margins are confirmed as clear. You have been asked to discuss the reconstructive options.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment of the defect
Describing the defect: full thickness loss of thirty percent of the lower eyelid involving skin, orbicularis oculi, tarsal plate, and conjunctiva.
Confirming clear margins before proceeding with reconstruction.
Assessing the patient's visual acuity and function of the remaining eyelid.

Goals and principles of eyelid reconstruction
Knowing the goals in order of priority: protecting the globe by maintaining corneal coverage, avoiding ectropion, preserving eyelid function including blink and tear drainage, and cosmesis.
Knowing that full thickness eyelid reconstruction requires restoration of the anterior lamella, which is skin and orbicularis, and the posterior lamella, which is tarsal plate and conjunctiva.

Reconstructive ladder for lower eyelid defects
Knowing the options based on the percentage of eyelid involved.
Up to twenty five to thirty percent: direct closure using a pentagon-shaped full thickness excision through skin, orbicularis, tarsal plate, and conjunctiva, closed in layers starting with six-oh Vicryl to the grey line and conjunctiva, then the tarsal plate and orbicularis together, and finally skin with six-oh Nylon.
Thirty to fifty percent: direct closure with a lateral canthotomy and cantholysis to gain additional length.
Up to sixty percent: Tenzel semicircular rotation flap which is a laterally based myocutaneous flap from the temple combined with a lateral canthotomy.
Other options: Tripier flap which is a bipedicled myocutaneous flap from the upper eyelid performed as a two-stage procedure.
Full eyelid: Mustarde cheek rotation flap.

Reconstructive ladder for upper eyelid defects
Knowing that upper eyelid reconstruction follows similar principles but the upper eyelid is more critical for corneal protection.
Up to thirty percent: direct closure.
Thirty to fifty percent: direct closure with lateral canthotomy.
Larger defects: Cutler-Beard flap which is a full thickness advancement flap from the lower eyelid passed behind the lower lid margin, performed as a two-stage procedure with division at six weeks.

Surgical technique
Describing the chosen technique for a thirty percent lower eyelid defect: direct closure or direct closure with lateral canthotomy.
Closing in layers: conjunctiva and tarsal plate with six-oh Vicryl, grey line alignment, orbicularis with absorbable sutures, and skin with fine non-absorbable sutures.
Ensuring meticulous alignment of the grey line to prevent notching.

Post-operative care
Lubricating eye drops to protect the cornea.
Suture removal at seven to ten days.
Follow up to assess for ectropion, entropion, or exposure keratopathy.
Oncological follow up.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment of the defect
Assessing the laxity of the remaining eyelid with a snap-back test and distraction test.
Noting the position of the punctum and assessing the lacrimal drainage system.
Ophthalmology involvement for assessment of the globe and visual function.

Goals and principles of eyelid reconstruction
Knowing that the posterior lamella must not be reconstructed with a graft if the anterior lamella is also grafted, as at least one lamella must have a vascularised flap to support the other.
Knowing that the tarsal plate provides structural rigidity to the eyelid and must be reconstructed or substituted.

Reconstructive ladder for lower eyelid defects
Knowing the Tenzel semicircular flap technique: a semicircular incision is made from the lateral canthus curving superolaterally, the flap is undermined in the subcutaneous plane and advanced medially. A lateral canthotomy and inferior cantholysis are performed to release the lateral attachment of the lower lid, allowing advancement.
Knowing that the Hughes tarsoconjunctival flap from the upper lid can provide posterior lamellar support for larger lower lid defects, with a skin graft or flap for the anterior lamella. This is a two-stage procedure with pedicle division at four to six weeks.
Knowing that the Mustarde cheek rotation flap provides skin and muscle but does not provide posterior lamellar support, which must be provided by a cartilage graft from the ear or hard palate mucosa.

Reconstructive ladder for upper eyelid defects
Knowing the Cutler-Beard technique in detail: a full thickness flap of lower eyelid skin, orbicularis, and conjunctiva is advanced under the intact lower lid margin into the upper lid defect. The flap is divided at six weeks.
Knowing that a free tarsoconjunctival graft from the contralateral upper lid can be used for posterior lamellar reconstruction.

Surgical technique
Knowing the blood supply to the eyelids: medial and lateral palpebral arteries forming a marginal and peripheral arcade.
Knowing the anatomy of the tarsal plate: approximately one centimetre height in the upper lid and four to five millimetres in the lower lid.
Knowing that corneal protection with a corneal shield or suturing the lids together temporarily may be needed during healing.

Post-operative care
Knowing the complications: ectropion, entropion, trichiasis, lagophthalmos, notching of the lid margin, exposure keratopathy, and epiphora.
Knowing that ectropion is the most common complication of lower eyelid reconstruction and may require secondary correction.
Knowing that tarsorrhaphy may be performed as a temporary measure to protect the cornea while the reconstruction heals.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
