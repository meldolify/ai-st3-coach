SCENARIO TITLE

Principles of Free Tissue Transfer

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A forty-five year old man has sustained a severe open fracture of his right tibia with a large soft tissue defect over the anteromedial aspect of the leg. The orthopaedic team have stabilised the fracture and the wound has been debrided. You have been asked to discuss your approach to soft tissue reconstruction using free tissue transfer. Please discuss the principles with the examiner.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Reconstructive Ladder:
- Direct closure, skin grafts (S S G, full thickness), local flaps, regional flaps, free flaps
- Free tissue transfer for defects not amenable to simpler options
- Lower limb: distal third defects almost always require free flap
- Godina principles: early free flap coverage (within 72 hours) gives best outcomes

Patient Assessment:
- Cardiovascular fitness for prolonged anaesthesia (6 to 12 hours)
- Smoking cessation: active smoking significantly increases failure rates
- Diabetes: optimise glycaemic control
- Anticoagulation and antiplatelet status
- Nutritional status and albumin level
- Previous vascular surgery or trauma to recipient vessels

Mathes-Nahai Classification:
- Type 1: one vascular pedicle (e.g. gastrocnemius, tensor fascia lata)
- Type 2: one dominant pedicle plus minor pedicles (e.g. gracilis)
- Type 3: two dominant pedicles (e.g. rectus abdominis, gluteus maximus)
- Type 4: segmental vascular pedicles (e.g. sartorius, tibialis anterior)
- Type 5: one dominant pedicle plus secondary segmental pedicles (e.g. latissimus dorsi, pectoralis major)

Flap Selection for Lower Limb:
- Proximal third: gastrocnemius muscle flap (local, Type 1)
- Middle third: soleus muscle flap (local) or free flap
- Distal third: free flap required (gracilis, A L T, latissimus dorsi)
- Muscle flaps traditionally preferred for infected or contaminated wounds
- Fasciocutaneous flaps increasingly used: easier monitoring, less bulk

Recipient Vessel Selection:
- C T angiography or conventional angiography to assess vessels
- Anterior tibial, posterior tibial, or peroneal artery
- Anastomosis outside the zone of injury
- End-to-side anastomosis preserves distal flow in single-vessel limbs
- Vein grafts if no suitable local recipient vessels

Post-operative Protocol:
- I T U or high dependency care for first 24 to 48 hours
- Hourly flap monitoring: colour, capillary refill, temperature, turgor, Doppler
- Keep patient warm, well perfused, adequate hydration
- Avoid vasoconstrictors and hypotension
- Limb elevation but avoid pressure on pedicle
- Anticoagulation: variable practice (aspirin, heparin, or both)

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

- Reconstructive elevator concept: choosing the optimal option rather than climbing each rung
- Perforator flaps and the evolution beyond the reconstructive ladder
- Cross-leg free flaps and flow-through configurations
- Chimeric and composite flaps for complex defects
- Supermicrosurgery for distal anastomoses (vessels less than 0.8 millimetres)
- Pre-operative vessel mapping with handheld Doppler
- Negative pressure wound therapy as bridge to definitive reconstruction
- Evidence base for timing: Godina's series showing superiority of early coverage

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
