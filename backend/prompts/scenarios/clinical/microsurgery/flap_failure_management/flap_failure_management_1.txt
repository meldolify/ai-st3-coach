SCENARIO TITLE

Management of a Failing Free Flap

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

You are called to the ward to review a forty-eight year old woman who is day one following a D I E P flap breast reconstruction. The nursing staff are concerned that the flap appears congested and has become blue-purple in colour over the last hour. Please discuss how you would assess and manage this situation.

MAIN CLINICAL DOMAINS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

- Clinical differentiation of arterial versus venous compromise
- Bedside assessment and monitoring techniques
- Ward-based salvage measures
- Indications and timing for re-exploration
- Operative salvage: thrombectomy, re-anastomosis, vein grafts
- Leech therapy and Aeromonas hydrophila prophylaxis
- Outcomes of flap salvage

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Clinical Signs of Venous Congestion:
- Blue or purple discolouration
- Brisk capillary refill with dark blood
- Increased turgor, flap feels tense and swollen
- Dark blood on needle prick test, rapid bleeding
- Doppler: arterial signal may still be present

Clinical Signs of Arterial Insufficiency:
- Pale or white flap
- Absent or sluggish capillary refill
- Decreased turgor, flap feels soft and cool
- No bleeding or minimal pale blood on needle prick
- Absent arterial Doppler signal

Immediate Ward Measures:
- Remove all dressings and sutures that may be compressing the pedicle
- Position the patient to avoid pressure or kinking of the pedicle
- Ensure the patient is warm, well hydrated, and haemodynamically stable
- Keep the patient nil by mouth in case theatre is needed
- Release any haematoma around the pedicle at the bedside if present
- Avoid vasoconstrictors

Decision to Re-explore:
- Any confirmed vascular compromise warrants urgent return to theatre
- Salvage rates highest when explored within 1 to 2 hours of onset
- Overall salvage rate approximately 50 to 75 percent
- Venous problems more common than arterial, but arterial has worse salvage rate

Operative Salvage Steps:
- Deliver the flap and inspect the pedicle along its full length
- Wash out any haematoma
- Unkink or reposition any twisted vessels
- Perform milking test to assess flow through the anastomosis
- If thrombus found: resect the thrombosed segment
- Re-anastomosis with fresh vessel ends
- Interposition vein graft if tension or insufficient vessel length
- Assess back-bleeding from the flap to confirm outflow
- Systemic heparinisation or local heparin flush

Leech Therapy:
- Indicated for venous congestion when surgical salvage alone is insufficient
- Leeches secrete hirudin (anticoagulant) promoting continued bleeding after detachment
- Apply 1 to 2 leeches to the congested area, allow to feed and detach naturally
- Monitor haemoglobin as patients can become significantly anaemic
- Must give antibiotic prophylaxis against Aeromonas hydrophila: ciprofloxacin is first line
- Continue until neovascularisation establishes venous drainage (typically 3 to 5 days)

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

- Implantable Cook Doppler for buried flap monitoring
- Indocyanine green angiography in theatre to assess flap perfusion
- Thrombolytic agents (t P A) applied locally to thrombosed anastomosis
- Dextran infusion as rheological adjunct (largely fallen out of favour)
- Aspirin and heparin protocols for free flap anticoagulation
- Risk factors for flap failure: smoking, diabetes, previous radiotherapy, hypercoagulable states
- Secondary reconstruction options if salvage fails

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
