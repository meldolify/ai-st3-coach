SCENARIO TITLE

Nerve Repair and Reconstruction

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A thirty year old man has presented to the emergency department with a laceration to his right wrist sustained from broken glass. On examination he has loss of sensation in the thumb, index, and middle fingers, and weakness of thumb opposition. You suspect a median nerve laceration at the wrist. Please discuss your assessment and management with the examiner.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Median Nerve at the Wrist:
- Lies between flexor carpi radialis and palmaris longus tendons
- Contains motor and sensory fascicles
- Motor branch to thenar muscles (A P B, opponens pollicis, superficial head of F P B, first and second lumbricals)
- Sensory to thumb, index, middle, and radial half of ring finger (palmar)
- Associated structures at risk: flexor tendons, radial and ulnar arteries, ulnar nerve

Classification:
- Seddon: neurapraxia (conduction block, full recovery), axonotmesis (axon disrupted, endoneurium intact, good recovery), neurotmesis (complete disruption, requires surgery)
- Sunderland: Grade 1 (myelin only), Grade 2 (axon disrupted, endoneurium intact), Grade 3 (endoneurium disrupted, perineurium intact), Grade 4 (perineurium disrupted, epineurium intact), Grade 5 (complete transection)
- Mackinnon Grade 6: mixed injury pattern within same nerve

Timing of Repair:
- Primary repair within 72 hours is ideal for sharp lacerations
- Delayed primary repair up to 2 to 3 weeks acceptable
- Beyond 3 weeks: nerve ends retract, scarring makes identification difficult
- Crush or avulsion injuries may benefit from delay to define zone of injury

Primary Nerve Repair Technique:
- Operating microscope at 10x to 16x magnification
- Identify nerve ends and debride to healthy fascicular pattern
- Epineurial repair: suture the outer epineurium (most common for mixed nerves)
- Use 8-0 or 9-0 nylon sutures, minimum number to achieve alignment
- Tension-free repair is critical: any tension leads to poor outcomes
- Align fascicular pattern using surface blood vessels and internal topography as landmarks
- Group fascicular repair: suture individual fascicular groups (for larger nerves or partial injuries)

Nerve Grafting:
- Indicated when a gap prevents tension-free primary repair
- Gaps greater than 2 to 3 centimetres generally require grafting
- Sural nerve: most common donor (up to 30 to 40 centimetres available)
- Medial cutaneous nerve of forearm: alternative donor
- Cable grafts: multiple lengths of graft to match cross-sectional area
- Reversed graft orientation to prevent axonal escape through branches
- Results inferior to primary repair: regenerating axons must cross two repair sites

Nerve Conduits:
- Synthetic or biological tubes bridging a nerve gap
- Suitable for gaps less than 3 centimetres in sensory or small mixed nerves
- Materials: collagen, polyglycolic acid, processed nerve allograft
- Advantages: avoids donor site morbidity, single repair site
- Limitations: less reliable for motor nerves, larger gaps, or larger diameter nerves

Expected Outcomes:
- Nerve regeneration rate approximately 1 millimetre per day (1 inch per month)
- Distal injuries recover better than proximal injuries (shorter distance to target)
- Young patients recover better than older patients
- Sharp injuries better than crush or avulsion
- Motor recovery: M3 or better in 60 to 70 percent after primary repair at wrist
- Sensory recovery: useful sensation (S3 plus) in majority after wrist level repair
- Counsel regarding cold intolerance, hypersensitivity during recovery

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

- Nerve transfers: motor nerve redundancy concept (e.g. anterior interosseous nerve to ulnar motor branch)
- End-to-side neurorrhaphy for sensory recovery
- Processed nerve allografts (e.g. Avance) as alternative to autograft for moderate gaps
- Role of intra-operative nerve stimulation to identify fascicular topography
- Fibrin glue as adjunct to reduce suture number at repair site
- Babysitter procedures for delayed motor reinnervation
- Wallerian degeneration and the role of Schwann cell tubes in guiding regeneration
- Tinels sign for tracking nerve regeneration clinically
- Sensory re-education programme in rehabilitation

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
