SCENARIO TITLE

Radial Forearm Free Flap for Intraoral Reconstruction

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A sixty year old man has undergone excision of a squamous cell carcinoma of the floor of mouth. The resultant defect involves the floor of mouth and ventral tongue mucosa. You have been asked to reconstruct this defect using a radial forearm free flap. Please discuss this case with the examiner.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Anatomy:
- Based on radial artery with venae comitantes and cephalic vein
- Fasciocutaneous flap: thin, pliable, excellent for intraoral lining
- Sensate option using lateral cutaneous nerve of forearm
- Pedicle length up to 15 to 20 centimetres
- Can include palmaris longus tendon or radius bone (osteocutaneous variant)

Allen Test:
- Essential pre-operative assessment to confirm adequate ulnar collateral circulation
- Compress both radial and ulnar arteries, patient opens and closes fist
- Release ulnar artery: hand should reperfuse within 5 to 10 seconds
- If abnormal, radial forearm flap is contraindicated on that arm
- Can supplement with pulse oximetry or Doppler assessment

Flap Harvest:
- Designed over volar forearm, proximal to wrist crease
- Tourniquet used, raise flap from ulnar to radial deep to deep fascia
- Include lateral intermuscular septum containing perforators
- Ligate radial artery distally, divide proximally for pedicle length
- Preserve superficial branch of radial nerve

Donor Site Management:
- S S G to exposed flexor tendons if primary closure not possible
- Tendon exposure: preserve paratenon during harvest for graft take
- Alternative: full thickness skin graft or dermal substitute
- Avoid wrist splinting beyond 5 to 7 days to prevent stiffness
- Donor site morbidity: aesthetically poor scar, reduced grip strength, cold intolerance

Recipient Vessels:
- Facial artery and vein or superior thyroid artery
- End-to-end anastomosis preferred for similar calibre vessels
- Vein grafts rarely needed due to long pedicle length

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

- Osteocutaneous radial forearm flap: risk of radius fracture (up to 15 percent), prophylactic plating
- Pre-laminated or pre-fabricated flap modifications
- Comparison with medial sural artery perforator flap as thin flap alternative
- Flow-through flap configuration
- Use in pharyngeal reconstruction as tubed flap
- Suprafascial dissection to improve donor site appearance
- Negative pressure wound therapy for donor site S S G

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
