SCENARIO TITLE

Microvascular Anastomosis Principles and Technique

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

You are in a clinical examination and the examiner would like to discuss the principles and technique of microvascular anastomosis. They ask you to describe how you would perform a microvascular repair, the equipment involved, and how you manage complications. Please discuss this topic with the examiner.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Equipment:
- Operating microscope with magnification range 4x to 40x (typically work at 10x to 16x)
- Microsurgical instruments: jeweller's forceps, micro needle holders, adventitia scissors, vessel dilators
- Irrigation: heparinised saline (100 units per millilitre)
- Background material: coloured rubber or plastic sheet for contrast
- Micro vessel clamps: single and double approximating clamps
- Suture: 8-0 to 10-0 nylon on taper-cut or taper needle (9-0 most common for 1 to 3 millimetre vessels)

Vessel Preparation:
- Adequate vessel length with no tension on the anastomosis
- Strip adventitia from vessel ends to prevent intimal dissection and thrombosis
- Gently dilate vessel ends with forceps or dilator
- Irrigate lumen with heparinised saline to flush clots and debris
- Ensure no intimal flaps: trim back to healthy vessel if damaged
- Size discrepancy management: cut smaller vessel obliquely, or fish-mouth larger vessel

End-to-End Arterial Anastomosis:
- Place vessel ends in approximating clamp
- Place two stay sutures 120 to 180 degrees apart on the back wall first (or front wall depending on technique)
- Triangulation technique (Carrel): 3 equidistant stay sutures, suture between each
- Alternative: back wall first, then front wall with interrupted sutures
- Full thickness bites: all layers including intima
- Sutures placed approximately one suture width apart and one width from the edge
- Typically 6 to 10 interrupted sutures for a 1 to 2 millimetre vessel
- Avoid catching the back wall when suturing the front wall
- Release clamps: venous clamp first, then arterial

End-to-Side Anastomosis:
- Indicated when preserving flow in the recipient vessel is important
- Create arteriotomy in recipient vessel with micro scissors or aortic punch
- Arteriotomy length approximately 1.5 to 3 times donor vessel diameter
- Suture donor end to recipient arteriotomy with interrupted or continuous sutures
- Particularly useful in single-vessel limbs or when size discrepancy is large

Venous Anastomosis:
- Technically more difficult: thinner walls, more fragile, collapse easily
- Same interrupted suture technique but vessel more prone to tearing
- Venous coupler device: mechanical device that everts vessel edges over pins on two rings
- Coupler advantages: faster, reliable patency rates over 95 percent, no suture needed
- Coupler suitable for veins 1 to 4 millimetres in diameter
- Always perform at least two venous anastomoses when possible for redundancy

Assessing Patency:
- Visual: pulsation in the artery distal to the anastomosis
- Milking test (strip test): empty a short segment of vessel and observe refill
- Flap perfusion: colour change, capillary refill, bleeding from the flap
- Patency test: occlude vessel distal to anastomosis, release, observe flow

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

- Supermicrosurgery for vessels less than 0.8 millimetres (lymphovenous anastomosis)
- Continuous suture technique for larger vessels (faster but less forgiving)
- Fibrin glue as adjunct to reduce suture count
- Robot-assisted microvascular anastomosis (emerging technology)
- No-touch technique: handle adventitia only, never grasp intima
- Antispasmodic agents: topical papaverine, lignocaine, or verapamil
- Systemic pharmacology: aspirin, heparin, dextran (variable evidence)
- Thrombectomy technique with Fogarty catheter for failed anastomosis
- Vein grafts: cephalic or saphenous vein interposition for vessel gaps

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
