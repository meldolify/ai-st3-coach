SCENARIO TITLE

Liposuction for Localised Adiposity

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A thirty-five year old woman has been referred to your clinic with localised fat deposits on her outer thighs and flanks that have not responded to diet and exercise. She exercises regularly and maintains a healthy diet but is frustrated by these areas which she feels are disproportionate to the rest of her body. Her B M I is twenty-four and she is otherwise fit and well.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Patient Assessment
Good candidate has localised fat deposits that are disproportionate and resistant to diet and exercise
B M I ideally in the normal range, liposuction is not a weight loss treatment
Good skin elasticity to allow skin retraction after fat removal
Realistic expectations about the outcome
Assessment for lipodystrophy or lipoedema as differential diagnoses
Psychological assessment including screening for body dysmorphic disorder

Skin Quality
Patients with good skin elasticity will have better results
Poor skin quality with striae or laxity may result in loose skin after liposuction
Older patients or those with significant weight fluctuations may have reduced skin retraction
May need to consider excisional surgery instead if skin quality is poor

Tumescent Technique
Infiltration of large volumes of dilute local anaesthetic with adrenaline into the subcutaneous fat
Klein solution: normal saline with lignocaine one milligram per kilogram and adrenaline one in one million
Provides anaesthesia, vasoconstriction, and hydrodissection of fat
Reduces blood loss compared to dry liposuction
Maximum safe lignocaine dose in tumescent is higher than standard injection at up to thirty-five milligrams per kilogram

Techniques
Suction-assisted liposuction which is the standard technique
Power-assisted liposuction with a vibrating cannula
Ultrasound-assisted liposuction such as V A S E R which emulsifies fat before aspiration
Laser-assisted liposuction
Cannula selection with smaller cannulas for superficial work and larger for debulking

Safe Volume
Maximum aspirate volume is typically five litres of total aspirate including tumescent fluid
Large-volume liposuction greater than five litres carries increased risks and may require overnight observation or general anaesthetic
Fluid replacement should be considered for large-volume cases

Consent
Contour irregularity which is the most common aesthetic complication
Asymmetry
Seroma
Haematoma
Infection
Skin necrosis from superficial liposuction
Numbness or altered sensation
Fat embolism which is rare but serious
Fluid overload
Thromboembolism
Under-correction or need for a second procedure

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of the wet, superwet, and tumescent classifications based on volume of infiltrate relative to aspirate
Discussion of V A S E R liposuction and its advantage in skin retraction
Understanding of lipoedema as a differential diagnosis and the difference from simple obesity
Knowledge of autologous fat transfer using lipoaspirate for other procedures such as fat grafting to the breast or face
Discussion of the role of compression garments and their duration of use
Understanding of the risks of combining liposuction with other procedures such as abdominoplasty
Knowledge of the safe anaesthetic environment for liposuction including the need for appropriate monitoring
Discussion of high-definition liposuction techniques for athletic patients

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
