SCENARIO TITLE

Prominent Ear Correction in a Child

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

An eight year old boy has been brought to your clinic by his mother because of prominent ears. He is being bullied at school and is becoming increasingly self-conscious. On examination he has bilateral prominent ears with a conchoscaphal angle of approximately one hundred and ten degrees and an auriculocephalic distance of twenty millimetres. The antihelical fold is poorly defined bilaterally.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Anatomy and Causes
Three main causes: underdeveloped antihelical fold which is the most common, deep or hypertrophic conchal bowl, and a combination of both
Understanding that prominent ears may also be due to a prominent lobule
Distinction from pseudo-prominent ears caused by conditions such as Stahl ear or Darwin tubercle

Normal Parameters
Normal conchoscaphal angle is approximately ninety degrees, greater than ninety degrees is considered prominent
Normal auriculocephalic distance is ten to fifteen millimetres from the helical rim, greater than fifteen to twenty millimetres is prominent
The ear reaches approximately eighty-five percent of adult size by age five to six years

Timing and Assessment
Surgery typically considered from age five onwards when the ear cartilage is firm enough and the ear is near adult size
The child should be old enough to understand and cooperate with postoperative care
Psychological readiness and the child's own desire for surgery are important
Both the child and parents should be involved in the consent process

Surgical Techniques
Mustarde sutures for creating or augmenting the antihelical fold using horizontal mattress sutures through the cartilage
Conchoscaphal sutures or conchal setback sutures to reduce auriculocephalic distance
Conchal cartilage excision for a deep conchal bowl
Posterior skin excision via a postauricular incision
Cartilage scoring techniques as an alternative to suture-only methods

Consent
Haematoma which is the most serious early complication and can cause cartilage necrosis
Infection and perichondritis
Suture extrusion or palpability
Asymmetry
Overcorrection leading to a telephone ear deformity
Undercorrection or recurrence
Altered sensation
Hypertrophic or keloid scarring

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of ear splinting in neonates within the first few weeks of life when cartilage is still mouldable
Discussion of the Furnas technique for conchal setback
Understanding of anterior scoring techniques and the rationale of Gibson's principle where cartilage bends away from the scored surface
Discussion of the role of cartilage-sparing versus cartilage-breaking techniques
Knowledge of complications specific to children including the need for a general anaesthetic
Understanding of the psychological impact of bullying and the evidence for otoplasty improving quality of life
Discussion of whether both ears should be corrected even if only one is prominent to improve symmetry
Knowledge of N H S funding criteria which often require documented psychological impact in children

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
