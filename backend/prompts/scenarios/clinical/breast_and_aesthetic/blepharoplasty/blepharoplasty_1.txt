SCENARIO TITLE

Upper Blepharoplasty for Dermatochalasis

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A sixty-five year old woman has been referred to your clinic with excess upper eyelid skin that she says is causing difficulty with her peripheral vision. She finds it particularly troublesome when driving and reading. On examination she has significant bilateral upper lid dermatochalasis with the redundant skin resting on her eyelashes. She has no history of thyroid disease or previous eye surgery.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Assessment
Distinction between dermatochalasis which is excess skin and true blepharoptosis which is a low lid margin relative to the pupil
Assessment of the margin reflex distance which is the distance from the upper lid margin to the corneal light reflex, normally three to four millimetres
Levator function assessment
Assessment for brow ptosis which may contribute to or be unmasked by blepharoplasty
Check for lagophthalmos and dry eye symptoms
Bell phenomenon to assess corneal protection
Assessment of lower lids for laxity

Functional Assessment
Visual field testing with the Humphrey or Goldmann perimeter demonstrating a restriction of the superior visual field
Testing with and without the excess skin taped up to show improvement
Documentation that the visual field deficit affects daily activities

Surgical Technique
Marking the upper lid crease which is normally eight to ten millimetres above the lash line
Pinch test to determine the amount of skin to remove ensuring the patient can still close the eye
Skin excision with or without a strip of orbicularis muscle
Conservative removal of any herniated orbital fat through the orbital septum if needed
The incision should not extend more than ten millimetres beyond the lateral canthus

Consent
Haematoma which can be sight-threatening if retrobulbar
Lagophthalmos and exposure keratopathy from over-resection
Dry eye
Asymmetry
Scarring
Under-correction requiring revision
Injury to the levator aponeurosis causing iatrogenic ptosis

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of the lacrimal gland position in the superolateral orbit and the importance of not excising it mistaking it for fat
Discussion of brow lift procedures as an adjunct or alternative including direct brow lift, endoscopic brow lift, or temporal lift
Understanding of the Asian eyelid anatomy where the supratarsal crease may be absent or low and the cultural considerations in blepharoplasty
Knowledge of lower blepharoplasty techniques including transconjunctival and subciliary approaches
Discussion of festoons versus malar bags and their management
Understanding of the retro-orbicularis oculi fat pad and its contribution to upper lid fullness
Knowledge of the emergency management of retrobulbar haematoma including lateral canthotomy and cantholysis

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
