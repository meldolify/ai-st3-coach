SCENARIO TITLE

Upper Limb Lymphoedema Following Breast Cancer Treatment

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A fifty-five year old woman has been referred to your clinic with a swollen left arm. She was treated for left-sided breast cancer three years ago with mastectomy, axillary lymph node clearance, and adjuvant radiotherapy. She first noticed the swelling about eighteen months ago and it has been gradually worsening. She finds it difficult to use the arm for daily activities and the skin is thickened. Her cancer surveillance is up to date with no evidence of recurrence.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Aetiology and Classification
Secondary lymphoedema due to axillary lymph node clearance and radiotherapy
Incidence of upper limb lymphoedema after axillary clearance is approximately twenty percent
Higher risk with combined surgery and radiotherapy
International Society of Lymphology staging: stage zero is subclinical, stage one is early with pitting oedema that reduces with elevation, stage two is established with non-pitting oedema and early skin changes, stage three is lymphostatic elephantiasis with severe skin changes

Assessment
Volume measurement comparing both limbs using circumferential tape measurements at fixed intervals
Assessment of skin changes including pitting, fibrosis, papillomatosis, and lymphangiectasia
Stemmer sign where the inability to pinch the skin at the base of the second toe or finger suggests lymphoedema
Range of movement and functional assessment
Exclusion of recurrent cancer as a cause of the swelling
Assessment of infection risk and history of cellulitis episodes

Investigations
Duplex ultrasound to exclude deep vein thrombosis
Lymphoscintigraphy which is the gold standard for confirming lymphatic dysfunction
Cross-sectional imaging C T or M R I if recurrence is suspected
Indocyanine green lymphography for mapping functional lymphatics preoperatively
Blood tests to exclude other causes of oedema

Conservative Management
Complete decongestive therapy which is the first-line treatment
Manual lymphatic drainage by a trained therapist
Multi-layer compression bandaging followed by fitted compression garments
Skin care to prevent infection
Exercise to promote lymphatic drainage
Intermittent pneumatic compression

Surgical Management
Lymphovenous anastomosis or lymphaticovenular bypass connecting functioning lymphatic vessels to small venules, most effective in early-stage disease
Vascularised lymph node transfer where lymph nodes from a donor site such as the groin or submental region are transferred as a free flap to the affected limb
Liposuction for chronic lymphoedema where the excess volume is predominantly fatty rather than fluid, requires lifelong compression garment use postoperatively
Debulking procedures such as the Charles procedure for end-stage elephantiasis which involves excision of affected tissue and skin grafting

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of the Campisi classification of lymphoedema severity
Discussion of sentinel lymph node biopsy as reducing the risk of lymphoedema compared to full axillary clearance
Understanding of the lymphatic microsurgical preventive healing approach or L Y M P H A where lymphovenous anastomosis is performed at the time of lymph node clearance
Knowledge of supermicrosurgical techniques for lymphovenous anastomosis
Discussion of the donor site options for vascularised lymph node transfer and the risk of donor site lymphoedema
Understanding of the role of indocyanine green lymphography in staging and surgical planning
Knowledge of the role of physiotherapy and patient self-management in long-term outcomes
Discussion of the psychological impact of lymphoedema and its effect on quality of life
Understanding of cellulitis prevention strategies including prophylactic antibiotics in patients with recurrent episodes

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
