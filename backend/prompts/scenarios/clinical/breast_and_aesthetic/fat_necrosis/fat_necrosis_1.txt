SCENARIO TITLE

Fat Necrosis in a Reconstructed Breast

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A forty-eight year old woman presents to your clinic with a palpable lump in her left breast eight months after a D I E P flap reconstruction following mastectomy for breast cancer. She noticed the lump four weeks ago and is anxious that the cancer may have returned. On examination there is a firm, non-tender lump approximately three centimetres in diameter in the lower pole of the reconstructed breast. There is no skin change or axillary lymphadenopathy.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Differential Diagnosis
Fat necrosis which is the most common cause of a lump in an autologous reconstruction
Local cancer recurrence which must be excluded
Oil cyst
Seroma
Scar tissue

Pathophysiology
Fat necrosis occurs due to ischaemia of the transferred fat, particularly at the periphery of the flap
More common in larger flaps and in areas furthest from the perforator blood supply
Can present as a firm lump, skin tethering, or calcification on imaging
May be indistinguishable from malignancy on clinical examination alone

Investigations
Ultrasound as the first-line investigation which may show an oil cyst or an echogenic mass
Mammography which may show coarse calcification typical of fat necrosis or suspicious microcalcifications
M R I which can help differentiate fat necrosis from recurrence, fat necrosis typically shows no enhancement on gadolinium
Core biopsy if imaging is indeterminate or there is any suspicion of malignancy
Triple assessment approach should be followed

Management
Observation and reassurance if imaging and biopsy confirm fat necrosis
Most cases of fat necrosis resolve or stabilise over time
Surgical excision if the lump is symptomatic, enlarging, or causing significant anxiety
Lipomodelling or fat grafting to correct any contour deformity after excision
Discussion at the breast M D T if there is any diagnostic uncertainty

Surveillance
Annual mammography of the reconstructed breast and the contralateral breast
Clinical examination as per the breast cancer follow-up protocol
Patient education about self-examination and awareness of new lumps

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of the imaging characteristics that distinguish fat necrosis from malignancy on each modality
Discussion of the rate of fat necrosis in D I E P flaps compared to T R A M flaps and implant reconstruction
Understanding of the role of indocyanine green angiography intraoperatively to predict areas at risk of fat necrosis
Knowledge of oil cysts as a specific type of fat necrosis and their typical imaging appearance
Discussion of the psychological impact of finding a lump in a patient with a cancer history
Understanding of the B I R A D S classification for reporting breast imaging findings
Knowledge of the role of P E T dash C T in equivocal cases
Discussion of how flap design and perforator selection can minimise fat necrosis risk
