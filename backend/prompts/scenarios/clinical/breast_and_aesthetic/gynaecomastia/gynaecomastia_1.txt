SCENARIO TITLE

Gynaecomastia in a Young Male

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A twenty-four year old man has been referred to your clinic with bilateral breast enlargement that has been present for approximately two years. He is embarrassed by the appearance and avoids removing his shirt in public. On examination there is significant bilateral glandular breast tissue with skin excess consistent with Simon grade three gynaecomastia. He is otherwise fit and well with a B M I of twenty-five.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and Aetiology
Drug history including anabolic steroids, cannabis, spironolactone, cimetidine, digoxin
Alcohol intake and liver disease
Endocrine causes including hypogonadism, hyperthyroidism, adrenal tumours
Testicular pathology including tumours producing h C G
Duration, onset, and progression of the breast enlargement
Psychological impact

Classification
Simon classification: grade one small breast enlargement without skin excess, grade two a moderate breast enlargement with skin excess, grade three severe breast enlargement resembling a female breast with significant skin excess
Distinction between true gynaecomastia with glandular tissue and pseudogynaecomastia which is fatty tissue only

Investigations
Blood tests including L F Ts, testosterone, oestradiol, h C G, prolactin, T F Ts, L H and F S H
Ultrasound of the testes to exclude testicular tumour
Mammography if there is any concern about male breast cancer

Surgical Treatment
Simon grade one and two: liposuction alone or combined with subcutaneous glandular excision through a periareolar incision
Simon grade three: glandular excision plus skin reduction, may require a Wise pattern approach or free nipple graft technique
Importance of excising the subareolar disc of glandular tissue

Consent
Haematoma
Seroma
Asymmetry
Contour irregularity or saucer deformity from over-resection
Altered nipple sensation
Recurrence
Scarring

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Knowledge of Klinefelter syndrome as an underlying cause and its association with gynaecomastia
Understanding that physiological gynaecomastia occurs in neonates, puberty, and the elderly
Discussion of the role of tamoxifen or raloxifene in early or painful gynaecomastia
Awareness that unilateral hard breast lumps in men should raise suspicion of male breast cancer
Discussion of ultrasound-assisted or power-assisted liposuction techniques
Knowledge of the risk of male breast cancer in Klinefelter syndrome
Discussion of N H S funding criteria for gynaecomastia surgery

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
