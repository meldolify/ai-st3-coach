SCENARIO TITLE

Ulnar Impaction Syndrome — Ulnar-Sided Wrist Pain

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A 35-year-old right-hand-dominant man presents with a six-month history of ulnar-sided pain in his right wrist. The pain is worse with gripping, forearm rotation, and ulnar deviation. He plays tennis regularly and the symptoms are affecting his game. On examination there is tenderness over the ulnar head and distal ulna, and the ulnar grind test is positive. He has full range of motion but pain at the extremes of ulnar deviation and forearm rotation.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Anatomy and biomechanics:
Load distribution across the wrist: approximately 80 percent radial, 20 percent ulnar in neutral variance
Each millimetre of positive ulnar variance increases ulnocarpal load by approximately 10 percent
T F C C (triangular fibrocartilage complex) acts as a cushion between the ulnar head and the lunate and triquetrum
Ulnar impaction causes degeneration of the T F C C and chondromalacia of the lunate and ulnar head

Ulnar variance:
Measured on a standard P A radiograph with forearm in neutral rotation, shoulder abducted 90 degrees, elbow flexed 90 degrees
Positive ulnar variance means the ulna is longer than the radius at the distal radioulnar joint
Can be congenital or acquired (after distal radius malunion, radial head excision, Essex-Lopresti)

Clinical assessment:
Tenderness over the ulnar head and the fovea
Ulnar grind test: axial load through the ring and little finger metacarpals with ulnar deviation
Fovea sign: tenderness on deep palpation between the ulnar styloid and the F C U tendon
Piano key sign for D R U J instability (must differentiate from D R U J pathology)
Assess forearm rotation for pain

Differential diagnosis:
T F C C tear
D R U J instability or arthritis
E C U tendinopathy or subluxation
Lunotriquetral ligament injury
Pisotriquetral arthritis
Ulnar styloid impaction

Investigations:
P A radiograph in neutral rotation to measure ulnar variance
M R I showing T F C C degeneration, lunate chondromalacia, lunate or ulnar head oedema
Wrist arthroscopy as both diagnostic and therapeutic

Conservative management:
Activity modification
Wrist splinting
Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory medication
Corticosteroid injection into the ulnocarpal joint
Hand therapy

Ulnar shortening osteotomy:
Standard surgical treatment for symptomatic ulnar impaction with positive variance
Oblique or transverse osteotomy through the ulnar diaphysis
Shorten by the amount of positive variance, typically 2 to 4 millimetres
Plate fixation with a dedicated ulnar shortening plate
Unloads the ulnocarpal joint
Complications: non-union (5 to 10 percent), plate prominence, D R U J impingement

Wafer procedure:
Arthroscopic or open resection of 2 to 4 millimetres of the distal ulnar dome
Performed through a central T F C C perforation
Advantages: avoids osteotomy, no hardware, shorter recovery
Requires a pre-existing T F C C perforation or tear
Less predictable for variance greater than 4 millimetres positive

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Palmer classification of T F C C tears: Class 1 (traumatic) and Class 2 (degenerative)
Palmer 2C and 2D lesions represent progressive ulnar impaction changes
Feldon wafer resection technique
Role of wrist arthroscopy in confirming the diagnosis and performing concomitant T F C C debridement
Dynamic ulnar variance increasing with grip (grip radiograph)
Comparison of oblique versus transverse ulnar shortening osteotomy techniques
Sauve-Kapandji procedure as a salvage option
