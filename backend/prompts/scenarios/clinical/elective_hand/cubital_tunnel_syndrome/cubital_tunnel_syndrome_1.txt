SCENARIO TITLE

Cubital Tunnel Syndrome — Ulnar Nerve Compression at the Elbow

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A 38-year-old man presents with a four-month history of numbness and tingling in his right ring and little fingers. The symptoms are worse when he leans on his elbow or holds a phone to his ear for prolonged periods. He has noticed some weakness in his grip and difficulty opening jars. On examination you note some wasting of the first dorsal interosseous muscle.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Anatomy:
Ulnar nerve passes behind the medial epicondyle in the cubital tunnel
Roof formed by Osborne's ligament between the two heads of flexor carpi ulnaris
Five potential sites of compression: arcade of Struthers, medial intermuscular septum, medial epicondyle, Osborne's ligament, deep flexor pronator aponeurosis

Clinical assessment:
Sensory loss in ring and little fingers, ulnar one and a half digits
Motor weakness of intrinsics: first dorsal interosseous, adductor pollicis, hypothenar muscles
Froment's sign positive indicating adductor pollicis weakness
Wartenberg's sign with little finger abduction from weak third palmar interosseous
Claw hand deformity more pronounced in low ulnar nerve lesions
Tinel's test at the cubital tunnel
Elbow flexion test reproducing symptoms with sustained elbow flexion

Investigations:
N C S to confirm diagnosis and localise compression to the elbow
Reduced conduction velocity across the elbow segment
E M G showing denervation changes in ulnar innervated muscles
X-ray of the elbow if bony pathology suspected
Ultrasound or M R I if space-occupying lesion suspected

Conservative management:
Activity modification avoiding prolonged elbow flexion and direct pressure
Night splinting with elbow in extension or slight flexion
Elbow pad to protect the nerve
Appropriate for mild intermittent symptoms without motor weakness

Surgical options:
Simple in situ decompression dividing Osborne's ligament
Medial epicondylectomy
Anterior transposition: subcutaneous, intramuscular, or submuscular
In situ decompression is simplest with lowest complication rate
Transposition preferred for subluxing nerve or revision surgery

Complications:
Medial antebrachial cutaneous nerve injury causing painful neuroma
Elbow stiffness
Recurrence or persistent symptoms
Flexor carpi ulnaris weakness after submuscular transposition
Haematoma and wound infection

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

McGowan grading system for severity of cubital tunnel syndrome
Ulnar paradox: higher lesions cause less clawing because F D P to ring and little is also affected
Difference between cubital tunnel syndrome and Guyon's canal compression
Endoscopic in situ decompression as a newer technique
Role of internal neurolysis, which is controversial
Recovery of intrinsic function is slow and often incomplete in severe cases

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
