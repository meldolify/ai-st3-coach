SCENARIO TITLE

Hand Osteoarthritis — Painful Stiff Fingers with Heberden's Nodes

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A 65-year-old right-hand-dominant woman is referred to your clinic with painful stiff fingers. She has noticed bony swellings at the ends of her fingers over the past few years and the pain has been gradually worsening. She has difficulty with fine tasks such as buttoning clothes and turning keys. On examination there are hard bony swellings at the D I P joints of her index, middle, and ring fingers bilaterally, with some lateral deviation of the fingertips.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Patterns of hand osteoarthritis:
D I P J most commonly affected with Heberden's nodes
P I P J affected with Bouchard's nodes
Thumb C M C joint commonly affected (covered separately)
Erosive or inflammatory osteoarthritis is a more aggressive variant at the P I P J

Clinical assessment:
Hard bony swellings at D I P J and P I P J
Reduced range of motion
Lateral angulation and instability
No synovitis, no warmth, no soft tissue swelling (unlike rheumatoid)
Mucous cysts may be present at the D I P J
Assess grip and pinch strength
Examine all hand joints systematically

Differentiating from inflammatory arthritis:
Osteoarthritis affects D I P J primarily, spares M C P J
Rheumatoid affects M C P J and P I P J, spares D I P J
Osteoarthritis: asymmetric, no systemic features
Rheumatoid: symmetric, morning stiffness greater than 30 minutes, systemic features
X-ray: osteoarthritis shows asymmetric joint space narrowing, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis, cysts
X-ray: rheumatoid shows periarticular osteopenia, symmetric narrowing, erosions

Blood tests:
Rheumatoid factor and anti-C C P to exclude rheumatoid arthritis
E S R and C R P usually normal in osteoarthritis
Consider if clinical picture is unclear

Conservative management:
Patient education and reassurance about benign nature
Hand therapy for range of motion and strengthening
Thermoplastic splints for painful joints
Topical non-steroidal anti-inflammatory gels as first line
Oral analgesia with paracetamol and oral non-steroidals
Steroid injection into symptomatic joints for temporary relief

D I P J surgical options:
D I P J fusion (arthrodesis) is the standard surgical treatment
Fuse in 0 to 10 degrees flexion, slight pronation for index finger
Headless compression screw or K-wire fixation
Mucous cyst excision with or without osteophyte debridement
D I P J arthroplasty is rarely performed

P I P J surgical options:
Silicone arthroplasty (Swanson) for low-demand patients
Surface replacement arthroplasty (pyrocarbon) for higher-demand patients
P I P J fusion in functional position: index 25 degrees, cascade increasing to 40 degrees for the little finger
Arthroplasty preserves motion but has limited lifespan
Fusion is reliable but sacrifices motion

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Erosive osteoarthritis with saw-tooth pattern on X-ray and more acute inflammatory presentation
Herbert screw technique for D I P J fusion
Comparison of pyrocarbon versus silicone P I P J arthroplasty outcomes
Role of hyaluronic acid injection which has limited evidence in small joints
Denervation procedures for painful joints as an alternative to fusion
Natural history of Heberden's nodes: painful inflammatory phase followed by painless stiff phase
