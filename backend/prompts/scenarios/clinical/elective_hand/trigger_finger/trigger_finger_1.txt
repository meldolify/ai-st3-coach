SCENARIO TITLE

Trigger Finger — Painful Clicking of the Ring Finger

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A 55-year-old right-hand-dominant woman presents with a three-month history of painful clicking in her right ring finger. She notices the finger catches when she makes a fist and she sometimes has to use her other hand to straighten it. The symptoms are worse in the morning. She has a background of type 2 diabetes. On examination there is a palpable nodule at the base of the ring finger and the finger locks in flexion on active movement.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Pathology:
Stenosing tenosynovitis at the A1 pulley
Thickening of the A1 pulley and swelling of the flexor tendon
Nodule formation on the flexor tendon that catches at the pulley
A1 pulley located at the level of the M C P J and distal palmar crease

Risk factors:
Diabetes mellitus with higher rate and multiple finger involvement
Rheumatoid arthritis
Repetitive gripping activities
Female more common than male
Age 50 to 60 years most common
Association with C T S and de Quervain's

Clinical grading (Quinnell):
Grade 0: normal
Grade 1: uneven movement
Grade 2: actively correctable locking
Grade 3: passively correctable locking
Grade 4: fixed locked finger

Conservative management:
Activity modification and splinting of M C P J in extension
Corticosteroid injection into the tendon sheath at A1 pulley level
Success rate approximately 60 to 70 percent with first injection
Maximum of two injections before considering surgery
Higher failure rate in diabetic patients
Risk of tendon rupture with repeated injections

Surgical technique:
Open A1 pulley release through a small transverse or longitudinal incision
Incise the A1 pulley longitudinally on its ulnar side
Confirm free tendon gliding and check for triggering at A2 pulley
Do not release A2 pulley as this causes bowstringing

Structures at risk:
Digital nerves, particularly the radial digital nerve of the index finger and ring finger
In the thumb the radial digital nerve crosses obliquely over the A1 pulley
Flexor tendons if incision is too deep

Trigger thumb considerations:
A1 pulley of the thumb overlies the M C P J
Radial digital nerve of the thumb is at particular risk
Oblique pulley must be preserved to prevent bowstringing

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Percutaneous A1 pulley release as an alternative to open surgery
Trigger finger in children is a distinct entity with different natural history
Locked trigger finger may mimic a flexor tendon injury
Differential diagnosis includes flexor tendon nodule, M C P J pathology, and Dupuytren's nodule
Multiple trigger fingers in a diabetic patient may indicate poor glycaemic control
Role of ultrasound-guided injection to improve accuracy

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
