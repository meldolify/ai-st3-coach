SCENARIO TITLE

Dupuytren's Disease — Progressive Ring Finger Contracture

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

You are seeing a 60-year-old gentleman in your hand clinic who has been referred by his G P with a progressive flexion contracture of his right ring finger. He reports difficulty placing his hand flat on a table and is struggling to wear gloves at work. He has a family history of similar problems and mentions he also has some thickening on the sole of his right foot.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Pathoanatomy:
Myofibroblast proliferation in palmar fascia
Pretendinous cord causes M C P J contracture
Spiral cord displaces the neurovascular bundle centrally and superficially
Central cord, lateral cord, retrovascular cord, natatory cord

Risk factors and diathesis:
Northern European and Japanese descent
Dupuytren's diathesis: early onset under 45, bilateral disease, positive family history, ectopic disease
Ectopic sites: Ledderhose plantar fibromatosis, Peyronie's disease, Garrod's knuckle pads, frozen shoulder
Other associations: diabetes, epilepsy, alcohol, smoking, manual labour controversial

Clinical assessment:
Hueston tabletop test positive when hand cannot be placed flat
Measure M C P J and P I P J contracture angles with goniometer
Assess neurovascular status distal to contracture
Examine both hands and feet for bilateral or ectopic disease

Indications for surgery:
M C P J contracture greater than 30 to 40 degrees
Any P I P J contracture
Functional impairment
Rapid progression

Non-operative options:
Observation for mild disease
Collagenase injection with Clostridium histolyticum, inject into cord, manipulate at 24 hours
Needle fasciotomy as percutaneous division of cord, office procedure
Both have higher recurrence than surgery

Surgical options:
Limited or selective fasciectomy to excise diseased fascia as standard surgical treatment
Dermofasciectomy to excise skin and fascia with full thickness skin graft for recurrent or diathesis patients
Amputation rarely for severe recurrent P I P J contracture with stiff functionless finger

P I P J versus M C P J correction:
M C P J corrects well surgically with low recurrence
P I P J contracture harder to correct with higher recurrence and joint capsule contribution
Must distinguish cord contracture from capsular contracture
Checkrein ligament release and accessory collateral release for P I P J

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

McCash open palm technique to reduce haematoma and flap complications
Z-plasty or Bruner incision design for skin closure
Risk of digital nerve injury highest with spiral cord displacing neurovascular bundle
Skeletal traction and gradual correction for severe P I P J contracture
Disease-free interval concept as surgery is not curative and recurrence is the norm over time
Flatt classification of recurrence versus extension versus disease elsewhere
Night extension splinting post-operatively for 3 to 6 months

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
