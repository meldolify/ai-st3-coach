SCENARIO TITLE

De Quervain's Tenosynovitis — Radial Wrist Pain in a New Mother

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A 35-year-old woman presents with a three-month history of pain on the radial side of her right wrist. She is a new mother and the symptoms started shortly after the birth of her first child. The pain is worse when she lifts and holds her baby. On examination there is tenderness and swelling over the radial styloid, and her Finkelstein test is positive.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Anatomy and pathology:
First dorsal compartment contains abductor pollicis longus and extensor pollicis brevis
Stenosing tenosynovitis with thickening of the extensor retinaculum
Not a true inflammatory condition but rather myxoid degeneration

Risk factors:
New mothers from repetitive lifting of the baby
Repetitive thumb use and gripping
Female to male ratio approximately 6 to 1
Peak age 30 to 50 years

Clinical assessment:
Tenderness and swelling over the radial styloid at the first dorsal compartment
Finkelstein's test: patient makes a fist with thumb inside fingers, examiner ulnar deviates the wrist
Eichhoff's test is the patient actively performing ulnar deviation which is less specific
Must distinguish from C M C joint arthritis, scaphoid pathology, and intersection syndrome

Conservative management:
Thumb spica splint for 4 to 6 weeks
Activity modification
Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory medication
Corticosteroid injection into the first dorsal compartment sheath
Injection success rate approximately 70 to 80 percent
May need repeat injection once

Surgical technique:
Longitudinal or transverse incision over the first dorsal compartment
Identify and protect branches of the superficial radial nerve
Open the first dorsal compartment sheath longitudinally
Must identify and release any separate sub-compartment for E P B
Confirm free tendon gliding of both A P L and E P B

Anatomical variations:
Separate sub-compartment for E P B present in up to 30 percent of patients
Multiple slips of A P L, sometimes 3 to 5 separate tendons
Failure to release the E P B sub-compartment is the most common cause of surgical failure

Complications:
Superficial radial nerve injury causing numbness or painful neuroma
Tendon subluxation if too much retinaculum is excised
Scar tenderness
Recurrence if E P B sub-compartment not released

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Intersection syndrome as a differential affecting the second dorsal compartment
Wartenberg syndrome with compression of the superficial radial nerve as a differential
Ultrasound can identify the septum between A P L and E P B compartments pre-operatively
Use of local anaesthetic with steroid injection as both diagnostic and therapeutic
Role of ultrasound-guided injection to ensure delivery into the correct compartment
Relationship to lateral epicondylitis and other overuse tendinopathies

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
