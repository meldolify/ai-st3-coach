SCENARIO TITLE

Mucous Cyst — D I P J Swelling with Nail Ridging

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A 60-year-old woman presents with a small translucent swelling over the D I P joint of her right index finger that has been present for about four months. She has noticed a longitudinal ridge developing in the nail of the same finger. The swelling is occasionally tender and she is worried it might burst. On examination there is a firm, transilluminant, 8 millimetre swelling on the dorsal aspect of the D I P J, a longitudinal nail groove, and underlying bony Heberden's nodes.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Pathology:
Mucous cyst is a ganglion arising from the D I P J
Strongly associated with underlying D I P J osteoarthritis and osteophytes
Cyst communicates with the joint via a pedicle from the dorsal capsule
Contains clear viscous mucin
Not a true cyst as it lacks a synovial lining

Nail changes:
Cyst compresses the germinal matrix of the nail
Causes longitudinal nail grooving or ridging
Nail changes may improve after cyst excision if germinal matrix is not permanently damaged
Nail ridging location corresponds to the position of the cyst

Clinical assessment:
Translucent or skin-coloured swelling on dorsum of D I P J
Transillumination positive
Usually dorsal and to one side of the extensor tendon
Palpate for underlying Heberden's nodes
Assess D I P J range of motion
Examine the nail for grooves and ridges

Risks of non-treatment:
Spontaneous rupture creating a communication between the joint and the skin surface
Risk of septic arthritis through the open communication
Skin thinning over the cyst making it vulnerable to trauma
Persistent nail deformity

Investigation:
X-ray of the affected finger showing D I P J osteoarthritis
Osteophytes, joint space narrowing, subchondral sclerosis
No further imaging usually required

Surgical technique:
Excise the cyst with its pedicle back to the joint capsule
Excise the underlying dorsal osteophyte as this reduces recurrence
Take care to preserve the extensor tendon terminal slip insertion
Skin may be thinned and atrophic over the cyst

Managing the skin defect:
If skin is healthy, direct closure with or without local rotation flap
If skin is very thin, excise the attenuated skin with the cyst
Rotation flap from adjacent skin to cover the defect
Full thickness skin graft as a last resort
Do not close under tension

Outcomes:
Recurrence rate low if osteophyte is excised, approximately 5 percent
Nail ridging improves in most patients over 4 to 6 months
D I P J stiffness may persist as it relates to the underlying arthritis

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Distinction between mucous cyst and myxoid cyst terminology
Role of repeated aspiration and pressure dressing as non-operative management
Injection of steroid into the cyst is generally not recommended
Use of the Atasoy flap or other local flaps for skin coverage
Concurrent D I P J fusion if the joint is severely arthritic and painful
Differential diagnosis includes epidermoid cyst, giant cell tumour, glomus tumour
Risk of extensor tendon damage during surgery

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
