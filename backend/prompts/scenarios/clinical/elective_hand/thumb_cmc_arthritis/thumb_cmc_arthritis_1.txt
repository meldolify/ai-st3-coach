SCENARIO TITLE

Thumb C M C Joint Arthritis — Basal Thumb Pain

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A 62-year-old right-hand-dominant woman presents with a two-year history of worsening pain at the base of her right thumb. The pain is aggravated by gripping, pinching, and opening jars. She has noticed a bony prominence developing at the base of her thumb and her grip strength has decreased. On examination there is squaring of the thumb base, tenderness over the C M C joint, and a positive grind test.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Anatomy:
Thumb C M C joint is a biconcave saddle joint
Permits flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, opposition, and circumduction
Stability provided by the anterior oblique ligament (beak ligament) and dorsoradial ligament
Most common site of upper limb osteoarthritis after the D I P J

Risk factors:
Female more common than male, ratio approximately 6 to 1
Post-menopausal women
Ligament laxity leading to joint instability and secondary arthritis
Previous Bennett's fracture or other trauma

Clinical assessment:
Squaring of the thumb base from dorsal subluxation and osteophyte formation
Grind test: axial load with rotation reproducing pain and crepitus
Assess thumb opposition, pinch strength, and grip strength
Hyperextension at the M C P J as a compensatory deformity
Shoulder sign from prominent metacarpal base subluxation
Examine for concurrent C T S and trigger thumb

Eaton-Littler classification:
Stage 1: normal articular surfaces, widened joint space from synovitis
Stage 2: joint space narrowing, osteophytes under 2 millimetres, slight subluxation
Stage 3: significant joint space loss, large osteophytes, subluxation greater than one third
Stage 4: scaphotrapezial joint also involved (pantrapezial arthritis)

Conservative management:
Thumb spica splint to rest the C M C joint
Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory medication
Activity modification avoiding pinching and gripping
Corticosteroid injection into the C M C joint for pain relief
Hand therapy to strengthen thenar muscles

Surgical options:
Trapeziectomy alone with good evidence of pain relief and patient satisfaction
Trapeziectomy with ligament reconstruction and tendon interposition using F C R or A P L
Trapeziectomy with suture button suspension arthroplasty
Joint replacement with silicone or pyrocarbon implant for younger patients (less proven)
Arthrodesis for young active patients with Stage 2 or 3 but reduces mobility
M C P J fusion if concurrent M C P J hyperextension greater than 30 degrees

Post-operative rehabilitation:
Thumb spica cast for 4 to 6 weeks after trapeziectomy
Graduated hand therapy from 6 weeks
Full recovery 3 to 6 months
Proximal migration of the metacarpal is expected but does not correlate with outcome

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Burton and Pellegrini L R T I technique using a strip of F C R
Suture button suspensionplasty (TightRope) as a newer technique
No consistent evidence that L R T I is superior to simple trapeziectomy alone
Role of arthroscopy in early-stage disease for debridement and thermal shrinkage
Denervation of the C M C joint as an alternative for early disease
Zancolli lasso procedure for M C P J hyperextension correction

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
