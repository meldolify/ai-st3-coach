SCENARIO TITLE

Madelung's Deformity — Bilateral Wrist Deformity in an Adolescent

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A 16-year-old girl is referred by her G P with visible deformity of both wrists that has been gradually developing over the past two years. She complains of bilateral wrist pain, reduced grip strength, and limited forearm rotation. She is self-conscious about the appearance of her wrists. On examination there is increased volar tilt of the distal radius bilaterally, prominent dorsal subluxation of the ulnar head, and reduced forearm supination.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Aetiology:
Abnormal tethering of the volar-ulnar distal radial physis by Vickers ligament
Vickers ligament is an anomalous ligament from the volar-ulnar radius to the lunate
Premature closure of the volar-ulnar portion of the distal radial physis
Ulnar and volar part of the radius stops growing while dorsal and radial part continues
Results in increased volar tilt, increased radial inclination, and relative ulnar overgrowth

Associations:
Most commonly associated with Leri-Weill dyschondrosteosis
S H O X gene mutation (short stature homeobox) on the pseudoautosomal region of the X chromosome
Autosomal dominant inheritance
Associated with short stature and mesomelic limb shortening
Can be idiopathic or associated with Turner syndrome, multiple hereditary exostoses

Clinical features:
Increased volar tilt of the distal radius giving a dinner fork deformity in reverse
Dorsal prominence of the distal ulna from relative ulnar overgrowth
Reduced forearm rotation particularly supination
Bayonet deformity of the wrist
Bilateral in most cases
Pain and weakness with gripping

Radiological features:
Increased volar tilt of the distal radial articular surface
Increased radial inclination
V-shaped proximal carpal row wedged between the radius and ulna
Ulnar and volar displacement of the lunate
Dorsal subluxation of the distal ulna (positive ulnar variance)
Premature closure of the ulnar portion of the distal radial physis
Lucency at the ulnar-volar corner of the radius where Vickers ligament inserts

Conservative management:
Observation if asymptomatic
Activity modification and analgesia for mild symptoms
Splinting for symptom relief
Many patients function well despite the deformity

Surgical options in the skeletally immature:
Vickers ligament release and physiolysis (excision of the bony bar and interposition of fat)
Best performed while significant growth remains (early adolescence)
Aims to allow more normal growth of the radius
May prevent progression but may not correct established deformity

Surgical options in the skeletally mature:
Dome or opening wedge corrective osteotomy of the distal radius to correct volar tilt
Ulnar shortening osteotomy or Darrach procedure for the prominent ulnar head
Sauve-Kapandji procedure to address D R U J incongruity while preserving forearm rotation
Combined radial osteotomy and ulnar procedure often needed

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Reverse Madelung deformity with dorsal tilt (seen in gymnasts from repetitive loading)
Genetic counselling for Leri-Weill dyschondrosteosis and family screening
Role of M R I in identifying Vickers ligament pre-operatively
Three-dimensional C T planning for corrective osteotomy
Patient-specific cutting guides for precise osteotomy
Comparison of different ulnar procedures: Darrach, Sauve-Kapandji, ulnar shortening
Long-term natural history showing many patients manage well without surgery
