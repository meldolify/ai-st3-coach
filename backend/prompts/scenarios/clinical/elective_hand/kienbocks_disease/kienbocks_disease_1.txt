SCENARIO TITLE

Kienbock's Disease — Lunate Avascular Necrosis

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A 30-year-old right-hand-dominant man presents with a six-month history of dorsal wrist pain and stiffness in his dominant hand. He works as a carpenter and the symptoms are affecting his ability to work. On examination there is tenderness over the dorsal lunate, reduced wrist extension, and weak grip. X-rays show increased density and flattening of the lunate consistent with avascular necrosis.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Pathology and aetiology:
Avascular necrosis of the lunate
Aetiology is multifactorial and not fully understood
Negative ulnar variance (short ulna) concentrates load through the lunate
Anatomical variation in lunate blood supply with some having a single palmar vessel
Repetitive trauma and microtrauma may contribute
Young adult males most commonly affected

Clinical assessment:
Dorsal wrist tenderness localised to the lunate
Reduced wrist range of motion particularly extension
Weak grip strength
Swelling over the dorsum of the wrist
Must assess ulnar variance on a standard P A wrist radiograph with neutral forearm rotation

Lichtman classification:
Stage 1: normal X-ray, M R I shows decreased T1 signal in the lunate
Stage 2: increased lunate density (sclerosis) on X-ray, no collapse
Stage 3A: lunate collapse but carpal alignment preserved, no scaphoid rotation
Stage 3B: lunate collapse with fixed scaphoid rotation (increased scapholunate angle)
Stage 4: generalised degenerative arthritis

Role of ulnar variance:
Negative ulnar variance increases load on the radiolunate articulation
Measured on standard P A radiograph with forearm in neutral rotation
Guides surgical decision-making: negative variance favours radial shortening or ulnar lengthening
Neutral or positive variance favours other unloading procedures

Stage-based management:
Stage 1: immobilisation and observation, or revascularisation procedures
Stage 2: unloading procedures — radial shortening osteotomy if ulnar negative, capitate shortening if ulnar neutral
Stage 3A: radial shortening osteotomy, capitate shortening, or S T T fusion (scaphotrapeziotrapezoid)
Stage 3B: scaphocapitate fusion, proximal row carpectomy
Stage 4: proximal row carpectomy or total wrist fusion

Radial shortening osteotomy:
Standard procedure for Stages 2 and 3A with negative ulnar variance
Shorten the radius by 2 to 3 millimetres to equalise variance
Plate fixation of the osteotomy
Reduces load on the lunate and may allow revascularisation
Alternative is ulnar lengthening osteotomy but higher complication rate

Salvage procedures:
Proximal row carpectomy removes the entire proximal carpal row
Requires intact capitate articular surface and lunate fossa
Wrist fusion is the final salvage option
Provides pain relief at the cost of all wrist motion

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Vascularised bone graft from the distal radius (4 plus 5 E C A pedicled graft) for early stages
Core decompression of the lunate as a minimally invasive option for Stage 1
Temporary scaphocapitate pinning to unload the lunate
Comparison with Preiser's disease (scaphoid avascular necrosis)
Role of M R I in monitoring revascularisation after unloading procedures
Lunate prosthetic replacement (historical, largely abandoned)
Graner procedure (capitate transfer into lunate fossa)

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
