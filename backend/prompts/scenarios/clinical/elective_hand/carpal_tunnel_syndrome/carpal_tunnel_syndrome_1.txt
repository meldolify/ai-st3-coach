SCENARIO TITLE

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome — Nocturnal Numbness and Tingling

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

A 45-year-old right-hand-dominant woman presents to your clinic with a six-month history of numbness and tingling in her right thumb, index, and middle fingers. The symptoms are worse at night and she frequently wakes up shaking her hand to relieve them. She works as a secretary and is finding it increasingly difficult to type. She has noticed she has been dropping things more frequently.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Anatomy:
Carpal tunnel bounded by carpal bones and transverse carpal ligament
Contains median nerve and nine flexor tendons
Median nerve most superficial structure beneath the ligament
Palmar cutaneous branch arises proximal to the tunnel and passes superficially

Aetiology and associations:
Idiopathic most common
Pregnancy, hypothyroidism, diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, acromegaly
Space-occupying lesions such as ganglion or lipoma
Wrist fractures particularly distal radius

Clinical assessment:
Phalen's test with wrist flexion reproducing symptoms within 60 seconds
Tinel's test tapping over the carpal tunnel
Durkan's test with direct compression over the carpal tunnel
Assess thenar muscle bulk for wasting
Semmes-Weinstein monofilament testing for sensory threshold
Two-point discrimination testing

Investigations:
N C S for diagnosis, grading, and localisation
Mild: prolonged sensory latency only
Moderate: prolonged sensory and motor latency
Severe: absent sensory response with prolonged motor latency or denervation
Ultrasound can show increased cross-sectional area of the median nerve

Conservative management:
Night splinting in neutral wrist position for mild symptoms
Activity modification
Corticosteroid injection into the carpal tunnel, maximum of two injections
Injection useful as diagnostic and therapeutic tool

Surgical management:
Open carpal tunnel release through longitudinal incision ulnar to thenar crease
Divide transverse carpal ligament under direct vision
Endoscopic release has quicker return to work but similar long-term outcomes
Protect palmar cutaneous branch, recurrent motor branch, and superficial palmar arch

Complications:
Pillar pain is the most common post-operative complaint
Incomplete release requiring revision
Nerve injury particularly recurrent motor branch
Scar tenderness
Grip weakness in the early post-operative period

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recurrent motor branch anatomical variations: extraligamentous, subligamentous, transligamentous
Martin-Gruber anastomosis as a source of atypical findings on N C S
Thenar atrophy as a late sign indicating severe and potentially irreversible damage
Role of ultrasound-guided injection
Mini-open technique as a compromise between open and endoscopic
Double crush syndrome with concurrent cervical radiculopathy

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
