SCENARIO TITLE

Nail Bed Injury

CASE INTRODUCTION

This is a 7 year old child who has been brought to the emergency department after trapping his right ring finger in a door at home about two hours ago. The finger is swollen, bruised with a lcaeration extending under the nail plate. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Establishing the mechanism of injury: crush injury from a door in a 7 year old child.
Asking about hand dominance, vaccination history and medical hisotory any allergies.

Examination of the injured finger
Examining the finger for swelling, deformity, and subungual haematoma.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the finger including capillary refill and sensation.
Assessing flexor and extensor tendon function if the child is cooperative.
Examining the nail plate for disruption and the nail fold for lacerations.
Assessing the viability of the distal skin.

Investigations
Requesting an X-ray of the finger to assess for an underlying distal phalanx fracture.
Knowing that a nail bed injury with a distal phalanx fracture is classified as an open fracture.

Timing of surgery: in the next available trauma list.

Surgical management and nail bed repair
The surgery will be under general anaesthesia
Removing the nail plate carefully using blunt dissection to expose the nail bed.
Rule of Seymour fracture
Washout the distal phalanx fracture
Repairing the nail bed laceration under magnification using fine absorbable sutures such as six-oh or seven-oh Vicryl Rapide.
The nail bed repair acts as indirect reduction and stabilisation of the tuft fracture.

Post-operative care
Appropriate dressings.
Analgesia and wound care advice to the parents.
Follow up to monitor healing.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and mechanism of injury
Considering the possibility of non-accidental injury, particularly in young children with inconsistent history or delay in presentation.
Asking about the child's general health and any safeguarding concerns.

Examination of the injured finger
Examining the adjacent fingers for associated injuries.
Assessing the nail fold for germinal matrix injury which may affect future nail growth.

Investigations
Knowing that an A P and lateral X-ray should be requested.
Knowing that a Seymour fracture is a juxta-epiphyseal fracture of the distal phalanx in a child with nail bed injury and is effectively an open physeal fracture requiring urgent washout and fixation.

Diagnosis and classification
Knowing the different types of nail bed injury: simple laceration, stellate laceration, crush injury, and avulsion.
Knowing that germinal matrix injury carries a worse prognosis for nail regrowth.

Surgical management and nail bed repair
Describing the technique of nail plate removal using fine scissors or a freer elevator inserted between the nail plate and the nail bed.
Knowing that the nail bed should be inspected under loupe magnification or an operating microscope for accurate repair.
Knowing that sterile foil from a suture packet can be used as a nail plate substitute if the original nail is too damaged to replace.
Knowing that K-wire fixation of the distal phalanx may be needed for unstable fractures.
Managing a Seymour fracture with washout, reduction, nail bed repair, and antibiotics.

Post-operative care
Advising the parents that it takes approximately three to four months for a new nail to grow.
Knowing the potential complications including nail ridging, split nail, non-adherent nail, and hook nail deformity.
Advising on wound care and signs of infection to watch for.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
