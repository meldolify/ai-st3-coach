SCENARIO TITLE

Compartment Syndrome

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

You are on call and have been asked to urgently review a 30 year old man on the orthopaedic ward. He had a closed tibial shaft fracture reduced and placed in a plaster cast six hours ago. The nurses are concerned because his pain is worsening despite increasing doses of morphine. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of the emergency
Recognising that compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency requiring immediate fasciotomy to prevent irreversible muscle and nerve ischaemia.
Knowing that delay in treatment leads to Volkmann ischaemic contracture in the upper limb or irreversible tissue necrosis in the lower limb with potential loss of limb.

Clinical features and diagnosis
Knowing the classic features: pain out of proportion to the injury, pain on passive stretch of the muscles in the affected compartment, tense swelling of the compartment, paraesthesia, and late signs include pallor, paralysis, and pulselessness.
Knowing that compartment syndrome is primarily a clinical diagnosis and treatment should not be delayed for compartment pressure measurements.
Knowing that the absence of a palpable pulse is a late sign and the presence of a pulse does not exclude compartment syndrome.

Immediate management
Removing all circumferential dressings, casts, and bandages immediately.
Elevating the limb to heart level, not above, to maintain perfusion pressure.
Giving analgesia but being suspicious if pain is out of proportion.
Informing the consultant immediately.

Compartment pressure measurement
Knowing that compartment pressures can be measured using a pressure monitor if the diagnosis is clinically uncertain, particularly in the unconscious or sedated patient.
Knowing the Whitesides criteria: fasciotomy is indicated when the compartment pressure rises to within ten to thirty millimetres of mercury of the diastolic blood pressure, or when the absolute compartment pressure exceeds thirty millimetres of mercury.

Fasciotomy technique
Knowing the compartments of the lower leg: anterior, lateral, superficial posterior, and deep posterior.
Knowing that all four compartments must be released.
Performing a two-incision four-compartment fasciotomy: an anterolateral incision to release the anterior and lateral compartments, and a posteromedial incision to release the superficial and deep posterior compartments.
The anterolateral incision is made two finger breadths lateral to the tibial crest.
The posteromedial incision is made two finger breadths posterior to the posteromedial border of the tibia.
Knowing to avoid the saphenous vein and nerve with the posteromedial incision.
Incisions should extend the full length of the compartment.
The fascia is opened along the full length of the incision.

Post-operative care and wound management
Leaving the fasciotomy wounds open initially.
Planning a return to theatre at forty eight hours for assessment and delayed primary closure, split thickness skin graft, or negative pressure wound therapy.
Monitoring the neurovascular status post-operatively.
Assessing muscle viability at second look: viable muscle should be pink, contractile, and bleed when cut.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of the emergency
Knowing the common causes of compartment syndrome: fractures particularly tibial shaft, crush injuries, tight casts or dressings, burns, high pressure injection injuries, electrical injuries, reperfusion after vascular repair, and anticoagulant therapy with compartment haematoma.
Knowing that compartment syndrome can also occur in the forearm, hand, thigh, foot, and abdomen.

Clinical features and diagnosis
Knowing that in electrical burn injuries the usual clinical symptoms are unreliable because sensory and motor nerve injury may be part of the initial injury, and compartment pressure measurement is essential.
Knowing that in the unconscious or intubated patient, clinical assessment is unreliable and pressure monitoring should be used.

Immediate management
Knowing that opioid analgesia may mask the symptoms and any escalating analgesia requirement should raise suspicion.
Knowing that fasciotomy should be performed as an emergency without waiting for further investigations.

Compartment pressure measurement
Knowing the different methods of pressure measurement including the Stryker I C P monitor and needle manometry.
Knowing the concept of delta pressure: diastolic blood pressure minus compartment pressure. A delta pressure of less than thirty millimetres of mercury is an indication for fasciotomy.

Fasciotomy technique
Knowing the compartments of the forearm: volar superficial, volar deep, dorsal, and mobile wad. Forearm fasciotomy uses a volar curvilinear incision and a separate dorsal incision if needed, with release of the carpal tunnel.
Knowing the compartments of the hand: thenar, hypothenar, adductor, and four dorsal and three volar interosseous compartments. Hand fasciotomy uses dorsal incisions over the second and fourth metacarpals.
Knowing to identify and protect named nerves during fasciotomy: the common peroneal nerve at the fibular head in the lower leg, and the ulnar nerve at the medial epicondyle in the forearm.
Knowing that non-viable muscle at second look should be debrided to prevent rhabdomyolysis, sepsis, and myoglobinuric renal failure.

Post-operative care and wound management
Knowing the systemic complications of compartment syndrome including rhabdomyolysis, hyperkalaemia, metabolic acidosis, and acute kidney injury from myoglobinuria.
Knowing the management of rhabdomyolysis: aggressive intravenous fluid resuscitation, monitoring urine output aiming for greater than one millilitre per kilogram per hour, monitoring serum creatine kinase and potassium, and alkalinisation of the urine with sodium bicarbonate if myoglobinuria is present.
Knowing the medico-legal significance of compartment syndrome and the importance of thorough documentation.
Knowing that a delayed or missed compartment syndrome may result in Volkmann ischaemic contracture in the upper limb, characterised by flexion contracture of the wrist and fingers with intrinsic muscle atrophy.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
