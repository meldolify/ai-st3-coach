SCENARIO TITLE

Extravasation Injury

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

You are on call and have been asked to review a neonate on the neonatal unit who has developed swelling and discolouration of the dorsum of the right hand following an intravenous infusion. The nurse noticed the swelling approximately thirty minutes ago and stopped the infusion immediately. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Initial first aid measures
Knowing that the infusion should be stopped immediately but the cannula left in situ.
Aspirating as much of the substance as possible through the cannula before removing it.
Do not apply pressure to the site.
Remove all splints and tapes.
Elevate the limb.
Take clinical photography.

History including substance identification
Establishing the substance that was extravasated and its properties: whether it is a vesicant, vasoconstrictor, cytotoxic, or calcium-containing.
Asking about the estimated volume of extravasation.
Asking about the timing of the extravasation.
Contacting pharmacy to get more information about the agent and whether an antidote is available.
In addition to the clinical aspects, recognising the duty of candour and ensuring an incident report is completed.

Examination and assessment of skin viability
Performing a rapid A to E assessment as some extravasated substances may have systemic effects.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the affected limb.
Assessing the skin viability including colour, capillary refill, and any blistering or mottling.
Ruling out compartment syndrome.

Assessment for compartment syndrome
Knowing the signs of compartment syndrome: pain out of proportion, pain on passive stretch, and tense swelling.
Knowing that compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency requiring immediate fasciotomy.

Management based on substance properties
Knowing the two main management strategies depending on the substance: dilute and disperse, or localise and neutralise.
Applying warm or cold compresses depending on the substance. Most substances require cold compresses for fifteen to twenty minutes four times a day. Non D N A binding vesicants such as calcium, vincristine, and dacarbazine require warm compresses.
Administering a specific antidote if available. For vasoconstrictor agents such as dopamine, adrenaline, and noradrenaline, phentolamine is the antidote. For hyperosmolar and acid or alkali solutions, irrigation is indicated.
Strict hand elevation and regular monitoring to see how the skin will declare.

Duty of candour and incident reporting
Knowing the legal and professional obligation to inform the patient or their family of the incident.
Completing a formal clinical incident report.

Reconstruction of any resultant defect
Knowing that reconstruction of any resultant tissue loss should be performed once the wound has declared.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Initial first aid measures
Knowing that dilution with isotonic saline through the cannula before removal may limit the damage.
Knowing that the site should be marked for monitoring.

History including substance identification
Knowing the classification of extravasated substances: irritants cause local inflammation, vesicants cause blistering and tissue necrosis which may be D N A binding or non D N A binding, vasoconstrictors cause ischaemia, and hyperosmolar solutions cause osmotic tissue injury.
Knowing the specific risks of common substances encountered in neonates including total parenteral nutrition, calcium, potassium, and sodium bicarbonate.

Examination and assessment of skin viability
Knowing that blistering indicates at least partial thickness skin injury.
Knowing that mottling or darkening of the skin suggests deeper tissue damage and possible full thickness necrosis.

Management based on substance properties
Knowing the Goult flush-out technique: under aseptic technique and local anaesthesia, four small puncture holes are made around the affected area using a fine scalpel or large needle. Hyaluronidase five hundred to one thousand units is infiltrated through the puncture holes into the affected area to help disperse the substance. A cannula is then inserted through one of the puncture holes and the subcutaneous tissues are irrigated with aliquots of three to five millilitres of normal saline until the overlying skin improves, up to a maximum of fifty millilitres. Fluid should extrude through the other puncture holes.
Knowing that the Goult technique should be considered after discussion with the consultant, particularly for dangerous substances or large volume extravasations.
Knowing that early intervention with the Goult technique produces better outcomes than delayed treatment.

Reconstruction of any resultant defect
Knowing the reconstructive options for tissue loss following extravasation including secondary intention healing for small defects, skin grafting, and local flap coverage for larger defects.
Knowing that neonatal skin has excellent healing capacity but contractures and scarring may require secondary procedures.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
