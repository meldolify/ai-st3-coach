SCENARIO TITLE

Bite Wounds

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 28 year old right hand dominant man who has presented to the emergency department with a small wound over the metacarpophalangeal joint of his right middle finger. He says he cut it on a fence but the wound looks suspicious for a human bite injury. The finger is swollen and there is limited range of movement. The injury occurred approximately twelve hours ago. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of a fight bite injury
Recognising that a wound over the M C P joint is a fight bite until proven otherwise, regardless of the history given.
Knowing that fight bite injuries carry a high risk of septic arthritis due to inoculation of oral flora into the M C P joint.

History and examination
Taking a careful history noting the exact mechanism, timing, and any treatment given.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, tetanus status, allergies, and H I V and hepatitis B risk.
Examining the wound with the M C P joint flexed to determine whether the wound communicates with the joint. The skin wound and the tendon injury are at different levels when the fist is clenched versus extended.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the finger.
Testing extensor tendon function.
Assessing for signs of established infection including erythema, warmth, purulent discharge, and lymphangitis.

Assessment for joint involvement and tendon injury
Knowing that the extensor tendon overlying the M C P joint may be lacerated.
Knowing that the M C P joint capsule may be breached allowing bacterial contamination of the joint.
Knowing the importance of examining for joint involvement by testing the range of motion and assessing for joint effusion.

Investigations
X-ray of the hand to look for a tooth fragment in the wound, fracture, gas in the soft tissues, or joint involvement.
Routine bloods including inflammatory markers.
Wound swab for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity.

Antibiotic therapy
Starting intravenous antibiotics covering oral flora. Co-amoxiclav is the first line.
Knowing that Eikenella corrodens is the typical culprit organism in human bite injuries.
Updating tetanus prophylaxis.

Surgical management with joint washout
Knowing that any fight bite injury with suspected or confirmed joint involvement requires formal surgical exploration and joint washout.
Performing the procedure under general or regional anaesthesia with an arm tourniquet.
Extending the wound for adequate exposure.
Thorough debridement of any devitalised tissue.
Opening and irrigating the M C P joint with copious saline.
Taking tissue samples for microbiology.
Leaving the wound open. The wound must not be closed primarily due to the high risk of infection.
Loose dressing and splinting in the position of safe immobilisation.

Post-operative care
Continued intravenous antibiotics adjusted based on culture results.
Strict hand elevation.
Planned second look at forty eight hours.
Hand therapy once the wound is healing.
Counselling about blood-borne virus risk and arranging appropriate testing.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of a fight bite injury
Knowing that patients often give a misleading history about the mechanism of injury.
Knowing that a delay in presentation beyond twenty four hours significantly increases the risk of established infection and poor outcome.

History and examination
Knowing the difference between human bites, dog bites, and cat bites in terms of organisms and management.
Dog bites: Pasteurella multocida and Capnocytophaga canimorsus. Cat bites: Pasteurella multocida, deeper inoculation due to narrow pointed teeth.
Human bites: Eikenella corrodens, Streptococci, Staphylococci, and anaerobes.

Assessment for joint involvement and tendon injury
Knowing that even if the joint does not appear to be involved on initial examination, the dynamic nature of the injury when the fist is clenched means the tendon laceration and joint breach occur at a different level to the skin wound.

Investigations
Knowing that the absence of radiological findings does not exclude joint involvement.
Knowing that gas in the soft tissues on X-ray suggests established infection and is an ominous sign.

Antibiotic therapy
Knowing that bite wounds should not receive prophylactic antibiotics alone without surgical assessment for joint involvement.
Knowing the alternative antibiotics for penicillin-allergic patients.

Surgical management with joint washout
Knowing that even if the wound appears superficial, if it overlies the M C P joint it should be explored formally in theatre.
Knowing that the sagittal band and joint capsule should be inspected and repaired if damaged.
Knowing that the articular cartilage should be inspected for damage from the tooth.

Post-operative care
Knowing the complications of fight bite injuries including osteomyelitis, septic arthritis, tendon rupture, and chronic stiffness.
Knowing that animal bites to the hand should also be assessed for tendon and joint involvement and managed surgically if there is any concern.
Knowing the indications for rabies post-exposure prophylaxis for animal bites in at-risk geographical areas.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
