SCENARIO TITLE

Palmar Space Infection

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 48 year old right hand dominant woman who has had a worsening infection in her right hand over the past four days following a small cut while cooking. She has been taking oral antibiotics from her G P but the hand is now very swollen and painful, particularly in the palm. She has a fever and feels unwell. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and risk factors
Taking a focused history including the onset, progression, any preceding injury, and any treatment received so far.
Asking about diabetes, immunosuppression, and other risk factors for severe hand infection.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, tetanus status, and allergies.
Noting that the patient has failed oral antibiotic therapy and now has systemic signs of infection.

Clinical assessment and localisation of the infection
Performing a rapid A to E assessment given the systemic signs.
Examining the hand for swelling, erythema, warmth, and point tenderness.
Identifying the site of maximal tenderness and swelling to determine which space is involved.
For a mid-palmar space infection: the palm is swollen and tender, the normal palmar concavity is lost, and there may be pain on passive extension of the middle and ring fingers.
For a thenar space infection: the thenar eminence is swollen with the thumb held in abduction, and there is pain on opposition of the thumb.
Assessing for web space involvement: fullness and tenderness between the fingers with the adjacent fingers held abducted.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the hand and fingers.
Examining for Kanavel signs to rule out concurrent flexor sheath infection.

Anatomy of the deep palmar spaces
Knowing the three potential deep spaces of the hand: the thenar space, the mid-palmar space, and the hypothenar space.
Knowing that the thenar and mid-palmar spaces are separated by the oblique septum from the third metacarpal to the palmar fascia.
Knowing that the space of Parona in the distal forearm communicates with the deep palmar spaces and may also be involved.
Knowing the web spaces as additional potential spaces for infection.

Sepsis assessment and investigations
Assessing for sepsis and initiating Sepsis six if criteria are met.
Routine bloods including full blood count, C R P, urea and electrolytes, glucose, and blood cultures if febrile.
X-ray of the hand to exclude foreign body, gas in the soft tissues, and osteomyelitis.

Surgical drainage
Knowing that deep space infections of the hand require urgent surgical drainage.
Performing the procedure under general or regional anaesthesia with an arm tourniquet.
Making appropriate incisions based on the space involved: a palmar incision along the distal palmar crease for the mid-palmar space, a palmar incision along the thenar crease for the thenar space.
Opening all involved spaces and breaking down any loculations.
Exploring the adjacent spaces as infection can track between compartments.
Taking pus samples for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity.
Thorough washout with saline.
Leaving the wound open.
Applying a bulky dressing and splinting in the position of safe immobilisation.

Post-operative care
Continued intravenous antibiotics adjusted based on culture results.
Strict hand elevation.
Planned second look at forty eight hours.
Hand therapy once infection has settled.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and risk factors
Knowing that deep space infections often arise from penetrating injuries, extension of flexor sheath infections, or haematogenous spread.
Knowing that diabetes is the most significant risk factor for severe hand infections.

Clinical assessment and localisation of the infection
Knowing the horseshoe abscess: infection tracking from the little finger flexor sheath through the ulnar bursa into the space of Parona, and then into the radial bursa and the thumb flexor sheath. This requires washout of all spaces including the carpal tunnel.
Knowing that dorsal hand swelling may be secondary to a deep palmar infection as the loose dorsal subcutaneous tissue swells more readily.
Knowing that the mid-palmar space infection may track proximally into the space of Parona and distally into the lumbrical canals.

Anatomy of the deep palmar spaces
Drawing or describing the anatomy: the mid-palmar space lies deep to the flexor tendons and superficial to the interossei and metacarpals. It is bounded radially by the oblique septum from the third metacarpal and ulnarly by the hypothenar septum.
Knowing that the space of Parona lies between the pronator quadratus and the flexor tendons in the distal forearm and communicates with both the radial and ulnar bursae.

Sepsis assessment and investigations
Knowing that ultrasound may help localise a deep collection.
Knowing that M R I may be indicated if osteomyelitis is suspected or if the extent of the infection is unclear.

Surgical drainage
For a horseshoe abscess, knowing the surgical approach: opening the flexor sheaths of the little finger and thumb via incisions over A one and A five pulleys, extending to open the carpal tunnel, and exploring the space of Parona, the ulnar bursa, the mid-palmar space, the radial bursa, and the web spaces as needed.
Using a Brunner incision or midlateral approach if frank pus is encountered in the flexor sheath.
Knowing that inadequate drainage is the most common cause of persistent infection and that all involved spaces must be opened.

Post-operative care
Knowing that multiple washouts may be required.
Knowing the importance of early hand therapy once infection is controlled to prevent the significant stiffness that is common after deep space infections.
Knowing that the functional outcome correlates with the severity of the initial infection and the delay to surgical drainage.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
