SCENARIO TITLE

Hand Abscess

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 55 year old right hand dominant diabetic man who has presented with a three day history of increasing pain, swelling, and redness over the dorsum of his right hand. He thinks he may have pricked himself on a thorn a few days earlier. His hand is now very swollen and he has a fever. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and risk factors
Taking a focused history including the onset and progression of symptoms, any preceding injury such as a thorn prick, bite, or penetrating wound, and any previous episodes.
Asking about diabetes and current glycaemic control, immunosuppression, intravenous drug use, and any other risk factors for hand infection.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, tetanus status, and allergies.

Examination and identification of the abscess
Examining the hand for the site and extent of swelling, erythema, warmth, fluctuance, and point tenderness.
Identifying which space is involved: the dorsal subcutaneous space is the most common site of hand abscess.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the hand and fingers.
Marking the extent of erythema with the date and time for monitoring.
Assessing for signs of tracking infection: lymphangitis, lymphadenopathy in the axilla, and any signs of deeper space involvement.

Sepsis assessment
Performing an A to E assessment.
Knowing the signs of sepsis: fever, tachycardia, hypotension, and raised inflammatory markers.
Initiating Sepsis six management if sepsis criteria are met.

Investigations
Routine bloods including full blood count, C R P, urea and electrolytes, glucose, and H B A one C in a diabetic patient.
Blood cultures if the patient is febrile or septic.
X-ray of the hand to exclude a foreign body, osteomyelitis, or gas in the soft tissues.
Wound swab or pus swab for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity at the time of drainage.

Initial management including antibiotics
Intravenous antibiotics. First line is flucloxacillin for staphylococcal cover, or co-amoxiclav for broader cover if a penetrating injury or bite is suspected.
Analgesia.
Hand elevation in a Bradford sling.
Tetanus prophylaxis.
Optimising blood glucose control in the diabetic patient.
Keeping the patient nil by mouth and preparing for theatre.

Surgical drainage
Performing incision and drainage under general or regional anaesthesia with an arm tourniquet.
Making the incision directly over the point of maximal fluctuance.
Draining all pus and breaking down any loculations.
Sending pus for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity.
Thorough washout with saline.
Leaving the wound open to drain or inserting a small drain.
Applying a bulky dressing and splinting in the position of safe immobilisation.

Post-operative care
Continued intravenous antibiotics adjusted based on culture results.
Strict hand elevation.
Regular wound review and planned second look at forty eight hours if there is any concern about residual infection.
Hand therapy once infection has settled.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and risk factors
Knowing the common organisms causing hand abscesses: Staphylococcus aureus is the most common, Streptococci, and in diabetic or immunosuppressed patients consider M R S A and atypical organisms.
Asking about intravenous drug use which may cause unusual sites of hand infection.
Knowing that diabetes is the most important risk factor for hand infections and is associated with more severe disease and poorer outcomes.

Examination and identification of the abscess
Knowing the anatomy of the potential spaces in the hand: dorsal subcutaneous, thenar, hypothenar, mid-palmar, web spaces, and the space of Parona in the distal forearm.
Knowing that a dorsal hand abscess with erythema may be secondary to a palmar space infection, as the loose dorsal subcutaneous tissue swells more easily than the tight palmar fascia.
Knowing that a collar stud abscess has both superficial and deep components communicating through the palmar fascia.

Sepsis assessment
Knowing the Sepsis six bundle: take blood cultures, measure lactate, give high flow oxygen, give intravenous antibiotics, give intravenous fluid resuscitation, and monitor urine output.

Investigations
Knowing that ultrasound may be useful to identify and localise a deep abscess or to distinguish cellulitis from an abscess.
Knowing that M R I may be indicated if osteomyelitis is suspected.

Initial management including antibiotics
Knowing that established abscess will not resolve with antibiotics alone and requires surgical drainage.
Knowing that antibiotics should be given intravenously at induction of anaesthesia before tourniquet application.

Surgical drainage
Knowing the specific incision sites for different spaces: mid-lateral incisions for digital abscesses, palmar incisions along skin creases for thenar and mid-palmar abscesses, and dorsal incisions for dorsal subcutaneous abscesses.
Knowing the importance of exploring adjacent spaces as infection can track between compartments.
Knowing the appropriate use of a counter-incision for through-and-through drainage if needed.

Post-operative care
Knowing that a prolonged course of antibiotics may be needed for deep space infections or osteomyelitis.
Knowing that the most common complication is stiffness, particularly if drainage is delayed.
Knowing the importance of checking H B A one C and optimising diabetic control as part of the overall management.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
