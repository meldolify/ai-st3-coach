SCENARIO TITLE

High Pressure Injection Injury

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 42 year old right hand dominant mechanic who has accidentally injected grease into his left index finger while using a grease gun at work approximately three hours ago. The entry wound is small but his finger is swollen and very painful. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of the severity of the injury
Recognising that a high pressure injection injury is a surgical emergency despite the innocuous appearance of the entry wound.
Knowing that the injected material can track along tissue planes causing extensive damage including chemical inflammation, tissue necrosis, and compartment syndrome.

History including substance details
Establishing the exact substance injected as this determines the prognosis. Grease, paint, and organic solvents carry a worse prognosis than air or water.
Asking about the pressure of the device in P S I.
Asking about the estimated volume of material injected.
Asking about the timing of the injury.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, tetanus status, and allergies.

Examination and assessment for compartment syndrome
Noting that the entry wound may appear innocuous.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the finger and hand.
Assessing the extent of tenderness along the finger, hand, and forearm which indicates how far the material has tracked.
Ruling out compartment syndrome.

Investigations
X-ray of the hand and forearm which may show the extent of material tracking and help identify the substance. Some materials such as grease and paint are radio-opaque.
Routine bloods.

Urgent surgical management
Knowing that urgent surgical exploration should be performed within six hours of injury.
Performing the operation under general or regional anaesthesia with an arm tourniquet.
Surgical exploration through extended incisions to excise all contaminated and necrotic tissue.
Performing fasciotomy if there is any concern about compartment syndrome.
Thorough washout of the wound.
Leaving the wound open with planned second look at forty eight hours.

Post-operative care
Strict hand elevation.
Intravenous antibiotics.
Strong analgesia.
Tetanus prophylaxis.
Regular monitoring of the neurovascular status.
Planned return to theatre for second look and further debridement as needed.
Delayed wound closure or reconstruction once the wound is clean.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of the severity of the injury
Knowing that amputation rates for high pressure injection injuries are reported at up to forty eight percent, particularly for organic solvents and when treatment is delayed beyond six hours.
Knowing that the outcome is determined by the substance injected, the volume, the pressure, and the time to surgical exploration.

History including substance details
Knowing the specific risk profile of different substances: air and water at low pressure and low volume with intact neurovascular status may be managed conservatively, whereas grease, paint, paint thinner, and other organic solvents require urgent surgery regardless.

Examination and assessment for compartment syndrome
Palpating the forearm compartments for tightness.
Knowing that the material can track along tendon sheaths from the finger into the palm and forearm.
Knowing that the absence of pain does not exclude significant injury as digital nerve damage may mask the severity.

Investigations
Knowing that a C T scan may occasionally be useful to delineate the extent of tracking in complex cases.

Urgent surgical management
Knowing the criteria for conservative management: air or water, low P S I, low volume, neurovascular status intact. In this case, the patient should be admitted for close observation, intravenous antibiotics, strict hand elevation, tetanus prophylaxis, and strong analgesia. There is some evidence that non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs are beneficial.
Knowing that a mid-lateral or Brunner incision should be used and extended as needed to follow the track of injected material.
Knowing that all devitalised tissue should be excised aggressively.
Knowing that the carpal tunnel may need to be released if material has tracked proximally.

Post-operative care
Knowing that multiple debridements are often required.
Knowing that despite adequate surgical management, outcomes for organic solvent injuries remain poor with high rates of stiffness, chronic pain, and amputation.
Knowing the importance of early hand therapy involvement once the wound is stable.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
