SCENARIO TITLE

Electrical Injury

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 25 year old electrician who has sustained a high voltage electrical injury at work. He was found unconscious at the scene and was resuscitated by the paramedics. He is now in the emergency department resuscitation bay, intubated and ventilated. He has visible burns to both hands. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of the severity and systemic nature of the injury
Recognising that a high voltage electrical injury is a life-threatening emergency with implications far beyond the visible skin burns.
Knowing the mechanisms of electrical injury: thermal injury from the current, direct electrical forces causing cell membrane damage through electroporation, and protein denaturation.
Knowing that the visible burns represent only a fraction of the total injury as the current travels through deep tissues causing extensive muscle necrosis.

A T L S and E M S B assessment
Following A T L S and E M S B principles with cervical spine immobilisation.
Airway assessment and management, noting the patient is already intubated and ventilated.
Breathing assessment including chest expansion and the need for chest escharotomy if circumferential chest burns are present.
Circulation assessment: continuous E C G monitoring is mandatory given the high risk of cardiac arrhythmias and cardiac arrest.
Gaining intravenous access through unburnt skin and sending bloods including full blood count, urea and electrolytes with particular attention to potassium, creatine kinase, troponin, clotting, and cross-match.
Estimating T B S A using Lund and Browder charts. Identifying entry and exit wounds.
A B G including carboxyhaemoglobin and lactate.

Cardiac monitoring and arrhythmia management
Knowing that cardiac arrhythmias are a major cause of death in electrical injury.
Continuous E C G monitoring.
Cardiology involvement if any arrhythmias are detected.
Electrolyte correction, particularly potassium, calcium, and magnesium.

Assessment for compartment syndrome and rhabdomyolysis
Examining all limb compartments for tightness and measuring compartment pressures if the patient is sedated and clinical assessment is unreliable.
Monitoring for rhabdomyolysis: dark urine, rising creatine kinase, hyperkalaemia, and acute kidney injury.
Inserting a urinary catheter and monitoring urine output. The urine output should be maintained at greater than one millilitre per kilogram per hour as per E M S B guidance.

Fluid resuscitation
Aggressive intravenous fluid resuscitation using the modified Parkland formula as a guide, but knowing that electrical injuries often require more fluid than the formula predicts because the visible burn underestimates the total tissue injury.
Titrating fluids to urine output.

Surgical management including fasciotomy
Prophylactic fasciotomy of affected limbs given the high likelihood of underlying muscle burns that will develop into compartment syndrome.
Arranging urgent surgery in a warm operating theatre.
Performing fasciotomy along standard incision lines, releasing all compartments.
Identifying and protecting named nerves.
Excision of any clearly necrotic muscle and skin, taking care to avoid excessive bleeding.

I T U care and monitoring
Post-operative I T U care for close monitoring of haemodynamic status.
Continued cardiac monitoring.
Ongoing fluid management and electrolyte correction.
Planning for second look at forty eight hours with further excision and debridement as needed.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Recognition of the severity and systemic nature of the injury
Knowing the difference between high voltage and low voltage injuries. High voltage is greater than one thousand volts and carries a much worse prognosis.
Knowing the different mechanisms of contact: direct contact with the source, caught in an electrical arc, flash burn from nearby arc, and current transferrance through the ground.
Knowing that lightning is an extremely high voltage injury with very high mortality.

A T L S and E M S B assessment
Asking about associated injuries: was the patient thrown, was there a flame fire or explosion following the electrical injury.
Asking about loss of consciousness at the scene and whether there was a cardiac arrest requiring resuscitation or cardioversion.
Examining the eyes for cataracts and corneal damage.
Examining for neurological injury.
Performing an abdominal examination to rule out visceral perforation.
Requesting a C T trauma series to exclude other injuries.

Cardiac monitoring and arrhythmia management
Knowing that hyperkalaemia from rhabdomyolysis can cause fatal arrhythmias and requires urgent treatment with calcium gluconate for myocardial protection, insulin and dextrose, and sodium bicarbonate.
Knowing that troponin should be measured to assess for myocardial injury.

Assessment for compartment syndrome and rhabdomyolysis
Knowing that urine should be tested for myoglobin.
Knowing the management of myoglobinuria: aggressive fluid resuscitation, consideration of mannitol to enhance diuresis, and alkalinisation of the urine with sodium bicarbonate.
Knowing that if mannitol is used, urine output can no longer be used reliably as a marker of resuscitation adequacy and a central venous pressure line or pulmonary capillary wedge pressure monitoring may be needed.
Knowing that M R I can provide detail on the anatomical site and severity of muscle injury.

Fluid resuscitation
Knowing the Parkland formula: three to four millilitres per kilogram per percentage T B S A, with the first half given in the first eight hours from the time of injury and the second half over the next sixteen hours using Hartmann solution.
Knowing that in electrical injuries the fluid requirements are often significantly greater than calculated because the deep tissue injury is not reflected in the T B S A.

Surgical management including fasciotomy
Knowing that the surgical strategy should be performed in a warm burns theatre to prevent hypothermia.
Knowing the surgical endpoints: two to three hours operating time, up to ten units transfused, temperature dropping below thirty six degrees, or the lethal triad of hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy.
Knowing that entry and exit wounds should be examined thoroughly for underlying structural damage.
Knowing that serial debridements are often needed as the extent of tissue necrosis may declare over several days.

I T U care and monitoring
Knowing the nutritional requirements of a burns patient: the Curreri formula calculates calorie requirements as twenty five times body weight in kilograms plus forty times T B S A percentage.
Knowing that enteral feeding via a nasogastric tube should be commenced early.
Knowing the importance of a multidisciplinary approach including I T U, burns surgery, cardiology, renal medicine, physiotherapy, and psychology.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
