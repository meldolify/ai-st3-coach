SCENARIO TITLE

Septic Arthritis of the Hand

CASE INTRODUCTION

(Read verbatim in the Case Introduction state.)

This is a 60 year old right hand dominant man with rheumatoid arthritis who has presented with a two day history of a hot, swollen, and extremely painful right index finger metacarpophalangeal joint. He has not had any injury that he can recall. He is on methotrexate and has a low-grade fever. You have been asked to assess and manage this patient.

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and risk factors
Taking a focused history including the onset and progression of symptoms, any preceding injury or injection, and any recent infections elsewhere in the body.
Asking about immunosuppression including methotrexate, rheumatoid arthritis, diabetes, and other risk factors for septic arthritis.
Asking about previous joint problems, gout, or pseudogout which may mimic septic arthritis.
Asking about hand dominance, occupation, and allergies.

Clinical assessment and differential diagnosis
Examining the affected joint for swelling, erythema, warmth, and an effusion.
Assessing that the joint is held in a position of comfort, typically slight flexion.
Pain on any attempt to move the joint actively or passively is characteristic.
Differential diagnosis including septic arthritis, gout, pseudogout, rheumatoid flare, and reactive arthritis.
Knowing that septic arthritis must be excluded urgently as delayed treatment leads to joint destruction.
Assessing the neurovascular status of the finger.
Examining for other potential sources of infection.

Joint aspiration
Knowing that joint aspiration is a critical diagnostic step.
Aspirated fluid should be sent urgently for microscopy including Gram stain and crystal analysis, culture and sensitivity, and cell count.
Knowing that purulent aspirate or a positive Gram stain confirms the diagnosis and mandates urgent surgical washout.
Knowing that crystal arthropathy may coexist with septic arthritis and one does not exclude the other.

Investigations
Routine bloods including full blood count, C R P, E S R, urea and electrolytes, uric acid, and blood cultures.
X-ray of the hand which may show soft tissue swelling, joint space widening, or in late cases joint destruction.
Knowing that normal inflammatory markers do not exclude septic arthritis, particularly in immunosuppressed patients.

Antibiotic therapy
Starting empirical intravenous antibiotics after joint aspiration and blood cultures have been taken.
First line antibiotics covering staphylococcal and streptococcal organisms such as flucloxacillin, adjusted based on culture results.
Discussing with microbiology for guidance, particularly in immunosuppressed patients.
Knowing the most common organism is Staphylococcus aureus.

Surgical joint washout
Knowing that confirmed or suspected septic arthritis of the hand requires urgent surgical washout.
Performing the procedure under general or regional anaesthesia with an arm tourniquet.
Making a dorsal incision over the affected joint.
Opening the joint capsule, draining all pus, and irrigating with copious saline.
Taking tissue and fluid samples for microbiology.
Assessing the articular cartilage for damage.
Leaving the wound open or loosely closed over a drain.
Splinting in the position of safe immobilisation.

Post-operative care
Continued intravenous antibiotics for a minimum of two weeks, adjusted based on culture and sensitivity results, followed by oral antibiotics typically for a further four weeks.
Strict hand elevation.
Planned second look at forty eight hours if there is any concern about ongoing infection.
Hand therapy for mobilisation once infection is controlled.
Liaison with the rheumatology team regarding the patient's immunosuppressive medication.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

History and risk factors
Knowing that haematogenous spread is the most common route of infection in native joint septic arthritis.
Knowing that immunosuppressed patients including those on methotrexate, biological agents, or steroids are at significantly increased risk.
Knowing that intravenous drug use and recent joint injection are important risk factors.
Asking about gonococcal arthritis in sexually active young adults presenting with a hot joint.

Clinical assessment and differential diagnosis
Knowing Kocher criteria for septic arthritis, originally described for the hip but applicable in principle: fever, non-weight-bearing or inability to use the joint, raised E S R or C R P, and raised white cell count.
Knowing that in rheumatoid patients, distinguishing a flare from septic arthritis can be very difficult and a low threshold for aspiration should be maintained.

Joint aspiration
Knowing that a white cell count greater than fifty thousand cells per microlitre in the aspirate is highly suggestive of septic arthritis.
Knowing the aspiration technique for the M C P joint: dorsal approach with the joint in slight flexion to open the joint space.
Knowing that if aspiration is dry or equivocal, the clinical picture should guide management and surgical exploration may still be warranted.

Investigations
Knowing that ultrasound may be useful to confirm the presence of a joint effusion and guide aspiration.
Knowing that M R I may show marrow oedema, synovial enhancement, and joint effusion and can help differentiate septic arthritis from other causes.

Antibiotic therapy
Knowing the recommended duration of antibiotic therapy: typically two weeks of intravenous treatment followed by four weeks of oral antibiotics, guided by clinical response and inflammatory markers.
Knowing that M R S A cover should be considered in patients with risk factors or in areas of high prevalence.
Knowing that the methotrexate should be held during the acute infection after discussion with rheumatology.

Surgical joint washout
Knowing that repeated washouts may be necessary if the infection does not settle.
Knowing that the articular cartilage damage from septic arthritis is caused by both bacterial enzymes and the host inflammatory response, which is why prompt drainage is critical.
Knowing that late complications include joint stiffness, chronic osteomyelitis, and secondary osteoarthritis which may eventually require arthrodesis.

Post-operative care
Knowing that monitoring of inflammatory markers, C R P in particular, is useful to assess response to treatment.
Knowing the importance of early mobilisation to prevent stiffness once infection is controlled.
Knowing that the functional outcome depends on the duration of symptoms before treatment, the virulence of the organism, and the degree of cartilage damage at the time of washout.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
