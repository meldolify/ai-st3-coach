CLINICAL SCENARIO BLOCK

(CASE-SPECIFIC)

SCENARIO TITLE

Major Burn

PROBING QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the consultant may ask during the call.
Use these to challenge or guide the candidate after they finish presenting.
Delivery specificity is controlled by the personality layer.)

"What is the T B S A and how did you calculate it?"
"Tell me about the airway — why was he intubated?"
"What does the carboxyhaemoglobin level of 18 percent mean and how would you manage it?"
"How are you fluid resuscitating this patient — talk me through the Parkland formula."
"Do you think he needs an escharotomy tonight? Which limbs and why?"
"What are the criteria for transfer to a burns centre?"
"Who else have you informed — I T U, anaesthetics?"
"What is the surgical plan for the coming days?"

MAIN COMMUNICATION AND CLINICAL DOMAINS

Structured clinical presentation covering mechanism, primary survey, and burn assessment
Airway assessment and management of inhalation injury
Burns assessment including T B S A estimation and depth
Fluid resuscitation using the Parkland formula
Recognition of the need for escharotomy
Escalation, transfer criteria, and I T U planning

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation covering mechanism, primary survey, and burn assessment
The candidate must introduce themselves clearly, state the urgency, and present the case in a structured manner.
The candidate must include the mechanism of injury (house fire, trapped, intubated at scene), the patient's background, and the current clinical status.
The candidate must present the primary survey findings systematically.

Airway assessment and management of inhalation injury
The candidate must recognise the signs of inhalation injury: singed nasal hairs, soot in the airway, intubation at scene, and reduced saturations despite high-flow oxygen.
The candidate must recognise that the patient was appropriately intubated early at scene.
Knowing that the carboxyhaemoglobin level of 18 percent confirms carbon monoxide poisoning and should be treated with 100 percent oxygen.

Burns assessment including T B S A estimation and depth
The candidate must estimate the T B S A at approximately 35 percent using the Lund and Browder chart or rule of nines.
The candidate must describe the burn depths: full thickness circumferential burns to both forearms and hands, mixed depth burns to the anterior chest and abdomen, and facial burns with periorbital oedema.
The candidate must identify the circumferential full thickness burns as a concern for compartment syndrome requiring escharotomy.

Fluid resuscitation using the Parkland formula
The candidate must describe the Parkland formula: 3 to 4 ml per kg per percentage T B S A, with the first half given in the first 8 hours from the time of injury and the second half over the following 16 hours using Hartmann solution.
The candidate must mention titrating fluids to urine output of greater than 0.5 ml per kg per hour.
The candidate must mention that fluid resuscitation has been commenced.

Recognition of the need for escharotomy
The candidate must recognise that circumferential full thickness burns to both forearms require urgent assessment for escharotomy.
The candidate must describe the escharotomy incisions along the mid-lateral lines of the forearms.
The candidate must mention monitoring the neurovascular status of the hands before and after escharotomy.

Escalation, transfer criteria, and I T U planning
The candidate must request the consultant to come in for assessment and potential escharotomy.
The candidate must mention arranging an I T U bed for ongoing monitoring and ventilatory support.
The candidate must know the criteria for transfer to a burns centre and discuss whether transfer is appropriate.
The candidate must mention informing the anaesthetic team and C E P O D theatres.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation covering mechanism, primary survey, and burn assessment
The candidate uses A M P L E history format: allergies, medications, past medical history, last meal, and events.
The candidate mentions clinical photography of the burns.

Airway assessment and management of inhalation injury
The candidate discusses the risk of cyanide poisoning from synthetic materials in a house fire and the treatment with Cyanokit (hydroxocobalamin).
The candidate mentions that the A B G showing metabolic acidosis with raised lactate may indicate cyanide poisoning in addition to poor tissue perfusion.
The candidate discusses arranging a baseline chest X-ray to monitor for A R D S.

Burns assessment including T B S A estimation and depth
The candidate mentions that the facial burns with periorbital oedema may prevent eye examination and requests an ophthalmology review.
The candidate discusses that burn depth may evolve over the first 48 to 72 hours and areas initially assessed as partial thickness may convert to full thickness.

Fluid resuscitation using the Parkland formula
The candidate recognises that the formula is a guide and clinical response should drive fluid management.
The candidate mentions monitoring serum lactate and urine output as markers of adequate resuscitation.
The candidate discusses inserting a nasogastric tube for early enteral nutrition.

Recognition of the need for escharotomy
The candidate describes the technique of escharotomy: full-thickness incision through the eschar down to subcutaneous fat, along the mid-axial lines, avoiding neurovascular structures.
The candidate mentions that chest escharotomy may be needed if circumferential chest burns restrict ventilation.

Escalation, transfer criteria, and I T U planning
The candidate discusses the nutritional requirements using the Curreri formula.
The candidate mentions the multidisciplinary team including I T U, burns surgery, physiotherapy, dietetics, and psychology.
The candidate discusses the surgical strategy for the coming days: tangential excision and skin grafting in a warm theatre, with sheet grafts for hands and face.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main communication and clinical domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
