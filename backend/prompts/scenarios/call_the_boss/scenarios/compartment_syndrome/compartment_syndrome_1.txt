CLINICAL SCENARIO BLOCK

(CASE-SPECIFIC)

SCENARIO TITLE

Compartment Syndrome

PROBING QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the consultant may ask during the call.
Use these to challenge or guide the candidate after they finish presenting.
Delivery specificity is controlled by the personality layer.)

"Have you split the cast yet? Why not?"
"What are the clinical signs that make you think this is compartment syndrome?"
"Do you need to measure compartment pressures or is this a clinical diagnosis?"
"How urgently does this need to go to theatre?"
"Can you describe the fasciotomy incisions for the lower leg?"
"How many compartments are there and which ones need releasing?"
"What would your post-operative plan look like?"
"What happens if we miss this — what are the consequences?"

MAIN COMMUNICATION AND CLINICAL DOMAINS

Structured clinical presentation with recognition of compartment syndrome as a surgical emergency
Immediate bedside management including cast removal

Understanding of consequences of delayed treatment

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation with recognition of compartment syndrome as a surgical emergency
The candidate must recognise that this presentation is consistent with compartment syndrome and that it is a surgical emergency requiring immediate fasciotomy.
The candidate must convey the urgency of the situation and request the consultant's support for theatre tonight.
The candidate must present the key clinical features clearly: pain out of proportion, escalating analgesia requirement, pain on passive stretch, cool toes with delayed capillary refill, and reduced sensation.

Immediate bedside management including cast removal
The candidate must state that the first and most urgent step is to immediately split and bivalve the cast and all underlying padding down to skin.
The candidate must recognise that this should have been done already and should be done before any other assessment.
The candidate must mention lowering the limb to heart level rather than keeping it elevated, to maintain perfusion pressure.
The candidate must provide analgesia but recognise that escalating opioid requirements are suspicious for compartment syndrome.

Clinical features and diagnostic approach
The candidate must know the classic features of compartment syndrome: pain out of proportion, pain on passive stretch, tense swelling, paraesthesia, and the late signs of pallor, paralysis, and pulselessness.
The candidate must state that compartment syndrome is primarily a clinical diagnosis and treatment should not be delayed for compartment pressure measurements.
The candidate must recognise that the presence of a palpable pulse does not exclude compartment syndrome as pulselessness is a late sign.

Fasciotomy technique for the lower leg
The candidate must know there are four compartments: anterior, lateral, superficial posterior, and deep posterior.
The candidate must describe a two-incision four-compartment fasciotomy: an anterolateral incision two finger breadths lateral to the tibial crest to release the anterior and lateral compartments, and a posteromedial incision two finger breadths posterior to the posteromedial border of the tibia to release the superficial and deep posterior compartments.
The candidate must mention that incisions should extend the full length of the compartment and the fascia must be opened along the full length.
The candidate must mention protecting the common peroneal nerve at the fibular head and the saphenous vein and nerve with the posteromedial incision.

Post-operative care and wound management
The candidate must state that the fasciotomy wounds are left open initially.
The candidate must plan a return to theatre at 48 hours for assessment and delayed primary closure, split-thickness skin graft, or negative pressure wound therapy.
The candidate must mention monitoring the neurovascular status post-operatively.
The candidate must mention assessing muscle viability at second look: viable muscle should be pink, contractile, and bleed when cut.

Understanding of consequences of delayed treatment
The candidate must recognise that delayed fasciotomy leads to irreversible muscle and nerve ischaemia.
The candidate must know that the consequence in the lower limb is irreversible tissue necrosis with potential limb loss.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation with recognition of compartment syndrome as a surgical emergency
The candidate demonstrates urgency in their communication and makes it clear that this cannot wait.
The candidate mentions the medico-legal significance of compartment syndrome and the importance of thorough documentation.

Immediate bedside management including cast removal
The candidate discusses compartment pressure measurement using the Whitesides criteria if the diagnosis is uncertain: fasciotomy is indicated when compartment pressure rises to within 10 to 30 mmHg of the diastolic blood pressure, or absolute pressure exceeds 30 mmHg.
The candidate mentions using a Stryker I C P monitor or needle manometry.

Clinical features and diagnostic approach
The candidate mentions that opioid analgesia may mask symptoms and that escalating analgesia requirements should raise suspicion.
The candidate discusses the common causes of compartment syndrome: fractures, crush injuries, tight casts, burns, high-pressure injection injuries, and reperfusion after vascular repair.

Fasciotomy technique for the lower leg
The candidate describes the compartments of the forearm and hand for comparison, showing broader knowledge.
The candidate mentions that non-viable muscle at second look should be debrided to prevent rhabdomyolysis, sepsis, and myoglobinuric renal failure.

Post-operative care and wound management
The candidate mentions systemic complications including rhabdomyolysis, hyperkalaemia, metabolic acidosis, and acute kidney injury from myoglobinuria.
The candidate discusses the management of rhabdomyolysis: aggressive I V fluid resuscitation, monitoring urine output, monitoring serum creatine kinase and potassium, and alkalinisation of the urine with sodium bicarbonate.
The candidate mentions that Volkmann ischaemic contracture is the consequence of a missed compartment syndrome in the upper limb.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main communication and clinical domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
