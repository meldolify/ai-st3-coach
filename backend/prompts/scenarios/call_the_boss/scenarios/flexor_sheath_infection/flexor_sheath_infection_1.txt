SCENARIO TITLE

Flexor Sheath Infection

PROBING QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the consultant may ask during the call.
Use these to challenge or guide the candidate after they finish presenting.
Delivery specificity is controlled by the personality layer.)

"What are the Kanavel signs and which ones does this patient have?"
"Are you worried about a horseshoe abscess? Why?"
"What antibiotics are you going to use — she is penicillin allergic?"
"Does this definitely need to go to theatre tonight or can it wait until morning?"
"Talk me through the surgical approach step by step."
"Which spaces would you explore in theatre?"
"What is your post-operative plan?"
"Do you need me to come in for this or are you comfortable doing it yourself?"

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation with recognition of flexor sheath infection as a surgical emergency
The candidate must identify the diagnosis as flexor sheath infection (flexor tenosynovitis) which is a surgical emergency.
The candidate must recognise the systemic sepsis with tachycardia, hypotension, pyrexia, raised lactate, and raised inflammatory markers.
The candidate must convey that this patient needs urgent surgical washout tonight.
The candidate must mention initiating the Sepsis Six bundle.

Clinical assessment including Kanavel signs
The candidate must describe the four Kanavel signs: fusiform swelling of the finger, flexed posture of the finger, pain on passive extension, and tenderness along the flexor sheath.
The candidate must state that all four Kanavel signs are present in this patient.
The candidate must mention assessing the neurovascular status of all fingers.

Recognition of potential horseshoe abscess and deep space involvement
The candidate must recognise that the extension of swelling and tenderness into the palm and thenar eminence raises the possibility of a horseshoe abscess.
The candidate must explain that in a horseshoe abscess, the little finger and thumb flexor sheaths communicate through the ulnar and radial bursae, requiring thorough washout of all connected spaces.
The candidate must mention the spaces that may be involved: ulnar bursa, radial bursa, midpalmar space, thenar space, and the space of Parona in the distal forearm.

Antibiotic strategy in a penicillin-allergic patient
The candidate must recognise that oral flucloxacillin is inadequate and I V antibiotics are required.
The candidate must know that in a penicillin-allergic patient, alternatives include I V teicoplanin, or clarithromycin, or clindamycin, guided by local trust guidelines.
The candidate must mention discussing with microbiology for guidance.
The candidate must mention tetanus prophylaxis.

Surgical planning and technique for flexor sheath washout
The candidate must describe the surgical approach: general or regional anaesthesia with arm tourniquet without exsanguination.
The candidate must describe the minimal access approach: incisions over the A1 and A5 pulleys, with washout using saline through a venflon cannula from proximal to distal.
The candidate must state that if frank pus is found, the incision should be extended using a Brunner or mid-lateral approach.
The candidate must mention taking microbiology swabs and excising the original puncture wound.
The candidate must mention exploring the potential spaces including the thenar, hypothenar, midpalmar, web spaces, and the space of Parona if there is any concern about proximal spread.

Post-operative care and second look planning
The candidate must mention splinting the hand in the position of safe immobilisation.
The candidate must mention strict hand elevation in a Bradford sling.
The candidate must continue I V antibiotics and plan a second look in 48 hours.
The candidate must mention early hand therapy once the infection is controlled.
The candidate must mention optimising blood glucose control in this diabetic patient.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation with recognition of flexor sheath infection as a surgical emergency
The candidate mentions risk factors for flexor sheath infection in this patient: diabetes, immunocompromise, penetrating thorn injury, and delayed presentation.
The candidate mentions that the patient is showing signs of sepsis and may need critical care input if she deteriorates.

Clinical assessment including Kanavel signs
The candidate mentions looking for tracking cellulitis, lymphangitis, and lymphadenopathy as signs of systemic spread.
The candidate mentions palpating for foreign bodies in the wound.

Recognition of potential horseshoe abscess and deep space involvement
The candidate describes the anatomy of the ulnar and radial bursae and how infection can track between the little finger and thumb sheaths.
The candidate mentions that a horseshoe abscess carries a significantly worse prognosis than isolated flexor sheath infection.

Antibiotic strategy in a penicillin-allergic patient
The candidate discusses common organisms in flexor sheath infection: Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococci, and mixed flora from penetrating injuries, including atypical organisms from soil and plant matter such as Nocardia and atypical Mycobacteria.
The candidate mentions sending wound cultures and blood cultures and tailoring antibiotics accordingly.

Surgical planning and technique for flexor sheath washout
The candidate describes the importance of preserving the A2 and A4 pulleys during surgical exposure to prevent bowstringing.
The candidate discusses the option of placing a catheter in the sheath for continuous post-operative irrigation in severe cases.

Post-operative care and second look planning
The candidate discusses the potential complications including tendon adhesions, tendon necrosis, digital stiffness, and the risk of amputation in severe cases.
The candidate mentions the importance of the position of safe immobilisation: wrist in 20 degrees extension, M C P joints in 70 to 90 degrees flexion, I P joints in full extension.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main communication and clinical domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
