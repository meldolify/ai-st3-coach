CLINICAL SCENARIO BLOCK

(CASE-SPECIFIC)

SCENARIO TITLE

Free Flap Compromise

PROBING QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the consultant may ask during the call.
Use these to challenge or guide the candidate after they finish presenting.
Delivery specificity is controlled by the personality layer.)

"Is this arterial or venous compromise — how can you tell?"
"What bedside measures have you already tried?"
"Has the flap pinked up after loosening the dressings and releasing sutures?"
"Do you think this needs to go back to theatre?"
"How quickly do we need to re-explore — what is the salvage window?"
"What would you do in theatre if you found a venous thrombus?"
"Would you consider using leeches? What antibiotic would you need?"
"What is the overall salvage rate for free flaps?"

MAIN COMMUNICATION AND CLINICAL DOMAINS

Structured clinical presentation with accurate differentiation of arterial versus venous compromise
Immediate bedside salvage measures
Decision to re-explore and theatre preparation
Operative salvage approach including thrombectomy and vein grafting
Leech therapy and its management
Post-operative monitoring and communication with the patient

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation with accurate differentiation of arterial versus venous compromise
The candidate must correctly identify that this is venous congestion based on the clinical signs: blue-purple colour, brisk capillary refill with dark blood, tense and swollen flap, rapid dark bleeding on needle prick, present arterial Doppler signal, and absent venous Doppler signal.
The candidate must contrast these with the signs of arterial insufficiency: pale or white flap, absent or sluggish capillary refill, soft cool flap, absent arterial Doppler, and no bleeding on needle prick.
The candidate must convey the urgency and inform the consultant that the flap is in jeopardy and may need to return to theatre.

Immediate bedside salvage measures
The candidate must immediately remove all dressings and sutures that may be compressing the pedicle.
The candidate must reposition the patient to avoid any kinking or pressure on the pedicle.
The candidate must strip the drains to relieve any clots that may be preventing evacuation of haematoma.
The candidate must ensure the patient is warm, well hydrated, and haemodynamically stable.
The candidate must avoid vasoconstrictors.
The candidate must keep the patient N B M in case theatre is needed.

Decision to re-explore and theatre preparation
The candidate must state that if bedside measures do not improve the flap within a short period, urgent return to theatre is required.
The candidate must know that salvage rates are highest when explored within 1 to 2 hours of onset.
The candidate must inform theatres, request an anaesthetic review, and prepare the patient for theatre including cross-matching blood.
The candidate must consent the patient for flap re-exploration including the possibility of vein graft, revision of the anastomosis, and the risk of flap loss.
The candidate must inform the consultant and request them to come in.

Operative salvage approach including thrombectomy and vein grafting
The candidate must describe the operative approach: deliver the flap and inspect the pedicle along its full length, wash out any haematoma, unkink or reposition any twisted vessels, and perform a milking test to assess flow.
The candidate must describe management of a venous thrombus: resect the thrombosed segment, re-anastomose with fresh vessel ends, and use an interposition vein graft if there is tension or insufficient vessel length.
The candidate must mention systemic heparinisation or local heparin flush.

Leech therapy and its management
The candidate must know that leeches are indicated for venous congestion when surgical salvage alone is insufficient.
The candidate must know that leeches secrete hirudin, an anticoagulant, which promotes continued bleeding after detachment.
The candidate must know that antibiotic prophylaxis against Aeromonas hydrophila is mandatory — ciprofloxacin is first line.
The candidate must mention monitoring haemoglobin closely as patients can become significantly anaemic from leech therapy.

Post-operative monitoring and communication with the patient
The candidate must describe the post-operative monitoring protocol: hourly flap observations for colour, capillary refill, temperature, turgor, and Doppler signal.
The candidate must discuss informing the patient of the situation and the need to return to theatre, explaining the risks including potential flap loss.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation with accurate differentiation of arterial versus venous compromise
The candidate mentions implantable Cook Doppler for monitoring buried flaps.
The candidate discusses indocyanine green angiography in theatre to assess flap perfusion.

Immediate bedside salvage measures
The candidate mentions releasing any haematoma at the bedside if there is a visible collection around the pedicle.
The candidate discusses that in a D I E P flap, patient positioning is important to avoid abdominal wall tension that could kink the pedicle.

Decision to re-explore and theatre preparation
The candidate knows that overall free flap salvage rates are approximately 50 to 75 percent, and that venous problems are more common than arterial but arterial problems have a worse salvage rate.
The candidate discusses risk factors for flap failure: smoking, diabetes, previous radiotherapy, hypercoagulable states, and obesity.

Operative salvage approach including thrombectomy and vein grafting
The candidate mentions the use of local thrombolytic agents (t P A) applied to a thrombosed anastomosis.
The candidate discusses the saphenous vein or cephalic vein as sources for vein grafts.

Leech therapy and its management
The candidate knows that leeches should continue until neovascularisation establishes venous drainage, typically 3 to 5 days.
The candidate discusses dextran infusion and aspirin protocols for free flap anticoagulation, noting that dextran has largely fallen out of favour.

Post-operative monitoring and communication with the patient
The candidate discusses secondary reconstruction options if salvage fails.
The candidate mentions the psychological impact on the patient and the importance of sensitive communication.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main communication and clinical domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
