SCENARIO TITLE

Replantation

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation covering injury details and storage of amputated part
The candidate must introduce themselves, state their role, and convey the urgency.
The candidate must present the mechanism of injury, timing, hand dominance, and how the amputated part has been stored and transported.
The candidate must mention the ischaemia time and note that the digit was appropriately transported on moist gauze on ice.

Indications and contraindications for replantation
The candidate must know the absolute indications for replantation: thumb amputation, multiple digit amputation, hand or wrist amputation, paediatric amputation, and amputation proximal to the superficial palmar arch.
The candidate must recognise that a single index finger amputation is not an absolute indication but there are strong relative indications in this case: dominant hand, important finger for occupation and hobbies.
The candidate must identify the relative contraindications: smoking, diabetes, zone 2 flexor tendon involvement, and comminuted fracture extending into the P I P J.

Patient factors and counselling for informed decision-making
The candidate must recognise the importance of the index finger for this patient given his occupation as a construction worker and his hobby of playing the saxophone.
The candidate must explain that the patient should be counselled about both options: replantation with a long recovery versus revision amputation with a quicker return to work.
The candidate must mention consenting for replantation with the additional consent for revision amputation if replantation is not feasible intraoperatively, and consent for vein graft.

Theatre preparation and surgical planning
The candidate must state they would like the consultant to come in to assist with the microsurgery.
The candidate must mention: keeping the patient N B M, I V antibiotics, tetanus booster, analgesia, bloods including group and save and cross-match, urgent anaesthetic review, and informing theatre staff.
The candidate must mention obtaining an X-ray of the rest of the hand to exclude other injuries.
The candidate must mention medical photography.

Operative approach and order of repair
The candidate must describe the order of repair: skeletal fixation first with K-wires, then tendon repair, then vascular repair (artery and vein), and finally nerve repair.
The candidate must mention working under the microscope and the need for a microsurgical set.
The candidate must state they can begin the operation by preparing the amputated part and identifying structures while waiting for the consultant.

Post-operative monitoring and hand therapy
The candidate must mention post-operative hand elevation and keeping the hand warm.
The candidate must describe hourly monitoring of the replanted digit for colour, capillary refill, temperature, and turgor.
The candidate must mention early hand therapy within 3 to 5 days.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation covering injury details and storage of amputated part
The candidate mentions the Tamai classification for level of amputation.
The candidate discusses warm ischaemia time of up to 12 hours for digits and cold ischaemia time of up to 24 hours.

Indications and contraindications for replantation
The candidate discusses that avulsion injuries carry a worse prognosis than clean sharp amputations.
The candidate mentions the ribbon sign as an indicator of vessel avulsion with poor prognosis.
The candidate knows that self-mutilation and severe psychiatric illness are contraindications.

Patient factors and counselling for informed decision-making
The candidate discusses the expected functional outcome honestly, including the fact that replanted fingers rarely achieve normal function.
The candidate mentions that even a partially functioning replanted finger may be functionally superior to a prosthesis for this patient.
The candidate discusses the approximately 50 percent success rate given this patient's risk factors.

Theatre preparation and surgical planning
The candidate proactively offers to begin the operation and prepare both the stump and amputated part while waiting for the consultant.
The candidate mentions the need for a C-arm machine in addition to the microscope and micro set.

Operative approach and order of repair
The candidate describes tagging the structures in the amputated part during back-table preparation before the patient is anaesthetised.
The candidate discusses the option of shortening the bone to allow tension-free vascular repair without vein grafts.
The candidate mentions the four-strand core suture technique for tendon repair.

Post-operative monitoring and hand therapy
The candidate mentions monitoring for signs of arterial insufficiency versus venous congestion and knows the clinical differences.
The candidate discusses the anticoagulation protocol post-replantation.
The candidate mentions monitoring urine output aiming for at least 0.5 ml per kg per hour.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main communication and clinical domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
