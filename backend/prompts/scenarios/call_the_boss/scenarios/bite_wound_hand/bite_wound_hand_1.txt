SCENARIO TITLE

Bite Wound to the Hand — Fight Bite

PROBING QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the consultant may ask during the call.
Use these to challenge or guide the candidate after they finish presenting.
Delivery specificity is controlled by the personality layer.)

"Why are you suspicious this is a fight bite rather than a fence injury?"
"What structures are at risk over the M C P joint?"
"What organisms are you concerned about in a human bite?"
"Does this patient definitely need to go to theatre or can we just treat with antibiotics?"
"Talk me through what you would do in theatre."
"Why must you not close this wound primarily?"
"Have you considered blood-borne virus risk?"

MAIN COMMUNICATION AND CLINICAL DOMAINS

Structured clinical presentation with recognition of a fight bite injury

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation with recognition of a fight bite injury
The candidate must recognise that a wound over the M C P joint is a fight bite until proven otherwise, regardless of the patient's reported history.
The candidate must present the case clearly including the suspicious mechanism, the 12-hour delay, the signs of early infection, and the location over the M C P J.
The candidate must convey that this requires urgent surgical assessment.

Assessment for joint involvement and tendon injury
The candidate must explain that the wound needs to be examined with the M C P joint in flexion to assess whether it communicates with the joint space, because the skin wound and tendon laceration occur at different levels when the fist is clenched versus extended.
The candidate must mention assessing the extensor tendon function, the joint capsule integrity, and the neurovascular status of the finger.
The candidate must mention that even if the wound appears superficial, formal surgical exploration is required if it overlies the M C P J.

Antibiotic therapy and organism awareness
The candidate must start I V antibiotics covering oral flora. Co-amoxiclav is the first-line agent.
The candidate must know that Eikenella corrodens is the typical culprit organism in human bite injuries, alongside Streptococci, Staphylococci, and anaerobes.
The candidate must mention tetanus prophylaxis.

Surgical planning including joint washout
The candidate must state that this patient requires formal surgical exploration and joint washout, not just antibiotics.
The candidate must describe the procedure: general or regional anaesthesia with arm tourniquet, extending the wound for adequate exposure, thorough debridement, opening and irrigating the M C P joint with copious saline, taking tissue and fluid samples for microbiology, and leaving the wound open.
The candidate must state that the wound must not be closed primarily due to the high risk of infection.
The candidate must mention splinting in the position of safe immobilisation.

Post-operative care and follow-up
The candidate must mention continued I V antibiotics adjusted based on culture results.
The candidate must mention strict hand elevation.
The candidate must plan a second look at 48 hours.
The candidate must mention early hand therapy once the wound is healing.

Blood-borne virus risk assessment
The candidate must mention counselling the patient about blood-borne virus risk from the human bite and arranging appropriate testing for H I V and hepatitis B.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation with recognition of a fight bite injury
The candidate discusses that patients often give a misleading history about the mechanism and that the wound location is the most important clue.
The candidate mentions that a delay in presentation beyond 24 hours significantly increases the risk of established infection.

Assessment for joint involvement and tendon injury
The candidate discusses the dynamic nature of the injury when the fist is clenched and explains why the tendon injury and joint breach occur at a different level to the skin wound.
The candidate mentions that the sagittal band and articular cartilage should be inspected for damage.
The candidate mentions looking for gas in the soft tissues on X-ray as an ominous sign of established infection.

Antibiotic therapy and organism awareness
The candidate knows the difference between organisms in human bites, dog bites, and cat bites.
The candidate mentions alternative antibiotics for penicillin-allergic patients.

Surgical planning including joint washout
The candidate discusses that even a clean-looking wound over the M C P J should be explored formally in theatre.
The candidate mentions the importance of not performing a simple washout in A and E as a substitute for formal theatre exploration.

Post-operative care and follow-up
The candidate discusses the complications of fight bite injuries including osteomyelitis, septic arthritis, tendon rupture, and chronic stiffness.
The candidate mentions documentation for potential medico-legal purposes given the likely assault.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main communication and clinical domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
