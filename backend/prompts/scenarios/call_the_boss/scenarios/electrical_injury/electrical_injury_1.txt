SCENARIO TITLE

Electrical Injury

PROBING QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the consultant may ask during the call.
Use these to challenge or guide the candidate after they finish presenting.
Delivery specificity is controlled by the personality layer.)

"What voltage are we talking about — high or low voltage?"
"The C K is very high — what does that tell you and what are the implications?"
"His urine is dark — what is causing that and how do you manage it?"
"Do you think he needs a fasciotomy tonight? Which compartments?"
"How are you fluid resuscitating this patient — can you use the Parkland formula?"
"What about the cardiac arrest — what monitoring does he need?"
"Is I T U aware? Do they have a bed?"
"Talk me through your surgical plan in theatre."

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation covering the severity and systemic nature of the injury
The candidate must recognise that high voltage electrical injury is a life-threatening emergency with implications far beyond the visible skin burns.
The candidate must present the case urgently and request the consultant to come in for theatre tonight.
The candidate must identify the entry and exit wounds and explain that the visible burns represent only a fraction of the total tissue injury as the current travels through deep tissues causing extensive muscle necrosis.

A T L S and E M S B assessment with focus on cardiac monitoring
The candidate must describe the primary survey findings including the secured airway, the haemodynamic instability, and the need for ongoing resuscitation.
The candidate must emphasise the need for continuous E C G monitoring given the cardiac arrest at scene and the mildly elevated troponin.
The candidate must mention correcting the hyperkalaemia urgently as it poses a risk of fatal arrhythmias. Treatment includes calcium gluconate for myocardial protection, insulin and dextrose, and sodium bicarbonate.

Recognition and management of rhabdomyolysis and myoglobinuria
The candidate must recognise the markedly elevated C K of 12,500, dark urine, and positive dipstick for blood as evidence of rhabdomyolysis and myoglobinuria.
The candidate must state that urine output should be maintained at greater than 1 ml per kg per hour as per E M S B guidance for electrical injuries.
The candidate must mention aggressive I V fluid resuscitation and consideration of alkalinisation of the urine with sodium bicarbonate.
The candidate must recognise the risk of acute kidney injury.

Assessment for compartment syndrome and indication for fasciotomy
The candidate must recognise that both forearms are tense and the right hand digits are dusky, indicating likely compartment syndrome.
The candidate must state that prophylactic fasciotomy is indicated in high voltage electrical injuries given the high likelihood of underlying muscle damage.
The candidate must mention that in sedated patients, clinical assessment of compartment syndrome is unreliable and compartment pressure measurement should be used if there is any doubt.

Fluid resuscitation strategy for electrical injury
The candidate must recognise that the modified Parkland formula can be used as a guide but that electrical injuries require significantly more fluid than the formula predicts because the visible burn underestimates total tissue injury.
The candidate must titrate fluids to urine output.

Surgical planning and post-operative I T U care
The candidate must describe the surgical plan: urgent fasciotomy of the affected compartments, identification and protection of named nerves, excision of any clearly necrotic muscle and skin, and wound coverage with simple dressings.
The candidate must arrange an I T U bed for post-operative care with continued cardiac monitoring, fluid management, and electrolyte correction.
The candidate must plan for a second look at 48 hours with further excision and debridement as needed.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Structured clinical presentation covering the severity and systemic nature of the injury
The candidate knows the difference between high voltage (greater than 1000 volts) and low voltage injuries and that high voltage carries a much worse prognosis.
The candidate discusses the different mechanisms of electrical injury: direct contact, arc, flash burn, and ground current.

A T L S and E M S B assessment with focus on cardiac monitoring
The candidate mentions requesting cardiology input given the cardiac arrest and elevated troponin.
The candidate mentions examining the eyes for cataracts and corneal damage as a late complication.
The candidate mentions examining for neurological injury.

Recognition and management of rhabdomyolysis and myoglobinuria
The candidate mentions testing the urine for myoglobin specifically.
The candidate discusses the use of mannitol to enhance diuresis and notes that if mannitol is used, urine output can no longer be used as a reliable marker of resuscitation adequacy.
The candidate mentions that M R I can provide detail on the anatomical site and severity of muscle injury.

Assessment for compartment syndrome and indication for fasciotomy
The candidate describes the specific fasciotomy incisions for the forearm: volar curvilinear incision with release of the carpal tunnel.
The candidate mentions that serial debridements are often needed as the extent of tissue necrosis may declare over several days.

Fluid resuscitation strategy for electrical injury
The candidate knows the Parkland formula details: 3 to 4 ml per kg per percentage T B S A, with the first half in the first 8 hours from injury and the remainder over 16 hours.
The candidate discusses the surgical endpoints for burns surgery: 2 to 3 hours operating time, up to 10 units transfused, temperature below 36 degrees, or the lethal triad.

Surgical planning and post-operative I T U care
The candidate discusses nutritional requirements and early enteral feeding.
The candidate mentions the importance of a multidisciplinary approach including I T U, burns surgery, cardiology, renal medicine, physiotherapy, and psychology.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main communication and clinical domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
