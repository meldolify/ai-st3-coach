SCENARIO TITLE

Skin Lesion Excision and Split Thickness Skin Graft Consent

PATIENT QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the patient or patient's relative may ask during the consent discussion.
Use these after the candidate finishes their explanation.
How anxious or questioning you are is controlled by the personality layer.)

"Will I be awake during the operation?"
"Where will the skin come from — will it hurt?"
"Why can you not just stitch the hole closed?"
"How long does the operation take?"
"How long do the dressings need to stay on?"
"Will I be able to walk after the operation?"
"Will I need any help at home — I live on my own?"
"What does the area look like once it has healed?"
"Is there any chance the cancer could come back?"

MAIN CONSENT DOMAINS

Introduction and patient identification
Procedure explanation including excision and split thickness skin graft
Anaesthesia and pain management
Post-operative care including graft dressings, donor site care, and mobility
Risks and complications
Recovery timeline and follow-up

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Introduction and patient identification
Confirming the patient's identity and the planned procedure.
Establishing rapport and checking the patient's current understanding.
Acknowledging the patient's concerns about living independently and managing dressings at home.

Procedure explanation
Explaining in lay terms that the operation involves removing the skin cancer with a margin of normal skin around it.
Explaining why a skin graft is needed: the gap left after removing the cancer is too large to close directly with stitches, so a thin layer of skin is taken from another area of the body to cover the wound.
Explaining what a split thickness skin graft is: a thin shaving of skin is taken from the thigh using a special blade, similar to peeling a thin layer from the surface. The donor site heals on its own like a graze.
Explaining that the skin graft is placed over the wound on the leg, trimmed to fit, and held in place with stitches and a bulky dressing.
Mentioning the approximate duration of surgery, typically 30 to 45 minutes.

Anaesthesia and pain management
Explaining that the procedure is performed under local anaesthetic with the patient awake.
Explaining that local anaesthetic injections will be given at both the excision site and the donor site.
Reassuring the patient about the injection process and that there should be no sharp pain during the procedure.

Post-operative care
Explaining that the graft dressing is bulky and must not be disturbed for approximately five days, when it will be checked in the dressings clinic.
Explaining that the donor site on the thigh will have a special dressing that is left in place until it peels off on its own, usually within two to three weeks.
Advising the patient to keep the leg elevated as much as possible in the first week to help the graft take.
Advising avoidance of getting the dressings wet.
Advising the patient to take prescribed painkillers and antibiotics.
Discussing the need for district nurse or clinic visits for dressing changes, given the patient lives alone.
Advising against driving on the day of surgery.

Risks and complications
Mentioning graft failure, either partial or complete, where the grafted skin does not survive. Explaining that if this happens the wound will need regular dressing changes and will heal on its own over time, or a further graft may be needed.
Mentioning bleeding and haematoma under the graft which can prevent it from taking.
Mentioning infection at either the graft site or the donor site.
Mentioning scarring at both sites.
Mentioning that the grafted area may look different from the surrounding skin in colour and texture, and this may be permanent.
Mentioning altered sensation or numbness at the graft and donor sites.
Mentioning wound breakdown or delayed healing, particularly on the lower leg.
Mentioning donor site pain, which is often described as worse than the graft site.
Mentioning recurrence of the cancer, noting that follow-up is important.

Recovery timeline
Advising that mobility will be limited for the first one to two weeks while the graft establishes.
Advising avoidance of prolonged standing and strenuous exercise for approximately four weeks.
Explaining that the donor site heals like a graze over two to three weeks.
Explaining that the grafted area will initially look dark or bruised and will gradually lighten over several months, but may always look different from the surrounding skin.
Advising that follow-up will include checking the pathology results and ongoing skin surveillance.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Procedure explanation
Explaining the difference between split thickness and full thickness skin grafts in lay terms and why a split thickness graft is appropriate for this defect.
Mentioning the use of a tie-over dressing or negative pressure dressing to hold the graft in place and improve take rates.
Explaining the importance of immobilisation of the grafted area to prevent shearing.

Risks and complications
Discussing factors that impair graft take: haematoma, seroma, infection, shearing, and poor vascularity of the wound bed.
Mentioning that lower leg wounds heal more slowly due to reduced blood supply, and this is particularly relevant in elderly patients.
Mentioning the risk of contracture at the graft site, particularly over joints.
Discussing the importance of good skin care and moisturising of the grafted area long-term.

Post-operative care
Discussing the need for social support or package of care during recovery if the patient lives alone.
Mentioning the role of the community nursing team for ongoing dressing changes.
Advising sun protection of the graft and donor site for at least twelve months.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main consent domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
