MAIN CONSENT DOMAINS

Introduction and patient identification
Procedure explanation including wide local excision and sentinel lymph node biopsy technique

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Introduction and patient identification
Confirming the patient's identity and the planned procedure.
Establishing rapport and checking the patient's current understanding.
Sensitivity to the patient's anxiety about the melanoma diagnosis and the possibility of spread.

Procedure explanation
Explaining the wide local excision: in lay terms, the previous biopsy site needs a wider area of normal skin removed around it, with a margin of one centimetre, to reduce the risk of any remaining cancer cells. This area will be closed directly with stitches.
Explaining the sentinel lymph node biopsy in lay terms: the sentinel node is the first lymph gland that the cancer would drain to, like the first station on a railway line. If this gland is clear of cancer, it is very unlikely that the cancer has spread further.
Explaining the technique: before the operation a radioactive tracer and sometimes a blue dye are injected near the melanoma site. These travel through the lymphatic channels to the sentinel node, allowing the surgeon to identify and remove it through a small cut in the groin.
Explaining that the sentinel node is sent to the pathology laboratory for detailed analysis, which takes approximately two to three weeks.
Explaining what happens if the sentinel node is positive: this will be discussed at the multidisciplinary team meeting and further treatment options may include further surgery, immunotherapy, or surveillance.
Mentioning the approximate duration of surgery, typically one to two hours.

Anaesthesia and operative environment
Explaining that the procedure is performed under general anaesthetic.
Mentioning the patient will meet the anaesthetist beforehand.
Explaining that a hand-held probe will be used in theatre to detect the radioactive tracer and locate the sentinel node.

Post-operative care
Explaining that the calf wound will be closed with stitches and the groin wound with stitches or clips.
Advising that the patient should expect to stay in hospital overnight or may go home the same day.
Advising the patient to keep both wounds clean and dry.
Explaining that stitches will be removed at approximately ten to fourteen days.
Advising elevation of the leg to reduce swelling.
Mentioning that the blue dye may turn the skin near the injection site blue temporarily and may also cause the urine to appear blue or green for a day or two, which is harmless.

Risks and complications
Mentioning bleeding and bruising at both surgical sites.
Mentioning infection.
Mentioning wound breakdown, particularly on the lower leg where healing can be slower.
Mentioning scarring at both the calf and groin sites.
Mentioning seroma at the groin site.
Mentioning lymphoedema of the leg, although the risk is lower with sentinel node biopsy alone compared to full lymph node dissection.
Mentioning numbness or altered sensation around both wounds.
Mentioning the small risk of a false negative result where the sentinel node is reported as clear but microscopic cancer cells are present elsewhere.
Mentioning allergic reaction to the blue dye, which is rare.
Mentioning D V T and P E.

Recovery timeline
Advising avoidance of strenuous exercise and heavy lifting for approximately two to three weeks.
Advising that the patient should be able to walk from the day of surgery but should take it easy for the first week.
Advising that driving can usually resume when the patient can comfortably perform an emergency stop, typically one to two weeks.
Explaining that a follow-up appointment will be arranged to discuss the pathology results, usually within two to three weeks.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Procedure explanation
Explaining the concept of lymphatic mapping and the role of nuclear medicine in pre-operative lymphoscintigraphy.
Mentioning that sometimes more than one sentinel node is identified and all will be removed.
Explaining the false negative rate of sentinel lymph node biopsy, approximately five percent, in appropriate terms.
Mentioning that S L N B is a staging procedure that provides prognostic information and may influence decisions about adjuvant systemic therapy.

Risks and complications
Discussing the specific risk of lower leg wound healing, particularly in older patients or those with peripheral vascular disease.
Mentioning the risk of injury to structures in the groin such as the femoral vessels or the saphenous vein, which is very rare.
Discussing anaphylaxis risk from patent blue dye, estimated at one to two percent.

Recovery timeline
Mentioning the role of the specialist skin cancer nurse in providing support and information.
Discussing the psychological impact of waiting for sentinel node results and offering appropriate reassurance.
Mentioning long-term surveillance requirements: regular skin checks and lymph node examinations.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
