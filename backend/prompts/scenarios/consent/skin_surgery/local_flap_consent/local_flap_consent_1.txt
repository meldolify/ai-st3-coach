SCENARIO TITLE

Skin Cancer Excision and Local Flap Consent

PATIENT QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the patient or patient's relative may ask during the consent discussion.
Use these after the candidate finishes their explanation.
How anxious or questioning you are is controlled by the personality layer.)

"Will I be awake for the operation?"
"Will it affect my eye — it is quite close?"
"How big will the scar be?"
"What does a local flap actually mean — where does the skin come from?"
"How long does the operation take?"
"Could the cancer come back even after you remove it?"
"I have diabetes — does that cause any extra problems?"
"When do the stitches come out?"
"Can I drive home afterwards?"

MAIN CONSENT DOMAINS

Introduction and patient identification
Procedure explanation including excision margins and local flap reconstruction
Anaesthesia and pain management
Post-operative care and wound management
Risks and complications
Recovery timeline and follow-up

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Introduction and patient identification
Confirming the patient's identity and the planned procedure.
Establishing rapport and checking the patient's current understanding of his diagnosis and the planned operation.
Acknowledging the patient's specific concerns about proximity to the eye.

Procedure explanation
Explaining in lay terms that the operation involves removing the skin cancer along with a rim of normal skin around it, called a margin, to ensure all the cancer cells are removed.
Explaining that the removed tissue is sent to the pathology laboratory to check that the edges are clear of cancer.
Explaining what a local flap is: skin from next to the gap is loosened and moved across to cover the hole while remaining attached to its own blood supply, rather than taking skin from a distant area.
Explaining that the donor area where the flap comes from will also need to be closed with stitches.
Mentioning that the exact flap design will be decided during the operation based on the size and shape of the defect after excision.
Explaining the approximate duration of surgery, typically 45 minutes to one hour.

Anaesthesia and pain management
Explaining that the procedure is performed under local anaesthetic with the patient awake.
Explaining the injection of local anaesthetic around the area, noting the initial sting that settles quickly.
Reassuring that there should be no sharp pain during the procedure, only pressure and pulling.

Post-operative care
Explaining that the wound will be closed with stitches and a dressing applied.
Advising that the stitches will be removed at approximately seven to ten days.
Advising the patient to keep the wound clean, dry, and elevated where possible.
Mentioning the importance of diabetes control for wound healing.
Advising the patient to take prescribed antibiotics and painkillers.

Risks and complications
Mentioning bleeding and bruising, particularly in the temple and peri-orbital region.
Mentioning infection, with increased risk noted due to diabetes.
Mentioning partial or complete flap failure where the moved skin loses its blood supply and may die, potentially requiring further surgery or a skin graft.
Mentioning scarring, explaining that the scar will be permanent but is positioned to follow natural skin creases where possible.
Mentioning altered sensation or numbness around the surgical site.
Mentioning damage to underlying structures, specifically the temporal branch of the facial nerve which could affect eyebrow movement.
Mentioning recurrence of the cancer, explaining that this is uncommon if the margins are clear but that follow-up monitoring is important.
Mentioning incomplete excision requiring further surgery.

Recovery timeline
Advising avoidance of strenuous activity and bending forward for approximately two weeks to reduce bleeding risk.
Advising against driving on the day of surgery and possibly for a few days afterwards depending on comfort and visual field.
Explaining that bruising and swelling will settle over two to three weeks.
Explaining the importance of follow-up both for wound review and ongoing skin cancer surveillance.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Procedure explanation
Discussing different local flap options for the temple such as rotation flap, advancement flap, or rhomboid flap, and explaining the choice in lay terms.
Mentioning the reconstructive ladder in simple terms: direct closure, skin graft, local flap, and why a flap provides better cosmetic and functional results in this area.
Explaining that the pathology result will be discussed at the follow-up appointment and will confirm whether the margins are clear.

Risks and complications
Mentioning the specific risk of temporal hollowing if deeper tissues are involved.
Discussing the risk of haematoma formation under the flap which could compromise flap survival.
Mentioning dog ear deformity at the ends of the flap which may require later revision.
Discussing the importance of sun protection and skin surveillance for new or recurrent skin cancers.

Post-operative care
Mentioning the role of H b A 1 c optimisation in wound healing for diabetic patients.
Advising scar massage and sun protection once the wound is fully healed.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA
