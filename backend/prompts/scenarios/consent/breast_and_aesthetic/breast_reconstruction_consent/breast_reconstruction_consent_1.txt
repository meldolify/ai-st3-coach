SCENARIO TITLE

Breast Reconstruction with D I E P Flap Consent

"How long does the operation take?"
"Will the new breast look like my other one?"
"What do you mean you connect blood vessels — what happens if they block?"
"Will I have feeling in the new breast?"
"How long will I be in hospital?"

MAIN CONSENT DOMAINS

Introduction and patient identification
Procedure explanation including donor site and microsurgery
Anaesthesia and operative environment
Post-operative care and monitoring
Risks and complications including flap failure
Recovery timeline and further procedures

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Introduction and patient identification
Confirming the patient's identity and the planned procedure.
Establishing rapport and checking the patient's current understanding of the reconstruction.
Sensitivity to the emotional context of breast cancer and body image.

Procedure explanation
Explaining in lay terms that the operation takes skin, fat, and blood vessels from the lower tummy and uses this tissue to create a new breast mound.
Explaining that the blood vessels from the tummy tissue are connected to blood vessels in the chest using a microscope, which is called microsurgery.
Explaining that the tummy wound is closed similarly to an abdominoplasty, leaving a low horizontal scar across the lower abdomen.
Explaining that the belly button will be repositioned.
Mentioning that the breast will be shaped during the operation but will not look exactly like the other side.
Explaining the approximate duration of surgery, typically six to eight hours with two surgical teams often working simultaneously.

Anaesthesia and operative environment
Explaining that the procedure is performed under general anaesthetic and may require a stay in a high dependency area or intensive care overnight for flap monitoring.
Mentioning the patient will meet the anaesthetist to discuss the anaesthetic and its specific risks.
Mentioning that an arterial line, urinary catheter, and multiple I V lines will be placed while asleep.

Post-operative care and monitoring
Explaining that the new breast will be monitored very closely in the first 24 to 48 hours, including regular checks of the flap colour, temperature, and blood flow using a small probe.
Explaining that drains will be placed at both the breast and the tummy and will be removed when output is minimal.
Advising that the patient should expect to stay in hospital for approximately five to seven days.
Explaining that movement will be restricted initially and mobilisation will be gradual with physiotherapy support.

Risks and complications
Mentioning the risk of flap failure, either partial or total, where the transferred tissue loses its blood supply and may need to be removed.
Explaining that if the blood vessels become blocked, the patient may need to return to theatre urgently, sometimes within hours, to try to save the flap.
Mentioning bleeding and haematoma.
Mentioning infection at either the breast or the donor site.
Mentioning wound breakdown at either site.
Mentioning scarring at both sites.
Mentioning numbness or altered sensation in the reconstructed breast and the lower abdomen.
Mentioning D V T and P E given the long operating time.
Mentioning abdominal wall weakness or hernia at the donor site.

Recovery timeline
Advising avoidance of heavy lifting for six to eight weeks.
Advising return to sedentary work at approximately four to six weeks.
Explaining that the breast shape will continue to change over several months as swelling settles.
Mentioning that further procedures may be needed, including nipple reconstruction, symmetrisation of the opposite breast, or tattooing of the nipple areola complex.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Procedure explanation
Explaining the difference between a D I E P flap and a T R A M flap, noting that the D I E P preserves the abdominal muscle and reduces the risk of abdominal wall weakness.
Mentioning alternative reconstruction options such as implant-based reconstruction or latissimus dorsi flap and briefly explaining why the D I E P was chosen.
Mentioning that a C T angiogram may have been performed to map the blood vessels in the tummy beforehand.

Risks and complications
Quoting approximate flap failure rates of one to five percent for total flap loss.
Mentioning fat necrosis in the reconstructed breast causing firmness or lumps that may mimic cancer recurrence on imaging.
Explaining that previous radiotherapy increases the risk of wound complications and may affect the final cosmetic result.
Mentioning the possibility of asymmetry requiring further surgery to the opposite breast.

Post-operative care
Explaining the use of anticoagulation and compression stockings to reduce the risk of D V T and P E.
Mentioning that the patient should avoid caffeine, nicotine, and constrictive clothing in the early post-operative period as these can affect blood flow to the flap.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main consent domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
