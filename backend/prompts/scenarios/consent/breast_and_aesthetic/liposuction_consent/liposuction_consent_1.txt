SCENARIO TITLE

Liposuction Consent

PATIENT QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the patient or patient's relative may ask during the consent discussion.

"Will I be asleep or awake for this?"
"How much fat will you remove?"
"Will the fat come back if I put weight on?"
"Will the skin sag after the fat is removed?"
"How long until I see the final result?"
"What does the compression garment look like — how long do I wear it?"
"Will I have scars?"
"I read online about fat embolism — should I be worried?"

MAIN CONSENT DOMAINS

Introduction and patient identification
Procedure explanation including tumescent technique and fat aspiration
Anaesthesia and operative environment
Post-operative care including compression garment
Risks and complications
Recovery timeline and realistic expectations

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Introduction and patient identification
Confirming the patient's identity and the planned procedure.
Establishing rapport and checking the patient's current understanding.
Clarifying that liposuction is a body contouring procedure, not a weight loss operation.

Procedure explanation
Explaining in lay terms that the operation removes localised deposits of fat using a thin tube called a cannula inserted through small cuts in the skin.
Explaining that a fluid containing local anaesthetic and adrenaline is injected into the fat first, called the tumescent technique, which numbs the area, reduces bleeding, and makes the fat easier to remove.
Explaining the areas being treated: bilateral flanks and lower abdomen.
Mentioning that the small incisions are typically five to ten millimetres long and are placed in natural creases where possible.
Explaining the approximate duration of surgery, typically one to two hours depending on the volume treated.
Mentioning that the amount of fat removed will be decided during the operation based on the response and contour.

Anaesthesia and operative environment
Explaining the type of anaesthesia, either general anaesthetic or local anaesthetic with sedation, depending on the extent of the procedure.
Explaining the sterile theatre environment.
Mentioning the patient will meet the anaesthetist if a general anaesthetic is planned.

Post-operative care
Explaining that a compression garment must be worn for approximately four to six weeks to help the skin conform to the new contour and reduce swelling.
Advising that the small incisions may leak fluid tinged with blood for the first 24 to 48 hours, which is normal.
Explaining that the patient may go home the same day or stay overnight.
Advising the patient to keep wounds clean and dry, take prescribed painkillers, and avoid smoking.

Risks and complications
Mentioning bleeding and bruising, which is expected and usually settles.
Mentioning infection.
Mentioning contour irregularities such as lumps, bumps, or asymmetry.
Mentioning skin surface irregularities or dimpling.
Mentioning seroma, a collection of fluid under the skin.
Mentioning numbness or altered sensation in the treated areas, usually temporary.
Mentioning scarring at the incision sites.
Mentioning the possibility of skin laxity if the skin does not contract adequately.
Mentioning D V T and P E as general risks.

Recovery timeline
Advising that bruising and swelling will be significant for the first two to three weeks.
Advising return to sedentary work at approximately one week.
Advising avoidance of strenuous exercise for approximately four weeks.
Explaining that the final result will not be apparent for approximately three to six months as swelling resolves.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Procedure explanation
Explaining the difference between liposuction techniques such as suction-assisted, power-assisted, and ultrasound-assisted in lay terms.
Mentioning the maximum safe volume of fat that can be removed in a single session, typically up to five percent of body weight.
Discussing the concept of fat redistribution, explaining that treated areas will not regain fat in the same way but untreated areas may become more prominent with weight gain.

Risks and complications
Mentioning fat embolism as a very rare but serious risk and explaining it in reassuring terms.
Mentioning lidocaine toxicity from the tumescent fluid as a rare risk, particularly with large volume liposuction.
Mentioning thermal injury if ultrasound or laser-assisted techniques are used.
Discussing the importance of maintaining a stable weight for long-term results.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main consent domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
