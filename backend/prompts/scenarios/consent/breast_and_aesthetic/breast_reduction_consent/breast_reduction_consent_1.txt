SCENARIO TITLE

Bilateral Breast Reduction Consent

PATIENT QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the patient or patient's relative may ask during the consent discussion.
Use these after the candidate finishes their explanation.
How anxious or questioning you are is controlled by the personality layer.)

"Will I still be able to breastfeed afterwards?"
"How much will you take away — can I choose my size?"
"What will the scars look like?"
"Will I lose sensation in my nipples?"
"How long will I be in hospital?"
"When can I go back to work — I am a teacher and quite active?"
"Do I need to wear a special bra afterwards?"
"You mentioned the nipple could lose its blood supply — how often does that happen?"
"Will my breasts stay the same size or could they grow back?"

MAIN CONSENT DOMAINS

Introduction and patient identification
Procedure explanation including incision pattern, tissue removal, and nipple repositioning
Anaesthesia and operative environment
Post-operative care including drains, compression, and wound care
Risks and complications
Recovery timeline and return to activities

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Introduction and patient identification
Confirming the patient's identity and the planned procedure.
Establishing rapport and checking the patient's current understanding.
Acknowledging the patient's symptoms of neck pain, shoulder grooving, and skin irritation as the indication for surgery.

Procedure explanation
Explaining in lay terms that the operation reduces the size and weight of the breasts by removing excess breast tissue, fat, and skin.
Explaining that the nipple is moved up to a higher position on the breast while keeping it attached to its blood supply.
Describing the typical incision pattern: around the nipple, a vertical line from the nipple downwards, and a horizontal line along the breast crease, creating an anchor-shaped or inverted T scar.
Explaining that pre-operative markings will be drawn on the breasts before surgery while standing, and a chaperone will be present.
Mentioning that the exact amount of tissue removed cannot be guaranteed and perfect symmetry is not possible.
Explaining the approximate duration of surgery, typically two to three hours.

Anaesthesia and operative environment
Explaining that the procedure is performed under general anaesthetic.
Mentioning that the patient will meet the anaesthetist beforehand.
Explaining the sterile theatre environment.

Post-operative care
Explaining that drains may be left in each breast and are usually removed before discharge.
Advising that the patient should expect to stay in hospital for one to two nights.
Explaining the need for a supportive sports bra to be worn day and night for approximately six weeks.
Advising the patient to keep wounds clean and dry, take prescribed antibiotics and painkillers, and avoid smoking.
Explaining that the first dressing check will be at approximately one week.

Risks and complications
Mentioning bleeding and haematoma, with possible return to theatre or need for blood transfusion.
Mentioning infection.
Mentioning scarring, including the anchor-shaped scar pattern, and the possibility of hypertrophic or keloid scarring.
Mentioning altered or loss of nipple sensation, which may be temporary or permanent.
Mentioning partial or total nipple necrosis if the blood supply is compromised, noting this is rare.
Mentioning fat necrosis causing lumps in the breast.
Mentioning asymmetry between the two breasts.
Mentioning wound breakdown, particularly where the scars meet.
Mentioning the potential impact on breastfeeding, which cannot be guaranteed after reduction.

Recovery timeline
Advising avoidance of heavy lifting, strenuous exercise, and contact sports for approximately six weeks.
Advising return to sedentary work at approximately two weeks.
Advising avoidance of swimming until wounds are fully healed.
Explaining that the breasts will be swollen and bruised initially and the final shape will not be apparent for several months.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Procedure explanation
Explaining the different pedicle techniques in lay terms, noting that the nipple stays connected to underlying tissue to maintain its blood supply and sensation.
Mentioning that the removed breast tissue is sent to pathology as a routine measure, particularly in patients with a family history of breast cancer.
Discussing realistic expectations about cup size, noting the surgeon aims for a proportionate result but exact cup size cannot be guaranteed.

Risks and complications
Mentioning the possibility of requiring further surgery for asymmetry correction or dog ear revision.
Explaining that weight changes after surgery may alter the breast size and shape.
Discussing the impact on mammographic screening and the need to inform the radiologist of previous breast surgery.
Mentioning D V T and P E as general risks of surgery under general anaesthetic, with discussion of prophylactic measures.

Recovery timeline
Advising scar massage with moisturiser once wounds are fully healed.
Explaining that scars will initially be red and raised but typically fade over twelve to eighteen months.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main consent domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
