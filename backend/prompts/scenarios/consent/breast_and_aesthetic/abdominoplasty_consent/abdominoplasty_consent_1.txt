SCENARIO TITLE

Abdominoplasty Consent

PATIENT QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the patient or patient's relative may ask during the consent discussion.
Use these after the candidate finishes their explanation.
How anxious or questioning you are is controlled by the personality layer.)

"Will I be asleep for the whole thing?"
"How long does the operation take?"
"What will the scar look like — will people see it if I wear a bikini?"
"What happens to my belly button — do you move it?"
"How long will I be in hospital?"
"When can I drive again?"
"When can I go back to work — I have a desk job?"
"You mentioned drains — what are those and when do they come out?"
"What if I put weight on again afterwards — will it come back?"
"What is the worst thing that could go wrong?"

MAIN CONSENT DOMAINS

Introduction and patient identification
Procedure explanation including skin excision, muscle tightening, and umbilical repositioning
Anaesthesia and operative environment
Post-operative care including drains, binder, and restrictions
Risks and complications
Recovery timeline and return to activities

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Introduction and patient identification
Confirming the patient's identity and the planned procedure.
Establishing rapport and checking the patient's existing understanding before starting the explanation.
Ensuring privacy and offering a chaperone if the patient would like one for the pre-operative marking.

Procedure explanation
Explaining in lay terms that the operation removes excess skin and fat from the lower abdomen through a long cut low across the tummy, usually hidden within the bikini line.
Explaining that the belly button will need to be repositioned by making a small cut around it and bringing it out through the tightened skin in its new position.
Mentioning that in some cases the underlying abdominal muscles are tightened with stitches to improve the contour, a procedure called repair of the rectus diastasis.
Explaining that the removed tissue is weighed but the exact amount cannot be guaranteed beforehand.
Mentioning that in cases with significant excess skin a vertical scar may also be needed, giving a T-shaped scar.

Anaesthesia and operative environment
Explaining that the procedure is performed under general anaesthetic and that the patient will meet the anaesthetist beforehand to discuss the anaesthetic and its risks.
Explaining the sterile theatre environment in a reassuring way.
Mentioning the approximate duration of surgery, typically two to three hours.

Post-operative care
Explaining that one or two drains will be left in place to collect fluid and that these are usually removed before discharge once output is minimal.
Explaining the abdominal binder or compression garment and how long it should be worn, typically four to six weeks.
Advising that the patient should expect to stay in hospital for one to two nights.
Explaining that dressings will be checked at a follow-up appointment in approximately one week.
Advising the patient to keep wounds clean and dry, avoid smoking, take prescribed antibiotics and painkillers, and avoid heavy lifting.

Risks and complications
Mentioning bleeding and the possibility of a haematoma requiring return to theatre.
Mentioning infection.
Mentioning wound breakdown or delayed healing, particularly at the T-junction if present.
Mentioning scarring including hypertrophic or keloid scars and explaining the scar will be permanent but should fade over time.
Mentioning seroma, a collection of fluid under the skin.
Mentioning altered sensation or numbness of the abdominal skin which may be permanent.
Mentioning asymmetry or an unsatisfactory cosmetic result.
Mentioning D V T and P E as risks of a general anaesthetic and prolonged surgery.

Recovery timeline
Advising return to sedentary work at approximately two weeks.
Advising no heavy lifting, manual work, or strenuous exercise for approximately six weeks.
Advising avoidance of driving for a minimum of two weeks.
Explaining that swelling and bruising will settle gradually over several weeks and the final result will not be apparent for several months.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Procedure explanation
Explaining the concept of flap undermining and how the blood supply to the remaining skin is preserved, and why smoking increases the risk of skin flap necrosis.
Mentioning liposuction may be combined with abdominoplasty in some cases to improve contour.
Explaining the difference between a full abdominoplasty and a mini-abdominoplasty and why the full procedure has been recommended for this patient.

Risks and complications
Mentioning fat necrosis and palpable lumps.
Mentioning the risk of injury to deeper structures including bowel, although extremely rare.
Mentioning dog ears at the lateral ends of the scar that may require later revision.
Mentioning the possibility of unsatisfactory belly button appearance requiring revision.
Discussing V T E prophylaxis measures including T E D stockings, early mobilisation, and pharmacological thromboprophylaxis.

Recovery timeline
Advising the patient to start scar massage with moisturiser once the wound is fully healed, usually at around four to six weeks.
Discussing realistic expectations and that the result may not be perfect or symmetrical.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main consent domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
