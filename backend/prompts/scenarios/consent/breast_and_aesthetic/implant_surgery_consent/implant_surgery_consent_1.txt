SCENARIO TITLE

Breast Reconstruction with Tissue Expander and Implant Consent

PATIENT QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the patient or patient's relative may ask during the consent discussion.
Use these after the candidate finishes their explanation.
How anxious or questioning you are is controlled by the personality layer.)

"What is the difference between the expander and the final implant?"
"How many operations will I need altogether?"
"Will the implant feel natural?"
"How long does the implant last — will it need replacing?"
"What happens if my body rejects it?"
"You mentioned something about capsular contracture — what does that mean in plain English?"
"Will I be able to have an M R I scan with the implant in?"
"When can I go back to work?"
"How long will I be in hospital this time?"

MAIN CONSENT DOMAINS

Introduction and patient identification
Procedure explanation including expander insertion and the two-stage process
Anaesthesia and operative environment
Post-operative care and expansion visits
Risks and complications including implant-specific risks
Recovery timeline and second stage procedure

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Introduction and patient identification
Confirming the patient's identity and the planned procedure.
Establishing rapport and checking the patient's current understanding of implant-based reconstruction.
Sensitivity to the emotional context of breast cancer and reconstruction.

Procedure explanation
Explaining in lay terms that this is the first of two operations: today a tissue expander is placed under the chest muscle, and once it is fully expanded it will be exchanged for a permanent implant at a second operation.
Explaining that the expander is like a deflated balloon that is gradually filled with salt water over several weeks through a small port, which is accessed by a needle through the skin.
Explaining that the expansion process takes approximately two to three months with visits every one to two weeks.
Explaining that the second operation to exchange the expander for the permanent implant is shorter and involves removing the expander and replacing it with a silicone implant.
Mentioning the approximate duration of today's operation, typically one to two hours.

Anaesthesia and operative environment
Explaining that the procedure is performed under general anaesthetic.
Mentioning the patient will meet the anaesthetist beforehand.
Explaining the sterile theatre environment.

Post-operative care
Explaining that a drain will be left in place and usually removed before discharge.
Advising that the patient should expect to stay in hospital for one to two nights.
Explaining the need for a supportive bra and avoidance of underwired bras.
Advising the patient to keep the wound clean and dry, take prescribed antibiotics and painkillers, and avoid smoking.
Explaining the expansion schedule and what to expect at each expansion visit.

Risks and complications
Mentioning bleeding and haematoma.
Mentioning infection, noting that infection around an implant may require removal of the expander.
Mentioning wound breakdown or skin flap necrosis, particularly in irradiated skin.
Mentioning expander or implant exposure or extrusion.
Mentioning capsular contracture, explaining in lay terms that the body forms a scar capsule around any implant and sometimes this tightens, making the breast feel hard and potentially painful, requiring further surgery.
Mentioning implant rupture or deflation.
Mentioning asymmetry compared to the other breast.
Mentioning altered or loss of sensation in the chest wall.
Mentioning the need for implant replacement in the future, typically every ten to fifteen years.

Recovery timeline
Advising avoidance of heavy lifting and strenuous upper body exercise for approximately six weeks.
Advising return to sedentary work at approximately two to three weeks.
Explaining that the final cosmetic result will not be apparent until after the second stage operation and further settling over several months.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Procedure explanation
Mentioning the option of an acellular dermal matrix or mesh to support the lower pole of the implant and its benefits for implant coverage.
Explaining the difference between subpectoral and prepectoral implant placement in lay terms.
Mentioning that symmetrisation surgery to the opposite breast may be offered at the second stage.
Discussing the choice between round and anatomical implants and silicone versus saline.

Risks and complications
Mentioning B I A dash A L C L, a rare type of lymphoma associated with textured implants, in an appropriate and reassuring way.
Mentioning breast implant illness as a concern some patients have, acknowledging uncertainty in the evidence.
Explaining that implant-based reconstruction may be less suitable in patients who have had radiotherapy due to higher complication rates.
Mentioning animation deformity with subpectoral placement, where the breast moves when the chest muscle contracts.
Mentioning that M R I screening is recommended periodically to check implant integrity.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main consent domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
