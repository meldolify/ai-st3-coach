Local Flap Reconstruction of Nasal Defect Consent

MAIN CONSENT DOMAINS

Introduction and patient identification
Procedure explanation including flap design and reconstruction of the nasal defect
Anaesthesia and pain management
Post-operative care including wound care and anticoagulation management
Risks and complications
Recovery timeline and cosmetic outcome

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Introduction and patient identification
Confirming the patient's identity and the planned procedure.
Establishing rapport and checking the patient's understanding of the Mohs surgery outcome and the need for reconstruction.
Acknowledging the patient's concerns about the appearance of her nose.

Procedure explanation
Explaining in lay terms that the cancer has been completely removed and today's operation is to close the hole using nearby skin that is moved across to fill the gap, which is called a local flap.
Explaining the concept of a local flap in simple terms: the skin next to the hole is loosened and rotated or advanced to cover the defect while remaining attached to its own blood supply.
Explaining that the specific flap design, such as a bilobed flap or nasolabial flap, will be decided based on the size and exact location of the defect.
Mentioning that the donor area where the skin is taken from will also be closed with stitches, resulting in additional scars.
Explaining the approximate duration of surgery, typically 45 minutes to one hour.
Explaining that pre-operative markings will be drawn on the nose and surrounding area.

Anaesthesia and pain management
Explaining that the procedure is performed under local anaesthetic with the patient awake.
Explaining that injections of local anaesthetic will be given around the nose and that the initial injection stings but the area then becomes numb.
Reassuring the patient that there should be no sharp pain during the procedure, only a sensation of pressure and pulling, and that additional local anaesthetic can be given if needed.

Post-operative care
Explaining that the wound will be closed with fine stitches and a dressing will be applied.
Explaining that the stitches will be removed at approximately seven to ten days.
Advising the patient to keep the wound clean and dry.
Addressing anticoagulation management: explaining when warfarin will be restarted and the plan for bridging anticoagulation, in coordination with the anticoagulation team.
Explaining that the area will be bruised and swollen initially, particularly around the eyes.

Risks and complications
Mentioning bleeding, noting the increased risk due to warfarin use and the facial blood supply.
Mentioning haematoma under the flap which may require return to theatre.
Mentioning infection.
Mentioning partial or complete flap failure where the transferred skin loses its blood supply and may die, requiring further surgery or healing by secondary intention.
Mentioning scarring on the nose and the donor area, noting that scars on the nose are generally well-concealed along natural creases.
Mentioning altered sensation or numbness around the nose.
Mentioning distortion of the nasal shape or nostril asymmetry.
Mentioning wound breakdown.

Recovery timeline
Advising that bruising and swelling will settle over two to three weeks.
Explaining that the scar will initially be red and firm but will soften and fade over six to twelve months.
Advising that the final cosmetic result may not be apparent for several months and a further minor revision procedure may be offered if needed.
Advising avoidance of strenuous activity, heavy lifting, and bending forward for approximately two weeks to reduce bleeding risk.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Procedure explanation
Explaining the specific flap options for nasal reconstruction: bilobed flap for small defects, nasolabial flap for larger defects, or paramedian forehead flap for full-thickness defects, and why the chosen option is appropriate.
Mentioning that reconstruction aims to replace tissue with like tissue, matching colour, texture, and thickness.
Explaining the subunit principle of nasal reconstruction in lay terms: the nose is made up of natural sections, and scars placed along the borders of these sections are less visible.

Risks and complications
Mentioning trapdoor deformity or pincushioning where the flap becomes raised and puffy, which may require later revision.
Mentioning the risk of skin graft being needed if the flap is insufficient to close the defect.
Discussing the specific anticoagulation-related bleeding risks and the balance between bleeding risk and thromboembolic risk.

Recovery timeline
Advising scar massage once the wound is fully healed.
Mentioning that sun protection of the scar is important for at least twelve months to prevent hyperpigmentation.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main consent domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
