SCENARIO TITLE

Radial Forearm Free Flap Consent

PATIENT QUESTIONS

(Internal reference only. Questions the patient or patient's relative may ask during the consent discussion.
Use these after the candidate finishes their explanation.
How anxious or questioning you are is controlled by the personality layer.)

"How long will the operation take?"
"What do you mean you connect the blood vessels — can you explain that more simply?"
"Will my arm still work properly after you take the skin?"
"What happens if the flap does not survive?"
"How long will I be in hospital?"
"Will I be able to eat and drink normally afterwards?"
"Will I have a tube in my throat — for how long?"
"What will the scar on my arm look like?"
"When can I go back to work?"
"How will you check the flap is working after the operation?"

MAIN CONSENT DOMAINS

Introduction and patient identification
Procedure explanation including donor site, flap harvest, and microsurgical anastomosis
Anaesthesia and operative environment
Post-operative care and flap monitoring
Risks and complications including flap failure
Recovery timeline and functional outcomes

ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

Introduction and patient identification
Confirming the patient's identity and the planned procedure.
Establishing rapport and checking the patient's current understanding.
Sensitivity to the anxiety of a cancer diagnosis and the magnitude of the planned surgery.

Procedure explanation
Explaining in lay terms that the operation has two parts: the head and neck surgeons will remove the cancer from the floor of the mouth, and the plastic surgery team will take a piece of skin and underlying tissue from the inner aspect of the forearm to fill the gap.
Explaining that the blood vessels supplying the forearm skin are carefully divided and then reconnected to blood vessels in the neck using a microscope, called microsurgery, to establish blood flow to the transferred tissue.
Explaining that before surgery an Allen test will be performed to check the hand has adequate blood supply from the other artery before the radial artery is taken.
Explaining that the forearm donor site is usually closed with a skin graft taken from the upper thigh.
Mentioning the approximate duration of surgery, typically six to ten hours with two teams often working at the same time to reduce overall operating time.

Anaesthesia and operative environment
Explaining that the procedure is performed under general anaesthetic and may require a night in intensive care or high dependency for close monitoring.
Mentioning that a tracheostomy may be performed to protect the airway in the early post-operative period, and explaining what this means in lay terms.
Mentioning that a nasogastric tube may be placed for feeding while the mouth heals.

Post-operative care and monitoring
Explaining that the flap will be closely monitored every one to two hours for the first 48 to 72 hours, checking colour, warmth, and blood flow.
Explaining that if a problem with the blood supply is detected, the patient may need to return to theatre urgently to try to save the flap.
Explaining that drains will be placed in the neck and removed when output is minimal.
Advising that the patient should expect to be in hospital for approximately seven to fourteen days.
Explaining that a speech and language therapist and dietician will be involved in the recovery.

Risks and complications
Mentioning flap failure, either partial or total, where the tissue loses its blood supply and may need to be removed, requiring alternative reconstruction.
Mentioning bleeding and haematoma in the neck, which can compromise the airway and require urgent return to theatre.
Mentioning infection at any of the surgical sites.
Mentioning wound breakdown at the donor site, mouth, or neck.
Mentioning scarring at the forearm, thigh, and neck.
Mentioning altered sensation or numbness at the forearm donor site.
Mentioning weakness in grip strength, usually temporary.
Mentioning changes to speech, swallowing, and eating, which may be temporary or long-term depending on the extent of the resection.
Mentioning D V T and P E given the prolonged surgery.
Mentioning damage to nerves in the neck including the marginal mandibular branch of the facial nerve.

Recovery timeline
Advising that recovery from this type of surgery is gradual over several weeks to months.
Explaining that diet will progress from liquid to soft to normal food over several weeks.
Advising that speech therapy may be needed.
Mentioning that follow-up will include oncological surveillance as well as monitoring of the reconstruction.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

Procedure explanation
Explaining the concept of the Allen test in lay terms: we check that the other artery in your wrist can keep your hand alive before we use one for the flap.
Mentioning alternative flap options such as anterolateral thigh flap and explaining why the radial forearm flap was chosen for this defect.
Explaining the role of the multidisciplinary team including head and neck surgeons, plastic surgeons, anaesthetists, oncologists, speech therapists, and specialist nurses.

Risks and complications
Quoting approximate flap failure rates of two to five percent.
Mentioning the risk of fistula formation between the mouth and the neck.
Mentioning the possibility of exposed tendons at the forearm donor site if the skin graft does not take fully.
Discussing the impact of previous radiotherapy or adjuvant radiotherapy on healing.
Mentioning cold intolerance at the donor forearm.

Post-operative care
Explaining the rationale for keeping the patient nil by mouth initially and how nutrition is maintained via the nasogastric tube.
Mentioning that the tracheostomy is temporary and is usually removed within a few days once the airway swelling has settled.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.

END OF SCENARIO CRITERIA

Once the main consent domains have been reasonably covered, or you judge that the scenario time has elapsed, end the scenario naturally.
