ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Urgency and rationale for the procedure
Explaining compartment syndrome in simple terms: the muscles in the leg sit in tight compartments wrapped in a strong tissue called fascia. After the fracture, swelling inside these compartments has built up and the pressure is squeezing the blood vessels and nerves, cutting off blood supply to the muscles.
Explaining clearly that this is an emergency. If the pressure is not released within hours, the muscles and nerves will be permanently damaged. The patient could lose function in the leg or, in the worst case, lose the leg.
Emphasising that the operation cannot wait until the morning. Every hour of delay increases the risk of irreversible damage.
Acknowledging the patient's frustration at needing another operation and validating their feelings.

Operative environment and anaesthesia
Explaining that the procedure is performed under G A. The patient will be put to sleep again.
The anaesthetist will review the patient and explain the anaesthetic.
Mentioning the sterile theatre environment.
Explaining that the operation will happen as soon as a theatre is available.

Operative technique
Explaining that the surgeon makes two long incisions along the leg to release all four compartments.
Describing in simple terms: one incision on the outer side of the leg to release the front and side compartments, and one on the inner side to release the two compartments at the back.
Explaining that the fascia, the tight wrapping around the muscles, is cut open along the full length of the incision to release the pressure.
Explaining that the surgeon will check the muscles underneath to make sure they are healthy and have blood supply.
Being honest that the wounds will be long and will be left open. They cannot be stitched closed today because the tissues are swollen. They will be covered with dressings.
Stating the approximate duration: 30 to 60 minutes.

Post-operative care and wound management
Explaining that the patient will return to the ward with the wounds open and dressed. The leg will be elevated to reduce swelling.
Explaining that the patient will need to return to theatre in approximately 48 hours for a second look. At that stage the surgeon will assess the muscles and plan wound closure.
Explaining the options for wound closure: if the swelling has settled, the wounds may be stitched closed directly. If not, a skin graft may be needed, or a negative pressure dressing may be applied to help the wounds close gradually.
Mentioning that the orthopaedic team will also plan the definitive management of the fracture.
Advising that the hospital stay will be at least several days, potentially longer depending on wound healing and fracture management.
Explaining that physiotherapy will begin as soon as possible to maintain movement and prevent stiffness.

Risks and complications
Muscle damage and necrosis: despite releasing the pressure, some muscle may already be damaged. Dead muscle will be removed to prevent infection and complications.
Nerve damage: the nerves may be damaged by the compartment syndrome itself, causing weakness or numbness in the foot. This may be temporary or permanent.
Bleeding: the incisions will bleed and blood transfusion may occasionally be required.
Infection: open wounds are at risk of infection. Antibiotics will be given.
Scarring: the incisions are long and the scars will be significant. If a skin graft is needed, there will also be a scar at the donor site.
Need for skin grafting: common if the wounds cannot be closed directly.
Stiffness and contracture: prolonged immobility and swelling can cause stiffness in the knee and ankle.
Rhabdomyolysis: dead muscle releases proteins into the bloodstream that can damage the kidneys. Blood tests and urine output will be monitored closely.
Amputation: in the most severe cases, if the muscles are found to be non-viable, amputation may be necessary. This is rare but must be mentioned.
D V T and P E: risk of blood clots in the legs, particularly with immobility. Preventive measures including blood thinning injections and compression stockings will be used.
Further surgery: additional operations for wound management, fracture fixation, and reconstruction are likely.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Urgency and rationale for the procedure
Explaining the pathophysiology: increased intra-compartmental pressure exceeds capillary perfusion pressure, leading to ischaemia. The threshold for fasciotomy is a compartment pressure within 30 millimetres of mercury of the diastolic blood pressure.
Mentioning that clinical signs are the primary basis for decision-making and that compartment pressure measurement is an adjunct in doubtful cases.

Operative technique
Naming the four compartments of the lower leg: anterior, lateral, superficial posterior, and deep posterior.
Explaining the landmarks for the two incisions: anterolateral incision two finger breadths lateral to the tibial crest, and posteromedial incision two finger breadths behind the posteromedial tibial border.
Mentioning the need to protect the superficial peroneal nerve in the lateral compartment and the saphenous vein and nerve in the posteromedial incision.

Post-operative care and wound management
Explaining the concept of muscle viability assessment at second look: viable muscle should be pink, contractile when stimulated, and bleed when cut.
Mentioning the role of negative pressure wound therapy in managing open fasciotomy wounds.
Explaining the systemic complications including hyperkalaemia, metabolic acidosis, and acute kidney injury from myoglobinuria, and the importance of aggressive I V fluid resuscitation.

Risks and complications
Mentioning Volkmann ischaemic contracture as the consequence of missed or delayed compartment syndrome in the forearm.
Mentioning the medico-legal significance of compartment syndrome and the importance of contemporaneous documentation.
Explaining that post-operative foot drop from common peroneal nerve damage is a recognised complication of lower leg compartment syndrome.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
