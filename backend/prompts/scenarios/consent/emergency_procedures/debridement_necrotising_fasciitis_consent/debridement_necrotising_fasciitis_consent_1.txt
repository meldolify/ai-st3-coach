ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Urgency and life-threatening nature
Explaining clearly and honestly that necrotising fasciitis is a life-threatening emergency. The infection spreads rapidly through the tissue beneath the skin and can cause organ failure if not treated urgently.
Emphasising that surgery is the most important part of treatment alongside antibiotics. The infection cannot be treated with antibiotics alone.
Explaining that the operation needs to happen as soon as possible, ideally within hours. Delay worsens the outcome.
Acknowledging the patient's fear and distress. Being honest about the severity while remaining calm and supportive.

Operative environment and anaesthesia
Explaining that the procedure is performed under G A. The patient will be put to sleep.
The anaesthetist will explain the details of the G A including any additional lines or monitoring that may be needed given the patient's current condition.
Mentioning that the I T U team is involved and the patient may go to intensive care after the operation rather than a normal ward.

Operative technique and extent of surgery
Explaining what debridement means in plain language: the surgeon will remove all the dead and infected tissue to stop the infection from spreading. This means cutting away skin, fat, and the tissue layer underneath called the fascia until healthy, bleeding tissue is reached.
Being honest that the amount of tissue removed may be extensive. It is not possible to know exactly how much needs to be removed until the operation is underway.
Explaining that the wound will be left open after surgery. It will not be stitched closed today. It will be dressed and covered.
Mentioning that tissue samples will be sent to the laboratory to identify the bacteria causing the infection.
Stating that the operation may take 1 to 2 hours but this depends on the extent of the infection.

Post-operative care and further procedures
Explaining that the patient will almost certainly need to go to intensive care after the operation for close monitoring, I V antibiotics, and organ support if needed.
Explaining that the patient will need to return to theatre, likely within 24 to 48 hours, for a second look to check that all the infected tissue has been removed. Further debridement may be needed.
Being honest that multiple operations are often required. Typically 2 to 4 trips to theatre.
Explaining that once the infection is controlled and the wound is clean, reconstruction will be planned. This may include skin grafts, flaps, or negative pressure wound therapy depending on the size and location of the wound.
Mentioning that the recovery is prolonged. The patient may be in hospital for weeks.

Risks and complications
Death: this must be mentioned sensitively. Necrotising fasciitis carries a significant mortality rate, approximately 20 to 30 percent, even with prompt treatment. The risk is higher with delayed diagnosis, older age, and co-morbidities.
Amputation: if the infection cannot be controlled, amputation of the limb may be necessary as a life-saving measure.
Extensive tissue loss: the amount of tissue removed may be greater than anticipated, resulting in significant wounds requiring reconstruction.
Organ failure: sepsis can cause failure of the kidneys, lungs, liver, and heart. The I T U team will manage these.
Bleeding: significant blood loss may occur. Blood transfusion may be needed.
Further surgery: multiple returns to theatre are expected, not a complication but an expected part of treatment.
Scarring: extensive scarring is inevitable given the nature of the surgery.
Prolonged hospital stay: weeks to months.
Psychological impact: the experience is traumatic. Psychological support will be offered.
Functional impairment: depending on the extent of tissue loss, there may be long-term effects on mobility and function.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Urgency and life-threatening nature
Explaining the pathophysiology in simple terms: the bacteria produce toxins that destroy the tissue and spread along the fascia faster than antibiotics can act, which is why surgery is essential.
Mentioning the LRINEC score as a clinical tool used to assess the likelihood of necrotising fasciitis.

Operative technique and extent of surgery
Explaining the finger test: if the tissue separates easily along the fascial plane with minimal resistance, this confirms necrotising fasciitis.
Mentioning that the dishwater grey appearance of the fascia and lack of bleeding are intraoperative signs of infection.
Mentioning that tissue may be sent for frozen section if available.

Post-operative care and further procedures
Explaining the role of negative pressure wound therapy, such as a V A C dressing, in managing the open wound between debridements.
Mentioning that hyperbaric oxygen therapy is used in some centres as an adjunct, though evidence is limited.
Mentioning that the microbiologist will guide antibiotic therapy based on culture and sensitivity results.

Risks and complications
Mentioning that the infection is not contagious to family members through normal contact.
Mentioning that risk factors for necrotising fasciitis include diabetes, immunosuppression, peripheral vascular disease, and obesity.
Discussing the possible need for long-term rehabilitation including physiotherapy and occupational therapy.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
