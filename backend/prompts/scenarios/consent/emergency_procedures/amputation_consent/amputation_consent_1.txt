ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Operative environment and anaesthesia
Explaining that the procedure may be performed under G A or regional anaesthesia such as an axillary block or wrist block depending on the level of amputation and patient preference.
If G A: meeting the anaesthetist who will explain the anaesthetic. A drip will be placed and a gas given to go to sleep.
Describing the sterile theatre environment and the team checking process.
Mentioning that a tourniquet will be used on the upper arm to control bleeding.

Operative technique and level of amputation
Explaining clearly and sensitively that the finger cannot be saved due to the extent of the crush injury and loss of blood supply, and that amputation is the safest option to prevent further complications.
Describing the level of amputation: through the proximal phalanx, preserving as much length as is safely possible.
Explaining that the surgeon will trim any damaged bone, identify and ligate the blood vessels, and identify and shorten the nerves cleanly to reduce the risk of painful nerve lumps.
Explaining that the skin will be closed over the stump using available local tissue to create a well-padded, pain-free stump. Absorbable or non-absorbable sutures will be used.
Explaining that the hand will be dressed with a bulky bandage.
Stating the approximate duration: 30 to 45 minutes.

Post-operative regime and rehabilitation
Explaining that the patient may go home the same day or stay overnight depending on pain control and how they are feeling.
Advising to keep the hand elevated to reduce swelling and pain.
Explaining the follow-up: dressing clinic in approximately 5 to 7 days for wound check. Sutures removed at 10 to 14 days if non-absorbable.
Advising to keep the wound clean and dry. Antibiotics and painkillers will be provided.
Explaining that a referral to hand therapy will be made to help with grip strengthening, adaptation, and return to function.
Advising on return to work: light duties from 2 to 3 weeks, heavier work once the wound is fully healed and comfortable, typically 4 to 6 weeks.
Advising against driving until comfortable gripping the steering wheel, and that insurance implications should be checked.

Psychological impact and functional expectations
Acknowledging the emotional impact of losing a finger and offering empathy. This is a significant event.
Explaining that most people adapt well and regain good hand function for daily activities.
Mentioning that the ring finger is not the most functionally critical digit and that grip and pinch can be preserved well.
Mentioning that prosthetic options exist, including cosmetic silicone finger prostheses, and a referral can be arranged.
Offering a referral for psychological support or counselling if needed.
Mentioning that the patient's wife or family can see them before the operation if time allows.

Risks and complications
Bleeding: may occur after the tourniquet is released. A haematoma may need drainage.
Infection: risk with any operation. Signs to watch for include increasing pain, redness, swelling, discharge, or fever.
Wound breakdown: the skin over the stump may break down, particularly if there is tension. This may require dressing changes or rarely further surgery.
Phantom sensation or phantom pain: the patient may feel as though the finger is still there, or may experience pain in the missing finger. This is common and usually settles but may require medication.
Neuroma: a painful lump of nerve tissue at the cut end of the nerve. May require further surgery.
Stiffness: of the remaining fingers, which should be kept moving.
Scar sensitivity: the stump may be tender for several weeks.
Revision surgery: rarely the stump may need to be revised if there are wound problems or a painful neuroma.
Cold intolerance: the stump may be sensitive to cold weather.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Operative technique and level of amputation
Explaining the principle of preserving maximum length while ensuring healthy, well-vascularised tissue for coverage.
Mentioning that the remaining metacarpal head is preserved to maintain the width of the palm and prevent objects falling through the gap.
Mentioning that a ray amputation, removing the entire metacarpal, is sometimes considered but narrows the hand and is not appropriate here.

Psychological impact and functional expectations
Discussing the stages of grief and loss that patients may experience.
Mentioning that occupational therapy can help with workplace adaptations.
Discussing the Industrial Injuries Disablement Benefit if the injury was work-related.

Risks and complications
Mentioning that pre-existing conditions such as diabetes or peripheral vascular disease can impair healing.
Mentioning D V T and P E as risks of any surgery under G A, though low for this short procedure.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
