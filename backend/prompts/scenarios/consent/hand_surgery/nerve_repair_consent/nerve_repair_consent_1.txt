ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Operative environment and anaesthesia
Explaining that the procedure is typically performed under L A with a digital nerve block or wrist block, with the patient awake.
Describing the injection: initial sting that settles, then numbness. Pressure and pulling will be felt but no sharp pain.
Explaining that the operation is performed under sterile conditions with cleaning solution, drapes, and the hand out on a side table.
Mentioning that a tourniquet may be used on the finger or upper arm to stop bleeding during the procedure.

Operative technique and findings
Explaining that the previous wound closure will be opened and the cut extended above and below in a zig-zag fashion to allow proper inspection of all structures.
Describing that the surgeon will check the tendons, nerves, and arteries on both sides of the finger.
Explaining the nerve repair: the two cut ends of the nerve are identified and repaired using very fine sutures under magnification with loupe glasses or a microscope. Typically 3 to 4 sutures are placed.
Mentioning that if a tendon injury is found it will be repaired at the same time. If a complete tendon injury is discovered, a G A may be needed and the plan may change.
Mentioning that occasionally the nerve may be bruised rather than cut and does not need repair, in which case sensation should recover on its own.
Explaining that the skin is closed with sutures, either absorbable or non-absorbable.
Stating the approximate duration: 30 to 45 minutes.

Post-operative regime and rehabilitation
Explaining that the patient can go home the same day after the operation.
Advising a follow-up in the hand therapy clinic in approximately 5 to 7 days for wound check and to begin gentle exercises.
Advising to keep the hand elevated, clean, and dry for the first week.
Explaining that antibiotics and painkillers will be given to take home.
Advising against driving with the operated hand until the wound heals, typically 10 to 14 days.
Advising 2 weeks off work, and avoiding manual labour, heavy lifting, and swimming for 4 weeks.
Offering to provide a sick note for the employer.

Sensory recovery expectations
Explaining clearly that the feeling does not come back immediately after nerve repair.
Stating that in adults sensory recovery can take 6 to 12 months and the sensation may not return to exactly what it was before.
Explaining that the recovered sensation may be different: the finger may feel less sensitive than before, or there may be an abnormal tingling quality.
Mentioning that younger patients tend to have better sensory recovery than older patients.

Risks and complications
Bleeding and infection.
Scar: may be longer than the original cut, may be raised, painful, or a different colour to surrounding skin.
Cold intolerance: the finger may be painful or uncomfortable in cold weather. This is common after nerve injury and repair.
Neuroma: a painful lump of nerve tissue that can develop at the repair site if the nerve does not heal properly. May require further surgery.
Complete loss of sensation: if the nerve does not recover, permanent numbness on the affected side of the finger.
Loss of sweating on the affected side of the finger.
Damage to adjacent structures: blood vessels, tendons, or the nerve on the other side of the finger.
Stiffness: of the finger joints, helped by hand therapy.
Complex Regional Pain Syndrome: rare, persistent pain, swelling, and stiffness requiring specialist referral.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Operative environment and anaesthesia
Offering sedation as an option if the patient is anxious about being awake.
Explaining that if the injury is more extensive than expected, the procedure may need to be converted to a G A.

Operative technique and findings
Mentioning that if there is a gap between the nerve ends, a nerve graft or conduit may be needed, though this is rare for digital nerve injuries.
Explaining that the quality of the repair depends on a tension-free anastomosis of the nerve ends.

Sensory recovery expectations
Explaining the concept of two-point discrimination and that it may not return to normal.
Mentioning that sensory re-education exercises prescribed by the hand therapist can improve the quality of recovery.

Risks and complications
Explaining that the original injury itself, not just the surgery, contributes to the final outcome.
Mentioning that chronic pain and hypersensitivity at the scar site may occur and can be helped with desensitisation exercises.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
