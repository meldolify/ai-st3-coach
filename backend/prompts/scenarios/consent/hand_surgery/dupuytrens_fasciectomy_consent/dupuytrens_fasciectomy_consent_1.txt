ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Operative environment and anaesthesia
Explaining the anaesthetic options: G A or regional anaesthesia such as an axillary nerve block, depending on the complexity and patient preference. Candidate should explain what being asleep involves or what a nerve block involves.
Describing the sterile theatre environment, checking in process, and that the team will introduce themselves and confirm details.
Mentioning the use of a tourniquet to provide a bloodless field.
Explaining that the procedure will be performed with the arm out on a side table.

Operative technique
Explaining that the surgeon makes a zig-zag or Brunner type incision over the affected area of the palm and finger.
Describing that the diseased thickened tissue called the Dupuytren's cord is carefully dissected away from the surrounding structures including the nerves, arteries, and tendons that run very close to it.
Explaining that the nerves and arteries in the finger may be displaced by the disease and must be carefully identified and protected.
Mentioning that the skin may be closed with sutures, or occasionally the wound may be left partially open to heal by itself if the skin is very tight, particularly over the finger.
Stating the approximate duration: 45 minutes to 1.5 hours depending on the extent of disease.

Post-operative regime and rehabilitation
Explaining that the hand will be placed in a bulky dressing and possibly a plaster splint.
Advising a follow-up appointment in the hand therapy clinic within 5 to 7 days for wound check and to start exercises.
Explaining the importance of hand therapy: the patient will need to attend regular sessions for exercises and may need a night splint to maintain correction, worn for up to 3 months.
Advising that driving should be avoided until the wound has healed and grip strength returns, typically 2 to 4 weeks.
Discussing return to work: light duties from 2 to 3 weeks, heavy manual work from 6 to 8 weeks.
Advising on keeping the wound clean and dry and avoiding smoking.

Risks and complications
Bleeding and haematoma: may require return to theatre for drainage.
Infection: higher risk if smoker or wound gets wet.
Nerve injury: the digital nerves run very close to the cord and may be displaced. They can be bruised or damaged during surgery, causing numbness or loss of sensation in the finger. This may be temporary or permanent.
Arterial injury: the digital arteries can also be damaged. If both arteries to a finger are injured, the blood supply to the finger tip may be compromised.
Scar: the incision is longer than expected. Scars can be thick, tender, or take time to soften.
Stiffness: the finger may remain stiff, particularly at the P I P joint, even after successful removal of the cord.
Recurrence: Dupuytren's disease can recur in the same finger or develop in other fingers or the other hand. Recurrence rates vary but can be up to 50 percent over time.
Incomplete correction: the finger may not become fully straight, particularly if there is a long-standing P I P joint contracture.
Wound healing problems: the skin may break down, particularly over the finger where it is tight. This may require dressing changes over several weeks.
Flare reaction: persistent swelling, stiffness, and pain similar to C R P S, which may require further treatment.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Operative environment and anaesthesia
Explaining the option of wide-awake local anaesthesia no tourniquet, known as W A L A N T, which avoids G A and tourniquet for some cases.

Operative technique
Mentioning the use of loupe magnification or an operating microscope to identify and protect the neurovascular bundles.
Explaining that Z-plasty or other local flap techniques may be used if the skin is contracted and cannot be closed primarily.
Explaining the difference between fasciectomy and the simpler needle fasciotomy or collagenase injection, and why surgery was chosen.

Post-operative regime and rehabilitation
Explaining that the night splint must be worn consistently to prevent the contracture from recurring during the healing phase.
Advising on scar massage and moisturising once the wound is healed.
Mentioning that a P I P joint contracture present for longer than a year may not fully correct even with surgery.

Risks and complications
Mentioning cold intolerance as a possible long-term symptom.
Mentioning C R P S as a rare but serious complication involving persistent pain, swelling, colour changes, and stiffness that may require specialist referral.
Explaining that if the disease is very extensive, a dermofasciectomy with skin graft may be needed to reduce recurrence risk, but this was not planned for this operation.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
