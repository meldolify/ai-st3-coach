ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Operative environment and anaesthesia
Explaining that the procedure is performed under L A with the patient awake.
Describing the L A injection process: injection at the base of the palm or wrist, initial stinging sensation that settles, then numbness. Candidate should reassure that sharp pain should not be felt, only pressure and pushing.
Explaining that the operation is performed under sterile conditions with cleaning solution and drapes.
Mentioning the use of a tourniquet on the upper arm similar to a blood pressure cuff to control bleeding, and that it may feel tight but is safe.

Operative technique
Explaining that a small incision is made at the base of the palm extending slightly towards the wrist.
Describing that the surgeon cuts through the ligament forming the roof of the carpal tunnel to release pressure on the median nerve.
Explaining that the skin is closed with non-dissolvable sutures that will need removal in approximately 10 to 14 days.
Mentioning that the hand will be dressed with a bandage and may be placed in a sling.
Stating the approximate duration of the operation: 15 to 30 minutes.

Post-operative regime and rehabilitation
Advising the patient they can go home the same day with an escort as they should not drive.
Explaining the importance of keeping the hand elevated for the first 48 hours to reduce swelling, using a pillow or sling.
Advising to keep the wound clean and dry until the dressing is reviewed.
Explaining that they will have a follow-up appointment in the hand therapy clinic in approximately 5 days for wound check and to start exercises.
Advising that sutures are typically removed at 10 to 14 days by the GP practice nurse or in clinic.
Discussing return to work: light duties from 2 weeks, manual or repetitive work from 4 to 6 weeks. A sick note can be provided.
Mentioning that driving should be avoided until sutures are removed and the patient can grip the steering wheel comfortably.
Stating that full recovery takes approximately 6 to 8 weeks.

Risks and complications
Bleeding: usually minor oozing that settles. Rarely a haematoma requiring return to theatre.
Infection: wound may become red, hot, swollen, or discharge pus. Advise to attend if this happens.
Scar: may not fade completely, may be a different colour to surrounding skin, rarely can be painful or thickened. Pillar pain at the base of the palm is common for several weeks.
Persistent or recurrent symptoms: numbness may not fully resolve, particularly if the nerve has been compressed for a long time. Occasionally symptoms are caused by compression elsewhere.
Damage to underlying structures: injury to the median nerve or its branches is rare but possible, which could worsen numbness or cause weakness. Injury to tendons is very rare.
Stiffness: usually temporary, helped by hand therapy exercises.
Complex Regional Pain Syndrome: rare, involving persistent pain, swelling, and stiffness. May require referral to a pain specialist.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Operative environment and anaesthesia
Explaining that if the nerve is very sensitive the patient may feel a brief electric shock sensation down the fingers during injection, which is temporary.
Offering sedation as an option if the patient is very anxious about being awake.

Operative technique
Mentioning that the transverse carpal ligament does not need to heal back together and the gap fills with scar tissue.
Explaining that care is taken to avoid the motor branch of the median nerve and the superficial palmar arch.

Post-operative regime and rehabilitation
Advising on scar massage once the wound is healed to help soften the scar.
Explaining that grip strength may take several months to return to full and that hand therapy exercises are important.
Advising the patient to take regular breaks if their job involves repetitive movements.

Risks and complications
Mentioning incomplete release of the ligament which may require reoperation.
Explaining that damage to the palmar cutaneous branch of the median nerve can cause a patch of numbness in the palm.
Mentioning D V T and P E as theoretical risks of any surgery, though extremely low for this short procedure.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
