ESSENTIAL MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Operative environment and anaesthesia
Explaining that the procedure is performed under L A with the patient awake.
Describing the injection of L A into the palm at the base of the affected finger. It will sting initially but the area will become numb within a minute or two. There should be no sharp pain during the operation, only pressure and pushing.
Explaining that the operation takes place in a minor operations theatre or main theatres under sterile conditions with cleaning solution and drapes.
Reassuring the patient that the team will introduce themselves and check details.

Operative technique
Explaining in simple terms what is being released: a tight tunnel at the base of the finger called the A1 pulley through which the tendon passes. The tendon has a nodule or thickening that catches on this tunnel, causing the clicking and locking.
Describing that a small incision, approximately 1 to 2 centimetres, is made in the palm at the base of the affected finger.
Explaining that the surgeon carefully divides the A1 pulley, which releases the tight tunnel and allows the tendon to glide freely.
Mentioning that the surgeon will ask the patient to bend and straighten the finger during the operation to confirm the triggering has resolved.
Explaining that the skin is closed with a few non-dissolvable sutures or sometimes adhesive strips.
Stating the approximate duration: 10 to 15 minutes.

Post-operative regime and rehabilitation
Advising the patient they can go home the same day. They should arrange an escort as driving immediately afterwards is not advisable.
Explaining that the hand should be kept elevated and the fingers should be moved regularly, bending and straightening, to prevent stiffness.
Advising to keep the wound clean and dry until it is reviewed.
Explaining the follow-up: dressing check in approximately 5 to 7 days. Sutures are removed at 10 to 14 days by the GP nurse or in clinic.
Advising that most patients can return to light work within a few days and to manual or gripping work within 2 to 3 weeks.
Advising against driving until the wound is comfortable and grip strength has returned, typically within 1 to 2 weeks.
Prescribing antibiotics and painkillers to take home.

Risks and complications
Bleeding: minor oozing is common and settles. Haematoma is rare.
Infection: risk is low. Signs to watch for include redness, swelling, discharge, or increasing pain.
Scar: small scar in the palm. May be tender for a few weeks. Rarely becomes thick or painful.
Nerve injury: the digital nerves run very close to the A1 pulley. Injury is rare but can cause numbness or tingling in the finger.
Tendon injury: very rare but possible during the release.
Stiffness: usually temporary and resolves with exercises. More common if the finger was locked in a bent position before surgery.
Recurrence: triggering can occasionally recur if the pulley is not fully released. Reoperation may be needed.
Incomplete release: if triggering persists after surgery, a further procedure may be required.
Bowstringing: very rare, where the tendon is no longer held close to the bone. Usually not clinically significant for the A1 pulley.

BONUS MARKING POINTS

(Internal reference only. Do not read aloud.)

Operative technique
Mentioning that care is taken to avoid the A2 pulley which is critical for finger function and must not be released.
Explaining the difference between open release and percutaneous needle release, and why the open approach was chosen for this patient.

Post-operative regime and rehabilitation
Advising on scar massage once the wound is healed.
Mentioning that some patients develop a fixed flexion deformity at the P I P joint if the trigger finger has been locked for a prolonged period, and this may not fully resolve after surgery.

Risks and complications
Mentioning that diabetic patients have a higher recurrence rate and may have multiple fingers involved.
Mentioning C R P S as a very rare complication.
Explaining that the A1 pulley does not need to heal back together and its division does not affect finger function.

These are points a strong candidate would mention but are not required to pass.
